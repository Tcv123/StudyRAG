1.3 Coordinate Geometry

Straight Lines

Key Formulae

FormulaExpression
Gradient\(m = \dfrac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}\)
Slope-intercept form\(y = mx + c\)
General form\(ax + by + c = 0\)
Point-gradient form\(y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)\)
Midpoint\(\left(\dfrac{x_1+x_2}{2},\ \dfrac{y_1+y_2}{2}\right)\)
Distance\(d = \sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2 + (y_2-y_1)^2}\)

Example: Find the equation of the line passing through \((2, 5)\) and \((6, 13)\).

Gradient: \(m = \dfrac{13 - 5}{6 - 2} = \dfrac{8}{4} = 2\)

Using point-gradient form with \((2, 5)\):

\(y - 5 = 2(x - 2) \Rightarrow y = 2x + 1\)

Parallel and Perpendicular Lines

  • Parallel lines have the same gradient: \(m_1 = m_2\)
  • Perpendicular lines have gradients whose product is \(-1\): \(m_1 \times m_2 = -1\), so \(m_2 = -\dfrac{1}{m_1}\)

Example: Find the equation of the line perpendicular to \(y = 3x - 1\) passing through \((6, 2)\).

Gradient of given line: \(m_1 = 3\). Perpendicular gradient: \(m_2 = -\dfrac{1}{3}\).

\(y - 2 = -\dfrac{1}{3}(x - 6) \Rightarrow y = -\dfrac{1}{3}x + 4\)

Exam Tip: When the question says "in the form \(ax + by + c = 0\)", multiply through to clear fractions and ensure \(a, b, c\) are integers. For example, \(y = -\frac{1}{3}x + 4\) becomes \(x + 3y - 12 = 0\).

Circles

Equation of a Circle

A circle with centre \((a, b)\) and radius \(r\) has equation:
$$(x - a)^2 + (y - b)^2 = r^2$$
The expanded (general) form is:
$$x^2 + y^2 + 2gx + 2fy + c = 0$$
where centre = \((-g, -f)\) and radius = \(\sqrt{g^2 + f^2 - c}\).

Example: Find the centre and radius of \(x^2 + y^2 - 6x + 4y - 12 = 0\).

Complete the square:

\((x^2 - 6x + 9) + (y^2 + 4y + 4) = 12 + 9 + 4\)

\((x - 3)^2 + (y + 2)^2 = 25\)

Centre: \((3, -2)\), Radius: \(5\).

Tangents and Normals to a Circle

  • The tangent to a circle at a point is perpendicular to the radius at that point.
  • The normal to a circle at a point passes through the centre (it is the radius).

Example: Find the equation of the tangent to \((x-2)^2 + (y-3)^2 = 25\) at the point \((5, 7)\).

Centre: \((2, 3)\). Gradient of radius to \((5, 7)\):

\(m_{\text{radius}} = \dfrac{7-3}{5-2} = \dfrac{4}{3}\)

Gradient of tangent: \(m_{\text{tangent}} = -\dfrac{3}{4}\)

Equation: \(y - 7 = -\dfrac{3}{4}(x - 5)\)

\(4y - 28 = -3x + 15 \Rightarrow 3x + 4y - 43 = 0\)

Intersection of a Line and Circle

Substitute the linear equation into the circle equation to get a quadratic. The discriminant tells you:

  • \(\Delta > 0\): line intersects circle at 2 points (secant)
  • \(\Delta = 0\): line is tangent to circle (touches at 1 point)
  • \(\Delta < 0\): line does not meet the circle

Example: Show that \(y = x + 5\) is a tangent to \(x^2 + y^2 = 12.5\).

Substitute: \(x^2 + (x+5)^2 = 12.5\)

\(x^2 + x^2 + 10x + 25 = 12.5\)

\(2x^2 + 10x + 12.5 = 0 \Rightarrow 4x^2 + 20x + 25 = 0\)

Discriminant: \(b^2 - 4ac = 400 - 400 = 0\).

Since \(\Delta = 0\), the line is a tangent to the circle. \(\square\)

Parametric Equations

Parametric equations define a curve by expressing \(x\) and \(y\) separately in terms of a third variable (the parameter), usually \(t\) or \(\theta\).
$$x = f(t), \quad y = g(t)$$

Converting Between Parametric and Cartesian Forms

Eliminate the parameter \(t\) to get a Cartesian equation relating \(x\) and \(y\) directly.

Example: A curve is defined by \(x = 2t\), \(y = t^2 - 1\). Find the Cartesian equation.

From \(x = 2t\): \(t = \dfrac{x}{2}\).

Substitute into \(y\): \(y = \left(\dfrac{x}{2}\right)^2 - 1 = \dfrac{x^2}{4} - 1\).

Cartesian equation: \(y = \dfrac{x^2}{4} - 1\) (a parabola).

Example: A curve has parametric equations \(x = 3\cos\theta\), \(y = 3\sin\theta\). Find the Cartesian equation.

Use the identity \(\cos^2\theta + \sin^2\theta = 1\):

\(\left(\dfrac{x}{3}\right)^2 + \left(\dfrac{y}{3}\right)^2 = 1 \Rightarrow x^2 + y^2 = 9\)

This is a circle with centre \((0,0)\) and radius 3.

Parametric Differentiation

If \(x = f(t)\) and \(y = g(t)\), then by the chain rule:
$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy/dt}{dx/dt}$$
This gives the gradient of the curve at the point with parameter \(t\).

Example: A curve is defined by \(x = t^2\), \(y = 2t^3 - t\). Find \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\) in terms of \(t\).

\(\dfrac{dx}{dt} = 2t\), \(\quad \dfrac{dy}{dt} = 6t^2 - 1\)

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{6t^2 - 1}{2t}$$

Example: Find the equation of the tangent to the curve \(x = 3t\), \(y = t^2\) at the point where \(t = 2\).

At \(t = 2\): \(x = 6\), \(y = 4\).

\(\dfrac{dx}{dt} = 3\), \(\quad \dfrac{dy}{dt} = 2t = 4\)

\(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{4}{3}\)

Tangent: \(y - 4 = \dfrac{4}{3}(x - 6) \Rightarrow y = \dfrac{4}{3}x - 4\)

Example: Find the equation of the normal to \(x = \sin\theta\), \(y = \cos 2\theta\) at \(\theta = \dfrac{\pi}{6}\).

At \(\theta = \frac{\pi}{6}\): \(x = \sin\frac{\pi}{6} = \frac{1}{2}\), \(y = \cos\frac{\pi}{3} = \frac{1}{2}\).

\(\dfrac{dx}{d\theta} = \cos\theta = \dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\), \(\quad \dfrac{dy}{d\theta} = -2\sin 2\theta = -2\sin\dfrac{\pi}{3} = -\sqrt{3}\)

\(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{-\sqrt{3}}{\sqrt{3}/2} = -2\)

Gradient of normal: \(\dfrac{1}{2}\).

Normal: \(y - \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{2}(x - \frac{1}{2}) \Rightarrow y = \frac{1}{2}x + \frac{1}{4}\)

Exam Tip: When finding tangent or normal equations from parametric forms:
  1. Find \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\) at the given parameter value.
  2. Find the \((x, y)\) coordinates by substituting the parameter value.
  3. Use the point-gradient form \(y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)\).
For the normal, use gradient \(-\dfrac{1}{m}\) where \(m\) is the tangent gradient.

Circle Geometry Theorems in Coordinate Problems

Several circle geometry theorems are useful tools in A-Level coordinate geometry questions:

  • Angle in a semicircle: If AB is a diameter and P is any other point on the circle, then \(\angle APB = 90°\). This means \(\overrightarrow{PA} \cdot \overrightarrow{PB} = 0\) in coordinate terms.
  • Perpendicular bisector of a chord: The perpendicular bisector of any chord passes through the centre of the circle. Use this to find the centre when given two chords.
  • Tangent from external point: The two tangents drawn from an external point to a circle are equal in length.

Example: The points \(A(1, 6)\) and \(B(7, -2)\) are the ends of a diameter. Find the equation of the circle and show that the point \(P(7, 6)\) lies on the circle. Verify that \(\angle APB = 90°\).

Centre = midpoint of AB = \(\left(\dfrac{1+7}{2}, \dfrac{6-2}{2}\right) = (4, 2)\)

Radius = \(\dfrac{1}{2}|AB| = \dfrac{1}{2}\sqrt{(7-1)^2+(-2-6)^2} = \dfrac{1}{2}\sqrt{36+64} = \dfrac{10}{2} = 5\)

Equation: \((x-4)^2 + (y-2)^2 = 25\)

P on circle: \((7-4)^2 + (6-2)^2 = 9 + 16 = 25\) ✓

Check \(\angle APB = 90°\):

\(\overrightarrow{PA} = A - P = (1-7, 6-6) = (-6, 0)\)

\(\overrightarrow{PB} = B - P = (7-7, -2-6) = (0, -8)\)

\(\overrightarrow{PA} \cdot \overrightarrow{PB} = (-6)(0) + (0)(-8) = 0\) ✓ — confirms \(\angle APB = 90°\) (angle in semicircle). \(\square\)

Example: A circle passes through \(A(2, 5)\), \(B(6, 1)\), and \(C(0, 3)\). Find the centre using perpendicular bisectors.

The centre lies on the perpendicular bisector of AB and of BC.

Perp bisector of AB: Midpoint = (4, 3). Gradient AB = \(\dfrac{1-5}{6-2} = -1\). Perp gradient = 1.

Equation: \(y - 3 = 1(x - 4) \Rightarrow y = x - 1\)   ...(1)

Perp bisector of BC: Midpoint = (3, 2). Gradient BC = \(\dfrac{3-1}{0-6} = -\dfrac{1}{3}\). Perp gradient = 3.

Equation: \(y - 2 = 3(x - 3) \Rightarrow y = 3x - 7\)   ...(2)

Solve: \(x - 1 = 3x - 7 \Rightarrow 6 = 2x \Rightarrow x = 3\), \(y = 2\). Centre = (3, 2).

Radius = \(|CA| = \sqrt{(2-3)^2+(5-2)^2} = \sqrt{1+9} = \sqrt{10}\).

Equation: \((x-3)^2 + (y-2)^2 = 10\).

Exam Tip: If a question gives three points on a circle, use perpendicular bisectors of two chords — their intersection is the centre. If it gives a diameter, the centre is the midpoint and you can use the "angle in semicircle = 90°" property directly.

Exam Strategy Summary

Key points for coordinate geometry questions:
  • Always draw a sketch -- even a rough one helps avoid sign errors.
  • For circle questions, identify the centre and radius first by completing the square if needed.
  • Use the perpendicular gradient relationship \(m_1 m_2 = -1\) for tangent/normal problems.
  • For parametric differentiation, always use \(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{dy/dt}{dx/dt}\), not the other way round.
  • When converting parametric to Cartesian, look for identities you can use (e.g. \(\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta = 1\)).
  • Check your answers by substituting back into the original equations.
  • Three points on a circle: find the centre by intersecting two perpendicular bisectors of chords.
  • Diameter endpoints given: use midpoint for centre, half-distance for radius, and \(\angle\) in semicircle = 90°.
