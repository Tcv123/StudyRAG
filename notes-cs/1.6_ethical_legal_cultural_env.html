1.6 Ethical, Legal, Cultural and Environmental Impacts

OCR GCSE Computer Science (J277) — Paper 1

Digital technology affects every aspect of modern society. Exam questions on this topic expect you to discuss both sides of issues (benefits AND drawbacks), reference specific legislation by name and year, and give considered, balanced arguments. This is one of the few areas where there are no definitively "right" answers — the marks come from the quality of your reasoning and the breadth of your discussion.

1.6.1 Ethical Issues

Ethics in computing refers to the moral principles that guide how technology should be developed, deployed, and used. Ethical issues are not the same as legal issues — something can be legal but unethical (e.g., a company collecting excessive personal data within the law), or illegal but arguably ethical (e.g., a whistleblower exposing harmful practices).

Privacy and Surveillance

Mass data collection is now routine. Companies track browsing history, location, purchases, social media activity, and communications — often without users' full understanding or informed consent. Governments use surveillance cameras, facial recognition, and communications monitoring for law enforcement and national security.

  • Arguments for surveillance: Helps prevent and solve crime and terrorism; protects national security; can improve public services (e.g., traffic management); assists in finding missing persons; deters antisocial behaviour.
  • Arguments against: Infringes the right to privacy (a fundamental human right); creates a "chilling effect" on free speech (people self-censor if they know they are being watched); risk of misuse by authoritarian governments; disproportionately affects minority communities; data breaches can expose sensitive personal information; the power imbalance between those surveilling and those being surveilled.

AI and Algorithmic Bias

Artificial intelligence systems increasingly make or influence decisions about people — in hiring, criminal sentencing, credit scoring, healthcare, insurance, and content recommendation. These systems learn from historical data, which may contain biases.

  • Arguments for AI decision-making: Can process more information than humans; more consistent (does not have bad days); faster; can identify patterns humans miss; potentially removes individual human prejudice.
  • Arguments against: If trained on biased data, AI amplifies and systematises those biases at scale; "black box" decisions are difficult to explain, challenge, or appeal; accountability is unclear when an AI makes a harmful decision (who is responsible — the programmer, the company, the user?); can perpetuate discrimination against protected groups.

Digital Divide

The digital divide is the gap between those who have access to modern technology and internet connectivity and those who do not. This divide exists along several dimensions:

  • Economic: Low-income households may not be able to afford devices, broadband, or data plans.
  • Geographic: Rural areas often have slower or no broadband compared to urban areas.
  • Global: Developing nations have far lower internet penetration than developed nations.
  • Age: Older generations may lack digital skills or confidence with technology.
  • Impact: Those without access are excluded from online education, banking, healthcare appointments, government services, job applications, and social connections. The COVID-19 pandemic highlighted how those without internet access were severely disadvantaged when schools and workplaces moved online.

Automation and Employment

Robots, AI, and software automation are replacing human workers across many industries — manufacturing, customer service (chatbots), transport (self-driving vehicles), retail (self-checkout), data analysis, and even creative fields.

  • Arguments for automation: Increases efficiency and reduces costs; eliminates dangerous, repetitive, or unpleasant jobs; can work 24/7 without breaks; higher quality and consistency; frees humans for more creative, fulfilling work; new technology industries create new types of jobs.
  • Arguments against: Mass job losses cause economic hardship, particularly for low-skilled workers; widening income inequality (benefits go to company owners, not displaced workers); ethical duty to retrain affected workers; transition period can be painful for communities; new jobs may require different skills that displaced workers lack; social and psychological impact of unemployment.

Cyberbullying and Online Harassment

Technology enables harassment at unprecedented scale. Online abuse can be anonymous, persistent (difficult to remove permanently), searchable, and reach a global audience. Particularly harmful to young people.

  • Platforms have a responsibility to moderate content, provide reporting mechanisms, and protect vulnerable users.
  • Victims may suffer serious mental health impacts including anxiety, depression, isolation, and self-harm.
  • Schools, parents, and technology companies all share responsibility for education, prevention, and response.
  • Legislation (such as the Online Safety Act 2023) places legal duties on platforms to protect users from harmful content.

Misinformation and Deepfakes

Social media algorithms are designed to maximise engagement, which means they often amplify sensational, controversial, or emotionally provocative content — including false information. Deepfake technology (AI-generated fake video/audio) makes it increasingly difficult to distinguish real from fabricated content.

  • Misinformation undermines trust in institutions, science, and democratic processes.
  • Particularly dangerous during elections (propaganda), public health crises (anti-vaccine misinformation), and conflicts (disinformation).
  • Solutions include fact-checking, media literacy education, platform moderation, and technical detection tools — but none is fully effective.
For ethical questions, always present both sides of the argument. Structure your answer: state the issue, give arguments for, give arguments against, and (if asked) give your reasoned conclusion. Do not just list points — explain and develop them. Use specific examples where possible. The best answers show awareness that these issues involve genuine tensions between competing values (privacy vs security, efficiency vs employment, innovation vs equality).

1.6.2 Key UK Legislation

You must know four key pieces of legislation by name and year, understand what each covers, and be able to apply them to specific scenarios.

Data Protection Act 2018 / UK GDPR

The Data Protection Act 2018 (incorporating the UK GDPR) controls the collection, storage, processing, and use of personal data. It replaced the 1998 Act and strengthened individuals' rights over their data.

Key principles — organisations must:

  1. Lawfulness, fairness, transparency — process data lawfully and be transparent about how it is used.
  2. Purpose limitation — collect data only for specified, explicit purposes and not use it for anything else.
  3. Data minimisation — collect only the data that is necessary (do not collect data "just in case").
  4. Accuracy — keep data accurate and up to date.
  5. Storage limitation — do not keep data longer than necessary; delete it when no longer needed.
  6. Security — store data securely using appropriate technical measures (encryption, access controls, secure backups).

Individual rights:

  • Right of access — request a copy of all personal data held about you (Subject Access Request).
  • Right to rectification — have inaccurate data corrected.
  • Right to erasure — request deletion of your data ("right to be forgotten") in certain circumstances.
  • Right to object — object to processing of your data for direct marketing.
  • Right to data portability — receive your data in a machine-readable format to transfer to another service.

Enforced by the ICO (Information Commissioner's Office) with fines up to 17.5 million pounds or 4% of global annual turnover (whichever is greater).

Computer Misuse Act 1990

The Computer Misuse Act 1990 makes it a criminal offence to access or modify computer systems without authorisation. It was introduced in response to early hacking cases and has been amended several times to address evolving cyber threats.

Three key sections (frequently examined):

SectionOffenceExampleMaximum Penalty
Section 1Unauthorised access to computer materialGuessing or cracking someone's password to access their email or social media account. Even just looking at data you are not authorised to see, without modifying anything, is an offence.Up to 2 years imprisonment
Section 2Unauthorised access with intent to commit a further offenceHacking into a bank system with the intention of committing fraud; accessing a database to steal personal data for identity theft.Up to 5 years imprisonment
Section 3Unauthorised modification of computer materialDeploying malware (viruses, ransomware); deleting someone's files; launching a DDoS attack; modifying a website without permission (defacement).Up to 10 years imprisonment

Section 3A (added 2006) makes it illegal to make, supply, or obtain tools intended for use in computer misuse offences.

Important distinction: Penetration testing (ethical hacking) is legal when performed with written authorisation from the system owner. Without authorisation, the same actions are criminal offences under this Act.

Copyright, Designs and Patents Act 1988

The Copyright, Designs and Patents Act 1988 protects creative works from being copied, distributed, modified, or sold without the creator's permission. Copyright is automatic — no registration is needed. The creator owns the copyright as soon as the work is created.

What is protected: Software source code and executables; music, audio recordings, and song lyrics; photographs and digital images; videos and films; written content (articles, books, blog posts, documentation); website designs; databases; game designs; artistic works.

Common violations in computing:

  • Pirating software (downloading cracked versions or sharing licence keys)
  • Downloading music, films, or TV shows illegally (torrenting copyrighted content)
  • Copying code from another programmer without permission or proper licence
  • Using images from the internet without the photographer's permission
  • Copying and redistributing e-books or digital publications

Copyright lasts for the creator's lifetime plus 70 years. Penalties include fines and up to 10 years imprisonment for serious commercial piracy.

Freedom of Information Act 2000

Gives the public the right to request information held by public authorities (government departments, councils, NHS, police, schools, universities). Public authorities must respond within 20 working days. Exemptions exist for national security, personal data, law enforcement, and commercial sensitivity. Promotes government transparency and accountability. Enforced by the ICO.

1.6.3 Software Licences

Software licences define how software can be used, modified, and distributed. Understanding the difference between open source and proprietary software is essential.

FeatureOpen SourceProprietary (Closed Source)
Source codePublicly available — anyone can view, study, learn from, and audit itKept secret — only the compiled executable is distributed. Users cannot see how it works internally.
CostUsually free to download, use, and distribute. Some companies offer paid support or premium features.Usually requires purchase (one-time licence fee, subscription, or freemium model)
ModificationUsers can modify and customise the software. Can adapt it for specific needs, fix bugs themselves, or add features.Modification is not permitted. Users must use the software as-is and wait for the vendor to make changes.
DistributionCan be freely redistributed (subject to licence terms like GPL, MIT, Apache). GPL requires that derivative works also be open source.Cannot be copied, shared, or redistributed without the publisher's permission. Licence is typically per-user or per-device.
SupportCommunity-driven: forums, documentation, Stack Overflow, GitHub issues. Paid professional support available from specialist companies (e.g., Red Hat for Linux).Professional vendor support: phone, email, live chat, guaranteed response times (SLAs). Training and certification programmes.
SecurityTransparent code means vulnerabilities can be found and fixed by anyone in the community — "many eyes make all bugs shallow." But attackers can also read the code to find exploits."Security through obscurity" — code is hidden. Vulnerabilities are found and fixed by the vendor on their schedule. Users depend entirely on the vendor to issue patches promptly.
ExamplesLinux, Firefox, LibreOffice, Python, VLC, Android (AOSP), WordPress, Apache, MySQLMicrosoft Windows, Microsoft Office, Adobe Photoshop, macOS, iOS, Final Cut Pro

Recommending a Licence Type for a Scenario

Scenario: A small charity with limited budget needs office software.
→ Open source (LibreOffice). Free to use, reducing costs. Community
  support is available. The charity does not need advanced features
  that justify the cost of Microsoft Office.

Scenario: A large bank needs a database system for financial records.
→ Proprietary (e.g., Oracle). Professional vendor support with
  guaranteed response times (SLA) is critical for a bank. The bank
  can afford the licence cost and needs the reliability, compliance
  features, and support guarantees that come with enterprise software.

Scenario: A student learning to program.
→ Open source (Python, VS Code). Free to use, widely documented,
  large learning community. Being able to read source code aids
  learning. No cost barrier to getting started.

Scenario: A graphic design studio producing professional work.
→ Proprietary (Adobe Photoshop/Illustrator). Industry-standard
  tools with no open-source equivalent of the same quality.
  Client compatibility requires specific file formats. Professional
  support and training available.
