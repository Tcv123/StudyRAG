1.6 Ethical, Legal, Cultural and Environmental Impacts

OCR GCSE Computer Science (J277) — Paper 1

Digital technology affects society in many ways. Exam questions expect you to discuss both sides of issues (benefits AND drawbacks) and reference specific legislation by name and year.

1.6.1 Ethical Issues

IssueKey PointsArguments For / Against
Privacy and surveillanceMass data collection (browsing history, location, purchases) often happens without full informed consent. Facial recognition identifies individuals in public spaces. Governments and companies can monitor communications at scale.For: Helps prevent crime and terrorism; protects national security. Against: Infringes right to privacy; chilling effect on free speech; risk of misuse; disproportionately affects minorities.
AI and algorithmic biasAI systems make automated decisions in hiring, policing, credit scoring, healthcare, and justice. If trained on biased historical data, they perpetuate and amplify those biases.For: AI can be more consistent and faster than humans; removes some human biases. Against: "Black box" decisions are hard to explain or challenge; can discriminate against protected groups; accountability is unclear.
Digital divideUnequal access to technology across rich/poor, urban/rural, and developed/developing nations. Also an age divide (older generations may lack digital skills).Those without access are excluded from online education, banking, healthcare, government services, and employment opportunities. Widens existing inequality.
Automation and unemploymentRobots and AI replacing human workers in manufacturing, customer service, transport, retail, and data analysis.For: Increases efficiency, reduces costs, eliminates dangerous jobs. Against: Job losses cause economic hardship; ethical duty to retrain workers; benefits may not be shared equally. New job types may emerge but require different skills.
CyberbullyingTechnology enables harassment, trolling, and abuse at unprecedented scale. Online abuse can be anonymous, persistent (hard to remove), and reach a wide audience. Particularly harmful to young people.Platforms have a responsibility to moderate content and protect users. Victims may suffer serious mental health impacts including anxiety, depression, and self-harm. Schools and parents need education strategies.
MisinformationSocial media algorithms can amplify fake news, conspiracy theories, and propaganda because sensational content drives engagement. Deepfakes make it harder to distinguish real from fabricated content.Undermines trust in institutions, science, and democracy. Particularly dangerous during elections and public health crises. Fact-checking and media literacy are partial solutions.

1.6.2 Key UK Legislation

ActYearKey ProvisionsWhy It Matters for CS
Data Protection Act / UK GDPR 2018 Controls the collection, storage, processing, and use of personal data. Organisations must: process data lawfully and transparently; collect only what is necessary; keep data accurate and up to date; store it securely; delete it when no longer needed. Individuals have rights to: access their data, correct inaccuracies, request deletion ("right to be forgotten"), and object to processing. Enforced by the ICO (Information Commissioner's Office) with fines up to £17.5 million or 4% of global turnover. Any system that stores personal data (name, email, address, health records, browsing history) must comply. Developers must implement encryption, access controls, and data minimisation.
Computer Misuse Act 1990 Section 1: Unauthorised access to computer material (e.g., guessing someone's password to access their account) — up to 2 years imprisonment.
Section 2: Unauthorised access with intent to commit a further offence (e.g., hacking to commit fraud) — up to 5 years.
Section 3: Unauthorised modification of computer material (e.g., deploying malware, deleting files, DDoS attacks) — up to 10 years.
Section 3A: Making/supplying/obtaining tools for computer misuse.		 Protects computer systems from hacking, malware, and sabotage. Even accessing a system "just to look" without permission is a criminal offence under Section 1.
Copyright, Designs and Patents Act 1988 Protects creative works (software source code, music, images, videos, written content, website designs) from being copied, distributed, modified, or sold without the creator's permission. Copyright is automatic — no registration needed. Lasts for the creator's lifetime plus 70 years. Pirating software, downloading music illegally, copying code without a licence, and using images from the internet without permission are all violations.
Freedom of Information Act 2000 Gives the public the right to request information held by public authorities (government, councils, NHS, police, schools). Must respond within 20 working days. Exemptions exist for national security, personal data, and law enforcement. Promotes government transparency and accountability. Enforced by the ICO.

1.6.3 Software Licences

FeatureOpen SourceProprietary (Closed Source)
Source codePublicly available — anyone can view, study, and learn from itKept secret — only the compiled executable is distributed
CostUsually free to download, use, and distributeUsually requires purchase (one-time licence or subscription)
ModificationUsers can modify and customise the software to suit their needsModification is not permitted — users must use it as-is
DistributionCan be freely redistributed (subject to licence terms like GPL or MIT)Cannot be copied or shared without the publisher's permission
SupportCommunity-driven (forums, documentation); paid support available from specialist companiesProfessional vendor support (phone, email, SLA guarantees)
SecurityCode is transparent — vulnerabilities can be found and fixed by anyone. "Many eyes make all bugs shallow." But attackers can also read the code."Security through obscurity" — code is hidden. Vulnerabilities are fixed by the vendor on their schedule. Users depend on the vendor to issue patches promptly.
ExamplesLinux, Firefox, LibreOffice, Python, VLC, Android (AOSP), WordPressMicrosoft Windows, Microsoft Office, Adobe Photoshop, macOS, iOS

When to recommend each:

  • Open source is better when: budget is limited; customisation is needed; the user has technical skills; community support is sufficient; vendor independence is important.
  • Proprietary is better when: professional vendor support is essential; the software is specialised with no open-source equivalent; ease of use and polish are priorities; the organisation can afford the licence cost.

1.6.4 Cultural and Environmental Impacts

Cultural Impacts of Technology

  • Social media connects people globally but also spreads misinformation, creates echo chambers, and can harm mental health
  • Remote working enabled by video conferencing and cloud tools provides flexibility but can blur work-life boundaries and increase isolation
  • Streaming services (Netflix, Spotify) disrupted traditional TV, music, and film industries, changing how content is created and consumed
  • E-commerce (Amazon, online shopping) grew significantly; high street shops declined, changing town centres and communities
  • Online education increases access to learning but may disadvantage those without reliable internet or devices (digital divide)
  • Gaming culture is now mainstream entertainment but raises concerns about addiction, screen time, and online safety for young people
  • Digital communication (messaging, video calls) keeps families and friends connected but may reduce face-to-face interaction skills

Environmental Impacts

Negative ImpactsPositive Impacts
Data centres consume enormous amounts of electricity (1-2% of global electricity) for computing and cooling. AI training (large language models) has a significant carbon footprint.Smart grids and IoT sensors optimise energy distribution, reducing waste and integrating renewable sources more effectively.
E-waste — discarded electronics contain toxic materials (lead, mercury, cadmium, lithium). Much e-waste is exported to developing countries for unsafe informal recycling.Remote working reduces commuting (fewer cars on the road), office heating/cooling, and business travel, significantly cutting carbon emissions.
Mining rare earth metals (cobalt, lithium, tantalum) for device manufacturing causes environmental destruction, water pollution, and human exploitation in developing countries.Precision agriculture uses sensors, drones, and AI to optimise water usage, fertiliser application, and crop monitoring, reducing environmental impact.
Planned obsolescence — manufacturers design devices with short lifespans or stop providing software updates, creating unnecessary waste.Electric vehicle technology and smart traffic management reduce transport emissions.
Manufacturing carbon footprint — producing and shipping devices worldwide generates significant greenhouse gas emissions.Environmental monitoring using satellite imagery, sensor networks, and AI tracks deforestation, pollution, wildlife populations, and climate change.
Dematerialisation — digital alternatives reduce physical resource use (e-books, streaming music, video conferencing replacing travel, digital tickets).
