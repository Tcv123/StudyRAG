5.4 Cloning

Edexcel International GCSE Biology (4BI1) — Paper 2

5.4.1 What is a clone?

A clone is a genetically identical organism or cell produced asexually from a single parent. Some clones occur naturally (bacterial binary fission, strawberry runners, identical twins). Others are created in the lab.

5.4.2 Plant cloning — micropropagation

  1. Take small pieces (explants) of tissue from a chosen parent plant.
  2. Sterilise with bleach to kill microbes.
  3. Place on nutrient agar with auxin and cytokinin hormones in sterile conditions.
  4. Callus (undifferentiated cells) forms → plantlets develop.
  5. Transfer plantlets to compost; they grow into mature plants identical to the parent.

Uses: mass-produce virus-free commercial crops (bananas, orchids, strawberries); preserve rare varieties.

5.4.3 Animal cloning — nuclear transfer

Famous example: Dolly the sheep (1996).

  1. Nucleus removed from a Finn-Dorset ewe's udder cell.
  2. Egg cell from a Scottish Blackface ewe had its nucleus removed (enucleation).
  3. Udder-cell nucleus inserted into the enucleated egg.
  4. Electric shock fused the cell — it began dividing like a zygote.
  5. Embryo implanted into a Scottish Blackface surrogate.
  6. Dolly was born — a clone of the Finn-Dorset udder-cell donor.

5.4.4 Embryo splitting

Used to produce identical twin cattle from the embryo of a prize cow. The early embryo is split into cells, each implanted into a surrogate mother — giving multiple genetically identical calves.

5.4.5 Advantages and disadvantages

  • ✓ Preserves a desirable combination of alleles.
  • ✓ Many copies produced quickly.
  • ✓ Plant cloning: cheap mass production of virus-free stock.
  • ✗ Low genetic variation — population vulnerable to disease.
  • ✗ Animal cloning has a low success rate; many clones suffer premature ageing or developmental problems.
  • ✗ Ethical concerns, especially for cloning mammals and humans.
Plant cells often remain totipotent — can form a whole plant from a small piece. Animal cells differentiate early and are harder to reprogramme, which is why animal cloning is much less reliable than plant micropropagation.
