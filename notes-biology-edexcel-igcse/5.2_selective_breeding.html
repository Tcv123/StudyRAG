5.2 Selective Breeding

Edexcel International GCSE Biology (4BI1) — Paper 2

5.2.1 What it is

Selective breeding (artificial selection) is the process by which humans choose individuals with desired features to breed together, so that these features become more common in future generations.

5.2.2 How it works

  1. Identify individuals with desired traits (e.g. highest milk yield, disease resistance).
  2. Breed these together.
  3. From offspring, select those that show the desired trait most strongly.
  4. Repeat for many generations.

5.2.3 Examples

  • Dairy cattle bred for large milk yield.
  • Beef cattle bred for muscle mass.
  • Wheat bred for high grain yield, short stem (won't lodge), disease resistance.
  • Dogs bred for appearance, temperament, herding ability.

5.2.4 Advantages and disadvantages

  • ✓ Increases yield, disease resistance, quality.
  • ✓ Uses existing natural variation — no need for laboratory manipulation.
  • ✗ Reduces genetic diversity → whole population more vulnerable to disease or climate shift.
  • ✗ Inbreeding can bring out harmful recessive alleles (e.g. hip dysplasia in Labradors, breathing problems in bulldogs).
  • ✗ Slow — many generations required.
Compare selective breeding with genetic modification: both aim for desired traits. Selective breeding uses natural mating over many generations within a species; GM inserts a specific gene (often from another species) in one generation. GM is faster but more tightly regulated and more controversial.
