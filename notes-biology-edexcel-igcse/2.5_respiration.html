2.5 Respiration

Edexcel International GCSE Biology (4BI1) — Paper 1

2.5.1 What is respiration?

Respiration is the chemical breakdown of nutrients inside cells to release energy as ATP. It happens in every living cell, not just during exercise. Do not confuse it with "breathing" (ventilation).

2.5.2 Aerobic Respiration

Requires oxygen. Happens mainly in mitochondria.
Word: glucose + oxygen → carbon dioxide + water (+ lots of ATP)
Symbol: C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O.
Yields about 32 ATP per glucose.

2.5.4 Oxygen Debt

During intense exercise, O₂ cannot reach muscles fast enough — they respire anaerobically and produce lactic acid, which causes fatigue. After exercise, breathing and heart rate stay high to deliver extra O₂ to the liver, where lactic acid is oxidised back to CO₂ and water. This extra O₂ is the oxygen debt.

"Why does breathing stay fast after a sprint?" — because lactic acid built up during anaerobic respiration must be broken down in the liver, and this needs oxygen (oxygen debt repayment).

Uses of respiration energy

  • Muscle contraction
  • Active transport
  • Protein synthesis (on ribosomes)
  • Cell division
  • Maintaining body temperature (mammals & birds)
  • Nerve impulse conduction
