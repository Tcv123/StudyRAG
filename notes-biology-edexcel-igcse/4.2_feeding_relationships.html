4.2 Feeding Relationships

Edexcel International GCSE Biology (4BI1) — Paper 2

4.2.1 Food chains and trophic levels

Energy and biomass flow through ecosystems from producers (autotrophs) to consumers (heterotrophs). Arrows point from prey to predator — i.e. show the direction of energy and biomass transfer.

Example chain: grass → rabbit → fox → eagle. Trophic levels: producer → primary consumer → secondary consumer → tertiary consumer.

4.2.2 Energy loss at each step

Only ~10% of energy is transferred to the next level. Losses include:

  • Respiration (heat)
  • Movement
  • Undigested food (egested as faeces)
  • Nitrogenous waste (urine)
  • Parts not eaten (bones, fur, roots)

Pyramid of numbers vs pyramid of biomass

  • Numbers: can be inverted (e.g. one oak tree supports thousands of caterpillars).
  • Biomass: nearly always upright, because total mass has to fall as energy is lost.

4.2.3 Why food chains are short

After ~10% efficiency at each step, by the 4th or 5th level there isn't enough energy left to support another trophic level. This is why top predators are typically few in number and over wide territories.

Eating lower on the food chain (a vegetarian diet) is more energy-efficient — less energy is lost before it reaches the consumer.

4.2.4 Bioaccumulation

Fat-soluble toxins (e.g. DDT, mercury) pass up the food chain and accumulate, since they are not excreted. Top predators end up with the highest concentration — e.g. peregrine falcon eggshell thinning in the 20th century from DDT.
