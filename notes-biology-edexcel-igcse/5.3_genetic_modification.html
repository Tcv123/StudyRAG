5.3 Genetic Modification

Edexcel International GCSE Biology (4BI1) — Paper 2

5.3.1 What is GM?

Genetic modification (GM) is the direct alteration of an organism's genome using laboratory techniques. Often a useful gene from one species is inserted into another (transgenesis). Key tools: restriction enzymes (cut DNA at specific sequences), DNA ligase (seals cut ends), vectors (plasmids or viruses that carry the gene into the host).

5.3.3 Other examples

  • Bt maize — contains a gene from Bacillus thuringiensis encoding an insecticidal protein, reducing need for chemical pesticides.
  • Golden rice — contains genes from daffodil and a bacterium to produce β-carotene (vitamin A precursor). Addresses vitamin A deficiency blindness in developing countries.
  • Herbicide-tolerant soybean — allows farmers to spray broad-spectrum herbicides without harming crop.

5.3.4 Advantages and concerns

  • ✓ Rapid — a specific trait can be introduced in one generation.
  • ✓ Can use genes from unrelated species.
  • ✓ Produces pure human proteins (e.g. insulin, growth hormone, clotting factor).
  • ✗ Gene flow — transgenes may escape to wild relatives (e.g. herbicide-tolerance allele).
  • ✗ Ethical concerns about patented seeds and farmer dependence.
  • ✗ Long-term ecological effects less well understood.
A classic 5-mark question: "Describe how human insulin is produced using GM bacteria." Always include (a) restriction enzyme + insulin gene, (b) plasmid cut with same enzyme → sticky ends, (c) ligase joins, (d) E. coli transformed, (e) grown in fermenter, (f) insulin extracted and purified.
