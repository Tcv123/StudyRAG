2.6.1 Gas exchange in a leaf

Gases enter and leave through stomata (pores on the underside of the leaf, controlled by guard cells). The spongy mesophyll layer has many air spaces — giving a large surface area for gases to diffuse.

  • Day: Photosynthesis > respiration → net CO₂ in, O₂ out.
  • Night: Only respiration → net O₂ in, CO₂ out.

2.6.2 Gas exchange in humans

Breathing (ventilation)

  • Inhalation: external intercostals and diaphragm contract — ribs move up/out, diaphragm flattens — thorax volume increases, pressure drops, air rushes in.
  • Exhalation: muscles relax — ribs down/in, diaphragm domes up — thorax volume falls, pressure rises, air forced out.

Alveoli adaptations

  • Large total surface area (~70 m² in humans)
  • Wall one cell thick → short diffusion distance
  • Moist surface — gases dissolve
  • Rich capillary network — maintains steep concentration gradient

2.6.3 Effects of smoking

  • Tar — carcinogen; damages cilia → build-up of mucus → chronic bronchitis. Strongly linked to lung cancer.
  • Carbon monoxide — binds to haemoglobin >200× more strongly than O₂, reducing O₂ transport; raises blood pressure.
  • Nicotine — addictive; raises heart rate and BP.
  • Emphysema — damaged alveolar walls reduce surface area → breathlessness.
Link smoking effects to Fick's law: tar (increased diffusion distance) and emphysema (reduced surface area) both slow gas exchange.
