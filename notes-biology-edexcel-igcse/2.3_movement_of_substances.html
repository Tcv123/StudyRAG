2.3.2 Diffusion and Fick's Law

Fick's law: rate of diffusion ∝ (surface area × concentration gradient) ÷ distance. To maximise gas exchange or absorption, organisms therefore evolve large surfaces, steep gradients and thin barriers.

2.3.3 Osmosis

Osmosis is the net movement of water from a dilute solution (high water potential) to a concentrated solution (low water potential) through a partially permeable membrane.

  • Plant cell in dilute solution: water enters → cell becomes turgid (vacuole pushes cytoplasm firmly against cell wall — provides support).
  • Plant cell in concentrated solution: water leaves → cytoplasm shrinks from wall → plasmolysed, flaccid.
  • Animal cell in dilute solution: water enters and cell bursts (no cell wall to resist — lysis).
  • Animal cell in concentrated solution: shrivels (crenates).

2.3.4 Active Transport

Active transport moves substances against their concentration gradient using energy (ATP) from respiration, via carrier proteins in the membrane.

  • Root hair cells absorb mineral ions from soil (where they are dilute) into cells (where they are more concentrated) — hence many mitochondria in root hair cells.
  • Villi absorb glucose and amino acids when concentrations in blood already exceed those in gut.
  • Kidney tubules reabsorb glucose from filtrate back into blood.
A classic 3-mark question: "Explain why cells which carry out active transport have many mitochondria." → Mitochondria are the site of aerobic respiration, which releases ATP. Active transport uses ATP to move ions against the gradient through carrier proteins. Without ATP, active transport stops.
