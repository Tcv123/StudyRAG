3.2.1 Key terms

Gene — a length of DNA coding for a protein.
Allele — a different version of a gene.
Genotype — the alleles present for a gene (e.g. Bb).
Phenotype — the observable characteristic (e.g. brown eyes).
Homozygous — two identical alleles (BB or bb).
Heterozygous — two different alleles (Bb).
Dominant (upper-case) — expressed even with one copy.
Recessive (lower-case) — only expressed if homozygous.

Human body cells contain 46 chromosomes (23 pairs) — diploid.
Gametes contain 23 — haploid. Fertilisation restores the diploid number.

3.2.3 Sex determination

Males are XY, females XX. Mother gives X; father gives X or Y. Equal 50:50 chance of either sex.

3.2.4 Mutation

A random change in the DNA base sequence. Can be:

  • Neutral (no effect)
  • Harmful (most mutations)
  • Occasionally beneficial — raw material of evolution.

Rate increased by mutagens: ionising radiation, UV light, chemicals (tar, mustard gas).

3.2.5 Evolution by natural selection

  1. Variation exists in population (mutation + sexual reproduction).
  2. Too many offspring — competition.
  3. Best-adapted individuals survive to reproduce.
  4. They pass on advantageous alleles.
  5. Over generations, allele frequency shifts — evolution.
Antibiotic resistance in bacteria is a classic example. Random mutation gives a resistance allele; antibiotic kills non-resistant; resistant survive and reproduce → allele becomes common. Never say bacteria "try" or "want" to become resistant.

3.2.6 Variation

  • Continuous — many genes + environment, e.g. height, mass. Normal distribution curve.
  • Discontinuous — single gene, distinct categories, e.g. blood group, tongue rolling. Bar chart.
