5.1 Food Production

Edexcel International GCSE Biology (4BI1) — Paper 2

5.1.1 Crops in glasshouses and polytunnels

Growers control several limiting factors to maximise photosynthesis and therefore yield:

  • Temperature — heated to optimum for enzymes.
  • Light — artificial lighting for long days, even at night.
  • CO₂ — enriched to around 1000 ppm.
  • Water & mineral nutrition — irrigation, fertilisers.
  • Pest control — barriers, biological control or pesticides.

5.1.2 Fertilisers and pest control

  • Fertilisers supply N, P, K for protein synthesis, root/shoot development and fruit.
  • Overuse → runoff → eutrophication.
  • Pesticides kill pests but may harm pollinators, cause bioaccumulation and drive pest resistance.
  • Biological control uses natural enemies (e.g. ladybirds vs aphids) — no chemical residue, but slower and can upset ecological balance.

5.1.3 Fish farming

Fish farms increase yield by controlling:

  • Food supply (pellets)
  • Density (high, but kept in separate cages to avoid cannibalism)
  • Predators (netting)
  • Disease (medication)

Drawbacks: pollution from waste and uneaten food; disease outbreaks; escaped fish affect wild populations; reliance on wild fish for feed.

5.1.4 Microorganisms in food production

  • Yeast in bread: yeast respires anaerobically on sugars in dough, producing CO₂ (makes dough rise) and ethanol (evaporates during baking).
  • Yeast in brewing: anaerobic respiration converts sugar (from malted barley) into ethanol and CO₂. Optimum ~25 °C, anaerobic after initial aerobic growth.
  • Lactobacillus in yoghurt: bacteria ferment lactose in pasteurised milk → lactic acid. Acid curdles milk proteins giving characteristic texture and flavour.
Large-scale bioreactors ("fermenters") keep conditions optimal: pH, temperature, nutrient supply, O₂ (for aerobic processes) or its absence (for anaerobic), and sterility to prevent contamination.
