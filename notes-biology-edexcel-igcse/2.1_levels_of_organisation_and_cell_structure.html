2.1 Levels of Organisation & Cell Structure

Edexcel International GCSE Biology (4BI1) — Paper 1B/1BR

2.1.1 The Hierarchy of Biological Organisation

Living organisms are built from smaller units combined to form larger, more complex structures: organelles → cells → tissues → organs → organ systems → organism. Each level adds function and integration that the level below cannot achieve alone.
LevelDefinitionExample
OrganelleA specialised structure inside a cell with a specific function.Mitochondrion (site of respiration); nucleus (contains DNA).
CellThe basic unit of life — the smallest structure capable of carrying out MRS GREN alone.Red blood cell; palisade mesophyll cell.
TissueA group of similar cells working together to perform a specific function.Muscle tissue contracts; xylem tissue transports water.
OrganA group of different tissues working together to perform a broader function.Heart (cardiac muscle + valves + connective tissue); leaf.
Organ systemSeveral organs that work together to carry out a major process.Digestive system (stomach, intestine, liver, pancreas).
OrganismA whole, individual living thing.A person; an oak tree; a bacterium.

2.1.2 The Animal Cell

Functions of animal-cell components

StructureFunction
NucleusContains the genetic material (chromosomes of DNA) and controls all cell activities. The nucleolus inside makes ribosomes.
CytoplasmJelly-like fluid where most chemical reactions happen (e.g. glycolysis, protein synthesis). Holds organelles in place.
Cell membranePhospholipid bilayer with proteins. Partially permeable — controls which substances enter and leave the cell.
MitochondrionSite of aerobic respiration. Glucose + oxygen are converted to CO₂, water and ATP. Cells with high energy demands (e.g. muscle, liver) contain many mitochondria.
RibosomesTiny structures where proteins are made by joining amino acids in the sequence coded by mRNA. Found free in cytoplasm and on rough endoplasmic reticulum.

2.1.3 The Plant Cell

Additional plant-only components

StructureMaterialFunction
Cell wallCelluloseStrong and fully permeable. Supports the cell, prevents bursting when water enters, gives the plant rigidity.
ChloroplastsMembrane-bound, contain chlorophyll (pigment)Site of photosynthesis — absorb light energy to make glucose from CO₂ and water. Only present in green, photosynthesising parts of the plant.
Large permanent vacuoleMembrane-bound sac of cell sap (water + dissolved substances)Keeps the cell turgid so the plant is upright. Also stores dissolved sugars, pigments, waste and ions.
A frequent Edexcel question is: "State three structures found in a plant cell that are not found in an animal cell." Always list cellulose cell wall, chloroplasts, and large permanent vacuole. Never just "cell wall" — bacteria have a cell wall too but a different one.

2.1.4 Specialised Cells

Multicellular organisms have many cells that are specialised — their shape and contents are adapted to a specific function. Here are the key examples you must know for 4BI1.

CellFunctionAdaptations
Red blood cellTransport oxygen from lungs to tissuesBiconcave disc — large surface area for gas exchange; no nucleus — more room for haemoglobin; small and flexible — fits through capillaries.
Sperm cellFertilise an ovumStreamlined head + long tail (flagellum) for swimming; many mitochondria in mid-piece for ATP; acrosome with enzymes to penetrate egg; haploid nucleus.
Root hair cellAbsorb water and mineral ions from soilLong narrow extension — large surface area; thin wall — short diffusion distance; many mitochondria — ATP for active transport of ions.
Palisade mesophyll cellPhotosynthesis in leafMany chloroplasts concentrated at the top; long column shape packed together beneath upper epidermis — maximises light absorption.
Ciliated epithelial cellSweep mucus up and out of the tracheaCilia (hair-like projections) that beat rhythmically; goblet cells nearby produce mucus that traps dust and microbes.
Nerve cell (neurone)Transmit electrical impulsesLong axon — rapid signal over distance; myelin sheath — insulates and speeds impulse; dendrites — multiple connections to other neurones.
Muscle cellContract to produce movementPacked with actin and myosin filaments that slide past each other; many mitochondria for ATP.

2.1.5 The Microscope and Magnification

Most cells are too small to see with the naked eye. A light microscope uses glass lenses and visible light to magnify an image up to ×1500. Under favourable conditions it resolves features about 200 nm apart — good enough to see most cells and nuclei but not individual organelles in detail. An electron microscope uses a beam of electrons instead of light and can resolve features as small as 0.1 nm, revealing ribosomes, cristae in mitochondria, and even DNA.

Magnification formula

Magnification = image size ÷ actual size
The formula has no units — it is a ratio. Make sure both measurements are in the same units before dividing (convert mm ↔ µm where needed).

Useful unit conversions:

  • 1 mm = 1 000 µm = 1 000 000 nm
  • 1 µm = 1 000 nm
An image of a plant cell measures 30 mm across. The real cell is 75 µm across. Calculate the magnification.

Step 1 — Convert units: 30 mm = 30 000 µm.
Step 2 — Apply formula: magnification = 30 000 ÷ 75 = ×400.
Under a ×1000 microscope, a bacterium appears 4 mm long. What is its actual length?

Rearrange: actual = image ÷ magnification = 4 mm ÷ 1000 = 0.004 mm = 4 µm.

Resolution vs magnification

  • Magnification: how many times larger the image appears compared with the object. You can increase magnification indefinitely, but the image eventually becomes blurry ("empty magnification").
  • Resolution: the smallest distance between two points that can be seen as separate. Fixed by the wavelength of the illuminating beam — light microscopes cannot resolve features smaller than ~200 nm.

2.1.6 Preparing a Microscope Slide — Required Practical Skills

To view plant cells (e.g. onion epidermis) under a light microscope:

  1. Peel off a thin layer of epidermis from the inner side of an onion scale using forceps.
  2. Place the tissue flat on a clean microscope slide in a drop of water.
  3. Add a drop of iodine solution — this stains the nucleus and starch grains dark, making them visible.
  4. Lower a coverslip at a 45° angle using a mounted needle to avoid trapping air bubbles.
  5. Blot excess fluid. View on low power first, then higher power once the specimen is centred.
Why lower the coverslip at 45°? To push out air bubbles, which refract light and obscure the specimen. Always focus on low power (× 4 or × 10 objective) first to locate the cell, then switch to higher power.

2.1.8 Why Multicellularity and Specialisation Matter

Single-celled organisms rely on a single cell for all life processes — their size is limited by diffusion. Multicellular organisms overcome this through:

  • Specialisation: different cells efficient at different tasks (e.g. red blood cell for gas transport; ciliated cell for moving mucus).
  • Organisation: cells → tissues → organs → systems, coordinated by the nervous and hormonal systems.
  • Mass transport: circulatory and transport systems move substances over distances too great for diffusion alone.
  • Homeostasis: internal conditions are controlled, allowing much greater complexity and body size than single cells could support.
