2.2 Biological Molecules

Edexcel International GCSE Biology (4BI1) — Paper 1

2.2.1 The Three Main Food Groups

The major organic molecules in living organisms are carbohydrates, lipids and proteins. Each is a polymer built from smaller monomers. Water and nucleic acids (DNA/RNA) complete the picture.
Food groupMonomer (building block)ElementsExamples / function
CarbohydratesSimple sugars (e.g. glucose)C, H, OGlucose (energy source); starch (plant energy store); glycogen (animal store); cellulose (plant cell walls).
Lipids (fats & oils)1 glycerol + 3 fatty acidsC, H, OEnergy store (twice as much energy per gram as carbohydrate), insulation, cell membranes.
ProteinsAmino acids (joined by peptide bonds)C, H, O, N (sometimes S)Enzymes, structural (keratin, collagen), hormones, antibodies, transport (haemoglobin).

Safety: never heat ethanol with a naked flame — it is highly flammable. Use a water bath for Benedict's test rather than a Bunsen burner where possible.

2.2.3 Enzymes

An enzyme is a protein that acts as a biological catalyst — it speeds up a specific reaction without being used up. Each enzyme has an active site with a specific 3D shape complementary to its substrate (the lock-and-key model).

Factors affecting enzyme activity

  • Temperature: rate rises with temperature until the optimum (~37 °C in humans). Above this, enzymes denature — bonds holding the active site shape break, so the substrate no longer fits.
  • pH: each enzyme has an optimum pH. Pepsin (stomach) works at pH 2; amylase (mouth and pancreas) around pH 7. Extremes denature the enzyme.
  • Substrate concentration: rate increases with substrate up to a point, then plateaus — all active sites are occupied (enzyme becomes limiting).
When explaining denaturation, always mention: active site changes shape → substrate no longer fits → no enzyme-substrate complex forms → rate drops sharply. Do NOT say "the enzyme dies" — enzymes are not alive.
