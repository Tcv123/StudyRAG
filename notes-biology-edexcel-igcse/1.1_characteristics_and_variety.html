1.1 Characteristics & Variety of Living Organisms

Edexcel International GCSE Biology (4BI1) — Paper 1B/1BR

1.1.1 The Characteristics of Living Organisms

All living organisms share eight characteristic life processes, remembered by the mnemonic MRS GREN C. These processes distinguish living things from non-living matter and are the foundation of biology.
LetterProcessWhat it meansExample
MMovementAn action by an organism or part of an organism that changes position or place.A cheetah running; a sunflower head tracking the sun.
RRespirationChemical reactions in cells that break down nutrient molecules to release energy (ATP).Glucose + oxygen → CO₂ + water in mitochondria of all cells.

GGrowthA permanent increase in size and dry mass through increase in cell number or size.A tree growing taller; a child increasing in height and mass.

Edexcel will often ask "State the eight characteristics of living organisms" for 2–4 marks. Learn MRS GREN by rote — it never changes and is guaranteed free marks. Be careful: "photosynthesis" is NOT one of the eight (it is included under Nutrition).

Distinguishing life processes

  • Excretion vs egestion: Excretion is the removal of metabolic waste (e.g. CO₂ from respiration, urea from deamination). Egestion is the removal of undigested food — not produced by metabolism — and is NOT one of the eight.
  • Respiration vs breathing: Respiration is the chemical process in cells that releases energy. Breathing (ventilation) is the physical movement of air in and out of the lungs to supply oxygen for respiration.
  • Growth vs increase in mass: A balloon inflated with gas increases in mass but does not grow. True growth is a permanent increase in dry mass due to new cells or new biomass.

1.1.2 The Five Kingdoms of Living Organisms

All living organisms are classified into five kingdoms, grouped by cell type and by how they obtain nutrients. Three features matter for classification: whether cells are prokaryotic or eukaryotic, whether organisms are unicellular or multicellular, and their mode of nutrition.

The Animalia kingdom

  • Eukaryotic multicellular organisms with no cell wall and no chloroplasts.
  • Heterotrophic — they consume complex organic molecules made by other organisms (ingestion, then internal digestion).
  • Usually show nervous coordination and are motile at some stage of their life.
  • Store carbohydrate as glycogen in muscles and liver.

The Plantae kingdom

  • Eukaryotic multicellular organisms with a cellulose cell wall, chloroplasts containing chlorophyll, and a large permanent vacuole.
  • Autotrophic — make their own food by photosynthesis.
  • Store carbohydrate as starch; use sucrose for transport in phloem.
  • Examples include flowering plants (Magnoliophyta), ferns, mosses, and conifers.

The Fungi kingdom

  • Eukaryotic, mostly multicellular organisms. Bodies made of thread-like hyphae that form a network called the mycelium. (Yeast is an important unicellular exception.)
  • Cell walls of chitin (not cellulose).
  • Heterotrophic and feed by saprotrophic nutrition — secrete digestive enzymes onto dead organic matter and absorb the soluble products (extracellular digestion).
  • Reproduce by spores. Examples: Mucor (pin mould), Penicillium (antibiotic-producing mould), yeast.
  • Store carbohydrate as glycogen.

The Protoctista kingdom

  • Eukaryotic, usually unicellular organisms. A very varied kingdom; sometimes called a "dumping-ground" for eukaryotes that do not fit in the other kingdoms.
  • Some are plant-like (contain chloroplasts; photosynthesise) — e.g. Chlorella, Spirogyra.
  • Others are animal-like (feed by ingestion) — e.g. Amoeba, Plasmodium (malarial parasite).

The Prokaryota kingdom (bacteria)

  • Prokaryotic: no true membrane-bound nucleus. DNA is a single circular loop in the cytoplasm, plus smaller circular plasmids.
  • No mitochondria, no chloroplasts, no other membrane-bound organelles.
  • Cell wall made of peptidoglycan (murein), not cellulose or chitin.
  • Examples: Lactobacillus (used in yoghurt-making), Pneumococcus (causes pneumonia), E. coli.

1.1.3 Pathogens — Disease-Causing Organisms

A pathogen is an organism that causes infectious disease in another organism. Pathogens can come from almost every kingdom (bacteria, fungi, protoctists) and also from viruses (which aren't in any kingdom).

Why are viruses not considered truly alive?

  • A virus is simply nucleic acid (DNA or RNA) inside a protein coat (capsid) — sometimes with a lipid envelope. It is not made of cells.
  • It has no cytoplasm, no organelles, no ribosomes. It cannot respire, feed, grow or move.
  • It can only reproduce inside a living host cell by hijacking the host's machinery. So it does not carry out most life processes independently.
  • For these reasons most biologists place viruses outside the five kingdoms.
Malaria and Plasmodium. Plasmodium is a protoctist transmitted to humans by the bite of an infected female Anopheles mosquito. It infects liver cells, then red blood cells, causing fever, chills and anaemia. Malaria killed approximately 608 000 people worldwide in 2022, mostly children in sub-Saharan Africa. Control strategies include insecticide-treated bed nets, indoor residual spraying, antimalarial drugs, and — more recently — the RTS,S and R21 vaccines.
HIV and AIDS. HIV is a retrovirus that targets helper T-lymphocytes, gradually weakening the immune system. Without treatment, infected people eventually develop AIDS (acquired immune deficiency syndrome), leaving them vulnerable to opportunistic infections. Antiretroviral therapy (ART) suppresses the virus so people live long, healthy lives and greatly reduces transmission to partners and newborns.

1.1.4 Identifying Organisms Using Keys

A dichotomous key lets you identify an organism by answering a series of paired yes/no questions, each leading to two choices (hence "dichotomous"). Good keys focus on observable, reliable features, such as cell wall composition, number of legs, leaf shape, or wing pattern.

Example dichotomous key for five kingdoms

  1. Are cells present?
    Yes → Go to 2.   No → Virus (not in any kingdom).
  2. Does the organism have a nucleus?
    Yes → Go to 3.   No → Prokaryota.
  3. Does the organism have chloroplasts and a cellulose cell wall?
    Yes → Go to 4.   No → Go to 5.
  4. Is the organism multicellular?
    Yes → Plantae.   No → Protoctista (plant-like).
  5. Does the organism have a chitin cell wall and feed by absorption?
    Yes → Fungi.   No → Animalia or Protoctista (animal-like) depending on whether multicellular or unicellular.
In the exam, you'll often be shown an unfamiliar organism and asked to classify it. Read the whole question — key features (e.g. "has a cell wall of chitin" or "has no nucleus") are planted precisely so you can place the organism in the correct kingdom.

1.1.5 Summary — Why Classification Matters

  • Communication: Biologists worldwide can refer to the same species unambiguously using the two-part Latin binomial name (genus + species), e.g. Homo sapiens, E. coli.
  • Evolutionary relationships: Classification reflects ancestry — organisms in the same kingdom share a more recent common ancestor than those in different kingdoms.
  • Ecology and conservation: Categorising species allows us to identify and protect ecosystems and endangered species.
  • Medicine: Knowing whether a disease is caused by a bacterium, virus, protoctist or fungus determines the correct treatment — antibiotics only work against bacteria, not viruses.
