10.1 Basic Quantities

  • Current (I) — rate of flow of charge; unit ampere (A).
  • Potential difference (V) — work done per coulomb; unit volt (V).
  • Resistance (R) — opposition to flow; unit ohm (Ω).
  • Charge (Q) — measured in coulombs (C).
Q = I × t   (coulomb = ampere × second) V = I × R   (Ohm\'s law) P = V × I   = I² × R   = V² / R E = V × Q = V × I × t

10.4 I-V Characteristics

  • Fixed resistor (constant T): straight line through origin — obeys Ohm\'s law.
  • Filament lamp: curved S-shape — resistance increases with temperature.
  • Diode: conducts one way only — nearly zero current below ~0.6 V forward; no current reverse.

10.5 Mains Electricity

UK mains: 230 V, 50 Hz alternating current (AC).

Three-pin plug:

  • Live (brown) — carries the alternating potential ±230 V.
  • Neutral (blue) — 0 V; completes circuit.
  • Earth (green/yellow) — safety path; connects the metal case to ground.

A fuse melts if the current exceeds its rating, breaking the live line to protect the appliance. Circuit breakers trip faster and can be reset.

10.6 The National Grid

Power stations generate at ~25 kV. Step-up transformers raise to 400 kV for efficient long-distance transmission: higher V → lower I → much smaller I²R heat losses. Near towns, step-down transformers reduce voltage safely (e.g. 11 kV → 230 V).

A 2 kW kettle on 230 V draws I = P/V = 2000/230 ≈ 8.7 A. Fuse rating: 13 A (just above 8.7 A).

Key Points Summary

  • V = IR; P = VI; Q = It; E = VIt — the essential equations.
  • Series: add R\'s. Parallel: reciprocal rule; lower combined R.
  • Filament lamp and diode are non-Ohmic.
  • Mains in UK = 230 V AC 50 Hz; earth + fuse for safety.
  • High-voltage transmission is efficient — transformers need AC.
