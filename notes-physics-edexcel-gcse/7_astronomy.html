Topic 7 — Astronomy

Edexcel GCSE (9-1) Physics (1PH0) — Paper 1

7.1 The Solar System

  • The Sun is a main-sequence star at the centre.
  • Eight planets orbit the Sun: inner rocky (Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars), outer gas/ice giants (Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune).
  • Moons, dwarf planets, asteroids (asteroid belt) and comets (highly elliptical orbits) complete the system.

7.2 Satellites and Orbits

Natural satellites (e.g. the Moon) orbit planets. Artificial satellites (e.g. ISS, weather/comms satellites) are launched by humans.

Moving a satellite to a lower orbit requires higher speed. A geostationary satellite orbits once every 24 h above the equator → stays above the same spot (used for TV broadcasting).

7.3 Life Cycle of Stars

  • Nebula → protostar (gravity pulls gas together) → main-sequence (hydrogen fusion) → depending on mass: red giant → white dwarf (Sun-like), or red super-giant → supernova → neutron star or black hole (very massive stars).

7.4 Red-Shift and the Big Bang

Light from distant galaxies is shifted to longer (redder) wavelengths. More distant galaxies are more red-shifted — consistent with the universe expanding from a hot, dense initial state (the Big Bang, ~13.8 billion years ago).

The cosmic microwave background (CMB) is leftover thermal radiation from the early universe, detected uniformly across the sky at ≈ 2.7 K. It is powerful evidence for the Big Bang.

Key Points Summary

  • Solar System: Sun + 8 planets + moons + asteroids + comets.
  • Orbits are caused by gravity; lower orbit → higher speed.
  • Star life cycle depends on mass — Sun ends as white dwarf; massive stars go supernova → neutron star / black hole.
  • Red-shift of galaxies and the CMB support the Big Bang.
  • Elements heavier than hydrogen/helium are produced in stars and supernovae.
