1.2 Scalars and Vectors

Physical quantities are split into two groups:

  • Scalars have magnitude (size) only. Examples: mass, time, temperature, speed, distance, energy.
  • Vectors have both magnitude and direction. Examples: force, velocity, acceleration, displacement, momentum.
If an exam question says "calculate the velocity", you must give a direction (e.g. "10 m/s east"). For "speed", a number alone is enough.

1.3 Standard Form and Significant Figures

Physics uses a very wide range of numbers, so we write them in standard form:

a × 10ⁿ, where 1 ≤ a < 10, n is an integer
  • 0.0023 m = 2.3 × 10⁻³ m
  • 450 000 000 J = 4.5 × 10⁸ J
  • Speed of light ≈ 3.0 × 10⁸ m/s

Significant figures (s.f.)

The number of significant figures reflects the precision of a measurement. A final answer should be quoted to the same number of s.f. as the least precise value used to calculate it.

A force of 11.5 N (3 s.f.) acts over 0.47 m (2 s.f.). Calculate the work done.
W = F × d = 11.5 × 0.47 = 5.405 J → quote to 2 s.f. (least precise) → 5.4 J.

1.4 Measurement, Accuracy and Precision

Good scientific practice distinguishes between:

  • Resolution — the smallest change an instrument can detect (e.g. 1 mm on a metre rule).
  • Accuracy — how close a reading is to the true value.
  • Precision — how close repeated readings are to each other.

1.5 Useful Equations to Know

These "speed", "work done" and Newton-law equations are used everywhere; check you can rearrange them.

speed = distance ÷ time   ↔   v = s / t work done = force × distance   ↔   W = F × d resultant force = mass × acceleration   ↔   F = m × a weight = mass × gravitational field strength   ↔   W = m × g   (g ≈ 10 N/kg)
Rearranging: always isolate the required subject before substituting numbers. Check units at the end — they must match the quantity you calculated.

1.6 Graphs and Data

Graphs help identify relationships and reduce the impact of random errors. Always:

  • Label both axes with quantity and unit.
  • Use a sensible scale occupying at least ½ of the graph paper.
  • Plot points accurately (use a sharp pencil, small ×); draw a best-fit line (straight or smooth curve).
  • Find the gradient using a large triangle; calculate the y-intercept.
A student plots force (y) against extension (x) for a spring and finds a straight line through the origin of gradient 50 N/m. The spring constant is 50 N/m (F = kx).

Key Points Summary

  • Always work in SI units; convert prefixes first.
  • Scalars have magnitude; vectors have magnitude + direction.
  • Use standard form for very large/small numbers; match significant figures of data.
  • Accuracy ≠ precision. Repeat to reduce random error; calibrate to remove systematic error.
  • Learn the "core" physics equations (speed, work, Newton's 2nd law, weight).
  • Graphs often give the cleanest picture of a relationship — use gradient/intercept.
