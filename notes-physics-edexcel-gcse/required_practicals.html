Required (Core) Practicals — CP1 to CP8

Edexcel GCSE (9-1) Physics (1PH0) — Assessed in the written exams

Edexcel GCSE Physics specifies eight Core Practicals (CPs). You will not be assessed on performing them in an exam, but you must know the method, variables, measurements, analysis, sources of error and improvements for each. Work through each practical below; they are also covered as part of Topic 3, 4, 5, 10, 14 and 15.

CP1 — Investigating the extension and force on a spring (Hooke\'s Law)

Clamp stand, helical spring, metre rule, pointer, 100 g slotted masses (and hanger), safety mat, set square.

Method

  1. Hang the spring vertically from a clamp; attach a pointer at the bottom.
  2. Read the initial position of the pointer against the metre rule.
  3. Add a mass (known weight = mg), wait for it to settle, and read the new pointer position.
  4. Calculate extension (x) = new position − initial position.
  5. Repeat, adding masses in equal increments. Record at least 8 pairs (force, extension).
  6. Plot force (y-axis) against extension (x-axis).

Analysis

  • Straight line through origin → obeys Hooke\'s law. Gradient = spring constant k.
  • Line curves beyond the limit of proportionality.
Use a safety mat under the masses; wear eye protection; clamp the stand to the bench so it does not topple.
Use a set square to read the pointer horizontally — avoids parallax. Use 0.10 N (10 g) masses for small springs for more data points.

CP2 — Determining the density of solids and liquids

Balance, vernier callipers (or ruler), measuring cylinder, eureka can, beaker, water, selection of regular and irregular solids.

Method (regular solid)

  1. Measure mass on a balance.
  2. Measure dimensions (length × width × height) to find volume.
  3. ρ = m / V.

Method (irregular solid)

  1. Measure mass.
  2. Fill eureka can to spout with water; immerse the object fully; collect the displaced water in a measuring cylinder.
  3. Volume displaced = volume of object; calculate ρ.

Method (liquid)

  1. Measure mass of empty measuring cylinder.
  2. Add a known volume of liquid; measure new mass.
  3. Mass of liquid ÷ volume = density.
Take readings from the bottom of the water meniscus at eye level. Measure dimensions 3× and mean to reduce random error.

CP3 — Determining the specific heat capacity of a material

Metal block (aluminium / copper) with holes for heater + thermometer, 12 V electric heater, joulemeter (or ammeter, voltmeter and stopwatch), thermometer, insulation, balance.

Method

  1. Measure the mass m of the metal block on a balance.
  2. Insert the heater and thermometer. Wrap the block in insulation.
  3. Record the initial temperature T₁.
  4. Switch on the heater. Time for an interval t (e.g. 10 min). Read V and I throughout (or joulemeter reading).
  5. Switch off; the temperature may continue to rise briefly — record maximum T₂.
  6. Calculate energy supplied E = V × I × t (or joulemeter reading).
  7. Specific heat capacity c = E / (m × ΔT).
E = m × c × ΔT

Errors and Improvements

  • Some energy is lost to the surroundings → calculated c is too high.
  • Improve with insulation, a lid, and a smaller ΔT rise.
Heater gets hot — don\'t touch it when live. Check the thermometer is not damaged.

CP4 — Investigating wave properties (ripple tank and/or waves on a string)

Ripple tank, dipper, stroboscope (or freeze-frame imaging), ruler, stopwatch; OR vibration generator, string, pulley, masses, signal generator.

Ripple tank method

  1. Set up the tank with water to a constant depth. Turn on the dipper; observe the ripples on a screen beneath.
  2. Measure frequency: use the strobe / camera to freeze the waves, count the number seen in 1 s.
  3. Measure wavelength: freeze-frame the pattern, measure across ~5 wavelengths with a ruler and divide.
  4. Calculate wave speed v = f × λ. Repeat for different frequencies.

Waves on a string method

  1. String tied to a vibrator at one end, over a pulley with a weight at the other.
  2. Vary signal generator frequency until a clear standing wave forms.
  3. Record the frequency; measure the wavelength directly from the string pattern.
  4. v = f × λ.
Measure across several wavelengths to reduce uncertainty — the % error in a single wavelength reading is large.

CP5 — Investigating refraction of light through glass blocks

Rectangular glass block, ray box with slit, protractor, white paper, sharp pencil, ruler.

Method

  1. Place the block on white paper; draw round it.
  2. Shine a ray into the block at an angle. Mark three points on the incident ray before the block and three after exit.
  3. Remove the block; draw the incident, refracted and emerging rays.
  4. Draw the normal at the point of entry; measure angle of incidence (i) and angle of refraction (r).
  5. Repeat for at least 6 different values of i from 10° to 70°.
  6. Plot sin i against sin r — the gradient = refractive index (extension).
Always measure angles from the normal, not from the surface. Use a sharp pencil for accurate ray lines.

CP6 — I-V characteristics of a filament lamp, a diode and a resistor

Cell(s) and battery pack (or variable DC power supply), ammeter, voltmeter, the component under test, variable resistor (rheostat), connecting leads and switch.

Method

  1. Connect the component in series with the ammeter and variable resistor.
  2. Connect the voltmeter in parallel across the component.
  3. Adjust the variable resistor to get a range of currents; record V and I at each.
  4. Reverse the connections to the cell to get negative values of V and I; repeat readings.
  5. Plot I (y-axis) against V (x-axis).

Expected graphs

  • Resistor: straight line through origin (Ohm\'s law).
  • Filament lamp: curve — resistance increases with temperature.
  • Diode: conducts above ≈ 0.6 V forward bias only; nearly no current otherwise.

CP7 — Effect of length on resistance of a wire

1 m resistance wire on a metre rule, crocodile clips, ammeter, voltmeter, cell, switch.

Method

  1. Attach one crocodile clip at 0 cm and another at, say, 10 cm on the wire.
  2. Close the switch only briefly — record V and I quickly so the wire does not heat up.
  3. R = V / I.
  4. Move the movable clip to increase length in 10 cm steps up to 100 cm.
  5. Plot R against length — expect a straight line through the origin.
Switch off between readings; the wire can heat up rapidly. Avoid touching the bare wire while current flows.

CP8 — Investigating thermal insulators (or alternative: specific heat capacity of water)

Metal can / copper calorimeter, different insulating materials (bubble wrap, wool, cotton, foam), hot water, thermometer, stopwatch, lid.

Method

  1. Pour a fixed volume of hot water (e.g. 200 ml at 80 °C) into the calorimeter.
  2. Wrap it in one of the insulating materials.
  3. Record the temperature every minute for 10 minutes with a lid on.
  4. Plot temperature against time; compare the gradient for each material.
  5. The material with the smallest rate of temperature decrease is the best insulator.

Control Variables

  • Volume and starting temperature of water;
  • Thickness of insulation layer;
  • Shape and material of the calorimeter;
  • Surrounding room temperature.
For a valid comparison, change only the insulating material. Any difference in thickness, water volume or can shape will invalidate the test.

Key Practical Skills

  • Identify dependent, independent and control variables.
  • Plot graphs with labelled axes, units, and sensible scales; draw lines of best fit.
  • Estimate uncertainties: resolution of ruler / thermometer / stopwatch.
  • Explain random vs systematic errors and how you reduce them.
  • Improve methods: insulate, use light gates / data loggers, take more repeats.
  • Name the equipment precisely — e.g. "eureka can", "vernier callipers", "joulemeter".
