14.1 States of Matter

14.2 Density

ρ = m / V   (kg/m³)

Method for a solid regular shape: measure mass and volume directly. For irregular objects, use a eureka can to measure displaced water volume. For liquids: measure mass of a known volume.

14.3 Internal Energy and Changes of State

Internal energy = total kinetic + potential energy of particles. Heating can increase KE (temperature rises) or break bonds during a change of state (temperature stays constant).

E = m × c × ΔT   (specific heat capacity) E = m × L   (specific latent heat)
Heat 2 kg of water by 30 °C (c = 4200): E = 2 × 4200 × 30 = 252 000 J.
Melt 0.5 kg of ice at 0 °C (L_f = 334 000): E = 0.5 × 334 000 = 167 000 J.

14.4 Gas Pressure and Temperature

Gas particles collide with container walls, exerting pressure. Heating → faster particles → more frequent and harder collisions → higher pressure (at constant V). Compressing at constant T → smaller volume → more collisions per area → higher pressure (pV = constant).

p₁V₁ = p₂V₂   (for fixed mass, constant T)
Kelvin temperatures are essential for gas laws: 0 °C ≈ 273 K.

Key Points Summary

  • Solid → liquid → gas: particles spread out, move faster.
  • ρ = m/V — know a method for solids/liquids.
  • Specific heat (E = mcΔT) for temperature change.
  • Latent heat (E = mL) for change of state — no ΔT.
  • Boyle\'s law: pV = constant at constant T.
