Topic 2 — Motion and Forces

Edexcel GCSE (9-1) Physics (1PH0) — Paper 1

2.1 Describing Motion

Distance (scalar) is how far an object has moved. Displacement (vector) is the straight-line distance from start to finish, with direction.

Speed is a scalar (distance/time). Velocity is a vector (displacement/time).

speed = distance ÷ time   ↔   v = s / t
Typical speeds to remember: walking ≈ 1.5 m/s, running ≈ 3 m/s, cycling ≈ 6 m/s, car ≈ 25–30 m/s, sound in air ≈ 340 m/s, light ≈ 3 × 10⁸ m/s.

2.2 Acceleration

a = (v − u) / t

Where u = initial velocity, v = final velocity, t = time taken. Unit: m/s².

v² = u² + 2as
A car speeds up from 5 m/s to 15 m/s in 4 s. Acceleration a = (15 − 5)/4 = 2.5 m/s².

2.4 Newton's Laws of Motion

  1. First law: an object remains at rest or moves at constant velocity unless a resultant force acts on it.
  2. Second law: F = m × a — resultant force = mass × acceleration.
  3. Third law: when two objects interact, the forces they exert on each other are equal in magnitude and opposite in direction.
"Resultant force" means the single force that has the same effect as all the forces combined. Identify all forces on a diagram, then subtract opposing ones.

2.5 Weight and Mass

Mass (kg) — the amount of matter in a body; the same everywhere.

Weight (N) — the gravitational force on a body; changes with g.

W = m × g   (g ≈ 10 N/kg on Earth)

2.6 Stopping Distances

Stopping distance = thinking distance + braking distance.

  • Thinking distance: the distance covered during the driver's reaction time. Depends on speed and reaction time (increased by tiredness, alcohol, distractions).
  • Braking distance: the distance while decelerating. Depends on speed² (KE ∝ v²), mass of vehicle, condition of tyres/brakes, road surface.

2.7 Terminal Velocity

As a falling object speeds up, air resistance increases. When air resistance equals weight, the resultant force becomes zero and the object falls at constant (terminal) velocity.

2.8 Momentum and Collisions

p = m × v   (kg m/s)

Conservation of momentum: total momentum before = total momentum after a collision, provided no external resultant forces act.

A 2 kg trolley at 4 m/s hits a stationary 3 kg trolley and they stick together.
Before: p = 2 × 4 + 3 × 0 = 8 kg m/s.
After: (2 + 3) × v = 8 → v = 1.6 m/s.

Force and Change in Momentum

F = Δp / Δt

Crumple zones, air bags and seat belts all increase Δt — this reduces the average force on the occupants, saving lives.

Key Points Summary

  • Vectors (velocity, acceleration, force) must be given with direction.
  • Newton's laws relate forces to motion: F = ma is the key equation.
  • Distance-time gradient = speed; velocity-time area = distance; v-t gradient = acceleration.
  • Stopping distance = thinking + braking; braking distance ∝ v².
  • Terminal velocity: air resistance = weight; no net force, constant v.
  • Momentum is conserved; increasing Δt reduces collision force.
