Topic 4 — Waves

Edexcel GCSE (9-1) Physics (1PH0) — Paper 1

4.1 Transverse vs Longitudinal Waves

Waves transfer energy (and information) without transferring matter.

4.2 Wave Equation

v = f × λ
  • v — wave speed (m/s)
  • f — frequency (Hz = waves per second)
  • λ — wavelength (m)
T = 1 / f   (period = 1 / frequency)
A sound wave has frequency 500 Hz in air (v = 340 m/s).
λ = v / f = 340 / 500 = 0.68 m.

4.3 Wave Behaviour at Boundaries

  • Reflection — bounces off (angle of incidence = angle of reflection).
  • Refraction — changes speed and direction on entering a new medium; frequency unchanged.
  • Absorption — wave energy transferred into material.
  • Transmission — passes through without significant loss.

4.4 Sonar and Ultrasound

distance = speed × time (then halve, because the wave goes there and back)
A ship\'s sonar echo returns in 0.40 s (v_sound in water = 1 500 m/s).
Total path = 1 500 × 0.40 = 600 m → depth = 300 m.

Ultrasound (>20 kHz) is used in medicine because it is non-ionising and gives real-time images of soft tissue — e.g. fetal scans.

4.5 Seismic Waves

  • P-waves: longitudinal; travel through solids and liquids.
  • S-waves: transverse; cannot travel through liquids.

S-wave shadow on the far side of the Earth is strong evidence for a liquid outer core.

Key Points Summary

  • Transverse oscillations ⟂ direction of travel; longitudinal oscillations ∥ direction of travel.
  • v = fλ and T = 1/f — learn and rearrange.
  • Reflection, refraction, absorption, transmission are the four behaviours at boundaries.
  • Ultrasound in medicine is safe, non-ionising; sonar uses reflection to measure distance.
  • P-waves travel through everything; S-waves only solids — evidence for Earth\'s liquid core.
