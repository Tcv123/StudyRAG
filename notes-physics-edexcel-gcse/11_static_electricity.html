Topic 11 — Static Electricity

Edexcel GCSE (9-1) Physics (1PH0) — Paper 2

11.1 Charging by Friction

Insulators can become charged by friction. Rubbing transfers electrons from one surface to the other; neither material gains or loses protons. The object that gains electrons is negative; the one that loses them is positive.

11.2 Forces Between Charges

  • Like charges repel; unlike charges attract.
  • Non-contact force acting through an electric field.

11.3 Sparks, Earthing and Safety

A large build-up of static creates a strong field that ionises air, producing a spark. In the presence of flammable vapours (fuel depots), this can cause fire or explosion. Earthing provides a safe low-resistance path for charge to flow to ground, preventing build-up.

11.4 Uses of Static Electricity

  • Electrostatic paint-spraying: paint droplets are charged; they repel each other (giving even spread) and are attracted to the oppositely charged object.
  • Photocopiers and laser printers: toner is attracted to charged regions of a drum formed by light from the original.
  • Electrostatic precipitators in chimneys: charge smoke particles; opposite-charged plates collect them.

11.5 Lightning

Charge builds up in thunderclouds from collisions between ice and water. When the field is strong enough, the air ionises and a huge current (~10⁴ A) flows between cloud and ground — lightning. Lightning rods provide a safe path to earth.

Key Points Summary

  • Friction transfers electrons; charge is conserved.
  • Like charges repel, unlike attract; field lines run from + to −.
  • Earthing removes static build-up safely.
  • Uses: paint spraying, photocopiers, precipitators.
  • Hazards: sparks in flammable atmospheres, shocks, lightning.
