Topic 3 — Conservation of Energy

Edexcel GCSE (9-1) Physics (1PH0) — Paper 1

3.1 The Conservation Principle

Energy cannot be created or destroyed. It can only be stored, transferred between stores, or dissipated to the surroundings (usually as heat or sound).

Energy Stores and Pathways

Energy storesTransfer pathways
Kinetic, gravitational, chemical, elastic, thermal (internal), nuclear, magnetic, electrostaticMechanical working, electrical working, heating, radiation

3.2 Kinetic Energy and GPE

KE = ½ × m × v² GPE = m × g × h
A 0.2 kg ball is dropped from 5 m (g = 10, no air resistance). Find the speed on landing.
GPE = 0.2 × 10 × 5 = 10 J = KE = ½ × 0.2 × v² → v² = 100 → v = 10 m/s.

3.3 Power and Efficiency

P = E ÷ t   (watt = joule/second) Efficiency = useful output ÷ total input (× 100%)

3.5 Reducing Unwanted Energy Transfers

  • Lubrication reduces friction in moving parts.
  • Loft insulation traps air to cut conduction/convection.
  • Cavity wall insulation same principle for walls.
  • Double glazing uses an air gap for windows.
  • Draught excluders reduce unwanted airflow.
The "best" reduction often depends on where most of the heat is lost. In the UK, roofs are the single biggest loss, so loft insulation is usually the first upgrade.

3.6 Specific Heat Capacity

E = m × c × ΔT

c is in J/(kg °C). Water: c ≈ 4 200 J/(kg °C).

Heat 0.5 kg of water from 20 °C to 100 °C.
ΔT = 80 °C → E = 0.5 × 4 200 × 80 = 168 000 J.

Key Points Summary

  • Energy is always conserved — never created or destroyed.
  • KE = ½mv²; GPE = mgh; you should be able to rearrange both.
  • Efficiency = useful/total (≤ 100%).
  • Reduce waste with insulation, double glazing and lubrication.
  • Know the renewables/non-renewables list and pros/cons.
