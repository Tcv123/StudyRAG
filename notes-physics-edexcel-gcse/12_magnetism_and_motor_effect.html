Topic 12 — Magnetism and the Motor Effect

Edexcel GCSE (9-1) Physics (1PH0) — Paper 2

12.1 Magnets and Fields

  • Every magnet has a north and a south pole.
  • Like poles repel; unlike poles attract.
  • Field lines go from N to S outside the magnet.
  • Ferromagnetic materials: iron, nickel, cobalt, steel.

12.2 Permanent vs Induced Magnets

  • Permanent magnet — keeps its magnetism (e.g. hard steel).
  • Induced magnet — becomes a magnet only when placed in a field; always experiences attraction; loses most magnetism when removed (e.g. soft iron paperclip).

12.3 Electromagnets

A coil (solenoid) with current through it produces a magnetic field similar to a bar magnet. Adding a soft iron core makes it much stronger. Strength increases with:

  • More current;
  • More turns per unit length;
  • Using a soft iron core.

12.4 The Motor Effect

A current-carrying conductor in a magnetic field experiences a force. The force is perpendicular to both the current and the field, given by Fleming\'s left-hand rule.
F = B × I × L   (N, T, A, m)
A 20 cm wire carries 3 A perpendicular to a 0.40 T field.
F = BIL = 0.40 × 3 × 0.20 = 0.24 N, perpendicular to both the field and the current.

12.5 The DC Motor

A rectangular coil in a magnetic field carries a current. Each side of the coil feels a force in opposite directions, producing a turning moment. A split-ring commutator reverses the current direction every half-turn so the coil keeps spinning the same way.

12.6 Loudspeakers

AC through a coil in a magnetic field produces a varying force on the coil, which vibrates a cone and produces sound of the same frequency as the signal.

Key Points Summary

  • Every magnet has N and S poles; like repel, unlike attract.
  • Electromagnets: solenoid + soft iron core; strength varies with I and N.
  • Motor effect: F = BIL on a current-carrying wire in a field.
  • Fleming\'s LH rule gives force direction in motors.
  • DC motor uses a split-ring commutator to keep spin direction constant.
