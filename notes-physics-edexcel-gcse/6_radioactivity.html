6.1 Atomic Structure

Every atom has a tiny nucleus (protons + neutrons) orbited by electrons. The nucleus is ~10 000 × smaller than the atom but contains almost all the mass.

Atomic number (Z) = number of protons. Mass number (A) = protons + neutrons. Atoms are electrically neutral: #electrons = #protons.

Isotopes

Atoms of the same element with the same Z but different A (different numbers of neutrons). Example: ¹²C, ¹³C, ¹⁴C — all carbon, but ¹⁴C is radioactive.

6.3 Nuclear Decay Equations

Alpha decay: ²³⁸U → ²³⁴Th + ⁴₂He (A decreases by 4, Z decreases by 2).

Beta-minus decay: ¹⁴C → ¹⁴N + ⁰₋₁e (A stays, Z increases by 1).

Gamma emission: neither A nor Z changes — the nucleus just loses energy.

6.4 Half-Life and Activity

The half-life is the time taken for half of the undecayed nuclei (or the activity) in a sample to decay.

Activity is measured in becquerels (Bq) — decays per second.

A source has half-life 5 days and initial activity 1 600 Bq. Find activity after 20 days.
20 ÷ 5 = 4 half-lives. 1600 → 800 → 400 → 200 → 100 Bq.

6.5 Irradiation vs Contamination

  • Irradiation: exposure to radiation from an external source. Protect by distance, time, and shielding.
  • Contamination: radioactive material on/inside the body. Dangerous because radiation is continuous and close to sensitive tissue.
Alpha is most dangerous when inhaled or swallowed (contamination) because it deposits all its energy in a small volume of tissue.

6.6 Uses of Radioactive Sources

  • Smoke alarms — alpha from americium ionises air; smoke disrupts ionisation → alarm sounds.
  • Medical tracers — short half-life gamma emitters (e.g. Tc-99m) to image organ function.
  • Radiotherapy — high-energy gamma to kill cancer cells (multiple beams to spare healthy tissue).
  • Sterilising equipment — gamma rays kill bacteria without heating.
  • Thickness measurement — beta emitters used in paper/metal factories.

6.7 Fission and Fusion (overview)

  • Fission: a heavy nucleus (e.g. U-235) splits into two smaller ones, releasing energy and neutrons — used in nuclear reactors. Control rods absorb neutrons to prevent runaway reaction.
  • Fusion: two small nuclei (e.g. hydrogen) join to form a larger one, releasing huge energy — the process that powers stars. Requires very high temperatures.

Key Points Summary

  • Nucleus = protons (+1) + neutrons (0); electrons orbit at −1.
  • α stopped by paper, β by Al, γ by lead/concrete.
  • α is most ionising, γ is least but most penetrating.
  • Half-life ≡ time for activity to halve; use halvings to solve problems.
  • Contamination ≠ irradiation — different protections.
  • Uses match penetration and half-life to task at hand.
