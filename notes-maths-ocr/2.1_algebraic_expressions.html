OCR GCSE Mathematics (J560) — Paper 1, 2 & 3 — Foundation and Higher

2.1.1 Algebraic Notation and Writing Expressions

Key Vocabulary

Term: A single number, variable, or product of numbers and variables. E.g. $3x$, $-5y^2$, $7$.

Coefficient: The numerical factor of a term. In $-4x^3$ the coefficient is $-4$.

Variable: A letter representing an unknown or changing quantity, e.g. $x$, $y$, $t$.

Constant: A term with no variable part; its value does not change, e.g. $6$ in $3x + 6$.

Expression: A combination of terms without an equals sign, e.g. $5x^2 - 3x + 2$.

Equation: Two expressions connected by an equals sign, e.g. $2x + 1 = 9$.

Formula: A rule connecting two or more variables, e.g. $A = \pi r^2$.

Identity: True for all values of the variable, shown with $\equiv$, e.g. $2(x+3) \equiv 2x + 6$.

Never write multiplication signs between a number and a letter: $3 \times x$ is written $3x$. Also, $1 \times x = x$ (never write $1x$ unless expanding a bracket).

Understanding Index Notation

In algebra, powers follow the same rules as arithmetic:

$$a^m \times a^n = a^{m+n}, \qquad a^m \div a^n = a^{m-n}, \qquad (a^m)^n = a^{mn}$$ $$a^0 = 1 \quad (a \neq 0), \qquad a^{-n} = \frac{1}{a^n}, \qquad a^{1/n} = \sqrt[n]{a}$$
Simplify $\dfrac{x^5 \cdot x^{-2}}{x^3}$.

Numerator: $x^5 \cdot x^{-2} = x^{5+(-2)} = x^3$
Overall: $\dfrac{x^3}{x^3} = x^0 = 1$

2.1.2 Simplifying Expressions

Collecting Like Terms

Like terms have exactly the same variable part (including power). Only like terms can be added or subtracted.

  • $3x$ and $7x$ are like terms → $3x + 7x = 10x$
  • $5y^2$ and $-2y^2$ are like terms → $5y^2 - 2y^2 = 3y^2$
  • $4x$ and $4x^2$ are not like terms (different powers)
  • $3xy$ and $5yx$ are like terms ($xy = yx$)
Simplify $7a + 3b - 2a + 5b - b$.

Group $a$ terms: $7a - 2a = 5a$
Group $b$ terms: $3b + 5b - b = 7b$
Answer: $5a + 7b$
Simplify $4x^2 - 3x + 2 + x^2 + 5x - 7$.

$x^2$ terms: $4x^2 + x^2 = 5x^2$
$x$ terms: $-3x + 5x = 2x$
Constants: $2 - 7 = -5$
Answer: $5x^2 + 2x - 5$

Multiplying and Dividing Terms

When multiplying terms, multiply the coefficients and add the indices of like bases:

$$3x^2 \times 4x^3 = 12x^5 \qquad \text{(multiply coefficients: } 3 \times 4 = 12 \text{, add powers: } 2+3=5\text{)}$$

When dividing terms, divide the coefficients and subtract the indices:

$$\frac{15x^4}{5x} = 3x^3 \qquad \text{(divide coefficients: } 15 \div 5 = 3 \text{, subtract powers: } 4-1=3\text{)}$$
Simplify $\dfrac{6a^3b^2}{9a^2b^4}$.

Coefficients: $6 \div 9 = \dfrac{2}{3}$
$a$ terms: $a^{3-2} = a$
$b$ terms: $b^{2-4} = b^{-2} = \dfrac{1}{b^2}$
Answer: $\dfrac{2a}{3b^2}$
Multiplying two negatives gives a positive: $(-3x)(-4y) = 12xy$. Take extra care with signs.

2.1.3 Expanding Brackets

Single Brackets

Multiply every term inside the bracket by the term outside:

$$a(b + c) = ab + ac$$
Expand $5(3x - 4)$.

$5 \times 3x = 15x$
$5 \times (-4) = -20$
Answer: $15x - 20$
Expand and simplify $3(2x + 1) - 2(x - 5)$.

$3(2x+1) = 6x + 3$
$-2(x-5) = -2x + 10$
Combine: $6x + 3 - 2x + 10 = 4x + 13$

Double Brackets — FOIL Method

To expand $(a+b)(c+d)$, multiply each term in the first bracket by each term in the second:

$$\underbrace{ac}_{\text{F}} + \underbrace{ad}_{\text{O}} + \underbrace{bc}_{\text{I}} + \underbrace{bd}_{\text{L}}$$
Expand $(x+3)(x+5)$.

F: $x \cdot x = x^2$
O: $x \cdot 5 = 5x$
I: $3 \cdot x = 3x$
L: $3 \cdot 5 = 15$
Collect like terms: $x^2 + 5x + 3x + 15 = x^2 + 8x + 15$

Grid Method for Double Brackets

The grid method is a reliable alternative to FOIL. Each cell is the product of the row and column headers.

Special Cases

Perfect square: $(a+b)^2$

$$(a+b)^2 = a^2 + 2ab + b^2$$ $$(a-b)^2 = a^2 - 2ab + b^2$$
Expand $(3x - 2)^2$.

$(3x)^2 - 2(3x)(2) + 2^2$
$= 9x^2 - 12x + 4$

Difference of two squares: $(a+b)(a-b)$

$$(a+b)(a-b) = a^2 - b^2$$
Expand $(5x+3)(5x-3)$.

$(5x)^2 - 3^2 = 25x^2 - 9$
Recognising the difference of two squares is a key skill — both in expanding and factorising. Look for two brackets that are identical except for the sign between the terms.

2.1.4 Factorising

Taking Out the HCF

Find the highest common factor (HCF) of all terms and place it outside a bracket:

Factorise $12x^3 - 8x^2 + 4x$.

HCF of 12, 8, 4 is 4; HCF of $x^3, x^2, x$ is $x$
HCF = $4x$
$4x(3x^2 - 2x + 1)$
Check by expanding: $4x \cdot 3x^2 = 12x^3$ ✓
Factorise $6a^2b - 9ab^2$.

HCF = $3ab$
$3ab(2a - 3b)$

Factorising Quadratics $ax^2 + bx + c$

Case 1: $a = 1$ (simple quadratics)

Find two numbers that multiply to give $c$ and add to give $b$:

Factorise $x^2 + 7x + 12$.

Need two numbers that multiply to $+12$ and add to $+7$
Factors of 12: $(1,12),(2,6),(3,4)$ → $3 + 4 = 7$ ✓
Answer: $(x+3)(x+4)$
Factorise $x^2 - 5x - 6$.

Need two numbers that multiply to $-6$ and add to $-5$
$-6$ and $+1$: $(-6)(+1) = -6$ ✓, $-6 + 1 = -5$ ✓
Answer: $(x-6)(x+1)$
Sign pattern in $x^2 + bx + c$Signs in factorsExample
$b > 0$, $c > 0$Both positive$(x+3)(x+4)$
$b < 0$, $c > 0$Both negative$(x-3)(x-4)$
$c < 0$One positive, one negative$(x+3)(x-4)$ or $(x-3)(x+4)$

Case 2: $a \neq 1$ — The AC Method

  1. Find the product $ac$.
  2. Find two numbers that multiply to $ac$ and add to $b$.
  3. Rewrite the middle term using these two numbers, then factorise by grouping.
Factorise $2x^2 + 7x + 3$.

$a=2,\ b=7,\ c=3$ so $ac = 6$
Find two numbers: multiply to 6, add to 7 → $1$ and $6$
Rewrite: $2x^2 + x + 6x + 3$
Group: $x(2x+1) + 3(2x+1)$
Factor: $(2x+1)(x+3)$

Difference of Two Squares (Factorising)

$$a^2 - b^2 = (a+b)(a-b)$$
Factorise $9x^2 - 16$.

$9x^2 = (3x)^2$ and $16 = 4^2$
Answer: $(3x+4)(3x-4)$
Always check for a common factor first before using any other factorising method. E.g. $2x^2 - 8 = 2(x^2 - 4) = 2(x+2)(x-2)$.

Higher — Factorising by Grouping (4-term expressions)

When an expression has four terms, try grouping them in pairs and factorising each pair:

Factorise $ax + ay + bx + by$.

Group: $(ax + ay) + (bx + by)$
Factorise each pair: $a(x+y) + b(x+y)$
The common bracket is $(x+y)$: $(x+y)(a+b)$
Factorise $3x^2 - 6x + 2x - 4$.

Group: $3x(x-2) + 2(x-2)$
Answer: $(x-2)(3x+2)$

2.1.5 Algebraic Fractions [Higher]

Higher only

Simplifying Algebraic Fractions

Factorise the numerator and denominator completely, then cancel common factors:

Simplify $\dfrac{x^2 - 9}{x^2 - x - 6}$.

Numerator: $x^2 - 9 = (x+3)(x-3)$
Denominator: $x^2 - x - 6 = (x-3)(x+2)$
Cancel $(x-3)$: $\dfrac{(x+3)\cancel{(x-3)}}{\cancel{(x-3)}(x+2)} = \dfrac{x+3}{x+2}$

Multiplying and Dividing Algebraic Fractions

Multiply: multiply numerators together and denominators together (simplify first if possible).

Divide: flip the second fraction (reciprocal) and multiply.

Simplify $\dfrac{3x+6}{x^2} \times \dfrac{x}{x+2}$.

Factorise: $\dfrac{3(x+2)}{x^2} \times \dfrac{x}{x+2}$
Cancel $(x+2)$ and one $x$: $\dfrac{3 \cdot x}{x^2} = \dfrac{3}{x}$

Adding and Subtracting Algebraic Fractions

Find the lowest common denominator (LCD), rewrite each fraction, then combine numerators:

Simplify $\dfrac{3}{x+1} + \dfrac{2}{x-2}$.

LCD $= (x+1)(x-2)$
Rewrite: $\dfrac{3(x-2)}{(x+1)(x-2)} + \dfrac{2(x+1)}{(x+1)(x-2)}$
Combine: $\dfrac{3(x-2)+2(x+1)}{(x+1)(x-2)}$
Expand numerator: $3x - 6 + 2x + 2 = 5x - 4$
Answer: $\dfrac{5x-4}{(x+1)(x-2)}$
In Higher exams, algebraic fractions questions often require factorising before simplifying. Always factorise both top and bottom first.
