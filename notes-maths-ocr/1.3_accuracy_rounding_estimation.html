1.3.1 Rounding

Rounding replaces a number with an approximate value that is simpler to work with. The three main types of rounding you need at GCSE are: to a given number of decimal places (d.p.), to a given number of significant figures (s.f.), and to the nearest whole number, 10, 100 etc.

The Rounding Rule

Look at the digit immediately to the right of the position you are rounding to:
  • If it is 5 or more → round up
  • If it is 4 or less → round down (keep the digit unchanged)

Rounding to Decimal Places

Count from the decimal point. The digit in the required d.p. position is the last digit you keep. Look at the next digit to decide whether to round up or down.

When a question asks for 2 d.p., if the last digit rounds to zero you must write it: 8.30, not 8.3. The zero is significant — it shows the answer is given to 2 d.p.

Rounding to Significant Figures

Significant figures are counted from the first non-zero digit, left to right. Leading zeros are not significant; trailing zeros after a decimal point are significant.

Rules for counting significant figures:
  • Non-zero digits are always significant: 3.14 has 3 s.f.
  • Zeros between non-zero digits are significant: 2007 has 4 s.f.
  • Leading zeros (before the first non-zero digit) are NOT significant: 0.0045 has 2 s.f.
  • Trailing zeros after the decimal point ARE significant: 3.40 has 3 s.f.
  • Trailing zeros in a whole number are ambiguous without a decimal point: 200 might have 1, 2, or 3 s.f.

In 0.004705, the leading zeros are not significant. The significant figures are 4, 7, 0, 5 — giving 4 s.f.

When rounding to significant figures, use zeros as placeholders to maintain the correct magnitude. 4,836 rounded to 1 s.f. is 5,000 — not just 5.

1.3.2 Estimation

Estimation is used to check whether an exact answer is reasonable, or to get an approximate answer quickly — especially on Paper 1 (non-calculator). The standard approach is to round every number to 1 significant figure before calculating.

To estimate a calculation:
  1. Round every number in the calculation to 1 significant figure.
  2. Carry out the simplified calculation.
  3. State that your answer is an approximation (use the $\approx$ symbol).

Estimate $\dfrac{38.4 \times 5.9}{0.196}$

Round each number to 1 s.f.: $38.4 \approx 40$;  $5.9 \approx 6$;  $0.196 \approx 0.2$

$$\frac{40 \times 6}{0.2} = \frac{240}{0.2} = 1200$$

The actual answer is $\approx 1155$. The estimate 1200 is close — confirming no major error.

Estimate $\sqrt{97} \times 4.8$

$\sqrt{97} \approx \sqrt{100} = 10$;  $4.8 \approx 5$

$$10 \times 5 = 50$$

A cinema sells 287 tickets at £8.70 each. Estimate the total revenue.

$287 \approx 300$;  $8.70 \approx 9$

$$300 \times 9 = \pounds 2{,}700$$

When Not to Round to 1 s.f. Exactly

Sometimes rounding to 1 s.f. makes numbers awkward. If a number like 4.9 rounds to 5 but is close to 5 anyway, or if dividing by 0.2 is much harder than you'd like, you may choose convenient nearby numbers instead — the important thing is that your working is clear and your estimate is reasonable.

On the exam, show your rounded values clearly. Write something like "$38.4 \approx 40$, $5.9 \approx 6$" before calculating. This earns method marks even if you make an arithmetic slip.

1.3.3 Error Intervals and Bounds

When a measurement is given to a particular degree of accuracy, the true value lies in a range. This range is described using an error interval or by giving the lower and upper bounds.

  • Lower bound: the smallest value that would round to the given measurement.
  • Upper bound: the largest value that would round to the given measurement.
  • The upper bound is exclusive — it is the value that would just round up to the next value. Conventionally written with a strict inequality $<$.

Error interval notation: $\text{lower bound} \leq x < \text{upper bound}$

Finding Bounds When Rounding to a Unit

The key rule: the error is half the unit of rounding. Subtract and add this from the given value to find the bounds.

Calculations with Bounds (Maximum and Minimum Values)

When a calculation involves multiple rounded values, use the appropriate bounds to find the maximum or minimum result.

  • Maximum result of $a + b$: use upper bounds of both
  • Minimum result of $a + b$: use lower bounds of both
  • Maximum result of $a - b$: use upper bound of $a$ and lower bound of $b$
  • Minimum result of $a - b$: use lower bound of $a$ and upper bound of $b$
  • Maximum result of $a \times b$: use upper bounds of both (when both positive)
  • Maximum result of $a \div b$: use upper bound of $a$ and lower bound of $b$ (when both positive)
  • Minimum result of $a \div b$: use lower bound of $a$ and upper bound of $b$ (when both positive)

A rectangle has length $l = 8.4$ cm and width $w = 3.7$ cm, both measured to 1 d.p.

Find: (a) the maximum area   (b) the minimum perimeter.

Bounds for $l$: $8.35 \leq l < 8.45$

Bounds for $w$: $3.65 \leq w < 3.75$

(a) Maximum area uses upper bounds: $8.45 \times 3.75 = 31.6875 \text{ cm}^2$

(b) Minimum perimeter uses lower bounds: $2(8.35 + 3.65) = 2 \times 12 = 24 \text{ cm}$

$p = 5.8$ cm and $q = 2.3$ cm, both rounded to 1 d.p. Find the minimum value of $\dfrac{p}{q}$, giving your answer to 3 s.f.

Minimum of $p \div q$: use lower bound of $p$ and upper bound of $q$

Lower bound of $p = 5.75$; upper bound of $q = 2.35$

$$\frac{5.75}{2.35} = 2.446... \approx 2.45 \text{ (3 s.f.)}$$

A car travels 180 miles (to the nearest 10) in 3.2 hours (to 1 d.p.). Find the maximum possible average speed.

Maximum speed = maximum distance ÷ minimum time

Upper bound of distance: $185$ miles; lower bound of time: $3.15$ hours

$$\text{Max speed} = \frac{185}{3.15} = 58.73... \approx 58.7 \text{ mph (3 s.f.)}$$
The upper bound is always strictly less than (<), not ≤. A length of 7.35 would round to 7.4, not 7.3. Write error intervals as $7.25 \leq l < 7.35$, not $7.25 \leq l \leq 7.35$.
When finding the maximum value of a division $a \div b$, you want the biggest top and the smallest bottom. When finding the minimum, you want the smallest top and the biggest bottom. Always think about which combination makes the result biggest or smallest.

1.3.4 Truncation vs Rounding

There are two distinct ways a number can be approximated to a given number of digits:

  • Rounding: look at the next digit and round up if it is 5 or more, otherwise keep the last digit unchanged.
  • Truncation: simply remove (chop off) all digits beyond the required position — regardless of what they are. There is no rounding up.

Error Intervals for Truncated Values

When a value $x$ is truncated to $n$ decimal places, the error interval is one-sided: the true value is at least as large as the truncated value, but strictly less than one unit of the last place above it.

If $x$ is truncated to 1 decimal place to give 4.7:
$$4.7 \leq x < 4.8$$ The lower bound equals the truncated value (no rounding means the value could be exactly 4.7).
The upper bound is the next value at that precision (4.8), exclusive.
Truncation always gives a lower bound equal to the truncated value. Contrast with rounding, where the lower bound is below the given value (e.g. 4.7 rounded gives $4.65 \leq x < 4.75$, whereas 4.7 truncated gives $4.7 \leq x < 4.8$). If the question says "truncated", use the one-sided interval.

A calculator displays 7.3 after truncating to 1 decimal place. Write the error interval.

Truncation: lower bound = 7.3 (exact), upper bound = 7.4 (exclusive)

$$7.3 \leq x < 7.4$$

Compare: if 7.3 had been rounded to 1 d.p., the interval would be $7.25 \leq x < 7.35$.

1.3.5 Percentage Error

The percentage error (or relative error) compares the absolute error to the true value. It is used to judge how significant an error is in context.

$$\text{Percentage error} = \frac{|\text{measured value} - \text{true value}|}{|\text{true value}|} \times 100\%$$
Or equivalently, using the maximum absolute error (half the unit of rounding): $$\text{Maximum percentage error} = \frac{\text{half unit of rounding}}{\text{measured value}} \times 100\%$$

A length is measured as 8 cm, rounded to the nearest centimetre. Find the maximum percentage error.

Half unit of rounding = 0.5 cm

$$\text{Max \% error} = \frac{0.5}{8} \times 100\% = 6.25\%$$

A mass is given as 450 g, rounded to the nearest 10 g. Find the maximum percentage error to 3 s.f.

Half unit = 5 g

$$\text{Max \% error} = \frac{5}{450} \times 100\% = 1.11\% \text{ (3 s.f.)}$$

The true length of a piece of wood is 12.4 cm. A student measures it as 12.6 cm. Find the percentage error.

$$\text{Percentage error} = \frac{|12.6 - 12.4|}{12.4} \times 100\% = \frac{0.2}{12.4} \times 100\% \approx 1.61\%$$
Percentage error questions often ask you to use the measured value in the denominator (as above), because the true value is unknown. Always use absolute values (ignore the sign of the error). A smaller percentage error means a more accurate measurement — this is more meaningful than the raw error alone.
