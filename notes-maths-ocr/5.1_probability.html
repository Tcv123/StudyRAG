5.1 Probability

OCR GCSE Mathematics J560 — Unit 5 — Papers 1, 2 and 3 (Foundation and Higher)

5.1.1 Basic Probability Concepts

Probability is a measure of how likely an event is to occur. It is always a value between 0 and 1 (inclusive).

  • Probability = 0 → impossible (cannot happen)
  • Probability = 1 → certain (will definitely happen)
  • Probability = 0.5 → equally likely to happen or not happen

The Probability Scale

Theoretical Probability Formula

When all outcomes are equally likely:

$$P(\text{event}) = \frac{\text{number of favourable outcomes}}{\text{total number of possible outcomes}}$$

Complementary Events

The complement of event $A$, written $A'$ (or $\bar{A}$), is the event that $A$ does not happen.

$$P(A') = 1 - P(A)$$
The probabilities of all mutually exclusive, exhaustive outcomes must sum to 1.
A bag contains 3 red, 5 blue and 2 green counters. A counter is chosen at random.

(a) Find $P(\text{red})$.
Total outcomes = $3 + 5 + 2 = 10$
$P(\text{red}) = \dfrac{3}{10}$

(b) Find $P(\text{not red})$.
$P(\text{not red}) = 1 - \dfrac{3}{10} = \dfrac{7}{10}$

(c) Find $P(\text{not blue})$.
$P(\text{not blue}) = 1 - \dfrac{5}{10} = \dfrac{5}{10} = \dfrac{1}{2}$
Probability can be written as a fraction, decimal or percentage. Fractions are usually preferred in exams unless a decimal or percentage is asked for. Never write probability as a ratio (e.g. 3:10).

5.1.2 Listing Outcomes

For experiments with multiple stages, you must list all possible outcomes systematically to avoid missing any.

Sample Space Diagrams

A sample space diagram (also called a possibility space) shows all outcomes of two events in a grid.

Two fair dice are rolled. Find the probability that the sum is 7.

Total outcomes = 36. Outcomes giving sum of 7 (highlighted): 6.

$$P(\text{sum} = 7) = \frac{6}{36} = \frac{1}{6}$$

Systematic Listing

A coin is flipped and a spinner with sectors 1, 2, 3 is spun. List all outcomes.

Using a tree or ordered list: H1, H2, H3, T1, T2, T3 — 6 outcomes total.

$P(\text{Head and even number}) = \dfrac{1}{6}$ (only H2)
Always count the total number of outcomes carefully before writing any probability. Use a table or systematic list — never guess at the total.

5.1.3 Theoretical vs Experimental Probability

Theoretical probability is calculated from equally likely outcomes using the formula above.

Experimental probability (relative frequency) is calculated from the results of an actual experiment:

$$P(\text{event}) \approx \frac{\text{number of times event occurred}}{\text{total number of trials}}$$

Expected Frequency

$$\text{Expected frequency} = P(\text{event}) \times \text{number of trials}$$
A biased coin is flipped 200 times. It lands on heads 130 times.

(a) Estimate the probability of heads.
$P(\text{heads}) \approx \dfrac{130}{200} = 0.65$

(b) If the coin is flipped 500 more times, how many heads are expected?
Expected heads $= 0.65 \times 500 = 325$

Increasing Reliability

The more trials carried out, the closer the experimental (relative) frequency gets to the true theoretical probability. This is the law of large numbers.

FeatureTheoretical ProbabilityExperimental Probability
Based onEqually likely outcomes / logicActual results of an experiment
Exact?Yes (for fair/ideal situations)An estimate; varies each time
When to useFair coins, dice, known situationsBiased items, real-world data
If you are asked to comment on whether a dice or spinner is fair, compare the experimental probability to the theoretical value $\frac{1}{6}$ (or whatever it should be). The more trials, the more reliable the comparison.

5.1.4 Venn Diagrams

A Venn diagram uses overlapping circles within a rectangle (the universal set $\xi$) to show relationships between sets of outcomes.

  • $A \cup B$ — Union: outcomes in $A$ or $B$ (or both)
  • $A \cap B$ — Intersection: outcomes in both $A$ and $B$
  • $A'$ — Complement: outcomes not in $A$

Probability Notation from Venn Diagrams

If the Venn diagram shows frequencies (counts), convert to probabilities by dividing by the total.

In a class of 30 students: 18 study French (F), 12 study Spanish (S), 7 study both.

Step 1 — Fill in the Venn diagram:
Only French: $18 - 7 = 11$  |  Both: $7$  |  Only Spanish: $12 - 7 = 5$  |  Neither: $30 - 11 - 7 - 5 = 7$

Step 2 — Find probabilities:
$P(F \cup S) = \dfrac{11 + 7 + 5}{30} = \dfrac{23}{30}$

$P(F \cap S) = \dfrac{7}{30}$

$P(\text{neither}) = \dfrac{7}{30}$

$P(F \text{ only}) = \dfrac{11}{30}$

Mutually Exclusive Events

Two events are mutually exclusive if they cannot both happen at the same time — their circles do not overlap.

If $A$ and $B$ are mutually exclusive: $P(A \cap B) = 0$ and $P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B)$
Always check your Venn diagram frequencies add up to the total before calculating probabilities. It is a very common error to double-count the intersection.

5.1.5 Tree Diagrams

A tree diagram shows all possible outcomes of two or more successive events.

  • Multiply along branches to find the probability of a combined outcome.
  • Add the probabilities of branches that give the same overall outcome.
  • Probabilities on branches from each node must sum to 1.
A bag has 3 red and 2 blue counters. Two are drawn without replacement.

Find P(one of each colour).

$P(RB) = \dfrac{3}{5} \times \dfrac{2}{4} = \dfrac{6}{20}$  and  $P(BR) = \dfrac{2}{5} \times \dfrac{3}{4} = \dfrac{6}{20}$

$P(\text{one of each}) = \dfrac{6}{20} + \dfrac{6}{20} = \dfrac{12}{20} = \dfrac{3}{5}$

With Replacement vs Without Replacement

With ReplacementWithout Replacement
Item is returned before next drawItem is NOT returned
Probabilities stay the same each stageProbabilities change at each stage
Events are independentEvents are dependent
Denominator stays constantDenominator decreases by 1 each time
In tree diagrams, always check that the branches from each node sum to 1. Also check that all final outcome probabilities sum to 1 as a verification step.

5.1.6 Conditional Probability [Higher]

Conditional probability is the probability of event $A$ given that event $B$ has already occurred.

$$P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}$$
This is particularly useful with Venn diagrams and two-way tables.
In a group of 50 people: 30 drink tea (T), 20 drink coffee (C), 10 drink both.

Find P(drinks tea | drinks coffee).

$P(T \cap C) = \dfrac{10}{50} = \dfrac{1}{5}$

$P(C) = \dfrac{20}{50} = \dfrac{2}{5}$

$P(T \mid C) = \dfrac{P(T \cap C)}{P(C)} = \dfrac{1/5}{2/5} = \dfrac{1}{2}$

Alternative method: Of the 20 coffee drinkers, 10 also drink tea: $\dfrac{10}{20} = \dfrac{1}{2}$ ✓

Two-Way Tables

Two-way tables allow you to read off conditional probabilities directly by restricting to the relevant row or column.

PassedFailedTotal
Revised24630
Did not revise81220
Total321850
Find P(passed | revised).

Looking only at the "Revised" row: 24 out of 30 passed.
$$P(\text{passed} \mid \text{revised}) = \frac{24}{30} = \frac{4}{5}$$
For conditional probability from a two-way table, restrict your attention to only the relevant row or column — that becomes your new "total". Do not use the overall total.

Independent Events

Events $A$ and $B$ are independent if $P(A \mid B) = P(A)$ — knowing $B$ occurred gives no information about $A$.

For independent events: $P(A \cap B) = P(A) \times P(B)$
