T3 — Organisms Exchange Substances

3.1 Surface Area to Volume Ratio

Surface area to volume ratio (SA:V): As organisms increase in size, their volume increases more rapidly than their surface area (volume increases as the cube, surface area as the square of linear dimensions). This means larger organisms have a smaller SA:V ratio, making simple diffusion across the body surface insufficient for exchange.

Consequences of a small SA:V ratio in large organisms:

  • Diffusion alone is too slow to supply all cells with oxygen and nutrients
  • Specialised exchange surfaces are needed (lungs, gills, villi)
  • Mass transport systems are needed (circulatory system) to move substances between exchange surfaces and cells

Features of effective exchange surfaces:

  • Large surface area (increases rate of diffusion)
  • Thin barrier / short diffusion pathway (one or two cells thick)
  • Good blood supply / ventilation (maintains steep concentration gradient)
  • Moist surface (allows gases to dissolve)
Rate of diffusion ∝ (Surface area × Concentration difference) / Thickness of barrier
(Fick's Law)

3.2 Gas Exchange

Gas Exchange in Humans (Lungs)

The lungs provide a vast surface area for gas exchange between air and blood. The exchange surface is the alveolar epithelium.

Adaptations of alveoli for efficient gas exchange:

  • Large total surface area: ~70 m² from approximately 300 million alveoli
  • Thin walls: Alveolar epithelium is one cell thick (squamous); capillary endothelium is also one cell thick — total diffusion distance ~0.5 micrometres
  • Rich blood supply: Dense capillary network maintains steep concentration gradient
  • Ventilation: Breathing constantly refreshes air in alveoli, maintaining gradient
  • Moist lining: Gases dissolve before diffusing across membranes

Gas Exchange in Fish (Gills)

Fish use a countercurrent exchange system in their gills. Water flows over the gill lamellae in the opposite direction to blood flow. This maintains a concentration gradient along the entire length of the lamella, allowing up to 80% of dissolved oxygen to be extracted from the water.

If blood and water flowed in the same direction (parallel flow), equilibrium would be reached halfway along, and only about 50% extraction would be possible.

Gas Exchange in Insects (Tracheal System)

Insects have a system of air-filled tubes (tracheae) that branch into smaller tracheoles, delivering oxygen directly to respiring cells. Air enters through spiracles on the body surface. There is no oxygen-carrying blood — gas exchange occurs directly between tracheole fluid and cells by diffusion. Ventilation is assisted by body movements that compress and expand the thorax and abdomen.

Gas Exchange in Plants (Leaves)

Gas exchange in plants occurs through stomata (pores on the leaf surface, mainly the lower epidermis). Guard cells control stomatal opening and closing. Mesophyll cells have a large internal surface area with air spaces between them for efficient CO2 absorption and O2 release during photosynthesis.

Exam Tip: The countercurrent system in fish gills is a very common exam question. Draw a diagram showing blood flowing left-to-right and water flowing right-to-left, with percentage oxygen at each point, demonstrating that a gradient is maintained throughout.

3.3 Digestion and Absorption

Digestion

Digestion is the breakdown of large insoluble food molecules into smaller soluble molecules that can be absorbed into the blood. There are two types:

  • Mechanical digestion: Physical breakdown (teeth, churning) increases surface area for enzyme action
  • Chemical digestion: Hydrolysis by enzymes breaks bonds in polymers

Bile is produced by the liver, stored in the gall bladder, and released into the duodenum. It emulsifies fats (breaks large droplets into smaller droplets), increasing the surface area for lipase action. Bile also neutralises stomach acid, providing the alkaline pH needed for pancreatic enzymes.

Absorption in the Ileum

The ileum is adapted for absorption with villi and microvilli that hugely increase the surface area. Each villus has:

  • A single layer of epithelial cells (thin barrier)
  • Microvilli (brush border) on the apical surface — further increases SA by ~20x
  • Dense capillary network — maintains concentration gradient for amino acids and glucose
  • A lacteal — absorbs fatty acids and glycerol as lipoproteins (chylomicrons)
  • Mitochondria-rich cells — provide ATP for active transport and co-transport

3.4 Mass Transport — The Circulatory System

Haemoglobin and Oxygen Transport

Haemoglobin: A globular protein with quaternary structure consisting of four polypeptide subunits (2 alpha, 2 beta), each containing an iron-containing haem group that can reversibly bind one molecule of oxygen. Fully saturated haemoglobin carries four O2 molecules.
Hb + 4O2 ⇌ HbO8 (oxyhaemoglobin)
(this is simplified; loading occurs cooperatively)

Cooperative binding: When one O2 molecule binds to a haem group, it causes a conformational change that makes it easier for subsequent O2 molecules to bind. This produces the characteristic S-shaped (sigmoid) oxygen dissociation curve.

The Bohr Effect: In respiring tissues, high CO2 concentration lowers pH. This causes haemoglobin to have a reduced affinity for oxygen, releasing it more readily to active tissues. The dissociation curve shifts to the right.

The Heart

The heart is a muscular double pump. The right side pumps deoxygenated blood to the lungs (pulmonary circulation); the left side pumps oxygenated blood to the body (systemic circulation).

Cardiac Cycle

  1. Atrial systole: Atria contract, pushing blood through AV valves into ventricles.
  2. Ventricular systole: Ventricles contract (AV valves close — "lub" sound), blood forced through semilunar valves into aorta/pulmonary artery.
  3. Diastole: Both chambers relax; blood flows into atria from veins; semilunar valves close ("dub" sound) preventing backflow.

The heart is myogenic — the cardiac cycle is initiated within the heart itself:

  1. SAN (sinoatrial node) in right atrium wall generates electrical impulses → wave of excitation spreads across both atria causing atrial systole.
  2. AVN (atrioventricular node) receives impulse, delays briefly (allowing atria to fully empty), then transmits impulse down the Bundle of His.
  3. Bundle of His carries impulse down the interventricular septum, divides into Purkinje fibres which spread up through ventricular walls → ventricular systole from apex upward.

3.5 Water Transport in Plants

Water Uptake and Movement Through Roots

Water enters root hair cells by osmosis (soil water has higher Ψ than root hair cell cytoplasm). Water then moves across the cortex to the xylem via three pathways:

  • Apoplast pathway: Through cell walls and intercellular spaces (no membrane crossing). Blocked at the endodermis by the Casparian strip (forces water through the symplast).
  • Symplast pathway: Through cytoplasm of cells, via plasmodesmata.
  • Vacuolar pathway: Through vacuoles of successive cells (a sub-type of symplast).
Casparian strip: A waxy (suberin) band in the walls of endodermal cells that blocks the apoplast pathway, forcing water and dissolved minerals into the symplast. This allows selective uptake of minerals by active transport into the xylem.

Transpiration and the Cohesion-Tension Theory

Transpiration is the loss of water vapour from leaves through stomata. This creates a transpiration pull that draws water up the xylem in a continuous column:

  1. Water evaporates from mesophyll cell walls into air spaces and diffuses out through stomata.
  2. Water loss from mesophyll cells lowers their Ψ, drawing water from neighbouring cells and from the xylem by osmosis.
  3. Cohesion (hydrogen bonds between water molecules) and adhesion (attraction to xylem walls) maintain a continuous water column under tension.
  4. Tension is transmitted down to roots, pulling water from the soil.

Exam Tip: A potometer measures the rate of water uptake, which is closely related to transpiration rate but not exactly the same (some water is used in photosynthesis/cell expansion). Always state this distinction in exam answers about experimental methods.

Translocation (Mass Flow in Phloem)

Translocation is the movement of assimilates (mainly sucrose and amino acids) in phloem sieve tubes from sources (e.g. leaves — where sugars are produced) to sinks (e.g. roots, fruits, growing regions — where sugars are used or stored).

The mass flow hypothesis explains translocation:

  1. At the source: sucrose is actively loaded into sieve tube elements via companion cells (using H+ co-transport). This lowers Ψ in the sieve tube.
  2. Water enters by osmosis from nearby xylem, raising hydrostatic pressure.
  3. At the sink: sucrose is actively unloaded from sieve tubes into sink cells. This raises Ψ in sieve tubes at the sink.
  4. Water leaves by osmosis, lowering hydrostatic pressure.
  5. The pressure difference between source and sink drives mass flow of phloem sap from source to sink.
