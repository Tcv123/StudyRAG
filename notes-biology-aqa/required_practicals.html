⚗️ RP1: Effect of a Variable on Enzyme-Controlled Reaction Rate

Apparatus

  • Test tubes and rack
  • Catalase (potato extract) and hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂) solution
  • Gas syringe or inverted measuring cylinder
  • Delivery tube with bung
  • Water bath (for temperature control)
  • Buffer solutions (for pH investigation)
  • Thermometer, stopwatch, ruler

Method

  1. Set up water bath at required temperature (e.g. 20, 30, 40, 50, 60 °C). Equilibrate enzyme and substrate for 5 minutes.
  2. Add catalase to H₂O₂ solution, immediately fit bung and delivery tube connected to gas syringe.
  3. Record volume of O₂ gas collected every 30 seconds for 5 minutes.
  4. Repeat at each temperature (or pH/concentration). At least 3 repeats per condition.
  5. Calculate initial rate of reaction from the steepest part of the curve (first 60 s).

Safety

  • H₂O₂ is an irritant at higher concentrations — wear eye protection and gloves.
  • Care with hot water baths — do not exceed 60 °C.
  • O₂ is released — ensure good ventilation.

Analysis

  • Plot volume of O₂ (y-axis) against time (x-axis) for each condition.
  • Calculate initial rate = volume / time from the linear portion of each curve.
  • Plot rate (y-axis) against temperature/pH/concentration (x-axis).
  • Optimum temperature or pH is the peak of the curve.

Sources of Error

ErrorEffectImprovement
Delay fitting bungGas escapes, rate appears lowerPractise technique; use side-arm flask
Temperature fluctuationRate varies inconsistentlyUse thermostatically controlled water bath
Enzyme concentration variesUnreliable comparisonsUse same batch of enzyme throughout
Bubbles in delivery tubeVolume readings inaccurateEnsure tube is filled with water before start
Exam Tip: Always state that you would calculate the initial rate of reaction. The rate at time = 0 avoids issues with substrate depletion and product inhibition.

⚗️ RP2: Stained Squashes of Root Tip Cells (Mitosis)

Apparatus

  • Garlic or onion root tips (grown in water for 3–5 days)
  • Acetic orcein or toluidine blue stain
  • 1 M hydrochloric acid, watch glass, Bunsen burner
  • Microscope slides and coverslips
  • Optical microscope, mounted needle, pencil eraser

Method

  1. Cut approximately 2 mm of the growing root tip and place on a watch glass.
  2. Add 1 M HCl and warm gently at ~60 °C for 5 minutes to soften tissue and separate cells.
  3. Blot dry, then add a few drops of acetic orcein stain. Leave for 10 minutes.
  4. Transfer to a slide, add a coverslip, and squash firmly with a pencil eraser (blot excess).
  5. View under microscope, starting with low power (x100), then high power (x400).
  6. Identify and count cells in each stage of mitosis. Calculate the mitotic index.

Safety

  • HCl is corrosive — wear gloves and eye protection.
  • Acetic orcein stains skin and clothing; handle carefully.
  • Care with Bunsen burner; use water bath instead if possible.

Analysis

  • Mitotic index = number of cells in mitosis / total number of cells observed.
  • Record the number of cells at each stage: prophase, metaphase, anaphase, telophase.
  • The proportion of cells at each stage reflects the relative duration of that phase.

Sources of Error

ErrorEffectImprovement
Cells overlap after squashDifficult to identify stagesSquash more firmly; use thinner section
Insufficient stainingChromosomes not visibleLeave stain longer; ensure fresh stain used
Counting non-root-tip cellsLower mitotic indexOnly cut the very tip (meristematic region)
Small sample sizeUnreliable mitotic indexCount at least 200 cells from multiple fields
Exam Tip: Prophase takes the longest so will have the most cells. Anaphase is the shortest. Always state mitotic index = cells in mitosis / total cells.

⚗️ RP3: Dilution Series & Calibration Curve

Apparatus

  • Stock solution of known concentration (e.g. glucose or food dye)
  • Distilled water, graduated pipettes or syringes
  • Test tubes and rack, colorimeter and cuvettes
  • Appropriate colour filter for colorimeter

Method

  1. Prepare a serial dilution: transfer a fixed volume of stock to the first tube, add equal volume of distilled water, mix, transfer to next tube, and repeat (e.g. five 1-in-2 dilutions).
  2. Add appropriate reagent if needed (e.g. Benedict’s or biuret) and develop colour.
  3. Zero the colorimeter using a blank (distilled water + reagent).
  4. Measure the absorbance of each known concentration.
  5. Plot absorbance (y-axis) against concentration (x-axis) to produce a calibration curve.
  6. Measure the absorbance of the unknown solution and read off its concentration from the curve.

Safety

  • Benedict’s reagent contains copper sulfate — irritant. Wear eye protection.
  • Biuret reagent contains NaOH — corrosive. Handle with care.

Analysis

  • Draw a line of best fit through the calibration points.
  • Read unknown concentration by drawing a horizontal line from its absorbance to the curve, then vertically down to the x-axis.
  • The calibration curve should be linear at low concentrations.

Sources of Error

ErrorEffectImprovement
Air bubbles in cuvetteIncorrect absorbance readingTap cuvette gently; wipe with tissue
Inaccurate pipettingDilution concentrations wrongUse calibrated pipettes; practise technique
Fingerprints on cuvetteLight absorbed/scattered by greaseHandle by top; wipe with lens tissue
Wrong filter selectedLow sensitivity of readingsUse complementary colour filter
Exam Tip: A serial dilution gives a wide range of concentrations in equal ratio steps. Each dilution halves the concentration (if 1 in 2). Always use the same volume transferred at each step.

⚗️ RP4: Effect of a Variable on Membrane Permeability

Apparatus

  • Fresh beetroot, cork borer, white tile, scalpel
  • Test tubes and rack, distilled water
  • Water baths at range of temperatures (e.g. 20, 30, 40, 50, 60, 70 °C)
  • Colorimeter and cuvettes, thermometer

Method

  1. Use cork borer to cut equal-sized beetroot discs. Rinse thoroughly in cold distilled water to remove surface pigment.
  2. Place one disc in each test tube with 5 cm³ distilled water.
  3. Place tubes in water baths at each temperature for 20 minutes.
  4. Remove beetroot discs. Agitate solution gently.
  5. Measure absorbance of each solution using a colorimeter (green/blue filter).
  6. Repeat 3 times at each temperature and calculate mean absorbance.

Safety

  • Sharp instruments (scalpel, cork borer) — cut away from body on white tile.
  • Hot water baths — handle with care; use tongs for tubes.
  • Beetroot stains clothing — wear lab coat.

Analysis

  • Plot mean absorbance (y-axis) against temperature (x-axis).
  • Higher absorbance = more anthocyanin leakage = greater membrane disruption.
  • Above ~40 °C, proteins denature and phospholipids become more fluid, increasing permeability sharply.

Sources of Error

ErrorEffectImprovement
Unequal disc sizesVariable surface area affects leakageUse same cork borer; cut to uniform thickness
Insufficient rinsingSurface pigment inflates readingsRinse until water runs clear
Different time in water bathInconsistent resultsUse a stopwatch; start at timed intervals
Damaged cells from cuttingExtra pigment releasedDiscard end pieces; use middle sections
Exam Tip: Link to membrane structure: at high temperatures, phospholipids become more fluid and membrane proteins denature, creating gaps in the membrane through which anthocyanin leaks.

⚗️ RP5: Dissection of Gas Exchange / Mass Transport System

Apparatus

  • Fresh fish head (from fishmonger) or preserved specimen
  • Dissecting board, pins, scissors, forceps, scalpel
  • Magnifying glass or dissecting microscope
  • Paper towels, gloves, eye protection

Method

  1. Place fish head on dissecting board. Lift the operculum (gill cover) to expose the gills.
  2. Use scissors to carefully cut away the operculum.
  3. Identify the gill arches (4 on each side), gill filaments, and gill rakers.
  4. Remove one gill arch and place in water on a white tile. Use a magnifying glass to observe the lamellae (secondary filaments).
  5. Draw and label the structures observed. Note adaptations for gas exchange.

Safety

  • Wear gloves when handling biological specimens.
  • Sharp instruments — cut away from body, use a dissecting board.
  • Wash hands thoroughly after handling.
  • Dispose of biological material in designated bins.

Analysis

  • Identify adaptations: large surface area (many lamellae), thin walls (short diffusion distance), rich blood supply (counter-current system).
  • Produce a labelled biological drawing with clear lines, no shading.
  • Relate structure to function for gas exchange efficiency.

Sources of Error

ErrorEffectImprovement
Damaged structures during cuttingDifficult to identify featuresUse fine scissors and forceps; cut carefully
Dried-out tissueStructures shrink and distortKeep specimen moist with water
Difficulty identifying lamellaeIncomplete observationsUse dissecting microscope; place in water
Exam Tip: In dissection questions, always link structure to function. For gills: many lamellae = large surface area; thin epithelium = short diffusion distance; counter-current flow = maintains concentration gradient.

⚗️ RP6: Aseptic Technique & Antimicrobial Substances

Apparatus

  • Nutrient agar plates (pre-poured and sterilised)
  • Broth culture of bacteria (e.g. E. coli, non-pathogenic strain)
  • Antibiotic discs / plant extracts (e.g. garlic, mint)
  • Sterile spreader or inoculating loop, Bunsen burner
  • Forceps (flamed), marker pen, adhesive tape, ruler

Method

  1. Work near a lit Bunsen burner to create an updraft (aseptic zone).
  2. Flame the inoculating loop, dip into bacterial broth, and spread evenly across the agar plate using a zigzag pattern (or use a sterile spreader).
  3. Using flamed forceps, place antibiotic discs at equal distances on the agar surface.
  4. Seal the plate with two strips of tape (do not seal completely — anaerobic pathogens risk). Label the base.
  5. Incubate upside down at a maximum of 25 °C for 24–48 hours.
  6. Measure the diameter of each zone of inhibition with a ruler.

Safety

  • Incubate at ≤25 °C in schools (to reduce growth of human pathogens).
  • Do not open plates after incubation.
  • Autoclave all plates before disposal.
  • Aseptic technique throughout to prevent contamination.

Analysis

  • Measure diameter of each zone and calculate the area: A = πr².
  • Larger zone = more effective antimicrobial substance.
  • Compare areas (not diameters) for a valid comparison of effectiveness.

Sources of Error

ErrorEffectImprovement
Contamination from airUnwanted colonies growWork near Bunsen; flame equipment
Uneven bacterial spreadInconsistent zonesUse sterile spreader; rotate plate
Discs placed too close togetherOverlapping zonesSpace discs evenly; use template
Measuring diameter not areaUnderestimates differencesCalculate πr² for each zone
Exam Tip: Always calculate the area of the zone of inhibition (πr²) not just the diameter. This is because area is proportional to the amount of antibiotic that has diffused. State why plates are incubated at ≤25 °C in school labs.

⚗️ RP7: Chromatography of Leaf Pigments

Apparatus

  • Fresh leaves (spinach or nettle), pestle and mortar, sand
  • Propanone (acetone) or petroleum ether/acetone mix as solvent
  • Chromatography paper or TLC plate, pencil, ruler
  • Boiling tube or chromatography jar with lid, capillary tube

Method

  1. Grind leaves with a small amount of sand and propanone in a mortar. Filter the extract.
  2. Draw a pencil line 2 cm from the bottom of the chromatography paper.
  3. Apply leaf extract using a capillary tube in small, repeated spots on the origin line. Allow to dry between applications.
  4. Place paper in the jar with solvent level below the origin line. Seal with a lid.
  5. Allow solvent to rise until it nears the top. Mark the solvent front immediately.
  6. Remove paper, dry it, and measure distances from origin to centre of each pigment spot.

Safety

  • Propanone is highly flammable and produces fumes — no flames; use in well-ventilated area or fume cupboard.
  • Avoid skin contact with solvents — wear gloves.

Analysis

  • Calculate Rf value for each pigment: Rf = distance moved by pigment / distance moved by solvent front.
  • Compare Rf values to reference values to identify each pigment.
  • Typical order (highest Rf first): carotene, xanthophyll, chlorophyll a, chlorophyll b.

Sources of Error

ErrorEffectImprovement
Origin line below solventPigments dissolve into solventEnsure solvent level is below the origin
Lid not sealedSolvent evaporates, uneven frontUse well-sealed container
Too much extract appliedSpots smear and overlapApply small amounts; dry between applications
Using pen instead of pencilInk runs with solventAlways use pencil for origin line
Exam Tip: Use a pencil (not pen) for the origin line because ink would dissolve in the solvent. Carotene has the highest Rf because it is the most soluble in the non-polar solvent (it is non-polar itself).

⚗️ RP8: Effect of a Variable on Respiration Rate

Apparatus

  • Respirometer (two-tube type) or simple manometer
  • Organisms: germinating seeds, woodlice, or yeast suspension
  • Soda lime or potassium hydroxide (to absorb CO₂)
  • Capillary tube with scale, coloured fluid drop
  • Water bath, thermometer, stopwatch

Method

  1. Set up the respirometer: place organisms in one tube with soda lime at the base. Set up a control tube with glass beads (equal volume) and soda lime.
  2. Seal both tubes and connect to the manometer/capillary tube.
  3. Place in water bath at desired temperature. Allow to equilibrate for 5 minutes.
  4. Introduce a coloured fluid drop into the capillary tube.
  5. Record the distance the fluid drop moves towards the organisms every minute for 10 minutes.
  6. Repeat at different temperatures. Calculate rate of O₂ uptake per minute.

Safety

  • Soda lime and KOH are corrosive — wear gloves and eye protection.
  • Handle organisms ethically — return woodlice to habitat afterwards.
  • Do not heat organisms directly.

Analysis

  • Rate of O₂ consumption = distance moved by fluid / time.
  • Plot rate of O₂ uptake (y-axis) against temperature (x-axis).
  • The control tube corrects for changes in atmospheric pressure and temperature.

Sources of Error

ErrorEffectImprovement
Leaks in apparatusDrop does not move correctlyCheck all seals; use Vaseline on joints
Soda lime exhaustedCO₂ not absorbed, drop doesn’t moveUse fresh soda lime for each experiment
Temperature fluctuationAffects gas volumes regardless of respirationUse thermostatically controlled water bath
Organisms stressedAbnormal respiration rateAllow equilibration period; handle gently
Exam Tip: The soda lime absorbs CO₂, so only O₂ consumption causes the volume change. The control tube accounts for any changes in pressure or temperature that are not due to respiration. Always include a control.

⚗️ RP9: Choice Chamber (Animal Movement)

Apparatus

  • Choice chamber (two-sided with perforated divider and clear lid)
  • 10–20 woodlice (collected humanely)
  • Damp cotton wool, dry silica gel (desiccant)
  • Black card or aluminium foil (for dark conditions)
  • Stopwatch, lamp

Method

  1. Set up two contrasting environments in the choice chamber (e.g. one side dark and humid, the other light and dry).
  2. Place 10 woodlice in the centre of the chamber (on the divider).
  3. Replace the lid and start the timer.
  4. Count the number of woodlice on each side every 2 minutes for 20 minutes.
  5. Record results in a table. Repeat 3 times. Calculate means.
  6. Test only one variable at a time (e.g. humidity OR light, not both).

Safety

  • Handle woodlice gently — ethical treatment of organisms.
  • Return to habitat after the experiment.
  • Wash hands after handling.

Analysis

  • Calculate mean number on each side at each time interval.
  • Woodlice show a kinesis (non-directional change in speed): they move more slowly in preferred conditions (dark, humid), so accumulate there.
  • Use a chi-squared test to determine if the distribution is significantly different from chance.

Sources of Error

ErrorEffectImprovement
Woodlice on dividerDifficult to assign to a sideDecide rule beforehand (e.g. head position)
Two variables changed at onceCannot determine which causes preferenceOnly vary one factor per experiment
Small sample sizeResults not statistically validUse at least 10 woodlice; repeat 3 times
Temperature differences between sidesConfounding variableKeep both sides at same temperature
Exam Tip: Woodlice show kinesis, not taxis. Kinesis is a non-directional response where the organism changes its speed or rate of turning. They slow down in favourable conditions and speed up in unfavourable ones, leading to accumulation in the preferred area.

⚗️ RP10: Distribution of Organisms (Quadrats & Transects)

Apparatus

  • Quadrats (e.g. 0.5 m x 0.5 m frame)
  • Tape measure (30 m) for transect line
  • Random number generator or table
  • Identification keys, recording sheets
  • Abiotic measurement tools: light meter, soil pH probe, moisture meter, thermometer

Method

  1. Random sampling: Use random number pairs as coordinates to place quadrats. Count or estimate % cover of target species in each quadrat.
  2. Systematic sampling (transect): Lay a tape measure across a habitat (e.g. from shore to inland). Place quadrats at regular intervals along the transect.
  3. At each quadrat, record species present and abundance. Measure abiotic factors at the same point.
  4. Repeat to ensure reliability. Calculate mean species density or % cover.

Safety

  • Risk assessment for fieldwork (terrain, weather, lone working).
  • Wash hands after handling soil.
  • Be aware of hazardous plants (nettles, thorns) and animals.

Analysis

  • Calculate species density = mean count per quadrat / quadrat area.
  • Use Spearman’s rank correlation to test for association between distribution and an abiotic factor.
  • Use a kite diagram or bar chart to display distribution along a transect.
  • Simpson’s Diversity Index: D = 1 − Σ(n/N)²

Sources of Error

ErrorEffectImprovement
Non-random placementSampling biasUse random number table for coordinates
Too few quadratsUnrepresentative dataUse at least 10 quadrats per area
Misidentification of speciesInaccurate dataUse identification keys; take photos
Seasonal variationResults not comparableSample at the same time of year
Exam Tip: Random sampling avoids bias. Use coordinates from a random number table. For transects, explain why systematic sampling is appropriate (to study changes along an environmental gradient). Always state how you would measure one abiotic factor.

⚗️ RP11: Effect of a Variable on Organism Development

Apparatus

  • Seeds (e.g. cress, mustard, or bean seeds), Petri dishes or seed trays
  • Cotton wool or vermiculite, distilled water
  • Light source or dark cupboard
  • Ruler, thermometer, labels

Method

  1. Place equal numbers of seeds (e.g. 10) on damp cotton wool in labelled Petri dishes.
  2. Set up different conditions: vary one factor (e.g. light intensity, temperature, or water availability).
  3. Keep all other variables constant (same number of seeds, same volume of water, same temperature where possible).
  4. Record germination percentage and seedling growth (shoot height) daily for 10–14 days.
  5. Repeat each condition at least 3 times. Calculate means.

Safety

  • Seeds may be treated with fungicide — wash hands after handling.
  • Some seeds are toxic — do not consume. Avoid inhalation of vermiculite dust.

Analysis

  • Calculate germination percentage at each time point.
  • Plot mean shoot height (y-axis) against time (x-axis) for each condition.
  • Use statistical tests (t-test or Mann-Whitney U) to compare conditions.

Sources of Error

ErrorEffectImprovement
Variable seed viabilitySome seeds may not germinate regardlessUse seeds from same batch; test viability first
Unequal wateringConfounding variableMeasure exact volume of water added
Temperature differencesAffects growth rate independentlyKeep all dishes at same temperature
Fungal contaminationSeeds/seedlings may dieUse sterile cotton wool; handle aseptically
Exam Tip: Always identify the independent variable, dependent variable, and control variables. A large sample size (many seeds per dish) improves reliability. State a statistical test you could use to analyse the results.

⚗️ RP12: Immobilised Enzyme Investigation

Apparatus

  • Sodium alginate solution, calcium chloride solution (1–2%)
  • Enzyme (e.g. lactase), syringe (without needle), beaker
  • Syringe barrel or small column, glass wool plug
  • Substrate solution (e.g. milk for lactase)
  • Glucose test strips or Benedict’s reagent for testing product

Method

  1. Mix enzyme solution with sodium alginate solution.
  2. Using a syringe, drip the mixture into calcium chloride solution to form beads. Leave for a few minutes to harden.
  3. Rinse beads with distilled water. Pack into a syringe barrel (column) with glass wool at the bottom.
  4. Pour substrate (milk) through the column and collect the product.
  5. Test the product for glucose using test strips or Benedict’s reagent.
  6. Compare the rate and efficiency of immobilised enzyme vs free enzyme at different temperatures.

Safety

  • Calcium chloride is an irritant — wear eye protection.
  • Wash hands after handling enzymes and chemicals.
  • Do not consume any products.

Analysis

  • Compare glucose concentration in product from immobilised vs free enzyme.
  • Test reusability: pass substrate through the column multiple times and compare product each time.
  • Immobilised enzymes can be reused, tolerate higher temperatures, and do not contaminate the product.

Sources of Error

ErrorEffectImprovement
Beads of uneven sizeVariable surface area and reaction rateKeep syringe height constant; practise technique
Substrate flows too fastInsufficient contact time with enzymeReduce flow rate; use smaller column
Air gaps in columnSubstrate bypasses beadsPack column carefully; tap to remove air
Enzyme leaching from beadsRate decreases over reuseEnsure beads are well-hardened in CaCl₂
Exam Tip: State the advantages of immobilised enzymes: they can be reused, the product is not contaminated with enzyme, they are more tolerant of temperature and pH changes. These advantages reduce costs in industrial processes.
