2.1 Cell Structure

Cell: The basic structural and functional unit of all living organisms. Cells are classified as either prokaryotic (no membrane-bound nucleus) or eukaryotic (membrane-bound nucleus and organelles).

Prokaryotic Cells

Prokaryotic cells (bacteria) are smaller (typically 1-5 micrometres) and simpler than eukaryotic cells:

  • No membrane-bound nucleus — DNA is a single circular chromosome in the nucleoid region
  • Small circular plasmids may also be present (carry genes for antibiotic resistance)
  • 70S ribosomes (smaller than eukaryotic 80S)
  • No membrane-bound organelles (no mitochondria, ER, Golgi)
  • Cell wall made of peptidoglycan (murein), not cellulose
  • May have flagella for movement, pili for attachment, and a capsule (slime layer) for protection
Exam Tip: Mitochondria and chloroplasts both have their own DNA and 70S ribosomes — this is evidence for the endosymbiotic theory (they were once free-living prokaryotes engulfed by an ancestral eukaryote).

2.2 Cell Fractionation and Ultracentrifugation

Cell fractionation: The process of separating cell components by breaking open cells (homogenisation) and spinning at increasing speeds (differential centrifugation) to isolate organelles by size and density.

Steps in cell fractionation:

  1. Homogenisation: Cells are broken open in ice-cold, isotonic, buffered solution. Ice-cold prevents enzyme activity; isotonic prevents osmotic damage to organelles; buffered maintains constant pH.
  2. Filtration: The homogenate is filtered to remove large debris (cell walls, connective tissue).
  3. Differential centrifugation: The filtrate is centrifuged at increasing speeds. The heaviest organelles sediment first as a pellet; the supernatant is transferred to a new tube and spun faster.

Order of sedimentation (heaviest first): Nuclei → Mitochondria → Lysosomes → Ribosomes/ER

2.3 Transport Across Cell Membranes

The Fluid Mosaic Model

Fluid Mosaic Model: The cell membrane is described as a phospholipid bilayer with proteins embedded (integral/transmembrane) or attached to the surface (peripheral). It is "fluid" because phospholipids and proteins can move laterally, and "mosaic" because of the varied pattern of protein molecules.

Components of the membrane:

  • Phospholipid bilayer: Forms the basic barrier; hydrophobic core prevents passage of polar/charged molecules
  • Cholesterol: Regulates fluidity — prevents membranes becoming too fluid or too rigid
  • Channel proteins: Water-filled pores allowing specific ions/polar molecules through (some are gated)
  • Carrier proteins: Change shape to transport specific molecules (facilitated diffusion or active transport)
  • Glycoproteins/glycolipids: Cell recognition, signalling, and adhesion

Diffusion

Diffusion: The net movement of molecules or ions from a region of higher concentration to a region of lower concentration, down a concentration gradient. It is a passive process requiring no metabolic energy.

Factors affecting rate of diffusion: concentration gradient (steeper = faster), temperature (higher = faster kinetic energy), surface area, thickness of membrane/exchange surface, size of molecule (smaller = faster).

Facilitated diffusion: Diffusion of polar molecules or ions through channel proteins or carrier proteins. Still passive (down the concentration gradient) but requires specific membrane proteins.

Osmosis

Osmosis: The net movement of water molecules from a region of higher water potential (less negative) to a region of lower water potential (more negative) across a partially permeable membrane.
Water potential (Ψ) = Pressure potential (Ψp) + Solute potential (Ψs)

Pure water has Ψ = 0. Adding solute makes Ψs more negative, lowering overall Ψ. Water always moves from higher Ψ to lower Ψ.

Effects on cells:

  • Animal cell in hypotonic solution: Water enters by osmosis → cell swells → may lyse (cytolysis)
  • Animal cell in hypertonic solution: Water leaves → cell shrinks (crenation)
  • Plant cell in hypotonic solution: Water enters → vacuole expands → cell becomes turgid (cell wall prevents lysis)
  • Plant cell in hypertonic solution: Water leaves → membrane pulls away from wall (plasmolysis)

Active Transport

Active transport: The movement of molecules or ions across a membrane against their concentration gradient (from low to high concentration), requiring metabolic energy from ATP hydrolysis and specific carrier proteins.

Co-transport

Co-transport couples the movement of one substance down its concentration gradient with the transport of another substance against its gradient. In the ileum:

  1. Na+/K+ ATPase pumps Na+ out of the epithelial cell into blood, maintaining a low intracellular Na+ concentration.
  2. Na+ diffuses back into the cell from the gut lumen via a sodium-glucose co-transporter, carrying glucose with it against glucose's concentration gradient.
  3. Glucose exits the cell into blood via facilitated diffusion through GLUT2 transporters on the basal membrane.
Exam Tip: Co-transport still requires ATP indirectly — the Na+/K+ ATPase creates the sodium gradient that drives glucose uptake. It is not purely passive.

2.4 Cell Recognition and the Immune System

Self and Non-Self

Every cell displays specific proteins and glycoproteins on its surface. The immune system recognises "self" antigens (the organism's own markers) and mounts a response against "non-self" antigens (from pathogens or foreign cells).

Antigen: A molecule (usually a protein or glycoprotein) on the surface of a cell that can trigger an immune response. Pathogens carry foreign antigens that are recognised by the immune system.

Phagocytosis

  1. Phagocyte (e.g. macrophage) is attracted to pathogen by chemicals released at site of infection (chemotaxis).
  2. Phagocyte recognises non-self antigens on the pathogen surface.
  3. Phagocyte engulfs pathogen by endocytosis, forming a phagosome.
  4. Lysosomes fuse with phagosome, releasing hydrolytic enzymes (lysozymes) that digest the pathogen.
  5. Phagocyte presents pathogen antigens on its surface (becomes an antigen-presenting cell, APC) to activate the specific immune response.

T Lymphocytes (T Cells)

T cells mature in the thymus and have specific receptors complementary to one antigen. When activated by APCs:

  • Helper T cells (TH): Release cytokines that stimulate B cells to divide, activate cytotoxic T cells, and attract phagocytes.
  • Cytotoxic T cells (TC): Kill infected body cells by releasing perforin (creates pores in target cell membrane) causing lysis.
  • Memory T cells: Remain in the body long-term for a faster secondary response.

B Lymphocytes (B Cells) and Antibodies

B cells mature in the bone marrow. Each B cell produces one type of antibody complementary to one specific antigen. When activated (by helper T cells and antigen binding):

  • B cells undergo clonal expansion (rapid mitosis).
  • They differentiate into plasma cells (secrete large quantities of antibodies) and memory B cells.
Antibody: A Y-shaped glycoprotein (immunoglobulin) produced by plasma cells. It has two identical antigen-binding sites in the variable regions, which are complementary to one specific antigen. The constant region determines the class of antibody.

How antibodies work:

  • Agglutination: Clumping pathogens together so phagocytes can engulf them more easily
  • Neutralisation: Blocking binding sites on toxins or viruses
  • Opsonisation: Coating pathogens to make them more recognisable to phagocytes

Vaccination

Vaccination: The introduction of a weakened, inactivated, or fragment of a pathogen (containing antigens) to stimulate a primary immune response and production of memory cells, without causing the disease. This provides immunity against future infection.

Types of vaccines: live attenuated, inactivated whole pathogen, subunit (purified antigen), toxoid, mRNA vaccines.

HIV and the Immune System

HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus) is a retrovirus that infects and destroys helper T cells (CD4+ cells):

  1. HIV binds to CD4 receptor on helper T cells using its glycoprotein gp120.
  2. Viral RNA is reverse transcribed into DNA by reverse transcriptase.
  3. Viral DNA integrates into host genome.
  4. Host cell machinery produces new viral particles.
  5. Helper T cell is destroyed during viral budding.

As helper T cell count falls, the immune system becomes compromised. The patient becomes vulnerable to opportunistic infections — this stage is AIDS (Acquired Immunodeficiency Syndrome).

Exam Tip: HIV does not directly kill the person — it destroys helper T cells, meaning the body cannot mount effective immune responses to other infections. Always make this distinction clear in exam answers.
