T6 — Organisms Respond to Changes in Their Environment

6.1 Stimuli and Receptors

Stimulus: A detectable change in the internal or external environment of an organism that produces a response. Receptor: A cell or structure that detects a stimulus and converts it into an electrical impulse (a transducer).

Pacinian Corpuscle — A Model Receptor

The Pacinian corpuscle is a pressure receptor found in skin, joints, and mesentery. It demonstrates how receptors generate a nerve impulse:

  1. Pressure deforms the layered (lamellar) capsule.
  2. Deformation stretches the sensory neurone ending (node of Ranvier exposed).
  3. Stretch-mediated sodium ion channels open.
  4. Na+ ions flood into the neurone, depolarising the membrane.
  5. If the depolarisation reaches the threshold potential, an action potential (nerve impulse) is generated.

6.2 Nervous Coordination

The Nerve Impulse — Action Potential

Resting potential: The potential difference across a neurone membrane at rest (~-70 mV, inside negative relative to outside). Maintained by the Na+/K+ ATPase pump (3 Na+ out, 2 K+ in) and K+ leak channels.

Stages of an action potential:

  1. Stimulus received: Sodium ion channels open at the stimulated region.
  2. Depolarisation: Na+ rushes into the axon (down its electrochemical gradient). Membrane potential rises from -70 mV to about +40 mV.
  3. Repolarisation: Voltage-gated Na+ channels close; voltage-gated K+ channels open. K+ flows out, restoring negative potential inside.
  4. Hyperpolarisation: Slight overshoot below -70 mV as K+ channels are slow to close.
  5. Refractory period: Na+ channels are inactivated; another action potential cannot be generated. This ensures impulses travel in one direction and limits impulse frequency.

Transmission Speed

Factors affecting the speed of impulse transmission:

  • Myelination: Myelin sheath (Schwann cells) insulates the axon. Depolarisation only occurs at nodes of Ranvier — impulse "jumps" between nodes (saltatory conduction), greatly increasing speed.
  • Axon diameter: Wider axons have less resistance to ion flow, so faster transmission.
  • Temperature: Higher temperature increases rate of ion diffusion and enzyme activity (up to a point).

Synapses

Synapse: The junction between two neurones (or between a neurone and an effector). Transmission is typically chemical, using neurotransmitter molecules that diffuse across the synaptic cleft.

Synaptic transmission (cholinergic synapse):

  1. Action potential arrives at presynaptic knob.
  2. Voltage-gated Ca2+ channels open; Ca2+ influx into presynaptic knob.
  3. Ca2+ causes synaptic vesicles to fuse with presynaptic membrane (exocytosis), releasing acetylcholine (ACh) into the synaptic cleft.
  4. ACh diffuses across cleft and binds to specific receptors on the postsynaptic membrane.
  5. Na+ channels on postsynaptic membrane open → depolarisation → action potential in postsynaptic neurone (if threshold reached).
  6. ACh is broken down by acetylcholinesterase in the cleft. Choline is reabsorbed and recycled.

Functions of synapses:

  • Ensure unidirectional transmission (receptors only on postsynaptic membrane)
  • Allow summation (temporal: rapid repeated impulses; spatial: impulses from multiple presynaptic neurones)
  • Allow integration and processing of information
  • Allow inhibition (inhibitory neurotransmitters cause Cl- influx or K+ efflux — hyperpolarisation, making action potential less likely)

Reflex Arcs

A reflex arc is the neural pathway for a rapid, involuntary response (reflex). Components: receptor → sensory neurone → relay neurone (in CNS) → motor neurone → effector (muscle/gland). Reflexes are fast, protective, and do not require conscious thought.

Exam Tip: When describing synaptic transmission, include specific detail about calcium ions triggering vesicle fusion. A common mistake is to say neurotransmitter "travels" across the cleft — it diffuses. Always use precise terminology.

6.3 Muscle Contraction — The Sliding Filament Theory

Sliding Filament Theory: Muscle contraction occurs when actin (thin) filaments slide over myosin (thick) filaments, shortening the sarcomere. The filaments themselves do not shorten — they slide past each other using cross-bridge cycling powered by ATP.

Structure of Skeletal Muscle

Muscle → fascicles → muscle fibres (cells) → myofibrils → sarcomeres (functional units).

Sarcomere bands:

  • I band: Light band — actin only (shortens during contraction)
  • A band: Dark band — myosin (and overlapping actin). Length stays constant.
  • H zone: Centre of A band — myosin only (shortens during contraction)
  • Z line: Boundary of sarcomere; anchors actin filaments
  • M line: Centre of sarcomere; anchors myosin

Mechanism of Contraction

  1. Nerve impulse arrives at neuromuscular junction → ACh released → depolarisation of muscle fibre membrane (sarcolemma).
  2. Impulse travels along sarcolemma and down T-tubules to sarcoplasmic reticulum (SR).
  3. SR releases stored Ca2+ ions into the sarcoplasm.
  4. Ca2+ binds to troponin on actin filaments, causing tropomyosin to move and expose myosin binding sites.
  5. Cross-bridge formation: Myosin head (with ADP + Pi bound) attaches to exposed binding site on actin.
  6. Power stroke: Myosin head pivots, pulling actin towards centre of sarcomere. ADP + Pi released.
  7. Detachment: New ATP binds to myosin head, causing it to detach from actin.
  8. Recovery stroke: ATP is hydrolysed (by myosin ATPase) → myosin head re-cocks to high-energy position. Cycle repeats as long as Ca2+ and ATP are available.

When stimulation stops, Ca2+ is actively pumped back into the SR (requiring ATP). Troponin-tropomyosin complex re-covers binding sites. Muscle relaxes.

Exam Tip: ATP is needed for BOTH contraction (myosin head power stroke recovery) AND relaxation (active transport of Ca2+ back into SR). This explains rigor mortis — without ATP after death, myosin heads remain locked to actin.

6.4 Homeostasis

Homeostasis: The maintenance of a constant internal environment within tolerable limits despite changes in the external environment. It involves receptors detecting deviations, a coordination centre processing information, and effectors bringing about corrective responses.

Negative Feedback

Negative feedback is the mechanism by which a deviation from a set point triggers a response that returns the system to the set point. It involves: stimulus → receptor → coordination centre → effector → response that opposes the original change.

The Kidney and Osmoregulation

The kidney regulates water potential of blood by adjusting the volume and concentration of urine produced.

Nephron function:

  • Ultrafiltration (Bowman's capsule): High blood pressure in glomerulus forces small molecules (water, glucose, urea, ions) through basement membrane into capsule. Blood cells and proteins remain.
  • Selective reabsorption (PCT): Glucose, amino acids, and most water/ions are reabsorbed (active transport and osmosis). Na+/glucose co-transport on apical membrane.
  • Loop of Henle: Creates a concentration gradient in the medulla (countercurrent multiplier) — essential for concentrating urine.
  • Collecting duct: Permeability to water is controlled by ADH (antidiuretic hormone).

ADH and Water Reabsorption

When blood water potential is low (dehydration):

  1. Osmoreceptors in hypothalamus detect low Ψ (cells shrink).
  2. Posterior pituitary releases more ADH into blood.
  3. ADH binds to receptors on collecting duct cells → aquaporin channels inserted into membrane.
  4. More water is reabsorbed by osmosis (following the medullary gradient) → small volume of concentrated urine.

When blood water potential is high: less ADH released → fewer aquaporins → large volume of dilute urine.

Exam Tip: ADH makes the collecting duct MORE permeable to water — it does not "move water." Water moves by osmosis down the water potential gradient created by the loop of Henle. ADH simply opens the channels to allow this.
