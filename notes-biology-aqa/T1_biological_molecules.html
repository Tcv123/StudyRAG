T1 — Biological Molecules

1.1 Water

Water: A polar molecule consisting of two hydrogen atoms covalently bonded to one oxygen atom. The uneven distribution of charge creates a dipole, enabling hydrogen bonding between adjacent water molecules.

Properties of Water

Water is essential for life due to several biologically important properties that arise from hydrogen bonding:

PropertyExplanationBiological Importance
High specific heat capacityHydrogen bonds require significant energy to breakProvides a stable environment for organisms; resists temperature fluctuations
High latent heat of vaporisationMany hydrogen bonds must be broken for water to evaporateEvaporation of sweat cools the body effectively
CohesionHydrogen bonds hold water molecules togetherAllows water columns in xylem; surface tension supports small organisms
Solvent propertiesPolar molecules and ions dissolve easily due to attraction to water dipolesTransport medium for metabolites, ions, and waste products in blood and cytoplasm
High density below 4°C (ice floats)Hydrogen bonds in ice form an open lattice structureInsulates aquatic habitats in winter; organisms survive beneath ice
Exam Tip: When explaining water's properties, always link the molecular explanation (hydrogen bonds, polarity) to a specific biological function. Simply stating "water is a good solvent" without saying why is not enough for full marks.

1.2 Carbohydrates

Carbohydrate: An organic molecule containing carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen, with the general formula Cn(H2O)n. They function as energy sources, energy stores, and structural components.

Monosaccharides

Monosaccharides are the simplest sugars and monomers of carbohydrates. They are classified by the number of carbon atoms:

  • Trioses (C3): e.g. glyceraldehyde — intermediates in respiration
  • Pentoses (C5): e.g. ribose (in RNA, ATP) and deoxyribose (in DNA)
  • Hexoses (C6): e.g. glucose (alpha and beta isomers), fructose, galactose

Alpha-glucose and beta-glucose are isomers differing in the position of the hydroxyl group on carbon-1. Alpha-glucose has the -OH group below the ring; beta-glucose has it above.

Disaccharides

Two monosaccharides join via a condensation reaction forming a glycosidic bond and releasing water.

Monosaccharide + Monosaccharide → Disaccharide + H2O
DisaccharideMonomersBond
MaltoseGlucose + Glucoseα-1,4 glycosidic
SucroseGlucose + Fructoseα-1,2 glycosidic

Disaccharides are broken down by hydrolysis — the addition of water to break the glycosidic bond.

Polysaccharides

Starch is the storage polysaccharide in plants, made from alpha-glucose. It has two components:

  • Amylose: Unbranched chain with α-1,4 glycosidic bonds. Coils into a helix — compact for storage.
  • Amylopectin: Branched chain with α-1,4 and α-1,6 bonds. Branches allow rapid hydrolysis for quick glucose release.

Glycogen is the storage polysaccharide in animals. Similar to amylopectin but more highly branched — many free ends allow rapid glucose release during high metabolic demand.

Cellulose is a structural polysaccharide in plant cell walls, made from beta-glucose. Adjacent glucose molecules are rotated 180° forming straight, unbranched chains. These chains form hydrogen bonds with neighbouring chains, creating microfibrils with high tensile strength.

Exam Tip: Starch is insoluble and compact — explain that this means it does not affect water potential by osmosis, making it ideal for storage. Cellulose's strength comes from inter-chain hydrogen bonds, not from the glycosidic bonds within chains.

1.3 Lipids

Lipid: A diverse group of hydrophobic biological molecules that are insoluble in water but soluble in organic solvents. They contain carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen (less oxygen proportionally than carbohydrates).

Triglycerides

A triglyceride consists of one glycerol molecule bonded to three fatty acid chains by ester bonds, formed via condensation reactions.

Glycerol + 3 Fatty Acids → Triglyceride + 3H2O

Functions of triglycerides:

  • Energy storage: High ratio of hydrogen to oxygen atoms provides more energy per gram than carbohydrates (approximately 37 kJ/g vs 16 kJ/g)
  • Insulation: Subcutaneous fat reduces heat loss
  • Protection: Surrounds organs (e.g. kidneys)
  • Metabolic water: Oxidation of fat produces water (important in desert animals)

Saturated fatty acids: No C=C double bonds; straight chains pack tightly — solid at room temperature (animal fats).

Unsaturated fatty acids: One or more C=C double bonds; kinked chains prevent close packing — liquid at room temperature (plant oils).

Phospholipids

Phospholipids have a glycerol backbone with two fatty acid tails and one phosphate head group. The phosphate head is hydrophilic (polar) while the fatty acid tails are hydrophobic (non-polar). This amphipathic nature means phospholipids spontaneously form bilayers in aqueous environments — the basis of all cell membranes.

Cholesterol

Cholesterol has a compact hydrophobic structure that fits between phospholipid molecules in the membrane. It reduces membrane fluidity at high temperatures and prevents it becoming too rigid at low temperatures, maintaining optimal permeability.

1.4 Proteins

Protein: A large biological polymer made of amino acid monomers joined by peptide bonds. Proteins carry out a vast range of functions including catalysis, transport, structural support, immunity, and signalling.

Amino Acid Structure

All amino acids share a common structure: a central carbon atom bonded to an amino group (-NH2), a carboxyl group (-COOH), a hydrogen atom, and a variable R group that determines the amino acid's properties.

Amino Acid + Amino Acid → Dipeptide + H2O (condensation / peptide bond formation)

Levels of Protein Structure

LevelDescriptionBonds Involved

Globular vs Fibrous Proteins

  • Globular proteins (e.g. enzymes, haemoglobin): Compact spherical shape, hydrophilic R groups on outside making them soluble, metabolically active.
  • Fibrous proteins (e.g. collagen, keratin): Long repetitive structures, insoluble, structural roles — high tensile strength.

Enzymes

Enzyme: A biological catalyst that increases the rate of a metabolic reaction by lowering the activation energy, without being consumed in the reaction. Enzymes are globular proteins with a specific active site.

Lock and Key Model: The substrate has a complementary shape to the enzyme's active site. The substrate fits precisely into the active site, forming an enzyme-substrate complex.

Induced Fit Model (more accepted): The active site is not perfectly complementary initially. When the substrate binds, the active site changes shape slightly to fit more closely around the substrate, placing strain on bonds and lowering activation energy further.

Factors Affecting Enzyme Activity

  • Temperature: Increased kinetic energy increases collisions up to optimum; beyond this, hydrogen bonds break, the active site denatures, and activity drops to zero.
  • pH: Changes in H+ concentration affect ionic and hydrogen bonds, altering active site shape. Each enzyme has an optimum pH.
  • Substrate concentration: Rate increases until all active sites are saturated (Vmax).
  • Enzyme concentration: Rate increases linearly if substrate is in excess.

Enzyme Inhibitors

TypeMechanismEffect of Increasing [S]
CompetitiveInhibitor binds to active site; similar shape to substrate; blocks substrate accessOvercomes inhibition (Vmax unchanged, apparent Km increased)
Non-competitiveInhibitor binds to allosteric site; changes active site shape; substrate cannot form ES complexCannot overcome inhibition (Vmax reduced, Km unchanged)
Exam Tip: Always distinguish between denaturation and inhibition. Denaturation is permanent structural change due to broken bonds. Inhibition (especially competitive) is often reversible and does not alter the enzyme's overall structure.

1.5 Nucleic Acids

Nucleic Acid: A polymer of nucleotide monomers. DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid) stores genetic information; RNA (ribonucleic acid) is involved in protein synthesis.

Nucleotide Structure

A nucleotide consists of three components:

  • A pentose sugar (deoxyribose in DNA, ribose in RNA)
  • A phosphate group
  • A nitrogenous base (A, T/U, C, G)

Nucleotides join by phosphodiester bonds (condensation reactions between the phosphate of one nucleotide and the sugar of the next) forming a sugar-phosphate backbone.

DNA Structure

DNA is a double-stranded helix. Two antiparallel polynucleotide strands are held together by hydrogen bonds between complementary base pairs:

  • Adenine pairs with Thymine (2 hydrogen bonds)
  • Cytosine pairs with Guanine (3 hydrogen bonds)

The two strands run in opposite directions (antiparallel): one 5'→3' and the other 3'→5'. This is crucial for replication and transcription.

ATP (Adenosine Triphosphate)

ATP: A nucleotide derivative consisting of adenine, ribose, and three phosphate groups. It is the universal energy currency of cells, providing immediate energy for cellular processes.
ATP + H2O → ADP + Pi + Energy (catalysed by ATPase)

Properties making ATP suitable as an energy currency:

  • Releases a small, manageable amount of energy per hydrolysis (30.6 kJ/mol)
  • Readily reformed from ADP + Pi (in mitochondria during oxidative phosphorylation)
  • Soluble — easily transported within cells
  • Cannot pass out of the cell — energy stays where it is needed
  • Immediate energy source — single hydrolysis reaction, unlike glucose which requires many steps
Exam Tip: ATP is not an energy store (that role belongs to glycogen/fat). It is an immediate energy currency — think of it as the "cash" of the cell while glycogen is the "savings account."

1.7 Inorganic Ions

Inorganic ions play essential roles in biological processes:

  • Iron (Fe2+): Component of haemoglobin's haem group — binds oxygen for transport.
  • Hydrogen ions (H+): Determine pH; crucial in chemiosmosis during oxidative phosphorylation.
  • Sodium (Na+): Co-transport of glucose in the ileum; generation of nerve impulses.
  • Phosphate (PO43-): Component of nucleotides, ATP, and phospholipids; bone mineralisation.
