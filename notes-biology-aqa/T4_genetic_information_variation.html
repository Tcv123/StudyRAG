T4 — Genetic Information, Variation & Relationships

4.1 DNA, Genes, and Chromosomes

Gene: A sequence of DNA nucleotides that codes for a functional polypeptide (or functional RNA molecule such as tRNA or rRNA). Genes occupy specific positions (loci) on chromosomes.
Genome: The entire DNA sequence of an organism, including both coding and non-coding regions. In eukaryotes, much of the genome (over 98% in humans) is non-coding.

Non-coding DNA includes:

  • Introns: Non-coding sequences within genes, removed during mRNA processing (splicing)
  • Regulatory sequences: Promoters, enhancers that control gene expression
  • Repetitive sequences / satellite DNA: Used in DNA fingerprinting
  • Telomeres: Repetitive sequences at chromosome ends that protect against degradation

A chromosome is one very long DNA molecule associated with histone proteins. DNA wraps around histones forming nucleosomes, which coil further into chromatin, and condense fully during cell division into visible chromosomes. Homologous chromosomes carry the same genes at the same loci but may have different alleles.

4.2 DNA Replication

Semi-conservative replication: Each new DNA molecule consists of one original (parental) strand and one newly synthesised strand. This was demonstrated by the Meselson-Stahl experiment using heavy nitrogen (15N) and density gradient centrifugation.

Steps of DNA replication:

  1. Helicase unwinds the double helix and breaks hydrogen bonds between complementary base pairs, separating the two strands.
  2. Each exposed strand acts as a template. Free DNA nucleotides align with their complementary bases (A-T, C-G).
  3. DNA polymerase catalyses the formation of phosphodiester bonds between adjacent nucleotides on the new strand. It works in the 5'→3' direction only.
  4. The leading strand is synthesised continuously. The lagging strand is synthesised in short Okazaki fragments (because polymerase can only work 5'→3').
  5. DNA ligase joins Okazaki fragments together by forming phosphodiester bonds.
Exam Tip: The Meselson-Stahl experiment is a classic AQA question. Be able to explain why after one generation all DNA was intermediate density, and after two generations there were equal bands of intermediate and light DNA — ruling out conservative replication.

4.3 Protein Synthesis

The Genetic Code

The genetic code has the following features:

  • Triplet: Three bases (a codon) code for one amino acid
  • Degenerate: Most amino acids are coded for by more than one codon (64 codons for 20 amino acids)
  • Non-overlapping: Each base is read only once
  • Universal: The same codons code for the same amino acids in almost all organisms
  • Includes start codons (AUG — also codes for methionine) and stop codons (UAA, UAG, UGA)

Transcription (in the nucleus)

  1. RNA polymerase binds to the promoter region upstream of the gene.
  2. RNA polymerase unwinds DNA and moves along the template (antisense) strand in the 3'→5' direction.
  3. Free RNA nucleotides are assembled into a pre-mRNA strand complementary to the template strand (and identical to the coding/sense strand, except U replaces T).
  4. RNA polymerase reaches a terminator sequence and detaches; the pre-mRNA is released.
  5. Post-transcriptional modification: Introns are spliced out, a 5' cap and poly-A tail are added. The mature mRNA exits through nuclear pores.

Translation (at ribosomes in the cytoplasm)

  1. mRNA attaches to the small subunit of a ribosome. The start codon (AUG) is positioned in the ribosome's P site.
  2. A tRNA molecule with the complementary anticodon (and carrying methionine) binds to the start codon via complementary base pairing.
  3. A second tRNA brings the next amino acid to the A site.
  4. A peptide bond forms between adjacent amino acids (catalysed by ribosomal RNA — a ribozyme).
  5. The ribosome moves one codon along mRNA (translocation). The first tRNA is released from the E site.
  6. This continues until a stop codon is reached. A release factor binds, the polypeptide is released, and the ribosome dissociates.
Exam Tip: Remember that the template strand of DNA is the antisense (3'→5') strand. The mRNA produced is complementary to this and has the same base sequence as the sense (coding) strand (with U replacing T).

4.4 Mutations

Gene mutation: A change in the nucleotide sequence of DNA. This may affect the sequence of amino acids in the polypeptide and therefore its tertiary structure and function.

Types of point mutation:

TypeDescriptionEffect on Protein
SubstitutionOne base replaced by anotherMay cause: silent (same amino acid due to degeneracy), missense (different amino acid), or nonsense (premature stop codon)
InsertionOne or more extra bases addedFrameshift — all codons downstream are altered; usually produces a non-functional protein
DeletionOne or more bases removedFrameshift — all codons downstream are altered

Sickle cell anaemia results from a single base substitution in the beta-globin gene: GAG → GTG, causing glutamic acid to be replaced by valine. The non-polar valine causes haemoglobin molecules to polymerise under low O2 conditions, distorting red blood cells into a sickle shape.

4.5 Meiosis and Genetic Diversity

Meiosis: A type of cell division that produces four genetically different haploid daughter cells from one diploid parent cell. It involves two divisions: meiosis I (separation of homologous pairs) and meiosis II (separation of sister chromatids).

Sources of genetic variation in meiosis:

  • Crossing over (prophase I): Homologous chromosomes form bivalents; non-sister chromatids exchange genetic material at chiasmata, producing recombinant chromosomes.
  • Independent assortment (metaphase I): Homologous pairs line up randomly at the cell equator. With n pairs, there are 2n possible combinations (in humans, 223 = 8.4 million).
  • Random fertilisation: Any sperm can fuse with any egg, multiplying the possible genetic combinations.
Total combinations from independent assortment alone = 2n (where n = haploid number)

4.6 Genetic Diversity and Adaptation

Genetic diversity can be influenced by:

  • Mutations: Ultimate source of new alleles; increase genetic diversity
  • Gene flow: Migration between populations introduces new alleles
  • Genetic drift: Random changes in allele frequency, especially in small populations — can reduce diversity
  • Natural selection: Directional selection reduces diversity (favours one phenotype); stabilising selection maintains the mean
  • Genetic bottlenecks: Population crash dramatically reduces diversity (e.g. cheetahs)
  • Founder effect: Small group colonises new area; limited alleles in founding population

4.7 Taxonomy and Classification

Taxonomy: The science of classifying organisms into hierarchical groups based on shared characteristics. The Linnaean hierarchy: Domain → Kingdom → Phylum → Class → Order → Family → Genus → Species.

The three-domain system (Woese) classifies life into:

  • Bacteria: Prokaryotes with peptidoglycan cell walls
  • Archaea: Prokaryotes without peptidoglycan; often extremophiles; distinct rRNA sequences
  • Eukarya: All eukaryotes (animals, plants, fungi, protists)

Classification can be based on:

  • Observable features (morphology/anatomy): Traditional approach
  • Molecular phylogenetics: Comparing DNA/RNA base sequences or amino acid sequences; more objective and reliable
  • Immunological comparisons: Degree of antibody cross-reactivity indicates evolutionary closeness

4.8 Biodiversity

Biodiversity: The variety of living organisms in an area, considered at three levels: genetic diversity (variation within a species), species diversity (number of different species and their relative abundance), and ecosystem diversity (range of different habitats).

Species Diversity Index

d = N(N - 1) / Σn(n - 1)

Where N = total number of organisms of all species, n = total number of organisms of each species. A higher value of d indicates greater species diversity.

Sampling methods for measuring biodiversity:

  • Random quadrats: For sessile (non-moving) organisms; use random number generator for coordinates
  • Transects: Line or belt transects to study changes in communities across a gradient
  • Mark-release-recapture: For mobile animals. Lincoln index: Population size = (n1 × n2) / nm
Exam Tip: When calculating the diversity index, a common error is to forget it is n(n-1) for EACH species, then summed. Practice with numerical examples. Also remember that farming monocultures reduce biodiversity — this is a frequent extended response topic.
