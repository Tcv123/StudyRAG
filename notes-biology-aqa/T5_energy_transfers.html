T5 — Energy Transfers in and Between Organisms

5.1 Photosynthesis

Photosynthesis: The process by which light energy is used to convert carbon dioxide and water into glucose and oxygen. It occurs in chloroplasts and involves two main stages: the light-dependent reactions (on thylakoid membranes) and the light-independent reactions (in the stroma).
6CO2 + 6H2O → C6H12O6 + 6O2

Light-Dependent Reactions (Thylakoid Membranes)

These reactions require light energy directly and produce ATP and reduced NADP (NADPH) for use in the Calvin cycle, plus oxygen as a by-product.

  1. Photoexcitation: Light energy is absorbed by chlorophyll a in photosystem II (PSII). Electrons in the chlorophyll are excited to a higher energy level and released.
  2. Photolysis of water: Water is split to replace the lost electrons: 2H2O → 4H+ + 4e- + O2. The oxygen is released as a by-product.
  3. Electron transport chain: High-energy electrons pass along a chain of electron carriers in the thylakoid membrane, losing energy at each step. This energy is used to pump H+ ions from the stroma into the thylakoid lumen (creating a proton gradient).
  4. Chemiosmosis: H+ ions flow back through ATP synthase (down their concentration gradient) — this drives the synthesis of ATP from ADP + Pi (photophosphorylation).
  5. Photosystem I (PSI): Light re-excites electrons. These high-energy electrons are used to reduce NADP+ to NADPH (using NADP reductase), combining with H+ ions from photolysis.

Cyclic photophosphorylation: Electrons from PSI are passed back to the electron transport chain between PSII and PSI (rather than to NADP). This produces ATP only (no NADPH, no photolysis). Used when more ATP than NADPH is needed (e.g. for the Calvin cycle).

Light-Independent Reactions — The Calvin Cycle (Stroma)

  1. Carbon fixation: CO2 combines with ribulose bisphosphate (RuBP, a 5C molecule) catalysed by the enzyme RuBisCO, forming an unstable 6C compound that immediately splits into two molecules of glycerate-3-phosphate (GP, 3C).
  2. Reduction: GP is reduced to triose phosphate (G3P/TP, 3C) using ATP and NADPH from the light-dependent reactions.
  3. Regeneration of RuBP: Most TP (5 out of every 6 molecules) is used to regenerate RuBP, using ATP. One in six TP molecules is used to synthesise glucose and other organic molecules.
For every 3 CO2 fixed: 3 RuBP + 3 CO2 → 6 GP → 6 TP → 1 TP (net gain, used for glucose) + 5 TP (regenerate 3 RuBP)
Exam Tip: RuBisCO is the most abundant enzyme on Earth. A common exam question asks what happens to GP, TP, and RuBP levels when light or CO2 is suddenly removed. If light stops: NADPH and ATP are not produced, so GP accumulates (cannot be reduced) and TP/RuBP levels fall.

5.2 Respiration

Respiration: The metabolic process in which organic molecules (mainly glucose) are broken down to release energy for ATP synthesis. It occurs in all living cells continuously.

Glycolysis (Cytoplasm)

Glycolysis is the splitting of one glucose molecule (6C) into two molecules of pyruvate (3C). It is anaerobic (does not require oxygen).

  1. Phosphorylation: Glucose is phosphorylated using 2 ATP → fructose-1,6-bisphosphate (6C). This activates the molecule and prevents it leaving the cell.
  2. Lysis: The 6C molecule is split into two molecules of triose phosphate (3C).
  3. Oxidation: Each triose phosphate is oxidised to pyruvate (3C). For each triose phosphate: 2 ATP produced (substrate-level phosphorylation) and 1 NAD reduced to NADH.
Net yield per glucose: 2 ATP + 2 NADH + 2 pyruvate

Link Reaction (Mitochondrial Matrix)

Pyruvate is actively transported into the mitochondrial matrix. There it is:

  • Oxidatively decarboxylated: one carbon removed as CO2, and NAD is reduced to NADH
  • Combined with coenzyme A (CoA) to form acetyl CoA (2C)
Pyruvate (3C) + NAD + CoA → Acetyl CoA (2C) + CO2 + NADH (×2 per glucose)

Krebs Cycle (Mitochondrial Matrix)

  1. Acetyl CoA (2C) combines with oxaloacetate (4C) to form citrate (6C). CoA is recycled.
  2. Citrate is decarboxylated and oxidised in a series of steps, regenerating oxaloacetate.
  3. Per turn: 1 ATP (substrate-level), 3 NADH, 1 FADH2, 2 CO2 released.
Per glucose (2 turns): 2 ATP + 6 NADH + 2 FADH2 + 4 CO2

Oxidative Phosphorylation (Inner Mitochondrial Membrane — Cristae)

  1. NADH and FADH2 donate electrons to the electron transport chain (ETC) on the inner membrane (cristae).
  2. Electrons pass along a series of carriers, releasing energy at each step.
  3. This energy is used to pump H+ ions from the matrix into the intermembrane space, creating a proton gradient (chemiosmotic gradient).
  4. H+ ions flow back into the matrix through ATP synthase, driving ATP synthesis (chemiosmosis) — approximately 34 ATP per glucose.
  5. Oxygen is the final electron acceptor. It combines with electrons and H+ to form water. Without oxygen, the ETC stops, NADH cannot be reoxidised, and the Krebs cycle halts.
Exam Tip: Cyanide inhibits cytochrome c oxidase (the final ETC complex). This stops electron flow, H+ pumping ceases, no ATP is made by chemiosmosis, and aerobic respiration stops. The organism must rely on anaerobic respiration alone.

Anaerobic Respiration

When oxygen is unavailable, oxidative phosphorylation stops. NAD must be regenerated for glycolysis to continue:

  • In animals: Pyruvate + NADH → Lactate + NAD (catalysed by lactate dehydrogenase). Reversible — lactate can be converted back to pyruvate when oxygen is available (oxygen debt).
  • In yeast/plants: Pyruvate → Ethanal + CO2 (decarboxylation), then Ethanal + NADH → Ethanol + NAD (alcoholic fermentation). Irreversible.

5.3 Energy and Ecosystems

Energy Flow Through Ecosystems

Gross Primary Productivity (GPP): The total rate of energy fixation (photosynthesis) by producers in an ecosystem. Measured in kJ m-2 year-1.
Net Primary Productivity (NPP) = GPP - Respiration (R)
NPP is the energy available to consumers (stored in plant biomass)

Energy transfer between trophic levels is inefficient (typically 5-20%):

  • Not all organisms at one level are eaten (some die and enter detritivore food chains)
  • Not all parts eaten are digested (egested as faeces)
  • Energy lost as heat through respiration at each trophic level
  • Energy lost in excretory products (urine)
Efficiency of energy transfer = (Energy available at trophic level n+1 / Energy available at trophic level n) × 100%

Nutrient Cycles

The Carbon Cycle:

  • CO2 enters living systems via photosynthesis (carbon fixation by RuBisCO)
  • Carbon passes through food chains as organic molecules
  • CO2 returned to atmosphere by respiration (all organisms), combustion of fossil fuels, and decomposition
  • Carbon stored long-term in fossil fuels, limestone, and ocean carbonates

The Nitrogen Cycle:

  • Nitrogen fixation: N2 → NH4+ by Rhizobium (in root nodules of legumes) or by lightning
  • Nitrification: NH4+ → NO2- → NO3- by nitrifying bacteria (Nitrosomonas, Nitrobacter) — requires aerobic conditions
  • Assimilation: Plants absorb NO3- or NH4+ to make amino acids and nucleotides
  • Ammonification: Decomposers break down organic nitrogen in dead matter → NH4+
  • Denitrification: NO3- → N2 by denitrifying bacteria under anaerobic (waterlogged) conditions

The Phosphorus Cycle:

  • Phosphorus is found in rocks (as phosphate minerals); released by weathering
  • Phosphate ions (PO43-) absorbed by plants from soil solution
  • Passes through food chains in nucleic acids, ATP, phospholipids
  • Returned to soil by decomposition; eventually re-incorporated into sedimentary rock
Exam Tip: Farming reduces soil nutrient levels because crops are harvested and removed from the ecosystem. This is why farmers add fertilisers (NPK) — to replace nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium removed in harvested biomass.
