7.1 Inheritance

Allele: An alternative form of a gene occupying the same locus on homologous chromosomes. An organism's genotype is the combination of alleles it carries; the phenotype is the observable characteristic resulting from the genotype and environmental influences.

Monohybrid Inheritance

Monohybrid crosses involve a single gene with two alleles. Key terms:

  • Homozygous: Two identical alleles (AA or aa)
  • Heterozygous: Two different alleles (Aa)
  • Dominant: Allele expressed in both homozygous and heterozygous genotype
  • Recessive: Allele only expressed in homozygous recessive genotype

Example cross: Aa × Aa

Dihybrid Inheritance

Dihybrid crosses involve two genes on different chromosomes (independently assorted). A cross between two double heterozygotes (AaBb × AaBb) gives a 9:3:3:1 phenotypic ratio.

Codominance

Codominance: A pattern of inheritance where both alleles are expressed equally in the heterozygote. Neither allele is dominant over the other. Example: ABO blood groups where IA and IB are codominant (IAIB gives blood group AB).

Multiple Alleles

Some genes have more than two alleles in a population (though an individual still only carries two). ABO blood groups: three alleles IA, IB, and IO. IA and IB are codominant; both are dominant over IO.

Sex Linkage

Sex-linked gene: A gene located on the X chromosome (X-linked). Males (XY) have only one copy of X-linked genes, so a single recessive allele will be expressed (hemizygous). This makes males more likely to show X-linked recessive conditions (e.g. haemophilia, red-green colour blindness).

In a cross between a carrier female (XHXh) and a normal male (XHY): 50% of sons will be affected, 50% of daughters will be carriers.

Epistasis

Epistasis: The interaction between genes where one gene masks or modifies the expression of another gene at a different locus. The gene doing the masking is the epistatic gene; the gene being masked is the hypostatic gene.

The Chi-Squared Test

Chi-squared (χ²) test: A statistical test used to determine whether observed results differ significantly from expected results predicted by a genetic hypothesis. It tests the null hypothesis that there is no significant difference between observed and expected ratios.
χ² = Σ (O - E)² / E

Where O = observed frequency, E = expected frequency. Steps:

  1. State the null hypothesis (no significant difference between observed and expected).
  2. Calculate expected values from the predicted ratio.
  3. Calculate χ² using the formula.
  4. Determine degrees of freedom (df = number of categories - 1).
  5. Compare calculated χ² with the critical value at p = 0.05.
  6. If χ² < critical value: accept null hypothesis (results fit expected ratio — difference is due to chance).
  7. If χ² > critical value: reject null hypothesis (significant difference — results do not fit).
Exam Tip: Always state the null hypothesis clearly. The critical value at p = 0.05 means there is a 5% probability that the difference is due to chance. If your calculated value exceeds this, you reject the null hypothesis and conclude the difference is significant.

7.2 Populations

Hardy-Weinberg Principle

Hardy-Weinberg Principle: In a large, randomly mating population with no selection, mutation, migration, or genetic drift, allele and genotype frequencies remain constant from generation to generation.
p + q = 1 (allele frequencies, where p = dominant allele, q = recessive allele)
p² + 2pq + q² = 1 (genotype frequencies: p² = homozygous dominant, 2pq = heterozygous, q² = homozygous recessive)

To use Hardy-Weinberg in calculations:

  1. Identify the frequency of the homozygous recessive phenotype in the population = q²
  2. Take the square root to find q
  3. Calculate p = 1 - q
  4. Calculate carrier frequency (heterozygotes) = 2pq

Conditions for Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium (rarely met in nature):

  • Large population size (no genetic drift)
  • Random mating
  • No natural selection
  • No mutation
  • No migration (no gene flow in or out)

7.3 Evolution and Natural Selection

Evolution: A change in allele frequency in a population over successive generations, driven by natural selection, genetic drift, mutation, and gene flow.

Natural Selection

  1. Within a population there is genetic variation (caused by mutation, meiosis, sexual reproduction).
  2. Organisms produce more offspring than the environment can support (overproduction).
  3. There is competition for resources (intraspecific competition).
  4. Individuals with alleles conferring advantageous phenotypes are more likely to survive and reproduce (selection pressure).
  5. These alleles are passed to offspring at a higher frequency.
  6. Over many generations, the allele frequency changes — the population evolves.

Speciation

Species: A group of organisms that can interbreed to produce fertile offspring and are reproductively isolated from other species.

Allopatric speciation: Geographic isolation (physical barrier) separates a population. Different selection pressures and genetic drift in each sub-population lead to divergence. Eventually, reproductive isolation develops and they can no longer interbreed — two new species.

Sympatric speciation: Speciation without geographic isolation. Can occur through polyploidy (especially in plants), ecological niche differentiation, or temporal reproductive isolation.

7.4 Ecosystems

Succession

Succession: The progressive change in species composition of a community over time. Primary succession begins on bare substrate (e.g. bare rock, new volcanic island). Secondary succession occurs where soil already exists but vegetation has been removed (e.g. after fire).

Stages of succession:

  1. Pioneer community: Pioneer species (e.g. lichens, mosses) colonise bare substrate. They are tolerant of harsh conditions and modify the environment (e.g. weathering rock, adding organic matter).
  2. Intermediate (seral) stages: New species outcompete pioneers as conditions improve. Soil depth increases, nutrient availability improves, microclimate changes.
  3. Climax community: Stable, self-sustaining community in equilibrium with the environment (e.g. temperate deciduous woodland in the UK). Biodiversity is typically highest at this stage.

Changes during succession: increasing biomass, increasing biodiversity, more complex food webs, more stratification, soil becomes deeper and more nutrient-rich, abiotic conditions become less extreme.

Conservation and Management

Conservation may involve managing succession to maintain a particular seral stage (e.g. heathland, grassland) that supports specific species. Methods include:

  • Grazing — prevents succession to woodland
  • Mowing / cutting
  • Controlled burning
  • Coppicing — maintains woodland diversity

Reasons for conservation:

  • Ethical: All species have intrinsic value and a right to exist
  • Ecological: Maintaining ecosystem stability and function; maintaining food webs
  • Economic: Genetic resources for agriculture/medicine; tourism; ecosystem services (pollination, water purification, flood prevention)
  • Aesthetic: Natural beauty and cultural value
Exam Tip: In extended response questions on conservation, always include specific examples and link arguments to economic, ethical, and ecological reasons. Discuss both in situ (nature reserves, SSSIs) and ex situ (seed banks, zoos, captive breeding) conservation methods.
