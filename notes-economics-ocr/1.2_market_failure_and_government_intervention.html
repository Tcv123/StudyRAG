1.2 Market Failure & Government Intervention

Market Failure

Market Failure: A situation where the free market mechanism fails to allocate resources efficiently, leading to a net welfare loss. The market does not achieve allocative efficiency (P ≠ MC) or the socially optimal outcome.
Allocative Efficiency: Achieved when resources are distributed such that no reallocation can make someone better off without making someone else worse off. Occurs where Price = Marginal Cost (P = MC), meaning the value consumers place on the last unit equals the cost of producing it.

Types of Market Failure

  • Externalities (positive and negative)
  • Public goods and the free-rider problem
  • Information failure (asymmetric and incomplete)
  • Market power (monopoly/oligopoly)
  • Factor immobility
  • Inequality and inequity

Externalities

Externality: A cost or benefit that affects a third party who is not directly involved in the production or consumption of a good/service. Externalities cause a divergence between private and social costs/benefits.

Key relationships:

Social Cost = Private Cost + External Cost
Social Benefit = Private Benefit + External Benefit

Negative Externality of Production

Where production imposes costs on third parties (e.g., factory pollution affecting local residents).

  • Social cost > Private cost
  • MSC lies above MPC
  • Market overproduces relative to the socially optimal output
  • Creates a welfare loss (deadweight loss triangle)

Negative externality of production — MSC above MPC, market overproduces (Qm > Q*)

Negative Externality of Consumption

Where consumption imposes costs on third parties (e.g., passive smoking, traffic congestion).

  • Social benefit < Private benefit
  • MSB lies below MPB
  • Market overproduces relative to the socially optimal output

Negative externality of consumption — MSB below MPB, market overproduces

Positive Externality of Consumption

Where consumption generates benefits to third parties (e.g., education, vaccination).

  • Social benefit > Private benefit
  • MSB lies above MPB
  • Market underproduces relative to the socially optimal output

Positive Externality of Production

Where production generates benefits to third parties (e.g., R&D spillovers, training workers who move firms).

  • Social cost < Private cost
  • MSC lies below MPC
  • Market underproduces relative to the socially optimal output
Exam Tip: Always clearly label your externality diagrams with MPC, MSC, MPB, MSB. Identify the market quantity (Qm) vs socially optimal quantity (Q*) and shade the welfare/deadweight loss triangle. This is where most marks are gained.

Public Goods

Public Good: A good that is non-excludable (impossible to prevent people from using it) and non-rivalrous (one person's consumption does not reduce availability for others).
CharacteristicDefinitionImplication
Non-excludableCannot prevent non-payers from consumingFree-rider problem — no incentive to pay
Non-rivalrousConsumption by one does not diminish supply for othersMarginal cost of additional user = 0

Examples: Street lighting, national defence, flood defences, lighthouses

The Free-Rider Problem: Since non-payers cannot be excluded, rational consumers have no incentive to pay voluntarily. If everyone free-rides, no revenue is generated, and the private sector will not provide the good. This leads to market failure — the good is not provided despite being socially desirable.

Quasi-Public Goods

Goods that have some but not all characteristics of public goods (partially excludable or partially rivalrous). Examples: roads (rivalrous when congested), parks (excludable if fenced).

Information Failure

Information Failure: A situation where economic agents lack sufficient or accurate information to make rational decisions, leading to misallocation of resources.

Types of Information Failure

TypeDefinitionExample
Asymmetric informationOne party in a transaction has more/better information than the otherUsed car market (seller knows defects), insurance (buyer knows own risk)
Incomplete informationBoth parties lack full information about costs/benefitsLong-term health effects of new products

Merit Goods

Merit Good: A good that is underconsumed in a free market because individuals underestimate the private benefits (due to information failure) and/or the good generates positive externalities. Examples: education, healthcare, museums.

Demerit Goods

Demerit Good: A good that is overconsumed in a free market because individuals underestimate the private costs (due to information failure) and/or the good generates negative externalities. Examples: tobacco, alcohol, gambling.
Exam Tip: Merit/demerit goods involve TWO sources of market failure: (1) externalities AND (2) information failure. Always mention both for full marks. The information failure element distinguishes them from ordinary externality questions.

Government Intervention Methods

Indirect Taxes

A tax on expenditure that raises the cost of production, shifting supply left. Used to correct negative externalities by internalising external costs.

  • Specific tax: Fixed amount per unit (shifts supply curve up in parallel)
  • Ad valorem tax: Percentage of price (supply curve pivots — gap widens at higher prices)

Goal: Set tax = external cost so that MPC + tax = MSC, achieving socially optimal output.

Subsidies

A payment from government to producers that reduces costs, shifting supply right. Used to correct positive externalities by encouraging consumption/production to the socially optimal level.

Goal: Set subsidy = external benefit so that output expands from Qm to Q*.

Maximum and Minimum Prices

TypeSetPurposeEffectExample
Maximum price (price ceiling)Below equilibriumProtect consumers / affordabilityExcess demand, shortages, black marketsRent controls
Minimum price (price floor)Above equilibriumProtect producers / workersExcess supply, surplusMinimum wage, agricultural prices

Regulation and Legislation

  • Bans/restrictions (e.g., smoking ban in public places)
  • Standards (e.g., emission limits, food safety standards)
  • Planning laws and licensing
  • Competition law (preventing monopoly abuse)

Tradeable Pollution Permits

Government sets a cap on total emissions and issues permits. Firms can trade permits — those who can reduce pollution cheaply sell permits to those who cannot. Creates a market-based incentive to reduce pollution.

  • Advantages: Market-based (efficient), total cap guarantees environmental target, revenue for government
  • Disadvantages: Difficult to set correct cap, monitoring costs, firms may pass costs to consumers, permits may be lobbied for

Provision of Information

Government campaigns to correct information failure (e.g., health warnings on cigarettes, nutritional labelling, drink-driving campaigns).

Direct Provision

Government provides goods/services directly (e.g., NHS healthcare, state education, public parks). Essential for public goods where private provision fails entirely.

Government Failure

Government Failure: A situation where government intervention leads to a net welfare loss or a more inefficient allocation of resources than would occur without intervention. The costs of intervention exceed the benefits.

Causes of Government Failure

CauseExplanationExample
Unintended consequencesPolicies have unforeseen effects that create new problemsMinimum wage causing unemployment among unskilled workers
Information gapsGovernment lacks information to set optimal taxes/subsidiesSetting pollution tax too high or too low
Administrative costsCost of implementing and enforcing policy exceeds benefitsExpensive bureaucracy to monitor regulations
Distortion of market signalsIntervention disrupts the price mechanismSubsidies leading to overproduction and inefficiency
Regulatory captureRegulators act in interests of industry not publicFinancial regulators influenced by banks
Political short-termismPolicies favour electoral cycle over long-term efficiencyDelaying unpopular but necessary reforms
Moral hazardIntervention encourages riskier behaviourBank bailouts encouraging excessive risk-taking
Exam Tip: In evaluation, always consider the possibility of government failure when discussing intervention. Ask: Does the government have the information needed? Are there unintended consequences? Do the benefits outweigh costs of administration? This demonstrates analytical depth.

Exam Tip: For 25-mark essays, structure your answer: (1) Identify the market failure, (2) Explain using a diagram, (3) Discuss possible interventions, (4) Evaluate — consider government failure, elasticity effects, information problems, and alternative approaches.
