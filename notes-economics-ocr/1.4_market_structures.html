Perfect Competition

Perfect Competition: A theoretical market structure with many buyers and sellers, homogeneous products, perfect information, no barriers to entry/exit, and firms are price takers. It serves as a benchmark for evaluating real markets.

Assumptions

  • Many buyers and sellers — no individual can influence price
  • Homogeneous (identical) product
  • Perfect knowledge — all agents have complete information
  • No barriers to entry or exit
  • Firms are profit maximisers
  • Factors of production are perfectly mobile

Short-Run Equilibrium

In the short run, a perfectly competitive firm can make supernormal profit, normal profit, or a loss, depending on market conditions.

Perfectly competitive firm in the short run — earning supernormal profit (P > ATC at Q*)

Long-Run Equilibrium

In the long run, supernormal profits attract new entrants. Market supply increases, price falls until only normal profit remains. Losses cause firms to exit, supply falls, price rises until normal profit is restored.

Long-run equilibrium conditions:

  • AR = ATC (normal profit only)
  • P = MC (allocative efficiency)
  • Production at minimum ATC (productive efficiency)
  • No incentive for firms to enter or exit
Exam Tip: Perfect competition achieves BOTH allocative efficiency (P = MC) and productive efficiency (min ATC) in the long run. This is why it is the benchmark — no other market structure achieves both simultaneously in the long run.

Monopoly

Pure Monopoly: A market structure with a single seller producing a product with no close substitutes, protected by high barriers to entry. In practice, a firm with 25%+ market share may be considered a monopoly under UK competition law.

Sources of Monopoly Power (Barriers to Entry)

  • Economies of scale — natural monopoly (MES is so high that only one firm can operate efficiently)
  • Legal barriers — patents, copyrights, licences
  • Control of essential resources — ownership of key inputs
  • Brand loyalty — heavy sunk cost advertising deters entry
  • Predatory pricing — pricing below cost to drive out rivals
  • Network effects — value increases with number of users

Monopoly profit maximisation — higher price, lower output, deadweight loss (DWL), and supernormal profit sustained in long run

Price Discrimination

Price Discrimination: Charging different prices to different consumers for the same product, where the price differences are not justified by differences in cost of supply.

Conditions required:

  • Market power (price maker)
  • Ability to separate markets (prevent resale/arbitrage)
  • Different PED between groups
DegreeDescriptionExample
1st degree (perfect)Each consumer charged their maximum willingness to payAuctions, haggling
2nd degreeDifferent prices for different quantitiesBulk discounts, off-peak pricing
3rd degreeDifferent prices for different market segmentsStudent/senior discounts, peak/off-peak travel

Oligopoly

Oligopoly: A market structure dominated by a few large firms, each with significant market share. There is high interdependence between firms — each firm must consider the likely reactions of rivals when making decisions.

Characteristics

  • High concentration ratio (top 3-5 firms dominate)
  • High barriers to entry
  • Interdependence — strategic decision-making
  • Products may be differentiated or homogeneous
  • Non-price competition is dominant (branding, quality, service)
  • Price rigidity (prices tend to be "sticky")

The Kinked Demand Curve

Explains price rigidity in oligopoly. Firms assume:

  • If they raise price, rivals will NOT follow (demand is elastic above current price — loses customers)
  • If they cut price, rivals WILL follow (demand is inelastic below current price — no gain in market share)

Kinked demand curve — the discontinuity in MR means MC can shift within the gap without changing price

Game Theory and Collusion

Collusion: A formal or informal agreement between firms to restrict competition — typically by fixing prices, limiting output, or dividing markets. Illegal in most countries.

Types of collusion:

  • Overt (formal) collusion — cartel agreements (e.g., OPEC)
  • Tacit (informal) collusion — price leadership, parallel pricing without explicit agreement

Prisoner's Dilemma: Illustrates why collusion is unstable — each firm has an individual incentive to cheat (undercut) even though joint cooperation yields higher profits for all. This is the dominant strategy problem.

Contestable Markets

Contestable Market: A market where there is freedom of entry and exit, and low sunk costs. The THREAT of potential competition constrains the behaviour of existing firms, even if there are few actual firms in the market.

Key Features

  • No/low barriers to entry and exit
  • Low sunk costs (costs that cannot be recovered on exit)
  • Hit-and-run entry possible
  • Firms behave as if under competitive pressure even with few rivals

Implication: Even a monopoly may behave efficiently (pricing close to AC) if the market is highly contestable. The key determinant of behaviour is not the number of firms, but the threat of entry.

Exam Tip: Contestability is an excellent evaluation point. When discussing any market structure, consider: "How contestable is this market?" If contestable, even concentrated markets may produce efficient outcomes. Technology has increased contestability in many markets (e.g., digital platforms lowering entry costs).
