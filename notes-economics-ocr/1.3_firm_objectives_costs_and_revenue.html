1.3 Firm Objectives, Costs & Revenue

Firm Objectives

Profit Maximisation

Profit Maximisation: The primary assumed objective of firms in economic theory. A firm maximises profit where Marginal Revenue = Marginal Cost (MR = MC). At this output, the addition to revenue from selling one more unit exactly equals the addition to cost.
Profit = Total Revenue - Total Cost
Profit maximised where MR = MC

Exam Tip: The principal-agent problem explains why firms may not profit maximise. Shareholders (principals) want profit max but managers (agents) may pursue other goals — empire building, satisficing, or revenue max. Discuss separation of ownership and control.

Revenue

Total Revenue (TR): Price x Quantity sold. TR = P x Q
Average Revenue (AR): Revenue per unit sold. AR = TR / Q = Price (the demand curve IS the AR curve)
Marginal Revenue (MR): The addition to total revenue from selling one more unit. MR = ΔTR / ΔQ

Costs of Production

Short Run vs Long Run

Short Run: A time period in which at least one factor of production is fixed (usually capital). Firms can only alter variable factors (labour, raw materials).
Long Run: A time period in which all factors of production are variable. Firms can change the scale of production entirely.

Short-Run Costs

CostFormulaDescription
Total Fixed Cost (TFC)—Costs that do not vary with output (rent, insurance)
Total Variable Cost (TVC)—Costs that vary directly with output (wages, materials)
Total Cost (TC)TFC + TVCAll costs of production
Average Fixed Cost (AFC)TFC / QFixed cost per unit — always falls as output rises
Average Variable Cost (AVC)TVC / QVariable cost per unit — U-shaped
Average Total Cost (ATC/AC)TC / QTotal cost per unit — U-shaped
Marginal Cost (MC)ΔTC / ΔQCost of producing one more unit

The Law of Diminishing Marginal Returns

Diminishing Marginal Returns: In the short run, as additional units of a variable factor are added to a fixed factor, the marginal product of the variable factor will eventually fall. This is a SHORT-RUN concept (at least one factor fixed).

This law explains why MC is U-shaped:

  • Initially, marginal product rises (increasing returns) — MC falls
  • After the point of diminishing returns, marginal product falls — MC rises

Short-run cost curves — MC cuts AVC and ATC at their minimum points

Key Relationships

  • MC cuts AVC at the minimum point of AVC
  • MC cuts ATC at the minimum point of ATC
  • When MC < AC, AC is falling
  • When MC > AC, AC is rising
  • The gap between ATC and AVC = AFC (which always falls)

Long-Run Costs and Economies of Scale

Economies of Scale: The cost advantages that a firm gains as it increases its scale of production in the long run. Long-run average costs (LRAC) fall as output increases.

Internal Economies of Scale

TypeExplanation
TechnicalLarge-scale machinery, container principle (volume grows faster than surface area), specialisation of equipment
ManagerialSpecialist managers hired for different departments, increasing efficiency
FinancialLarge firms borrow at lower interest rates (lower risk), access to equity finance
MarketingAdvertising costs spread over larger output, bulk buying of media space
Purchasing/Bulk-buyingDiscounts negotiated on large orders of raw materials
Risk-bearingDiversification across products/markets reduces risk

External Economies of Scale

Benefits to all firms in an industry as the industry grows (e.g., skilled labour pool, specialist suppliers, infrastructure improvements, knowledge spillovers).

Diseconomies of Scale

Diseconomies of Scale: The cost disadvantages that arise when a firm grows too large, causing LRAC to rise. Typically caused by coordination problems, communication breakdown, and worker alienation.

Long-run average cost curve as envelope of SRAC curves — showing economies, constant returns, and diseconomies of scale. MES = Minimum Efficient Scale

Minimum Efficient Scale (MES): The lowest level of output at which long-run average costs are minimised. Industries with high MES relative to market size tend toward monopoly/oligopoly.

Profit

Normal vs Supernormal Profit

Normal Profit: The minimum return required to keep a firm in its current line of production. It is the opportunity cost of enterprise — included in the cost curves. Occurs where AR = ATC (or TR = TC).
Supernormal (Abnormal) Profit: Profit in excess of normal profit. Occurs where AR > ATC (or TR > TC). Acts as a signal for new firms to enter the market.

Profit maximisation at MR = MC — supernormal profit shown as shaded area between P and AC at output Q*

Shutdown Condition

  • Short run: A firm should shut down if AR < AVC (price cannot cover variable costs). If AR > AVC but AR < ATC, the firm makes a loss but should continue producing as it covers variable costs and contributes to fixed costs.
  • Long run: A firm should exit if AR < ATC (cannot cover all costs). There are no fixed costs in the long run.
Exam Tip: When drawing profit diagrams, always clearly identify: (1) the profit-maximising output (MC = MR), (2) the price (read from AR at that output), (3) the average cost at that output, (4) the profit/loss area (rectangle between P and AC, from 0 to Q*). Label everything clearly.

Returns to Scale

Returns to ScaleEffect on OutputEffect on LRAC
Increasing returns to scaleOutput rises proportionally more than inputsLRAC falls (economies of scale)

Exam Tip: Do NOT confuse diminishing marginal returns (short-run, one factor fixed) with decreasing returns to scale (long-run, all factors variable). They are different concepts. Diminishing returns explains the shape of short-run MC; decreasing returns to scale explains why LRAC eventually rises.
