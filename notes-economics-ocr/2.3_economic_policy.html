2.3 Economic Policy

Fiscal Policy

Fiscal Policy: The use of government spending and taxation to influence aggregate demand, the pattern of economic activity, and the distribution of income. Managed by the Chancellor of the Exchequer / HM Treasury.

Components

  • Government spending (G): Current spending (day-to-day, e.g., NHS, education) and capital spending (infrastructure, investment)
  • Taxation: Direct taxes (income tax, corporation tax) and indirect taxes (VAT, excise duties)
  • Budget position: Budget deficit (G > T), surplus (T > G), or balanced (G = T)

Types of Fiscal Policy

TypeActionEffect on ADWhen Used
ExpansionaryIncrease G and/or cut taxesAD increases (rightward shift)Recession, negative output gap
ContractionaryCut G and/or raise taxesAD decreases (leftward shift)Overheating economy, positive output gap

Expansionary fiscal policy shifts AD rightward — increasing output and price level

Automatic Stabilisers

Automatic Stabilisers: Mechanisms within the fiscal system that automatically dampen fluctuations in the economic cycle without deliberate policy changes. They smooth the cycle by injecting spending in downturns and withdrawing it in booms.
  • Progressive taxation: In a boom, incomes rise and more people enter higher tax bands → tax revenue rises automatically → dampens AD growth
  • Welfare benefits: In a recession, unemployment rises → benefit payments increase automatically → supports AD

Evaluation of Fiscal Policy

AdvantagesDisadvantages
Direct control over AD componentsTime lags (recognition, decision, implementation)
Can target specific sectors/regionsCrowding out (government borrowing raises interest rates)
Multiplier effect amplifies impactBudget deficit and rising national debt
Automatic stabilisers work without delayPolitical influences on spending decisions
Can address inequality via redistributionRicardian equivalence (consumers save expecting future taxes)

Monetary Policy

Monetary Policy: The use of interest rates, money supply, and related tools by the central bank (Bank of England) to influence aggregate demand, inflation, and economic activity. The MPC (Monetary Policy Committee) sets the base interest rate.

Transmission Mechanism

How a change in interest rates affects the economy:

  1. Bank of England changes the base rate
  2. Commercial banks adjust lending/savings rates
  3. Affects borrowing costs, savings returns, asset prices, exchange rate
  4. Changes in consumption (C) and investment (I)
  5. Changes in AD → output, employment, and price level affected

Interest Rate Channels

ChannelEffect of Lower Interest Rates
Cost of borrowingCheaper to borrow → more consumption and investment
Savings incentiveLower return on savings → less saving, more spending
Mortgage paymentsLower monthly payments → more disposable income
Asset pricesProperty and share prices rise → wealth effect → more spending
Exchange rateLower rates → capital outflow → depreciation → cheaper exports
ConfidenceRate cuts signal support → improved business/consumer confidence

Quantitative Easing (QE)

QE: An unconventional monetary policy where the central bank creates new money electronically to purchase government bonds (and sometimes corporate bonds) from financial institutions. This increases the money supply, lowers long-term interest rates, and encourages lending and spending.

Used when conventional interest rate cuts reach the zero lower bound (rates cannot go significantly below 0%).

Evaluation of Monetary Policy

AdvantagesDisadvantages
Independent central bank (no political bias)Time lags (up to 18-24 months for full effect)
Quick to implement (MPC meets 8 times/year)Blunt instrument (affects whole economy equally)
Flexible and reversibleLiquidity trap (near zero rates, pushing on a string)
Controls inflation effectivelyUnequal effects (hurts savers, helps borrowers)
Well-understood transmission mechanismAsset price inflation from QE (inequality)

Supply-Side Policies

Supply-Side Policies: Government measures aimed at increasing the productive capacity of the economy — shifting LRAS to the right. They improve the efficiency of factor and product markets, increasing potential output.

Market-Based (Free-Market) Supply-Side Policies

PolicyMechanismExample
Tax reformLower income/corporation tax increases incentives to work and investLaffer curve — lower rates may increase total revenue
DeregulationReducing red tape lowers costs, increases competitionLiberalising financial markets, planning reform
PrivatisationState enterprises sold to private sector — profit motive improves efficiencyRoyal Mail, British Gas
Trade union reformLimiting union power increases labour market flexibilityRestricting closed shops, strike ballots
Flexible labour marketsReducing minimum wage impact, relaxing employment protectionZero-hour contracts

Interventionist Supply-Side Policies

PolicyMechanismExample
Education and trainingIncreases human capital, labour productivity, occupational mobilityApprenticeships, university funding
Infrastructure investmentReduces transport costs, improves connectivity, attracts investmentHS2, broadband rollout
R&D subsidies / tax creditsEncourages innovation and technological progressPatent box, R&D tax relief
Regional policyAddresses geographical inequality and structural unemploymentEnterprise zones, regional grants
Healthcare investmentHealthier workforce, reduces absenteeism, increases productivityNHS investment, preventive care

Supply-side policies shift LRAS right — increasing potential output while reducing price level (non-inflationary growth)

AdvantagesDisadvantages

Policy Conflicts and Trade-Offs

The Phillips Curve

Phillips Curve: Shows the inverse relationship between unemployment and wage/price inflation. Lower unemployment is associated with higher inflation, and vice versa. Implies a trade-off — policymakers must choose between the two.

Key Policy Conflicts

ConflictExplanation
Growth vs InflationRapid AD growth causes demand-pull inflation. Controlling inflation via higher rates reduces growth.
Growth vs Current AccountHigher growth increases import spending, worsening the current account deficit.
Growth vs EnvironmentIncreased output may cause more pollution and resource depletion.
Low Inflation vs Low UnemploymentPhillips Curve trade-off (short run). Reducing unemployment below NAIRU causes accelerating inflation.
Fiscal Deficit vs GrowthAusterity to reduce deficit reduces AD and growth. Stimulus increases growth but worsens the deficit.
Equality vs EfficiencyRedistribution may reduce incentives; free markets may increase inequality.
Exam Tip: For policy essays, always discuss: (1) Which macroeconomic objective does the policy target? (2) What are the unintended consequences / conflicts with other objectives? (3) Short run vs long run effects. (4) Context-dependent effectiveness — a policy that works in a recession may not work in a boom. The best answers compare policy combinations rather than single instruments.

Policy Combinations

In practice, governments use a combination of fiscal, monetary, and supply-side policies. The optimal mix depends on the economic context:

  • Recession: Expansionary fiscal + monetary policy + supply-side reforms for long-term recovery
  • Stagflation: Supply-side policies (demand management alone cannot solve cost-push inflation without worsening unemployment)
  • Overheating boom: Contractionary monetary policy + fiscal tightening
  • Structural unemployment: Supply-side policies (education, retraining, regional aid)
