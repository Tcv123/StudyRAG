2.1 The National & International Economy

Macroeconomic Objectives

The UK government pursues four main macroeconomic objectives:

  1. Economic growth — sustained increase in real GDP
  2. Low and stable inflation — CPI target of 2% (±1%)
  3. Low unemployment — full employment
  4. Balance of payments equilibrium — sustainable current account

Additional objectives include: equitable income distribution, balanced budget, environmental sustainability.

Measuring Economic Performance

Gross Domestic Product (GDP)

GDP: The total monetary value of all final goods and services produced within a country's borders in a given time period (usually one year). It is the main measure of national output and economic activity.

Three Methods of Measuring GDP

MethodWhat it MeasuresApproach
Output (Production)Value added at each stage of productionSum of all value added by industries
IncomeTotal incomes earned by factors of productionWages + Rent + Interest + Profit
ExpenditureTotal spending on final goods/servicesC + I + G + (X - M)
GDP (Expenditure) = C + I + G + (X - M)

Real vs Nominal GDP

  • Nominal GDP: Measured at current prices — affected by inflation
  • Real GDP: Adjusted for inflation (constant prices) — gives true picture of output changes
  • GDP per capita: GDP / Population — better indicator of living standards than total GDP

Limitations of GDP as a Measure

  • Does not account for income distribution (inequality)
  • Ignores the informal/shadow economy
  • Excludes non-marketed output (housework, volunteering)
  • Does not measure quality of life, leisure, wellbeing
  • Ignores environmental degradation and sustainability
  • Purchasing power differences between countries

Inflation

Inflation: A sustained increase in the general price level over time. Measured in the UK by CPI (Consumer Price Index). The Bank of England targets 2% CPI inflation.

Measures of Inflation

MeasureDescriptionUse
CPIWeighted basket of 700+ goods/services, updated annuallyOfficial UK target measure, international comparison
CPIHCPI including owner-occupied housing costsONS preferred measure
RPIOlder measure, includes mortgage interest paymentsStill used for some contracts/pensions

Causes of Inflation

Demand-Pull Inflation: Too much money chasing too few goods. AD increases faster than AS can respond, pulling up the price level.

  • Consumer confidence rises → spending increases
  • Government increases spending
  • Exports rise / imports fall
  • Credit availability increases

Cost-Push Inflation: Rising costs of production push up prices. SRAS shifts left.

  • Rising raw material costs (oil, commodities)
  • Higher wages without productivity gains
  • Currency depreciation (imports more expensive)
  • Higher indirect taxes

Consequences of Inflation

LosersWinners
Savers (real return falls)Borrowers (real value of debt falls)
Fixed-income earners (real income falls)Asset owners (property/shares rise in nominal value)
Exporters (international competitiveness falls)Government (real value of national debt falls)
Workers (if wages lag behind prices)Firms with pricing power

Other consequences: Menu costs (changing prices), shoe-leather costs (shopping around), uncertainty discourages investment, fiscal drag (bracket creep).

Unemployment

Unemployment: Occurs when people who are willing and able to work cannot find employment at the prevailing wage rate.

Measures

  • Claimant Count: Number of people claiming unemployment-related benefits (JSA/Universal Credit). Simple but may under- or overstate true unemployment.
  • ILO/Labour Force Survey: Those available for work, actively seeking employment in last 4 weeks, able to start within 2 weeks. Internationally comparable.

Types of Unemployment

TypeCauseSolution
FrictionalBetween jobs, searching for workBetter information (job centres, websites)
StructuralMismatch of skills/location — decline of industriesEducation, retraining, regional policy
Cyclical (demand-deficient)Lack of AD during recessionExpansionary fiscal/monetary policy
SeasonalRegular fluctuations (tourism, agriculture)Diversification of local economy
Real wage / classicalWages above equilibrium (minimum wage, unions)Supply-side reforms, flexible labour markets

Consequences of Unemployment

  • Individual: Loss of income, skill atrophy, mental health issues, social exclusion
  • Government: Higher benefit spending, lower tax revenue, increased inequality
  • Economy: Lost output (inside PPF), hysteresis (long-term damage), deflationary pressure

Balance of Payments

Balance of Payments: A record of all financial transactions between one country and the rest of the world over a given period. It must always balance overall (surpluses in one account offset by deficits elsewhere).

Components

AccountIncludes
Current AccountTrade in goods, trade in services, primary income (investment income), secondary income (transfers)
Capital AccountCapital transfers, sale/purchase of non-produced assets
Financial AccountFDI, portfolio investment, reserve assets, other investment

UK Current Account Deficit

The UK has run a persistent current account deficit. Causes include:

  • Strong pound (makes exports expensive, imports cheap)
  • High import dependence (manufacturing decline)
  • UK consumer spending on imported goods
  • Low productivity relative to competitors

Significance of a current account deficit:

  • Must be financed by capital/financial account surplus (foreign borrowing/investment)
  • May indicate declining competitiveness
  • Could lead to currency depreciation
  • But: may reflect strong domestic demand and investment (positive sign)

The Economic (Business) Cycle

Economic Cycle: The periodic fluctuations in real GDP around its long-run trend rate of growth. The economy alternates between periods of above-trend growth (boom) and below-trend growth (recession).

The economic cycle — actual GDP fluctuates around the long-run trend rate of growth

Phases of the Cycle

PhaseCharacteristics
BoomHigh GDP growth, low unemployment, rising inflation, positive output gap, high confidence
Downturn/RecessionFalling GDP (2 consecutive quarters = technical recession), rising unemployment, falling confidence
Trough (Slump)Lowest point, high unemployment, low/negative inflation, negative output gap, excess capacity
Recovery (Expansion)GDP rising, unemployment falling, confidence returning, output gap closing
Output Gap: The difference between actual GDP and potential GDP (trend output). A positive output gap means the economy is operating beyond its sustainable capacity (inflationary pressure). A negative output gap means spare capacity exists (unemployment, deflationary pressure).

Causes of the Cycle

  • Demand-side shocks: Changes in confidence, credit conditions, fiscal/monetary policy
  • Supply-side shocks: Oil price spikes, technological change, natural disasters
  • External factors: Global recessions, exchange rate movements, financial crises
  • Multiplier and accelerator effects: Amplify initial changes in spending
Exam Tip: When discussing the economic cycle, always link phases to policy responses. In a recession, expansionary policy is appropriate. In a boom, contractionary policy prevents overheating. Discuss time lags — by the time data is available and policy is implemented, the economy may have moved to a different phase.
