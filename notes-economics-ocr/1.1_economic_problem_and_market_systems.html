1.1 The Economic Problem & Market Systems

The Economic Problem

Scarcity: The fundamental economic problem — human wants are unlimited but resources are finite. This forces societies to make choices about how to allocate resources.

Economics studies how societies allocate scarce resources among competing uses. Every society must answer three fundamental questions:

  • What to produce?
  • How to produce it?
  • For whom to produce?

Opportunity Cost: The next best alternative forgone when making a choice. It is the true cost of any decision in economics.

Opportunity cost applies to individuals (choosing between goods), firms (investment decisions), and governments (allocation of public spending). It is only relevant when resources are scarce — if there is no scarcity, there is no opportunity cost.

Exam Tip: Always define opportunity cost precisely as the next best alternative forgone — not just "what you give up." Examiners reward precision.

Production Possibility Frontiers (PPF)

PPF: A curve showing the maximum possible output combinations of two goods/services an economy can produce when all resources are fully and efficiently employed.

Production Possibility Frontier showing efficient, inefficient, and unattainable points

Key PPF Concepts

  • Points on the curve — productively efficient (all resources fully employed)
  • Points inside the curve — inefficient (unemployed resources or productive inefficiency)
  • Points outside the curve — currently unattainable with existing resources/technology
  • Movement along the curve — illustrates opportunity cost (trade-off between goods)
  • Outward shift — economic growth (increased quantity/quality of factors of production)
  • Inward shift — destruction of resources (war, natural disaster)

A concave (bowed-out) PPF reflects increasing opportunity costs — as you produce more of one good, you sacrifice increasing amounts of the other because resources are not perfectly substitutable.

Exam Tip: When drawing PPF shifts, make clear whether the shift is parallel (equal improvement in both goods) or pivoted (improvement in production of only one good, e.g., new technology in one sector).

Demand

Effective Demand: The quantity of a good/service consumers are willing and able to purchase at a given price in a given time period. It must be backed by purchasing power.

The Law of Demand

Ceteris paribus (all other things being equal), as price rises, quantity demanded falls. This gives a downward-sloping demand curve. Reasons:

  • Income effect: As price rises, consumers' real income falls, so they can afford less
  • Substitution effect: As price rises, relatively cheaper alternatives become more attractive
  • Diminishing marginal utility: Consumers value each additional unit less, so will only buy more at a lower price

Supply

Supply: The quantity of a good/service that producers are willing and able to offer for sale at a given price in a given time period.

The Law of Supply

Ceteris paribus, as price rises, quantity supplied rises. This creates an upward-sloping supply curve. Higher prices increase profit margins, incentivising firms to produce more and enabling less efficient firms to enter the market.

Shifts in Supply (Non-Price Determinants)

  • Costs of production — wage rises, raw material costs shift supply left
  • Technology — improvements shift supply right (lower unit costs)
  • Taxes — indirect taxes shift supply left; subsidies shift supply right
  • Number of firms — more firms = rightward shift
  • Weather/Natural factors — especially for agricultural goods
  • Regulations — stricter regulations increase costs, shift supply left

Market Equilibrium

Equilibrium: The price at which quantity demanded equals quantity supplied. There is no tendency for change — the market clears with no excess demand or excess supply.

Disequilibrium

  • Excess supply (surplus): Price is above equilibrium — Qs > Qd. Firms reduce prices to clear unsold stock, moving back to equilibrium.
  • Excess demand (shortage): Price is below equilibrium — Qd > Qs. Consumers bid up prices, firms increase output, moving back to equilibrium.

At price P1 (above equilibrium), quantity supplied exceeds quantity demanded creating a surplus

The Price Mechanism

The price mechanism is the means by which resources are allocated in a free market economy. Prices perform three key functions:

FunctionExplanation
SignallingPrices convey information to buyers and sellers about relative scarcity. Rising prices signal to producers that a good is in high demand; falling prices signal oversupply.
IncentiveChanges in price motivate economic agents to alter behaviour. Higher prices incentivise firms to supply more (higher profits) and consumers to buy less. Prices guide resources towards their most valued uses.
RationingPrices allocate scarce goods to those willing and able to pay. When supply is limited, rising prices ration the good to consumers who value it most highly.
Exam Tip: When discussing the price mechanism, always link the three functions together — a price rise signals scarcity, incentivises producers to supply more, and rations the good to those who value it most.

Elasticity of Demand

Price Elasticity of Demand (PED)

PED: A measure of the responsiveness of quantity demanded to a change in price. Calculated as the percentage change in quantity demanded divided by the percentage change in price.
PED = %ΔQd / %ΔP

PED is always negative (due to the inverse relationship) but we often refer to the absolute value.

Value of PEDClassificationMeaning
|PED| > 1Price elasticQd changes proportionally more than price
|PED| < 1Price inelasticQd changes proportionally less than price
|PED| = 1Unit elasticQd changes in same proportion as price
|PED| = 0Perfectly inelasticQd does not respond to price changes
|PED| = ∞Perfectly elasticAny price rise causes Qd to fall to zero

Determinants of PED

  • Availability of substitutes — more substitutes = more elastic (consumers can switch)
  • Necessity vs luxury — necessities tend to be inelastic
  • Proportion of income — expensive items are more elastic
  • Time period — demand is more elastic in the long run (more time to find alternatives)
  • Habit/addiction — addictive goods are inelastic
  • Brand loyalty — strong branding reduces elasticity

Comparing elastic (flat) vs inelastic (steep) demand curves

PED and Total Revenue

  • Elastic demand: Price cut increases total revenue (Qd rises proportionally more)
  • Inelastic demand: Price rise increases total revenue (Qd falls proportionally less)
  • Unit elastic: Total revenue unchanged regardless of price change
Exam Tip: Firms with inelastic demand (e.g., monopolies, addictive goods) can raise prices to increase revenue. This is key for evaluating pricing strategies and tax incidence questions.

Income Elasticity of Demand (YED)

YED: A measure of the responsiveness of quantity demanded to a change in consumer income.
YED = %ΔQd / %ΔY
Value of YEDType of GoodExample
YED > 1Luxury (superior) goodDesigner clothing, holidays abroad
0 < YED < 1Normal necessityFood, toothpaste

Cross Elasticity of Demand (XED)

XED: A measure of the responsiveness of quantity demanded of good A to a change in the price of good B.
XED = %ΔQd of Good A / %ΔP of Good B
Value of XEDRelationshipExample
XED > 0 (positive)SubstitutesCoke and Pepsi
XED < 0 (negative)ComplementsPrinters and ink cartridges
XED = 0Unrelated goodsBread and televisions

The higher the absolute value of XED, the stronger the relationship between the goods.

Price Elasticity of Supply (PES)

PES: A measure of the responsiveness of quantity supplied to a change in price.
PES = %ΔQs / %ΔP

Determinants of PES

  • Spare capacity — firms with excess capacity can respond quickly (more elastic)
  • Stock levels — high inventories allow rapid supply increase
  • Time period — supply is more elastic in the long run
  • Factor mobility — easily transferable resources increase PES
  • Nature of the product — agricultural goods have low PES due to growing seasons
  • Barriers to entry — lower barriers allow new firms to enter quickly

Consumer and Producer Surplus

Consumer Surplus: The difference between the price a consumer is willing to pay and the price they actually pay. Shown as the area below the demand curve and above the market price.
Producer Surplus: The difference between the price a producer receives and the minimum price they would be willing to accept. Shown as the area above the supply curve and below the market price.

Consumer surplus (blue) and producer surplus (green) at market equilibrium

At equilibrium, total economic welfare (community surplus) is maximised. This is the sum of consumer surplus and producer surplus. Any deviation from equilibrium (e.g., through price controls or taxes) reduces total welfare, creating deadweight loss.

Exam Tip: In evaluation, discuss how elasticity affects the distribution of surplus and the incidence of taxes. With inelastic demand, consumers bear more of a tax burden. With inelastic supply, producers bear more.

Specialisation and Trade

Specialisation: The concentration of production on a limited range of goods/services by an individual, firm, region, or country.

Division of Labour

The breaking down of production into separate tasks, each performed by different workers.

  • Advantages: Higher productivity, skill development, time savings, enables use of machinery
  • Disadvantages: Monotony reduces motivation, structural unemployment if demand changes, over-dependence on others

Functions of Money

Money overcomes the problems of barter (double coincidence of wants). Its four functions are:

  • Medium of exchange — accepted in return for goods/services
  • Store of value — retains purchasing power over time
  • Unit of account — allows comparison of values
  • Standard of deferred payment — enables borrowing and lending
