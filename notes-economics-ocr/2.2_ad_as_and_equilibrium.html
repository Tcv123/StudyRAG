2.2 AD/AS & National Income

Aggregate Demand (AD)

Aggregate Demand: The total planned expenditure on goods and services produced in an economy at a given price level over a given time period. AD = C + I + G + (X - M)
AD = C + I + G + (X - M)

Components of AD

ComponentMeaningApproximate % of UK GDP
C — ConsumptionHousehold spending on goods and services~63%
I — InvestmentSpending by firms on capital goods (also includes housing investment)~17%
G — Government SpendingGovernment expenditure on goods/services (excludes transfer payments)~20%
(X - M) — Net ExportsExports minus importsUsually negative for UK

Why AD Slopes Downward

  • Real balance (wealth) effect: Lower price level increases real value of wealth, encouraging spending
  • Interest rate effect: Lower price level reduces demand for money, lowering interest rates, encouraging borrowing and spending
  • International competitiveness effect: Lower price level makes exports cheaper and imports dearer, improving net exports

Factors Shifting AD

ComponentIncreases AD (rightward shift)Decreases AD (leftward shift)
ConsumptionLower interest rates, higher confidence, rising asset prices, tax cuts, credit availabilityHigher interest rates, falling confidence, higher taxes, debt levels
InvestmentLower interest rates, higher confidence, technological advances, lower corporation taxHigher interest rates, uncertainty, excess capacity
Government SpendingExpansionary fiscal policyAusterity / contractionary fiscal policy
Net ExportsDepreciation, trading partner growth, improved competitivenessAppreciation, recession abroad, higher domestic inflation

Aggregate Supply

Short-Run Aggregate Supply (SRAS)

SRAS: The total output that all firms in the economy are willing and able to supply at each price level in the short run, when at least some factor prices (especially wages) are fixed. SRAS slopes upward — firms can increase output by using existing capacity more intensively.

Shifts in SRAS:

  • Changes in costs of production (wages, raw materials, energy)
  • Exchange rate changes (import costs)
  • Indirect taxes / subsidies
  • Supply-side shocks (e.g., oil price spike)

Long-Run Aggregate Supply (LRAS)

LRAS: The total output the economy can produce when all resources are fully and efficiently employed — the economy is at its potential output (full capacity). In the classical model, LRAS is vertical at the full employment level of output.

Shifts in LRAS:

  • Changes in quantity of factors of production (labour force growth, capital investment, new land/resources)
  • Changes in quality of factors (education, training, technology)
  • Institutional improvements (property rights, rule of law, deregulation)

AD/AS model — SRAS upward sloping, LRAS vertical at full employment (Yf), macroeconomic equilibrium where AD = SRAS

Keynesian LRAS

The Keynesian view differs from the Classical model:

  • At low output: LRAS is horizontal (spare capacity, output can increase without inflation)
  • At intermediate output: LRAS slopes upward (approaching capacity, some inflationary pressure)
  • At full capacity: LRAS is vertical (economy cannot produce more, all increase in AD is inflationary)

Keynesian LRAS — the economy can operate below full capacity for extended periods due to deficient demand

Exam Tip: Know both Classical and Keynesian LRAS models and when to use each. Classical LRAS is better for analysing supply-side policies. Keynesian LRAS is better for explaining recessions and why demand management matters. State which model you are using in your answer.

Macroeconomic Equilibrium

Macroeconomic equilibrium occurs where AD intersects AS. Changes in AD or AS will shift the equilibrium, affecting real output, employment, and the price level.

AD decreases (leftward)FallsFalls (or slower growth)Recession, demand management needed

The Circular Flow of Income

Circular flow of income — injections (I, G, X) add to the flow; withdrawals (S, T, M) remove from it

Equilibrium condition: National income is in equilibrium when total injections = total withdrawals.

I + G + X = S + T + M
  • If injections > withdrawals → national income rises (expansion)
  • If withdrawals > injections → national income falls (contraction)

The Multiplier

Multiplier Effect: The process by which an initial change in spending leads to a larger final change in national income. An injection into the circular flow is re-spent multiple times, creating successive rounds of income and expenditure.
Multiplier (k) = 1 / (1 - MPC) = 1 / MPW

Where:

  • MPC = Marginal Propensity to Consume (fraction of additional income spent domestically)
  • MPW = Marginal Propensity to Withdraw = MPS + MPT + MPM
  • MPS = Marginal Propensity to Save
  • MPT = Marginal Propensity to Tax
  • MPM = Marginal Propensity to Import
Final change in Y = Initial injection x Multiplier

Example: If MPC = 0.8 → Multiplier = 1/(1-0.8) = 1/0.2 = 5. A £10m increase in government spending would lead to a £50m final increase in national income.

The multiplier effect — an initial shift in AD leads to a larger final increase in national income (Y₁ to Y₂)

Factors Affecting the Size of the Multiplier

Larger MultiplierSmaller Multiplier
High MPC (high consumer spending)Low MPC (high savings rate)
Low tax ratesHigh tax rates (large MPT)
Low propensity to importOpen economy (high MPM)
Spare capacity in economyEconomy near full capacity (crowding out)
Exam Tip: The multiplier works in BOTH directions — a withdrawal has a negative multiplier effect. Also, in practice, the multiplier is smaller than theory suggests due to: time lags, confidence effects, spare capacity constraints, and the economy being open. Always evaluate the real-world size of the multiplier when discussing fiscal policy.

National Income Determination

The Paradox of Thrift

If all households simultaneously increase their savings rate, total spending falls, causing national income to fall via the multiplier. The result may be lower total savings than before (as income falls, absolute savings may fall too). This illustrates the fallacy of composition — what is rational for an individual may be irrational for the economy as a whole.

Accelerator Effect

Accelerator: The theory that the level of investment depends on the rate of change of national income/output. When GDP growth accelerates, firms invest more to expand capacity. When growth slows (even if still positive), investment may fall sharply.

The multiplier-accelerator interaction can amplify business cycle fluctuations — an initial increase in spending triggers the multiplier, which raises GDP growth, triggering the accelerator (more investment), which in turn raises AD further via the multiplier.
