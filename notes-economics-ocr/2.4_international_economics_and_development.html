2.4 International Economics & Development

Theory of International Trade

Absolute Advantage

Absolute Advantage (Adam Smith): A country has an absolute advantage in producing a good if it can produce it using fewer resources (lower absolute cost) than another country. Countries should specialise in goods where they have an absolute advantage and trade.

Comparative Advantage

Comparative Advantage (David Ricardo): A country has a comparative advantage in producing a good if it can produce it at a lower opportunity cost than another country. Even if one country has an absolute advantage in all goods, both countries benefit from specialisation and trade based on comparative advantage.

Example:

CountryWheat (units per worker)Cloth (units per worker)Opportunity Cost of 1 Wheat
Country A1050.5 cloth
Country B441 cloth

Country A has a comparative advantage in wheat (lower opportunity cost: 0.5 < 1). Country B has a comparative advantage in cloth. Both benefit if A specialises in wheat and B in cloth, then they trade.

Assumptions and Limitations of Comparative Advantage

  • Assumes no transport costs
  • Assumes constant costs of production (no economies of scale)
  • Assumes perfect factor mobility within countries, immobility between countries
  • Ignores exchange rate effects
  • Assumes free trade (no tariffs or quotas)
  • Ignores quality differences between goods
  • Static model — does not account for changing comparative advantages over time

Benefits of Free Trade

  • Greater consumer choice and lower prices
  • Increased competition drives efficiency and innovation
  • Economies of scale from larger markets
  • Access to resources not available domestically
  • Technology and knowledge transfer
  • Higher economic growth

Protectionism

Protectionism: Government policies that restrict international trade to protect domestic industries from foreign competition. Methods include tariffs, quotas, subsidies, and non-tariff barriers.

Methods of Protectionism

MethodDescriptionEffect
TariffTax on imports, raising their priceReduces quantity of imports, raises revenue, protects domestic producers
QuotaPhysical limit on quantity of importsDirectly restricts import volume
Subsidy to domestic producersFinancial support to home firmsLowers domestic costs, makes them more competitive against imports
Non-tariff barriersRegulations, standards, bureaucracyRaises costs and complexity for importers
EmbargoComplete ban on trade with a countryPolitical tool, eliminates all trade

Arguments For Protectionism

  • Infant industry argument: New industries need temporary protection to develop before competing internationally
  • National security: Strategic industries (defence, food, energy) should not depend on foreign supply
  • Protecting employment: Prevent structural unemployment from cheap imports
  • Correcting trade deficits: Reduce imports to improve balance of payments
  • Anti-dumping: Prevent foreign firms selling below cost to destroy domestic competition
  • Revenue generation: Tariffs generate government income (especially in developing countries)

Arguments Against Protectionism

  • Higher prices for consumers (reduced consumer surplus)
  • Reduced competition leads to inefficiency
  • Retaliation from trading partners (trade wars)
  • Misallocation of resources (against comparative advantage)
  • Infant industries may never "grow up" (become dependent on protection)
  • Less innovation without competitive pressure
Exam Tip: When evaluating protectionism, always consider: (1) Short run vs long run effects, (2) Who gains and who loses (domestic producers vs consumers), (3) Risk of retaliation, (4) Whether the market failure justifying protection actually exists.

Exchange Rate: The price of one currency expressed in terms of another. It determines the international purchasing power of a currency.

Effects of Exchange Rate Changes

Depreciation (weaker £):

  • Exports cheaper in foreign currency → export volume rises
  • Imports more expensive → import volume falls
  • Current account may improve (Marshall-Lerner condition: if PED(X) + PED(M) > 1)
  • J-Curve effect: current account initially worsens before improving
  • Cost-push inflation (imported raw materials more expensive)

Appreciation (stronger £):

  • Exports more expensive → export volume falls
  • Imports cheaper → import volume rises
  • Downward pressure on inflation (cheaper imports)
  • May worsen current account

Globalisation

Globalisation: The increasing interconnectedness and interdependence of the world's economies through the growth of international trade, capital flows, migration, and the spread of technology and culture.

Causes of Globalisation

  • Reduction in trade barriers (WTO, free trade agreements)
  • Technological advances (internet, containerisation, telecommunications)
  • Reduced transport costs
  • Growth of multinational corporations (MNCs)
  • Financial deregulation and capital mobility
  • Political changes (fall of communism, opening of China/India)

Impact of Globalisation

Economic Development

Economic Development: A broader concept than economic growth — it encompasses improvements in living standards, reduction in poverty, better health and education, and greater economic choices. Growth is necessary but not sufficient for development.

Measures of Development

MeasureWhat it MeasuresLimitations
GDP per capita (PPP)Average income adjusted for purchasing powerIgnores distribution, informal economy, non-monetary wellbeing
HDI (Human Development Index)Life expectancy, education (mean/expected years), GNI per capitaNational averages hide inequality, limited indicators
MPI (Multidimensional Poverty Index)Deprivation in health, education, living standardsData collection challenges
Gini coefficientIncome/wealth inequality (0 = perfect equality, 1 = perfect inequality)Does not show absolute poverty levels
Gender Inequality IndexGender disparities in health, empowerment, labour marketLimited to gender dimension only

Barriers to Development

  • Poverty trap / vicious cycle: Low income → low savings → low investment → low growth → low income
  • Primary product dependency: Volatile commodity prices, declining terms of trade
  • Corruption and weak institutions: Deters investment, diverts resources
  • Debt burden: Debt servicing diverts funds from development spending
  • Poor infrastructure: Limits trade, market access, and productivity
  • Political instability and conflict: Destroys capital, deters investment
  • Capital flight: Domestic wealth leaves the country
  • Brain drain: Skilled workers emigrate to developed countries

Inequality

The Lorenz Curve and Gini Coefficient

Lorenz Curve — the further from the line of equality, the greater the inequality. Gini = A / (A + B)

Gini Coefficient: A measure of income inequality calculated as the area between the Lorenz curve and the line of perfect equality (A) divided by the total area under the line of equality (A + B). Ranges from 0 (perfect equality) to 1 (perfect inequality).

Causes of Inequality

  • Differences in skills, education, and human capital
  • Wealth inheritance and intergenerational transfers
  • Globalisation (benefits unevenly distributed)
  • Technological change (skill-biased, rewards high-skilled workers)
  • Tax system progressivity (or lack of)
  • Labour market structure (minimum wage, union power)
  • Discrimination (gender, race, disability)

Consequences of Inequality

EconomicSocial
Reduced social mobilityHealth inequalities
Lower aggregate demand (if low-income have high MPC)Higher crime rates
Reduced human capital investmentSocial unrest and political instability
Market failure (under-consumption of merit goods)Reduced social cohesion

Policies to Reduce Inequality

  • Progressive taxation (higher rates on higher incomes)
  • Transfer payments and benefits (Universal Credit, pensions)
  • Minimum wage legislation
  • Investment in education and training (equalise opportunity)
  • Universal public services (NHS, state education)
  • Anti-discrimination legislation
Exam Tip: When discussing development and inequality, always evaluate: Does growth alone lead to development? (No — Kuznets curve suggests inequality initially rises then falls with growth.) Is some inequality necessary for incentives? (Equity vs efficiency trade-off.) Are measures like HDI sufficient? (No single indicator captures development fully.) Always use real-world examples and data where possible.
