3.1.2 Price Determination in a Competitive Market

Demand

Demand is the quantity of a good or service that consumers are willing and able to buy at a given price in a given time period.

The Law of Demand

There is an inverse (negative) relationship between price and quantity demanded, ceteris paribus. As price rises, quantity demanded falls; as price falls, quantity demanded rises.

Reasons for the downward-sloping demand curve:

  • Income effect — a rise in price reduces real income (purchasing power), so consumers buy less
  • Substitution effect — a rise in price makes the good relatively more expensive than alternatives, so consumers switch to substitutes
  • Diminishing marginal utility — consumers gain less satisfaction from each additional unit, so they will only buy more at lower prices

Movements Along vs Shifts of the Demand Curve

  • Movement along — caused by a change in the good's own price (extension when price falls; contraction when price rises)
  • Shift of the curve — caused by a change in any non-price determinant of demand

Non-Price Determinants of Demand (Shifters)

  • Income — for normal goods, demand rises with income; for inferior goods, demand falls with income
  • Price of substitutes — if substitute price rises, demand for this good rises
  • Price of complements — if complement price rises, demand for this good falls
  • Tastes and preferences — advertising, fashion, health concerns
  • Population/demographics — larger or older population changes demand patterns
  • Expectations — expected future price increases can raise current demand
  • Interest rates — lower rates increase demand for goods bought on credit

Supply

Supply is the quantity of a good or service that producers are willing and able to sell at a given price in a given time period.

The Law of Supply

There is a positive (direct) relationship between price and quantity supplied, ceteris paribus. As price rises, quantity supplied rises because higher prices increase the profit incentive for firms.

Non-Price Determinants of Supply (Shifters)

  • Costs of production — higher costs (wages, raw materials, rent) shift supply left
  • Technology — improved technology reduces costs, shifting supply right
  • Indirect taxes — taxes on producers shift supply left (increase costs)
  • Subsidies — shift supply right (reduce effective costs)
  • Number of firms — more firms in the market shifts market supply right
  • Weather/natural factors — relevant for agricultural markets
  • Expectations of future prices — may affect current supply decisions

Market Equilibrium

Equilibrium occurs where the demand curve intersects the supply curve. At this point, quantity demanded equals quantity supplied, and there is no tendency for price to change. The equilibrium price is called the market-clearing price.

Disequilibrium

  • Excess supply (surplus) — price is above equilibrium. Quantity supplied exceeds quantity demanded. Price will fall as firms compete to sell unsold stock.
  • Excess demand (shortage) — price is below equilibrium. Quantity demanded exceeds quantity supplied. Price will rise as consumers compete for limited goods.

Changes in Equilibrium

When demand or supply curves shift, the equilibrium price and quantity change:

  • Increase in demand (shift right): price rises, quantity rises
  • Decrease in demand (shift left): price falls, quantity falls
  • Increase in supply (shift right): price falls, quantity rises
  • Decrease in supply (shift left): price rises, quantity falls

Consumer and Producer Surplus

Consumer surplus is the difference between the price a consumer is willing to pay and the price they actually pay. It is represented by the area below the demand curve and above the market price.

Producer surplus is the difference between the price a producer receives and the minimum price they would be willing to accept. It is represented by the area above the supply curve and below the market price.

Together, consumer surplus and producer surplus form the total economic welfare (or community surplus) generated by a market. At the free-market equilibrium, total welfare is maximised (allocative efficiency is achieved).

Price Elasticity of Demand (PED)

Price Elasticity of Demand (PED) measures the responsiveness of quantity demanded to a change in price.
PED = % change in quantity demanded ÷ % change in price

PED is always negative (due to the inverse price-quantity relationship), but we often refer to the absolute value.

ValueDescriptionMeaning
|PED| > 1ElasticQuantity demanded is highly responsive to price changes
|PED| = 1Unit elastic% change in Qd exactly equals % change in P
|PED| < 1InelasticQuantity demanded is relatively unresponsive to price changes
PED = 0Perfectly inelasticQuantity demanded does not change with price (vertical curve)
PED = -∞Perfectly elasticAny price rise causes demand to fall to zero (horizontal curve)

Determinants of PED

  • Availability of substitutes — more substitutes = more elastic (consumers can switch easily)
  • Necessity vs luxury — necessities are inelastic; luxuries are elastic
  • Proportion of income — goods taking a larger share of income tend to be more elastic
  • Time period — demand is more elastic in the long run (consumers can find alternatives)
  • Habit/addiction — addictive goods are inelastic (e.g., cigarettes)
  • Brand loyalty — strong loyalty makes demand more inelastic

PED and Total Revenue

  • If demand is elastic (|PED| > 1): a price cut increases total revenue (% rise in Qd > % fall in P)
  • If demand is inelastic (|PED| < 1): a price rise increases total revenue (% fall in Qd < % rise in P)
  • If demand is unit elastic (|PED| = 1): total revenue is unchanged by price changes
Exam Tip: The relationship between PED and revenue is frequently tested. Remember: for elastic demand, price and revenue move in opposite directions; for inelastic demand, they move in the same direction.

Income Elasticity of Demand (YED)

Income Elasticity of Demand (YED) measures the responsiveness of quantity demanded to a change in consumer income.
YED = % change in quantity demanded ÷ % change in income
ValueType of GoodExplanation
YED > 1Normal good (luxury)Demand rises more than proportionally with income
0 < YED < 1Normal good (necessity)Demand rises less than proportionally with income
YED < 0Inferior goodDemand falls as income rises (consumers switch to better alternatives)

Significance of YED:

  • Firms producing luxury goods (high positive YED) benefit more from economic growth but suffer more in recessions
  • Firms producing inferior goods (negative YED) may be "recession-proof"
  • Income elasticity helps firms plan production and investment decisions based on expected economic growth
  • Governments use YED data to predict how tax revenue from different goods will change with growth

Cross Elasticity of Demand (XED)

Cross Elasticity of Demand (XED) measures the responsiveness of quantity demanded of good A to a change in the price of good B.
XED = % change in quantity demanded of A ÷ % change in price of B
ValueRelationshipExplanation
XED > 0 (positive)SubstitutesRise in price of B causes rise in demand for A
XED < 0 (negative)ComplementsRise in price of B causes fall in demand for A
XED = 0Unrelated goodsPrice of B has no effect on demand for A

The magnitude of XED indicates the strength of the relationship. Close substitutes (e.g., Coke and Pepsi) have a high positive XED; close complements (e.g., printers and ink cartridges) have a high negative XED.

Significance:

  • Helps firms understand competitive threats (high XED with substitutes means vulnerability to competitor pricing)
  • Useful for pricing strategies — bundling complements, competitive pricing against substitutes
  • Competition authorities use XED to define markets (high XED goods are in the same market)

Price Elasticity of Supply (PES)

Price Elasticity of Supply (PES) measures the responsiveness of quantity supplied to a change in price.
PES = % change in quantity supplied ÷ % change in price

PES is always positive (due to the positive price-quantity supplied relationship).

Determinants of PES

  • Spare capacity — firms with spare capacity can increase output quickly (more elastic)
  • Availability of stocks — goods that can be stored can be supplied quickly from inventories
  • Time period — supply is more elastic in the long run (firms can build new factories, enter markets)
  • Factor mobility — if factors can easily switch between uses, supply is more elastic
  • Nature of the good — manufactured goods tend to have more elastic supply than agricultural goods (which depend on growing seasons)
  • Barriers to entry — low barriers allow new firms to enter quickly, making market supply more elastic
Exam Tip: Time is the most important determinant of PES. In the momentary period, supply is perfectly inelastic (fixed stock). In the short run, firms can increase output using existing capacity. In the long run, new firms can enter and existing firms can expand capacity.

Applications of Elasticity

Government Taxation

  • Indirect taxes on goods with inelastic demand raise more revenue and lead to smaller falls in quantity (e.g., taxes on cigarettes, petrol)
  • The burden of tax (incidence) falls mainly on the side with more inelastic response
  • If demand is inelastic relative to supply, consumers bear most of the tax burden

Business Pricing Decisions

  • Firms facing inelastic demand can raise prices to increase revenue
  • Firms in competitive markets (elastic demand) should lower prices to gain market share
  • Price discrimination relies on identifying groups with different PED

Primary Commodity Markets

  • Agricultural goods often have inelastic supply (growing seasons) and inelastic demand (necessities)
  • This leads to volatile prices when supply shocks occur (e.g., poor harvest)
  • Small shifts in supply cause large price changes, creating income instability for farmers

Evaluation Points for Elasticity

  • Elasticity values are estimates and can change over time
  • PED varies along a straight-line demand curve (elastic at top, inelastic at bottom)
  • Real-world elasticities are difficult to measure precisely due to ceteris paribus assumption rarely holding
  • Firms may not have perfect knowledge of their elasticity values
  • Short-run vs long-run elasticities differ significantly — policies may have different effects over different time horizons
