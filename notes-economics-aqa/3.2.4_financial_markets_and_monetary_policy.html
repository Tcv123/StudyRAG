3.2.4 Financial Markets and Monetary Policy

The Role of Financial Markets

Financial markets are markets where financial instruments (shares, bonds, currencies, derivatives) are traded. They facilitate the flow of funds from savers to borrowers and play a crucial role in the allocation of capital in the economy.

Functions of Financial Markets

  • Channelling funds from savers to borrowers — financial intermediation (banks take deposits and make loans)
  • Providing liquidity — allowing assets to be bought and sold easily
  • Pricing risk — the price of financial assets reflects the risk of the underlying investment
  • Facilitating exchange — currency markets enable international trade
  • Enabling firms to raise capital — through issuing shares (equity) or bonds (debt)
  • Forward markets — allowing hedging against future price/currency changes

The Role of Money

Money is anything widely accepted as a medium of exchange. It serves four functions:
1. Medium of exchange — eliminates the need for barter
2. Store of value — can be saved and used later (inflation erodes this)
3. Unit of account — allows prices to be compared
4. Standard of deferred payment — enables credit and future payment contracts

Money Supply

The money supply is the total stock of money circulating in an economy. Key measures:

  • Narrow money (M0/base money) — notes and coins in circulation plus banks' reserves at the central bank
  • Broad money (M4) — includes narrow money plus bank deposits and other liquid assets. This is the more relevant measure as most money exists as bank deposits, not physical cash.

How Banks Create Money

Commercial banks create money through the process of lending. When a bank makes a loan, it creates a new deposit (an asset for the borrower and a liability for the bank). This is the "credit creation" or "money multiplier" process:

  • A bank receives a deposit and keeps a fraction as reserves (reserve ratio)
  • It lends the remainder, which creates a new deposit when spent
  • This deposit enables further lending, and so on
  • The credit multiplier = 1 / reserve ratio

Money Demand (Liquidity Preference)

Keynes identified three motives for holding money:

  • Transactions motive — money needed for everyday purchases (increases with income)
  • Precautionary motive — money held for unexpected expenses
  • Speculative motive — money held to take advantage of future changes in asset prices (inversely related to interest rates — at low rates, the opportunity cost of holding money is low)

The Central Bank (Bank of England)

The Bank of England is the UK's central bank. Its primary objective is to maintain price stability (CPI inflation at 2%). It was granted operational independence in 1997 to set interest rates through the Monetary Policy Committee (MPC).

Roles of the Bank of England

  • Setting monetary policy — the MPC sets the Bank Rate (base interest rate)
  • Lender of last resort — providing emergency liquidity to banks to prevent systemic collapse
  • Managing the national debt
  • Banker to the government and commercial banks
  • Issuing banknotes
  • Maintaining financial stability — macro-prudential regulation
  • Managing foreign exchange reserves

Monetary Policy

Monetary policy involves the use of interest rates, money supply, and credit conditions to influence aggregate demand and achieve macroeconomic objectives (primarily the 2% inflation target).

Interest Rates

The Bank Rate is the main tool of monetary policy. The MPC meets 8 times a year to decide whether to change it.

The Transmission Mechanism of Monetary Policy

A change in the Bank Rate affects the economy through multiple channels:

  1. Borrowing/saving channel — lower rates reduce the cost of borrowing and reward for saving → more spending (C and I rise)
  2. Asset prices channel — lower rates boost asset prices (houses, shares) → positive wealth effect → higher consumption
  3. Exchange rate channel — lower rates make the UK less attractive for foreign investment → exchange rate depreciates → exports cheaper, imports dearer → net exports rise
  4. Expectations/confidence channel — rate cuts signal economic support, boosting confidence
  5. Cash flow channel — lower mortgage payments increase disposable income for homeowners

Quantitative Easing (QE)

Quantitative Easing (QE) is an unconventional monetary policy tool used when interest rates are at or near zero (the zero lower bound). The central bank creates new money electronically and uses it to purchase financial assets (mainly government bonds) from financial institutions.

How QE Works

  1. Bank of England creates new electronic money
  2. Uses it to buy government bonds from pension funds, insurance companies, and banks
  3. This pushes up bond prices and pushes down bond yields (long-term interest rates)
  4. Sellers of bonds now have cash which they may reinvest in riskier assets (shares, corporate bonds) or lend
  5. This stimulates investment and spending in the real economy
  6. Wealth effect from rising asset prices encourages consumption

Evaluation of QE

Advantages:

  • Provides stimulus when conventional monetary policy is exhausted (rates at zero)
  • Reduces long-term borrowing costs for firms and government
  • Prevents deflation and supports economic recovery
  • Can restore confidence in financial markets

Disadvantages/Limitations:

  • May increase inequality (benefits those with assets — house/share owners — more than those without)
  • Risk of future inflation if money supply grows too fast relative to output
  • Banks may not lend the extra reserves ("pushing on a string" — banks hoard liquidity)
  • May create asset bubbles (artificially inflated house/share prices)
  • Exit strategy is difficult — unwinding QE (selling bonds back) may disrupt markets
  • Weakens the exchange rate — imported inflation risk

Evaluation of Monetary Policy

Strengths

  • Independent from political interference (MPC makes decisions based on data, not elections)
  • Flexible — can be adjusted quickly (MPC meets every 6 weeks)
  • Proven track record — UK inflation remained close to 2% for much of 1997-2021
  • Credibility — if markets trust the central bank, expectations remain anchored

Weaknesses/Limitations

  • Time lags — takes 18-24 months for interest rate changes to fully affect the economy
  • Blunt instrument — affects all sectors equally, cannot target specific problems
  • Ineffective against cost-push inflation — raising rates does not address supply-side cost increases
  • Liquidity trap — at very low interest rates, further cuts may have no effect (consumers/firms save rather than spend)
  • Asymmetric effects — rate changes affect borrowers more than savers (or vice versa)
  • Depends on bank lending — if banks are unwilling to lend, lower rates may not translate to more credit
  • Exchange rate conflicts — rate changes to control inflation may have unwanted exchange rate effects
Exam Tip: When evaluating monetary policy, consider the current economic context. Monetary policy is most effective when: inflation is demand-pull, the banking system is functioning normally, consumer/business confidence is moderate, and there is room to cut or raise rates. It is least effective during supply shocks, financial crises, or when rates are already at zero.

Financial Market Failure and Regulation

Causes of Financial Instability

  • Excessive risk-taking by banks (moral hazard — "too big to fail")
  • Asset bubbles (property, shares) fuelled by cheap credit
  • Lack of transparency in complex financial products
  • Herding behaviour and speculation
  • Inadequate capital reserves in banks

Regulation of Financial Markets

  • Capital requirements — banks must hold minimum capital ratios (Basel III)
  • Stress testing — banks must prove they can withstand economic shocks
  • Deposit insurance — protects savers (FSCS in UK, up to £85,000)
  • Macro-prudential regulation — monitoring systemic risk (Financial Policy Committee)
  • Ring-fencing — separating retail banking from riskier investment banking activities
