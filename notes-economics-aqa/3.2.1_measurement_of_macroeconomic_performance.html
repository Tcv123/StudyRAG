3.2.1 The Measurement of Macroeconomic Performance

Macroeconomic Objectives

The UK government pursues four main macroeconomic objectives:

  1. Economic growth — sustained increase in real GDP over time
  2. Low and stable inflation — target of 2% CPI (set by the government for the Bank of England)
  3. Low unemployment — full employment (minimising involuntary unemployment)
  4. Balance of payments equilibrium — avoiding persistent current account deficits

Additional objectives include:

  • Fair distribution of income and wealth
  • Environmental sustainability
  • Sound government finances (manageable budget deficit and national debt)

Gross Domestic Product (GDP)

Gross Domestic Product (GDP) is the total value of all goods and services produced within a country's borders in a given time period (usually a year or quarter).

Three Methods of Measuring GDP

MethodWhat It MeasuresComponents
ExpenditureTotal spending on goods and servicesC + I + G + (X - M)
IncomeTotal income earned by factors of productionWages + Rent + Interest + Profit
OutputTotal value added by each industrySum of value added across all sectors

All three methods should give the same figure (in theory), as every act of expenditure creates income, which results from output.

Real vs Nominal GDP

Nominal (money) GDP — GDP measured at current prices. Changes reflect both output changes AND price changes.

Real GDP — GDP adjusted for inflation (measured at constant/base-year prices). Changes reflect only actual changes in output.
Real GDP = Nominal GDP ÷ Price Deflator × 100

Real GDP is the more meaningful measure because it strips out the effect of inflation, showing the true change in an economy's output of goods and services.

GDP Per Capita

GDP per capita = Real GDP ÷ Population

This is a better measure of average living standards than total GDP, as it accounts for population size. A country could have high GDP but low GDP per capita if its population is very large.

Limitations of GDP as a Measure of Living Standards

  • Distribution of income — GDP per capita is an average; it hides inequality
  • Shadow/informal economy — unreported economic activity (cash-in-hand, illegal markets) is excluded
  • Non-marketed output — household work, voluntary work, DIY is not counted
  • Quality of goods — GDP does not reflect improvements in product quality
  • Environmental degradation — pollution and resource depletion are not deducted
  • Leisure time — higher GDP may come at the cost of longer working hours
  • Composition of output — military spending increases GDP but may not improve welfare
  • PPP issues — international comparisons need Purchasing Power Parity adjustments
Exam Tip: When evaluating GDP data, always mention at least 2-3 limitations. Alternative measures include the Human Development Index (HDI), Genuine Progress Indicator (GPI), and the Measure of Economic Welfare (MEW).

Inflation

Inflation is a sustained increase in the general price level over time, resulting in a fall in the purchasing power of money.

Deflation is a sustained decrease in the general price level.

Disinflation is a fall in the rate of inflation (prices still rising, but more slowly).

How CPI Is Calculated

  1. A representative "basket of goods" is selected (updated annually)
  2. Prices of these goods are collected each month from across the UK
  3. Each item is given a weight based on its share of average household spending
  4. A weighted price index is calculated and compared to the base year (index = 100)

Limitations of CPI

  • The basket may not reflect individual spending patterns
  • Quality improvements are hard to account for
  • New products are added with a lag
  • Does not include housing costs (mortgage payments)
  • Different income groups experience different rates of inflation
  • Substitution bias — consumers switch to cheaper alternatives when prices rise

Unemployment

Unemployment occurs when people who are willing and able to work, and actively seeking employment, cannot find a job.

Costs of Unemployment

  • To individuals: loss of income, deskilling, health problems (stress, depression), social exclusion
  • To firms: reduced consumer spending, loss of skilled workers
  • To the economy: lost output (inside PPF), reduced tax revenue, increased government spending on benefits, hysteresis (long-term unemployment makes people less employable)
  • To society: increased crime, social problems, regional deprivation

Balance of Payments

The Balance of Payments is a record of all financial transactions between a country and the rest of the world over a given time period.
Figure 1: Structure of the Balance of Payments

The Current Account

The current account records trade in goods and services, plus income flows:

  • Trade in goods (visible trade) — exports minus imports of physical goods. The UK typically has a trade deficit in goods.
  • Trade in services (invisible trade) — exports minus imports of services (financial services, tourism, IT). The UK typically has a surplus in services.
  • Primary income — income earned from overseas investments (wages, dividends, interest) minus income paid to foreign investors in the UK.
  • Secondary income (transfers) — government transfers (e.g., foreign aid, EU contributions) and private transfers (e.g., remittances).

Current Account Deficit

A current account deficit means the country is spending more on imports of goods, services, and income payments than it earns from exports and income receipts. The UK has run a persistent current account deficit.

Causes of a deficit:

  • Strong exchange rate (makes exports expensive, imports cheap)
  • High domestic income (increases demand for imports)
  • Low productivity/competitiveness relative to trading partners
  • Deindustrialisation (reduced manufacturing exports)
  • High commodity prices (e.g., oil imports)

Significance of a deficit:

  • Must be financed by capital/financial account surplus (foreign investment inflows or borrowing)
  • May lead to currency depreciation in the long run
  • Could indicate lack of competitiveness
  • Not necessarily a problem if financing is sustainable (e.g., through FDI)
Exam Tip: A current account deficit is not always bad. It may reflect strong economic growth (high import demand) or successful attraction of foreign investment. The key question is whether the deficit is sustainable and what is causing it.

Index Numbers

Index numbers are used to measure changes in a variable relative to a base year (set at 100). They allow easy comparison of percentage changes over time.
Index = (Value in current year ÷ Value in base year) × 100

Uses of index numbers in economics:

  • CPI/RPI (price indices)
  • Real GDP index
  • Exchange rate index (trade-weighted)
  • Share price indices (FTSE 100)

Uses of National Income Data

  • Measuring economic growth over time
  • International comparisons (using GDP per capita at PPP)
  • Government policy evaluation
  • Assessing living standards (with limitations)
  • Identifying economic cycles and trends
  • Resource allocation decisions
