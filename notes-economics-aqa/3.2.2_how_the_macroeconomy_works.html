3.2.2 How the Macroeconomy Works

The Circular Flow of Income

The circular flow of income is a model showing how money flows between households and firms in the economy. Households provide factors of production to firms and receive income; firms produce goods and services which households purchase.
Figure 1: The circular flow of income showing injections and withdrawals

Injections and Withdrawals

  • Injections (J) — additions to the circular flow: Investment (I), Government spending (G), Exports (X)
  • Withdrawals/Leakages (W) — money leaving the circular flow: Savings (S), Taxation (T), Imports (M)

Equilibrium in the circular flow: National income is in equilibrium when total injections equal total withdrawals (J = W, i.e., I + G + X = S + T + M). If injections exceed withdrawals, national income rises; if withdrawals exceed injections, national income falls.

Aggregate Demand (AD)

Aggregate Demand (AD) is the total planned expenditure on goods and services produced in the economy at a given price level, in a given time period.
AD = C + I + G + (X - M)

Where:

  • C = Consumer spending (largest component, ~60% of AD in the UK)
  • I = Investment (spending by firms on capital goods)
  • G = Government spending (on public services and infrastructure)
  • (X - M) = Net exports (exports minus imports)

Why the AD Curve Slopes Downward

The AD curve shows an inverse relationship between the price level and real output demanded:

  • Real balance (wealth) effect — a higher price level reduces the real value of money/savings, reducing spending power and consumption
  • Interest rate effect — a higher price level may lead to higher interest rates, reducing investment and consumption
  • International competitiveness effect — a higher domestic price level makes exports more expensive and imports cheaper, reducing net exports

Shifts of the AD Curve

AD shifts right (increases) when any component of AD rises at every price level. Key shifters include:

ComponentFactors Causing AD to Shift Right
Consumption (C)Lower interest rates, rising house prices (wealth effect), rising consumer confidence, tax cuts, credit availability
Investment (I)Lower interest rates, improved business confidence, technological advances, tax incentives for investment
Government spending (G)Expansionary fiscal policy, increased public infrastructure projects
Net exports (X-M)Depreciation of exchange rate, growth in trading partner economies, improved competitiveness

The Consumption Function

The consumption function shows the relationship between disposable income and consumer spending. As income rises, consumption rises, but by less than the increase in income (some is saved).
C = a + bY

Where:

  • a = autonomous consumption (spending unrelated to income, e.g., using savings or borrowing)
  • b = marginal propensity to consume (MPC)
  • Y = disposable income

Marginal Propensity to Consume and Save

Marginal Propensity to Consume (MPC) = the proportion of each additional pound of income that is spent on consumption = ΔC ÷ ΔY

Marginal Propensity to Save (MPS) = the proportion of each additional pound of income that is saved = ΔS ÷ ΔY

MPC + MPS = 1 (in a simple two-sector model)

Factors Influencing Consumption

  • Disposable income — the most important determinant (Keynesian consumption function)
  • Interest rates — higher rates encourage saving over spending
  • Consumer confidence — optimism increases spending
  • Wealth effects — rising asset prices (houses, shares) make consumers feel wealthier
  • Credit availability — easier access to borrowing increases consumption
  • Distribution of income — lower-income households have higher MPC
  • Expectations of future income/prices

Factors Influencing Saving

  • Interest rates — higher rates increase the reward for saving
  • Income levels — higher income enables more saving
  • Confidence — uncertainty increases precautionary saving
  • Access to credit — easy credit reduces need to save
  • Cultural factors and age demographics

Factors Influencing Investment

  • Interest rates — the cost of borrowing; lower rates encourage investment
  • Business confidence/expectations — "animal spirits" (Keynes)
  • Rate of economic growth — accelerator effect (rising demand triggers investment)
  • Retained profit — internal funds available for investment
  • Technological change — need to invest in new technology
  • Government policy — tax allowances, subsidies, regulation
  • Spare capacity — firms are less likely to invest if they have excess capacity

Aggregate Supply (AS)

Aggregate Supply (AS) is the total output of goods and services that firms in the economy are willing and able to produce at a given price level, in a given time period.

Short-Run Aggregate Supply (SRAS)

SRAS slopes upward — as the price level rises, firms are willing to supply more output because:

  • Higher prices increase profit margins (wages and input costs are sticky in the short run)
  • Firms have incentive to increase production

Shifts in SRAS: caused by changes in costs of production:

  • Wage changes, raw material prices, energy costs
  • Exchange rate changes (affecting import costs)
  • Indirect taxes or subsidies
  • Supply shocks (e.g., oil price spike, pandemic)

Long-Run Aggregate Supply (LRAS)

The LRAS curve represents the economy's productive capacity — the maximum sustainable output when all resources are fully employed.

Classical view: LRAS is vertical at the full-employment level of output (Yfe). Changes in AD only affect the price level in the long run, not real output. The economy self-corrects to full employment.

Keynesian view: LRAS has three sections — a horizontal section (spare capacity), an upward-sloping section (approaching capacity), and a vertical section (full capacity). This suggests the economy can be stuck below full employment.

Figure 2: The AD/AS model showing SRAS, LRAS (classical vertical), and the effect of an increase in AD

Shifts in LRAS

LRAS shifts right (increased productive capacity) due to:

  • Increases in quality/quantity of labour (education, training, immigration)
  • Increases in quality/quantity of capital (investment, technological progress)
  • Discovery of new natural resources
  • Institutional improvements (better governance, rule of law, property rights)
  • Enterprise and innovation

The Multiplier Effect

The multiplier effect is the process by which an initial injection into the circular flow leads to a larger final increase in national income. This occurs because one person's spending becomes another person's income, which is then partly spent again.
Multiplier (k) = 1 ÷ (1 - MPC) = 1 ÷ MPW

Where MPW (marginal propensity to withdraw) = MPS + MPT + MPM

Figure 3: The multiplier process — with MPC of 0.8, an initial £100 injection creates £500 of national income

Example Calculation

If MPC = 0.8, then the multiplier = 1 / (1 - 0.8) = 1 / 0.2 = 5. A £10 billion increase in government spending would lead to a £50 billion increase in national income (assuming no change in other withdrawals).

Factors Affecting the Size of the Multiplier

  • Higher MPC → larger multiplier (more income is respent in each round)
  • Higher marginal rates of tax → smaller multiplier (more withdrawn at each stage)
  • Higher marginal propensity to import → smaller multiplier (spending leaks abroad)
  • Higher marginal propensity to save → smaller multiplier

The Negative (Reverse) Multiplier

The multiplier also works in reverse. A withdrawal from the circular flow (e.g., a cut in government spending) leads to a multiplied fall in national income.

Exam Tip: The multiplier is central to Keynesian economics. It suggests that fiscal policy can have a powerful impact on national income. However, evaluate by considering: time lags, crowding out (government spending may displace private spending), the impact on inflation if the economy is near full capacity, and the difficulty of estimating the actual MPC.

AD/AS Analysis: Key Scenarios

Demand-Pull Inflation

AD shifts right when the economy is near full capacity → price level rises significantly with little increase in real output.

Cost-Push Inflation

SRAS shifts left due to rising costs → price level rises while real output falls (stagflation).

Economic Growth

LRAS shifts right (increased productive capacity) → potential for higher output at all price levels. If AD also increases, the economy grows with moderate inflation.

Recession

AD shifts left → real output falls, unemployment rises. In the Keynesian model, the economy may not self-correct without intervention.
