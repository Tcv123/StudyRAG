3.1.1 The Economic Problem and Economic Methodology

The Nature of Economics

Economics is the study of how scarce resources are allocated among competing, unlimited wants. It examines the choices individuals, firms, and governments make when faced with scarcity.

Economics is divided into two main branches:

  • Microeconomics — the study of individual markets, consumers, and firms (e.g., price determination, market failure)
  • Macroeconomics — the study of the economy as a whole (e.g., GDP, inflation, unemployment, economic growth)

The Economic Problem: Scarcity

Scarcity is the fundamental economic problem: human wants are infinite, but the resources available to satisfy those wants are finite. This means choices must be made about how to allocate resources.

Scarcity exists because:

  • Human wants are unlimited — people always desire more goods and services
  • Resources (factors of production) are limited in supply at any given time
  • Therefore, not all wants can be satisfied simultaneously

Because of scarcity, every society must answer three fundamental questions:

  1. What to produce? — Which goods and services should be produced with limited resources?
  2. How to produce? — What combination of resources and techniques should be used?
  3. For whom to produce? — How should output be distributed among the population?

Opportunity Cost

Opportunity cost is the next best alternative foregone when a choice is made. It is the true cost of any decision, measured in terms of what must be given up.

Key points about opportunity cost:

  • It arises directly from scarcity — if resources were unlimited, there would be no opportunity cost
  • It applies to consumers (spending choices), firms (investment decisions), and governments (spending priorities)
  • It includes both monetary and non-monetary costs (e.g., time, enjoyment)
  • The opportunity cost of attending university includes not just tuition fees but also the wages foregone from working
Exam Tip: Always identify the specific next best alternative when discussing opportunity cost. Do not list multiple alternatives — opportunity cost is the single best option that was rejected.

The Production Possibility Frontier (PPF)

The Production Possibility Frontier (PPF) shows the maximum possible output combinations of two goods or services that an economy can produce when all resources are fully and efficiently employed, given the current state of technology.
Figure 1: The Production Possibility Frontier showing efficient (A), inefficient (B), and unattainable (C) points

Key Features of the PPF

  • Points on the PPF (e.g., A) — represent productive efficiency; all resources are fully employed
  • Points inside the PPF (e.g., B) — represent inefficiency; resources are unemployed or underutilised
  • Points outside the PPF (e.g., C) — are currently unattainable with existing resources and technology
  • Concave shape — reflects the law of increasing opportunity cost; resources are not perfectly substitutable between uses

Shifts in the PPF

The PPF can shift outward (economic growth) due to:

  • Increase in quantity of factors of production (e.g., immigration, investment)
  • Improvement in quality of factors (e.g., better education, training)
  • Technological progress

An inward shift indicates a reduction in productive capacity (e.g., natural disaster, emigration, resource depletion).

Opportunity Cost and the PPF

Movement along the PPF illustrates opportunity cost — producing more of one good requires producing less of another. The concave shape shows that opportunity cost increases as more of a good is produced, because resources become less well-suited to producing additional units of that good.

Capital and Consumer Goods

  • Capital goods — goods used to produce other goods (e.g., machinery, tools). Investment in capital goods sacrifices current consumption but increases future productive capacity.
  • Consumer goods — goods purchased for direct satisfaction of wants (e.g., food, clothing, entertainment)

Economic Methodology

Positive and Normative Statements

Positive statements are objective, factual claims that can be tested and either proven or disproven with evidence. They describe "what is."

Normative statements are subjective, value-based opinions that cannot be proven or disproven. They describe "what ought to be."
Positive StatementsNormative Statements
"Unemployment in the UK is 4.2%""Unemployment is too high"
"A rise in income tax reduces disposable income""The government should raise income tax"
"Minimum wage increases reduce employment in low-skill sectors""The minimum wage should be raised to £15 per hour"
"Inflation is currently 3%""Inflation is the most important economic problem"
Exam Tip: Look for value words like "should", "ought to", "too much/little", "fair", "better" — these indicate normative statements. Positive statements can be wrong but must still be testable.

The Role of Value Judgements in Economics

Value judgements influence:

  • Which economic problems are considered important
  • Which policies governments choose to implement
  • How we define concepts like "fairness" or "efficiency"
  • The weighting given to different policy objectives (e.g., growth vs. equality)

Ceteris Paribus

Ceteris paribus means "all other things being equal." Economists use this assumption to isolate the effect of one variable while holding all others constant. This simplifies complex economic relationships for analysis.

For example, the law of demand states that a rise in price leads to a fall in quantity demanded, ceteris paribus. In reality, many factors change simultaneously, but this assumption helps build theoretical models.

Economic Systems

Different societies answer the three fundamental economic questions in different ways. Economic systems exist on a spectrum:

Free Market Economy

Free market economy — an economic system where resources are allocated entirely through the price mechanism, with no government intervention. Private individuals and firms make all decisions about what, how, and for whom to produce.

Characteristics:

  • Private ownership of all factors of production
  • Profit motive drives production decisions
  • Consumer sovereignty — consumers determine what is produced through their spending
  • Prices act as signals and incentives to allocate resources
  • Competition among firms drives efficiency and innovation

Advantages:

  • Efficient allocation of resources through the price mechanism
  • Consumer choice and variety
  • Innovation driven by profit motive
  • No bureaucratic inefficiency

Disadvantages:

  • Market failure (externalities, public goods, inequality)
  • Income and wealth inequality
  • Under-provision of merit goods, over-provision of demerit goods
  • Monopoly power may develop
  • Instability (boom and bust cycles)

Command (Planned) Economy

Command economy — an economic system where the government owns the factors of production and centrally plans all economic decisions. The state determines what, how, and for whom to produce.

Characteristics:

  • State ownership of all resources
  • Central planning authority makes allocation decisions
  • Prices set by the government, not market forces
  • Workers assigned to jobs by the state
  • Output targets set for each industry

Advantages:

  • Greater equality of income and wealth
  • Can direct resources to merit goods (health, education)
  • No unemployment (in theory)
  • Can avoid wasteful duplication
  • Can plan for long-term economic development

Disadvantages:

  • Information problem — planners cannot know all consumer preferences
  • Lack of incentives leads to inefficiency
  • No consumer choice or sovereignty
  • Bureaucratic waste and corruption
  • Surpluses and shortages common due to absence of price signals

Mixed Economy

Mixed economy — an economic system that combines elements of both free market and command economies. Both the private sector and the government play a role in resource allocation.

Characteristics:

  • Both private and public sector ownership
  • Price mechanism operates alongside government intervention
  • Government provides public goods, regulates markets, and redistributes income
  • Most modern economies are mixed (including the UK)

The degree of government intervention varies across mixed economies. The UK economy is largely market-based but with significant government involvement in healthcare (NHS), education, welfare, and regulation.

The Price Mechanism

In a free market, the price mechanism performs three key functions:

  1. Signalling function — prices communicate information to buyers and sellers. Rising prices signal that a good is becoming scarcer or more desired; falling prices signal surplus or reduced demand.
  2. Incentive function — price changes motivate economic agents to alter behaviour. High prices incentivise producers to supply more and consumers to demand less.
  3. Rationing function — prices ration scarce resources among competing uses. Those willing and able to pay the market price obtain the good; others are excluded.
Exam Tip: When evaluating economic systems, consider the trade-off between efficiency and equity. Free markets tend to be efficient but unequal; command economies aim for equality but sacrifice efficiency. This is a recurring theme throughout the specification.

Specialisation and Division of Labour

Specialisation occurs when individuals, firms, or countries concentrate on producing a limited range of goods or services.

Division of labour is a form of specialisation where the production process is broken into separate tasks, each performed by different workers.

Advantages of Specialisation/Division of Labour

  • Higher productivity — workers become skilled at repetitive tasks
  • Time saved — no switching between different tasks
  • Allows use of specialised capital equipment
  • Economies of scale become possible
  • Higher output and lower average costs

Disadvantages of Specialisation/Division of Labour

  • Worker boredom and demotivation from repetitive tasks
  • Structural unemployment if demand for specialised skills declines
  • Over-dependence on other producers/countries
  • Loss of craftsmanship and flexibility
  • Vulnerability to supply chain disruptions

Evaluation: Is There a "Best" Economic System?

When evaluating economic systems in exam essays, consider:

  • Efficiency vs. equity trade-off — no system perfectly achieves both
  • Information problems — central planners face impossible information requirements; markets use prices to convey dispersed information (Hayek's argument)
  • Government failure — intervention can create new problems (bureaucracy, corruption, unintended consequences)
  • Dynamic efficiency — free markets may be better at innovation due to profit incentive
  • Historical evidence — collapse of Soviet Union demonstrated failures of pure command; 2008 financial crisis demonstrated failures of under-regulated markets
  • Context matters — the optimal degree of government intervention depends on the specific market and economic conditions
