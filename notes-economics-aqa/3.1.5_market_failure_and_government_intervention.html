3.1.5 Market Failure and Government Intervention

What Is Market Failure?

Market failure occurs when the free market mechanism fails to allocate resources efficiently, leading to a net welfare loss. The market equilibrium does not coincide with the socially optimal outcome (where MSB = MSC).

Types of market failure include:

  • Externalities (positive and negative)
  • Public goods
  • Merit and demerit goods
  • Information failure (asymmetric and imperfect information)
  • Market power (monopoly)
  • Factor immobility
  • Income and wealth inequality

Externalities

Externalities are costs or benefits that affect a third party not directly involved in the production or consumption of a good. They represent a divergence between private costs/benefits and social costs/benefits.

MSC = MPC + External Cost
MSB = MPB + External Benefit

Negative Externalities in Production

When firms impose costs on third parties (e.g., factory pollution affecting local residents). MSC > MPC. The free market over-produces relative to the socially optimal level.

Figure 1: Negative externality in production — overproduction at Qm compared to social optimum Q*

Negative Externalities in Consumption

When consumers impose costs on third parties (e.g., smoking, alcohol, driving). MSB < MPB. Over-consumption relative to social optimum.

Positive Externalities in Production

When production creates benefits for third parties (e.g., training workers who may move to other firms). MSC < MPC. Under-production relative to social optimum.

Positive Externalities in Consumption

When consumption creates benefits for third parties (e.g., vaccination — herd immunity, education — lower crime). MSB > MPB. Under-consumption relative to social optimum.

Public Goods

Public goods have two key characteristics:
1. Non-excludable — once provided, it is impossible to prevent anyone from using the good (whether they have paid or not).
2. Non-rivalrous — one person's consumption does not reduce the amount available for others (zero marginal cost of an extra user).

Examples: national defence, street lighting, flood defence systems, lighthouses.

The Free-Rider Problem

Because public goods are non-excludable, consumers have an incentive to "free ride" — to enjoy the good without paying. If everyone free rides, no revenue is generated, and private firms will not supply the good. This is a complete market failure — the good will not be produced by the market.

Therefore, public goods must be provided by the government, funded through taxation.

Quasi-Public Goods

Many goods exhibit some but not all characteristics of public goods. For example:

  • Roads are non-excludable (usually) but can be rivalrous (congestion)
  • Parks are rivalrous at peak times but generally non-excludable
  • Technology is making some goods more excludable (e.g., toll roads, encryption)

Merit and Demerit Goods

Merit goods are goods that are under-consumed in a free market because consumers undervalue their benefits (due to information failure). They generate positive externalities. Examples: education, healthcare, vaccinations.

Demerit goods are goods that are over-consumed in a free market because consumers undervalue their costs/harms (due to information failure). They generate negative externalities. Examples: cigarettes, alcohol, gambling.

Merit and demerit goods involve two sources of market failure:

  1. Information failure — consumers do not fully understand the private benefits/costs
  2. Externalities — third-party effects are not reflected in market prices
Exam Tip: Merit goods are NOT the same as public goods. Merit goods are excludable and rivalrous — they CAN be provided by the market, but the market provides too little. Public goods will not be provided at all by the market.

Information Failure

Asymmetric information — one party in a transaction has more or better information than the other. This can lead to adverse selection and moral hazard.

Imperfect information — consumers or producers lack complete knowledge about prices, quality, or costs, leading to sub-optimal decisions.

Examples:

  • Second-hand car market (seller knows more about car quality than buyer — "lemons problem")
  • Insurance markets (insured person knows more about their risk level — adverse selection)
  • Labour markets (workers know more about their abilities than employers)
  • Healthcare (doctors know more than patients — information asymmetry)

Government Intervention

Indirect Taxes

Used to correct negative externalities by raising the price of the good, reducing quantity demanded towards the social optimum.

  • Specific tax — fixed amount per unit (e.g., £1 per pack of cigarettes). Shifts supply curve up by a constant amount.
  • Ad valorem tax — percentage of price (e.g., VAT at 20%). Supply curve pivots upward.

Advantages: Market-based, raises revenue, discourages consumption.

Disadvantages: Difficult to calculate the exact external cost, regressive (hits low-income harder), may cause black markets, inelastic demand limits effectiveness (e.g., tobacco).

Subsidies

Used to correct positive externalities by reducing the price, encouraging consumption/production towards the social optimum.

Advantages: Increases output towards Q*, lowers prices for consumers, market-based.

Disadvantages: Costly for taxpayers (opportunity cost), difficult to calculate optimal subsidy, may encourage inefficiency, may benefit producers more than consumers.

Tradeable Pollution Permits

Government sets a total cap on pollution and issues permits that firms can buy/sell. Firms that can reduce pollution cheaply sell permits; firms that find it expensive to reduce pollution buy them.

Advantages: Market-based, creates incentive to reduce pollution, total pollution controlled, flexible.

Disadvantages: Difficult to set the correct cap, risk of market manipulation, firms may pass costs to consumers, requires monitoring/enforcement.

Regulation and Legislation

Direct government controls on behaviour (e.g., emission standards, health and safety laws, planning regulations, banning substances).

Advantages: Direct, clear rules, can target specific problems.

Disadvantages: Costly to enforce, reduces flexibility, may stifle innovation, risk of regulatory capture.

Provision of Public Goods

Government directly provides goods that the market would not (national defence, street lighting). Funded through taxation.

Provision of Information

Government campaigns to correct information failure (e.g., health warnings on cigarettes, nutritional labelling, education campaigns about drug dangers).

Government Failure

Government failure occurs when government intervention in the economy leads to a net welfare loss, or when intervention fails to correct market failure and may make the situation worse.

Causes of Government Failure

  • Unintended consequences — policies create unforeseen problems (e.g., rent controls reducing housing supply)
  • Information gaps — government may lack information to set optimal tax/subsidy levels
  • Administrative costs — cost of implementing and enforcing policy may exceed benefits
  • Distortion of market signals — intervention may distort price signals and create misallocation
  • Regulatory capture — regulators may serve the interests of the industry they regulate rather than consumers
  • Political self-interest — politicians may pursue policies for electoral gain rather than efficiency
  • Moral hazard — government safety nets may encourage risky behaviour
  • Time lags — policy implementation may be too slow to address rapidly changing conditions
Exam Tip: Always evaluate government intervention by considering the possibility of government failure. A strong answer will weigh market failure against government failure. Just because a market fails does not automatically mean intervention will improve outcomes — the cure may be worse than the disease.

Evaluation Framework for Intervention Policies

When evaluating any policy, consider:

  1. Does it correct the market failure effectively?
  2. Are there unintended consequences?
  3. What is the cost to taxpayers and the opportunity cost?
  4. Is it practically enforceable?
  5. What are the distributional effects (who gains, who loses)?
  6. Are there better alternative policies?
  7. Short-run vs long-run effects?
  8. Does the scale of market failure justify the scale of intervention?
