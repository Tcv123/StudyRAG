3.2.3 Economic Performance

Economic Growth

Economic growth is an increase in the real GDP of an economy over time. It represents an increase in the output of goods and services.

Actual growth — an increase in real GDP; utilising more of existing capacity (movement towards the PPF).

Potential growth — an increase in the productive capacity of the economy (outward shift of the PPF or rightward shift of LRAS).

Short-Run Growth vs Long-Run Growth

Short-Run GrowthLong-Run Growth
Increase in actual output (real GDP)Increase in potential/trend output
Caused by increase in ADCaused by increase in LRAS (productive capacity)
Uses up spare capacityExpands capacity itself
Movement towards PPFOutward shift of PPF
May be inflationary if near full capacityNon-inflationary (capacity grows with output)

Benefits of Economic Growth

  • Higher living standards (more goods and services per person)
  • Lower unemployment (derived demand for labour increases)
  • Higher tax revenues (without raising tax rates) — enables better public services
  • Improved business confidence and investment
  • Fiscal dividend — governments can reduce debt or increase spending

Costs of Economic Growth

  • Environmental degradation (pollution, resource depletion, carbon emissions)
  • Inflation if demand outstrips supply capacity
  • Inequality — growth may not be evenly distributed
  • Current account deterioration (rising imports)
  • Opportunity cost — investment in capital goods means less current consumption
  • Stress, longer working hours, reduced leisure time

The Trade (Business) Cycle

The trade cycle (business cycle) refers to the regular fluctuations in real GDP around the long-run trend rate of growth. The economy alternates between periods of expansion and contraction.
Figure 1: The trade/business cycle showing actual GDP fluctuating around the trend rate of growth

Phases of the Trade Cycle

PhaseCharacteristics
Boom (peak)High GDP growth, low unemployment, rising inflation, positive output gap, high consumer/business confidence, current account may deteriorate (high import demand)
Recession (contraction)Two consecutive quarters of negative GDP growth, rising unemployment, falling inflation/deflation, negative output gap, falling confidence, falling investment
Slump (trough)Output at its lowest, high unemployment, possible deflation, very low confidence
Recovery (expansion)GDP starts rising, unemployment begins to fall, confidence improves, investment picks up, output gap narrows

The Output Gap

Positive output gap — actual GDP exceeds potential GDP. The economy is overheating; inflationary pressures build.

Negative output gap — actual GDP is below potential GDP. There is spare capacity; unemployment above the natural rate; deflationary pressures.

Causes of the Trade Cycle

  • Demand-side shocks — changes in consumer/business confidence, credit cycles, government policy changes
  • Supply-side shocks — oil price changes, natural disasters, pandemics
  • Financial instability — credit bubbles, banking crises
  • External shocks — global recession, trade wars
  • The accelerator-multiplier interaction — investment responds to changes in demand (accelerator), and changes in investment have multiplied effects on income

Inflation

Causes of Inflation

Demand-pull inflation — caused by excess aggregate demand when the economy is near or at full capacity. AD shifts right faster than AS can respond.

  • Excessive growth in money supply
  • Consumer/government spending boom
  • Rapid credit expansion
  • Depreciation of the exchange rate (boosting export demand)

Cost-push inflation — caused by increases in costs of production, shifting SRAS left.

  • Rising oil/energy/commodity prices
  • Rising wages (above productivity growth)
  • Depreciation of exchange rate (raising import costs)
  • Higher indirect taxes

Consequences of Inflation

  • Reduced purchasing power of money (especially hits those on fixed incomes)
  • Menu costs (firms must update prices frequently)
  • Shoe-leather costs (consumers shop around more)
  • Reduced international competitiveness (if inflation is higher than trading partners)
  • Uncertainty — discourages investment and long-term planning
  • Fiscal drag — if tax thresholds are not adjusted, people are pushed into higher tax brackets
  • Redistribution — borrowers gain (real value of debt falls), savers lose

Consequences of Deflation

  • Consumers delay spending (expecting lower future prices) — further reduces AD
  • Rising real value of debt — hurts borrowers, increases defaults
  • Falling asset prices — negative wealth effect
  • "Deflationary spiral" — falling prices → reduced profits → job cuts → lower spending → further price falls
  • Difficulty of monetary policy — nominal interest rates cannot go below zero (zero lower bound)

The Phillips Curve

The Phillips Curve shows the inverse relationship between the rate of unemployment and the rate of inflation. As unemployment falls, inflation rises (and vice versa). This suggests a trade-off between the two objectives.
Figure 2: The Short-Run Phillips Curve (SRPC) and Long-Run Phillips Curve (LRPC) at the Natural Rate of Unemployment

Short-Run Phillips Curve (SRPC)

Shows the trade-off between inflation and unemployment in the short run. Policymakers can move along the curve by adjusting AD — reducing unemployment (boosting AD) but at the cost of higher inflation.

Long-Run Phillips Curve (LRPC) — Monetarist View

The LRPC is vertical at the Natural Rate of Unemployment (NRU). In the long run, there is no trade-off between inflation and unemployment. Attempts to reduce unemployment below the NRU through demand management only lead to accelerating inflation (the expectations-augmented Phillips Curve).

The Natural Rate of Unemployment (NRU) is the rate of unemployment that exists when the labour market is in equilibrium — it consists of frictional and structural unemployment. It is the rate consistent with stable inflation (also called NAIRU — Non-Accelerating Inflation Rate of Unemployment).

Reducing the NRU

Supply-side policies can shift the LRPC to the left (reducing the NRU):

  • Education and training (reduce structural unemployment)
  • Labour market reforms (reduce frictional unemployment)
  • Reducing welfare dependency (increase job search incentives)
  • Reducing trade union power
  • Improving labour market information

Policy Conflicts and Trade-Offs

Pursuing one macroeconomic objective often conflicts with another:

ConflictExplanation
Growth vs. InflationRapid growth (rising AD) may cause demand-pull inflation
Growth vs. Current AccountGrowth increases import spending, worsening the current account
Growth vs. EnvironmentHigher output may increase pollution and resource depletion
Low Unemployment vs. InflationPhillips curve trade-off — lower unemployment may mean higher inflation
Low Inflation vs. UnemploymentDeflationary policy (raising interest rates) may increase unemployment
Growth vs. InequalityGrowth may disproportionately benefit the wealthy
Exam Tip: In essays on macroeconomic policy, always discuss potential trade-offs and conflicts. High-quality analysis will explain why a policy that achieves one objective may compromise another, and evaluate whether this trade-off is worthwhile given the current economic context.

Evaluation: Growth at What Cost?

  • Short-run demand-led growth may be unsustainable and inflationary
  • Supply-side driven growth is more sustainable but takes longer to achieve
  • Quality of growth matters — inclusive growth that reduces inequality is more desirable
  • Environmental sustainability must be considered — GDP growth that depletes natural capital may reduce future welfare
  • The composition of growth matters — growth driven by debt-fuelled consumption is riskier than growth driven by investment
