3.1.3 Production, Costs and Revenue

Production and Productivity

Production is the process of converting inputs (factors of production) into outputs (goods and services).

Productivity is a measure of efficiency — the output per unit of input in a given time period (e.g., output per worker per hour).

Short Run vs Long Run

Short run — the time period in which at least one factor of production is fixed (typically capital). Only variable factors can be adjusted.

Long run — the time period in which all factors of production are variable. Firms can change scale of production, enter or exit the industry.

Law of Diminishing Marginal Returns (Short Run)

The Law of Diminishing Marginal Returns states that as additional units of a variable factor (e.g., labour) are added to a fixed factor (e.g., capital), there comes a point at which the marginal product (extra output from one more unit of the variable factor) begins to fall.

This law only applies in the short run (when at least one factor is fixed). It explains why short-run cost curves are U-shaped.

Example: A factory with 10 machines (fixed capital). As more workers are hired:

  • Initially, each extra worker adds more output (increasing returns — workers specialise)
  • Beyond a point, extra workers add less output (diminishing returns — machines become overcrowded)
  • Eventually, extra workers could reduce total output (negative returns — workers get in each other's way)

Short-Run Costs

Fixed costs (FC) — costs that do not vary with output in the short run (e.g., rent, insurance, capital depreciation).

Variable costs (VC) — costs that vary directly with output (e.g., raw materials, energy, hourly wages).

Total cost (TC) = FC + VC

Average and Marginal Costs

Cost ConceptFormulaDescription
Average Fixed Cost (AFC)FC ÷ QFixed cost per unit; always falls as output increases (spreading overheads)
Average Variable Cost (AVC)VC ÷ QVariable cost per unit; U-shaped due to diminishing returns
Average Total Cost (AC/ATC)TC ÷ Q = AFC + AVCTotal cost per unit; U-shaped
Marginal Cost (MC)ΔTC ÷ ΔQThe additional cost of producing one more unit of output
Figure 1: Short-run cost curves — MC intersects AC and AVC at their minimum points

Key Relationships

  • MC cuts AC at the minimum point of AC — when MC < AC, AC is falling; when MC > AC, AC is rising
  • MC cuts AVC at the minimum point of AVC
  • AFC continuously falls (fixed cost spread over more units)
  • The gap between AC and AVC equals AFC (which narrows as output increases)
  • The U-shape of MC, AVC, and AC is caused by diminishing marginal returns
Exam Tip: Always draw MC cutting AC at its lowest point. A common error is drawing MC crossing AC at the wrong position. Remember: if the marginal is below the average, it pulls the average down; if above, it pulls it up.

Long-Run Costs

In the long run, all factors are variable. The firm can choose its optimal scale of production. The long-run average cost (LRAC) curve is derived from a series of short-run average cost (SRAC) curves — it is the envelope of all possible SRAC curves.

Economies of Scale

Economies of scale are the cost advantages that a firm gains as it increases its scale of production. Average costs fall as output rises in the long run.
TypeExplanationExample
TechnicalLarger firms can use more efficient, specialised machinery. The "container principle" — doubling dimensions of a container more than doubles capacityOil tankers, assembly lines
ManagerialLarger firms can employ specialist managers, improving efficiencyMarketing director, finance team
Purchasing/Bulk-buyingLarger firms can negotiate lower unit prices due to buying in volumeSupermarket chains
FinancialLarger firms can borrow at lower interest rates (lower risk to lenders)Large PLCs vs small businesses
MarketingAdvertising costs spread over more units of outputNational TV campaigns
Risk-bearingLarge firms can diversify products/markets to reduce riskConglomerates like Unilever

Diseconomies of Scale

Diseconomies of scale occur when a firm becomes too large, causing average costs to rise as output increases. This is primarily due to management and coordination problems.
  • Communication problems — information gets lost or distorted in large organisations
  • Coordination difficulties — harder to organise and manage large numbers of workers/divisions
  • Motivation/morale — workers may feel alienated in large firms, reducing productivity
  • Bureaucracy — excessive layers of management slow decision-making

Minimum Efficient Scale (MES)

Minimum Efficient Scale (MES) is the lowest level of output at which all economies of scale have been fully exploited and LRAC is at its minimum.

The MES has implications for market structure. If MES is large relative to market demand, only a few firms can operate efficiently (natural oligopoly/monopoly). If MES is small relative to market demand, many firms can coexist (competitive market).

Revenue

Total Revenue (TR) = Price × Quantity sold
Average Revenue (AR) = TR ÷ Q = Price (in single-price markets)
Marginal Revenue (MR) = the change in total revenue from selling one additional unit = ΔTR ÷ ΔQ

Revenue in Different Market Structures

Perfect Competition: The firm is a price taker. It can sell any quantity at the market price.

  • AR = MR = Price (constant, horizontal line)
  • TR increases linearly with output

Monopoly/Imperfect Competition: The firm faces a downward-sloping demand curve. To sell more, it must lower price.

  • AR (demand curve) slopes downward
  • MR lies below AR (to sell an extra unit, the firm reduces price on ALL units)
  • MR falls twice as steeply as AR (for a linear demand curve)
  • TR is maximised where MR = 0

Profit

Profit = Total Revenue – Total Cost (TR – TC)

Normal profit — the minimum profit needed to keep a firm in the industry in the long run. It is an economic cost (the opportunity cost of enterprise). At normal profit, TR = TC.

Supernormal (abnormal) profit — profit above normal profit. TR > TC. This acts as a signal for new firms to enter the market.

Subnormal profit (loss) — profit below normal profit. TR < TC. This signals firms to leave the market.

Profit Maximisation

A firm maximises profit where MC = MR (and MC is rising). At this point:

  • The additional revenue from the last unit sold equals the additional cost of producing it
  • Any unit where MR > MC adds to profit (so should be produced)
  • Any unit where MR < MC reduces profit (so should not be produced)

Shut-Down Condition

  • Short run: A firm should continue producing as long as price (AR) covers AVC. If AR < AVC, the firm should shut down (it cannot even cover its variable costs).
  • Long run: A firm should exit the industry if AR < AC (it cannot cover all costs including fixed).

Alternative Objectives of Firms

Not all firms maximise profit. Alternative objectives include:

Exam Tip: In evaluation, always consider whether profit maximisation is realistic. The principal-agent problem (managers may not share owners' objectives), imperfect information (firms may not know exact MC and MR), and stakeholder pressures may all mean firms do not profit-maximise in practice.

Returns to Scale (Long Run)

  • Increasing returns to scale — output increases by more than the proportional increase in all inputs (economies of scale exist; LRAC falling)
  • Constant returns to scale — output increases in the same proportion as inputs (LRAC constant)
  • Decreasing returns to scale — output increases by less than the proportional increase in inputs (diseconomies of scale; LRAC rising)
Exam Tip: Do NOT confuse diminishing marginal returns (short run, one factor fixed) with decreasing returns to scale (long run, all factors variable). This is a very common mistake that loses marks.
