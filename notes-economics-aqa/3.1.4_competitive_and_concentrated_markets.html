Perfect Competition

Perfect competition is a market structure with many buyers and sellers trading a homogeneous product, with perfect information, no barriers to entry or exit, and where each firm is a price taker.

Assumptions of Perfect Competition

  • Many buyers and many sellers (each too small to influence price)
  • Homogeneous (identical) products
  • Perfect knowledge/information for all market participants
  • No barriers to entry or exit
  • Firms are price takers (must accept the market price)
  • Factors of production are perfectly mobile
  • No externalities or government intervention

Short Run: Supernormal Profit

In the short run, a perfectly competitive firm can earn supernormal profit if price exceeds average cost at the profit-maximising output (MC = MR).

Figure 1: Perfectly competitive firm earning supernormal profit in the short run (P > AC at MC=MR)

Long Run: Normal Profit

In the long run, supernormal profits attract new firms (no barriers to entry). Market supply increases, pushing price down until P = AC. Firms earn only normal profit.

  • If firms make supernormal profit: new firms enter → supply shifts right → price falls → profit eliminated
  • If firms make losses: firms exit → supply shifts left → price rises → losses eliminated
  • Long-run equilibrium: P = MC = AC (minimum) = AR = MR

Efficiency in Perfect Competition

  • Allocative efficiency (P = MC) — achieved in both short and long run
  • Productive efficiency (production at minimum AC) — achieved in long run
  • X-efficiency — competitive pressure ensures no organisational slack
  • Dynamic efficiency — debatable; firms may lack supernormal profits for R&D investment

Monopoly

Pure monopoly — a market with a single seller (100% market share). In UK law, a firm is a monopoly if it has 25%+ market share (legal monopoly).

Sources of Monopoly Power (Barriers to Entry)

  • Economies of scale — natural monopoly where one firm can serve the market at lower cost than two or more
  • Legal barriers — patents, copyrights, licences, government-granted franchises
  • Brand loyalty/advertising — heavy spending creates barriers for new entrants
  • Control of essential resources — ownership of key inputs
  • Network effects — product becomes more valuable with more users (e.g., social media)
  • Predatory pricing — temporarily cutting prices below cost to drive out competitors
  • Sunk costs — large irrecoverable investments deter entry
Figure 2: Monopolist profit maximising at MC = MR, charging price Pm and earning supernormal profit

Advantages of Monopoly

  • Supernormal profits can fund R&D and innovation (dynamic efficiency argument — Schumpeter)
  • Natural monopoly: one firm can achieve lower costs than many (e.g., water supply, railways)
  • Economies of scale passed on as lower prices (if regulated)
  • Cross-subsidisation of unprofitable but socially valuable services
  • International competitiveness — large firms compete globally

Disadvantages of Monopoly

  • Higher prices and lower output compared to perfect competition (allocative inefficiency: P > MC)
  • Productive inefficiency — no competitive pressure to minimise costs
  • X-inefficiency — organisational slack due to lack of competition
  • Deadweight welfare loss (consumer surplus transferred to producer or lost entirely)
  • Inequality — profits accrue to shareholders, not consumers
  • Rent-seeking behaviour — spending resources to maintain monopoly power rather than improve products

Oligopoly

Oligopoly is a market dominated by a small number of large firms, each with significant market share. Key feature: interdependence — each firm must consider the likely reactions of rivals when making decisions.

Characteristics of Oligopoly

  • High concentration ratio (top 3-5 firms have large combined market share)
  • High barriers to entry
  • Interdependence of firms
  • Products may be differentiated (cars, smartphones) or homogeneous (oil, steel)
  • Non-price competition common (advertising, branding, quality, loyalty schemes)
  • Price rigidity (prices tend to be "sticky")

The Kinked Demand Curve Model

This model explains price rigidity in oligopolistic markets. The firm believes:

  • If it raises price, rivals will NOT follow (firm loses customers → demand is elastic above current price)
  • If it cuts price, rivals WILL follow (firm gains little → demand is inelastic below current price)

This creates a "kink" at the current price, with a discontinuity in the MR curve. Costs can change within this discontinuous range without affecting the profit-maximising price.

Game Theory and Oligopoly

Game theory analyses strategic decision-making where the outcome for each player depends on the actions of others. It is used to model oligopolistic behaviour.

The Prisoner's Dilemma — illustrates why firms may not cooperate even when cooperation would benefit them:

Firm B: Low PriceFirm B: High Price
Firm A: Low PriceA: £2m, B: £2mA: £5m, B: £1m
Firm A: High PriceA: £1m, B: £5mA: £4m, B: £4m

The dominant strategy for both firms is to set a low price (regardless of what the rival does, each firm is better off with a low price). The Nash equilibrium is (Low, Low) with £2m each — even though both would earn more (£4m each) with cooperation (High, High).

Collusion

  • Overt (formal) collusion — firms openly agree on prices, output, or market sharing (a cartel, e.g., OPEC). Illegal in the UK/EU.
  • Tacit (informal) collusion — firms coordinate behaviour without explicit agreement (e.g., price leadership, parallel pricing). Difficult to prove illegal.

Factors favouring collusion: Few firms, similar costs, homogeneous products, stable demand, high barriers to entry, infrequent orders.

Factors making collusion unstable: Many firms, differentiated products, changing costs/demand, incentive to cheat (undercut), threat of punishment/detection.

Monopolistic Competition

Monopolistic competition is a market structure with many firms selling differentiated products, with low barriers to entry and exit. Each firm has a small degree of market power (downward-sloping demand curve).

Characteristics

  • Many buyers and sellers
  • Product differentiation (branding, quality, location, service)
  • Low barriers to entry and exit
  • Some price-setting power (but limited by close substitutes)
  • Non-price competition (advertising, branding)

Short Run vs Long Run

  • Short run: Can earn supernormal profit (similar to monopoly diagram)
  • Long run: Supernormal profit attracts entry → demand curve shifts left → normal profit only (AR tangent to AC)
  • In long run: P > MC (allocative inefficiency) and not at minimum AC (productive inefficiency)

Efficiency Comparisons

Allocative efficiency — achieved when P = MC (resources allocated according to consumer preferences).
Productive efficiency — achieved when production occurs at minimum average cost.
Dynamic efficiency — achieved when firms innovate and improve products/processes over time (often requires supernormal profit to fund R&D).
X-efficiency — achieved when firms operate without organisational slack (motivated by competitive pressure).

Contestable Markets

A contestable market is one where there is freedom of entry and exit, and where sunk costs are zero or very low. The threat of potential entry disciplines existing firms to behave competitively even if the market is concentrated.

Key Conditions for Contestability

  • No or low sunk costs (costs that cannot be recovered on exit)
  • Freedom of entry and exit
  • Access to same technology as incumbents
  • No brand loyalty barriers
  • Perfect information available to potential entrants

Implications of Contestability

  • Even a monopolist may be forced to set competitive prices if threatened by "hit-and-run" entry
  • Firms may engage in limit pricing (set price just below the level that would attract entry)
  • Supernormal profits may be constrained without actual competition
  • Government policy can focus on reducing barriers to entry rather than breaking up monopolies
Exam Tip: Contestability is a crucial evaluation tool. When discussing monopoly or oligopoly, always consider whether the market is contestable. A highly concentrated market can still produce competitive outcomes if the threat of entry is credible. However, in practice, most markets have some degree of sunk costs, making perfect contestability rare.

Evaluation: Which Market Structure is "Best"?

  • No single market structure is universally optimal — it depends on the criteria used (static vs dynamic efficiency, consumer welfare, innovation)
  • Monopolies may be justified for natural monopolies (regulated) or where dynamic efficiency gains outweigh static losses
  • Perfect competition is theoretically ideal for static efficiency but may lack dynamic efficiency
  • Oligopolies may deliver both innovation (through rivalry and supernormal profits) and some degree of competitive pricing
  • Context matters: the nature of the good, technology, and regulatory environment all influence outcomes
