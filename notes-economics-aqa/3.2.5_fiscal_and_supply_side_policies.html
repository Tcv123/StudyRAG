3.2.5 Fiscal Policy and Supply-Side Policies

Fiscal Policy

Fiscal policy involves the use of government spending (G) and taxation (T) to influence aggregate demand and achieve macroeconomic objectives. It is determined by the Chancellor of the Exchequer and announced in the Budget.

Government Spending

Government spending includes:

  • Current spending — day-to-day expenditure on public services (NHS wages, teacher salaries, welfare benefits)
  • Capital spending — investment in infrastructure, buildings, equipment (roads, hospitals, schools)
  • Transfer payments — redistribution of income through benefits (pensions, jobseeker's allowance, tax credits) — not included in GDP as no output is created

Taxation

Direct taxes — levied on income and wealth (e.g., income tax, corporation tax, capital gains tax, inheritance tax). Paid directly to HMRC by the taxpayer.

Indirect taxes — levied on expenditure/spending (e.g., VAT, excise duties on fuel/alcohol/tobacco, air passenger duty). Paid by the consumer via the seller.

Principles of Taxation (Adam Smith's Canons)

  • Equity/fairness — taxes should be related to ability to pay
  • Economy — collection costs should be low relative to revenue raised
  • Convenience — taxes should be easy for taxpayers to understand and pay
  • Certainty — taxpayers should know when, where, and how much to pay

The Budget Deficit and National Debt

Budget deficit — occurs when government spending exceeds tax revenue in a given year (G > T). The government must borrow to finance the gap.

Budget surplus — occurs when tax revenue exceeds government spending (T > G).

National debt — the total accumulated stock of government borrowing over time. It is the sum of all past deficits minus any surpluses.

Cyclical vs Structural Deficit

  • Cyclical deficit — the part of the deficit caused by the economic cycle. In a recession, tax revenue falls (lower incomes/profits) and spending rises (more benefits). This reverses automatically during recovery.
  • Structural deficit — the part of the deficit that persists even at full employment. It reflects a fundamental imbalance between spending and revenue that requires policy action to address.

Significance of National Debt

  • Debt interest payments — a large national debt means significant annual interest payments, reducing funds available for public services (opportunity cost)
  • Intergenerational equity — future generations may bear the cost of repaying today's borrowing
  • Credit rating — very high debt may reduce a country's credit rating, increasing borrowing costs
  • Crowding out — government borrowing may push up interest rates, reducing private sector investment
  • However: debt is sustainable as long as the economy grows faster than the interest rate on the debt (debt-to-GDP ratio falls over time)

Automatic Stabilisers

Automatic stabilisers are elements of fiscal policy that automatically adjust to offset fluctuations in the economy without deliberate government action. They smooth the business cycle.

Examples:

  • Progressive income tax — in a boom, incomes rise and people move into higher tax brackets, reducing disposable income and dampening demand. In recession, the reverse occurs.
  • Welfare benefits — unemployment benefits increase automatically in recession (more claimants), supporting spending. In a boom, benefit spending falls.

Discretionary Fiscal Policy

Deliberate changes to tax rates or spending levels to influence AD (e.g., a stimulus package in recession, or tax rises to reduce a deficit).

The Laffer Curve

The Laffer Curve illustrates the relationship between tax rates and total tax revenue. It suggests that beyond a certain point, increasing tax rates actually reduces total revenue because it discourages work, encourages tax avoidance/evasion, and reduces economic activity.
Figure 1: The Laffer Curve — beyond the revenue-maximising rate (t*), higher tax rates reduce total revenue

Implications of the Laffer Curve

  • There is an optimal tax rate that maximises revenue — going beyond this is counterproductive
  • Used to justify tax cuts — supply-siders argue that if rates are above t*, cutting taxes will increase revenue
  • The difficulty is knowing where the economy is on the curve
  • Evidence is mixed — the revenue-maximising rate varies by country, tax type, and economic conditions
Exam Tip: The Laffer Curve is often used in evaluation of tax policy. While theoretically valid, its practical application is uncertain because the revenue-maximising rate is unknown and debated. Be critical — saying "we should cut taxes to raise revenue" requires evidence that current rates are above t*.

Supply-Side Policies

Supply-side policies are government policies designed to increase the productive capacity of the economy by improving the quality and quantity of factors of production. They shift LRAS to the right, enabling non-inflationary growth.

Evaluation of Supply-Side Policies

Advantages

  • Can achieve non-inflationary growth (expanding capacity alongside demand)
  • Address root causes of economic problems (not just symptoms)
  • Improve international competitiveness in the long run
  • Can reduce the natural rate of unemployment (shift LRPC left)
  • Benefits are long-lasting once embedded

Disadvantages/Limitations

  • Time lags — most supply-side policies take years or decades to have full effect (education takes a generation)
  • Cost — interventionist policies require significant government spending (opportunity cost)
  • Inequality — free-market policies (tax cuts, deregulation) may increase inequality
  • Uncertainty — difficult to predict whether policies will achieve intended outcomes
  • Cannot solve demand-deficiency — in a recession, supply-side policies alone will not restore demand
  • Political resistance — reforms such as welfare cuts or privatisation may be unpopular
  • Government failure — government may choose wrong industries or policies to support
Exam Tip: When comparing demand-side (fiscal/monetary) and supply-side policies, a strong answer will argue that they are complementary, not alternatives. In a recession, demand-side stimulus provides short-term support while supply-side policies build long-term capacity. The best policy mix depends on the nature of the economic problem and the time horizon.

Evaluation of Fiscal Policy

Strengths

  • Can target specific sectors or regions (unlike monetary policy)
  • Powerful multiplier effects (especially in recession with spare capacity)
  • Can address inequality through progressive taxation and targeted spending
  • Automatic stabilisers work without policy action

Weaknesses

  • Time lags — recognition, decision, and implementation lags mean fiscal policy may be poorly timed
  • Crowding out — government borrowing may raise interest rates and displace private investment
  • Political influence — spending/tax decisions may be driven by electoral considerations
  • Forecasting errors — policy based on incorrect forecasts may be destabilising
  • Irreversibility — once spending programmes are created, they are politically difficult to reverse
  • Sustainability — large deficits may become unsustainable, requiring painful future adjustments
  • Ricardian equivalence — consumers may save more if they expect future tax rises to repay government debt, offsetting the fiscal stimulus

Policy Mix

In practice, governments use a combination of monetary, fiscal, and supply-side policies. The optimal mix depends on:

  • The nature of the economic problem (demand-side vs supply-side)
  • The current state of the economy (recession vs boom)
  • The government's fiscal position (level of debt)
  • The effectiveness of monetary policy (are rates already at zero?)
  • Political constraints and objectives
  • Time horizon (short-run stabilisation vs long-run growth)
