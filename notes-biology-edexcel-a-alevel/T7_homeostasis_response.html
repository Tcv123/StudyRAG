7.1 Principles of Homeostasis

Homeostasis: The maintenance of a constant internal environment within restricted limits, despite changes in the external environment. The internal environment includes blood glucose concentration, body temperature, blood pH, water potential, and CO2 concentration. Homeostasis is essential because enzyme-controlled reactions require specific conditions (temperature, pH) to function at optimal rates. Significant deviations denature enzymes, alter membrane fluidity, or change water potential — all potentially fatal.

Negative Feedback

Negative feedback is the fundamental mechanism of homeostasis. It detects a deviation from the set point (normal value) and activates responses that oppose the change, returning the variable to its optimal level. The system operates as a continuous loop:

  1. Stimulus — a change in the internal environment away from the set point (e.g. blood glucose rises after a meal)
  2. Receptor — a sensory cell or structure that detects the change (e.g. beta cells in pancreatic islets detect elevated glucose)
  3. Coordination centre — processes the information and determines the appropriate response (e.g. hypothalamus for temperature, pancreas for glucose, medulla oblongata for heart rate)
  4. Effector — carries out the corrective response; either a muscle (contracts) or a gland (secretes a hormone)
  5. Response — counteracts the original change, restoring the variable towards the set point

Because negative feedback takes time to act and then correct, homeostatic variables oscillate around the set point rather than being held at a fixed value. The system is self-correcting: as the variable returns to normal, the stimulus diminishes and the response decreases.

Positive Feedback

In positive feedback, the response amplifies the original change, moving the variable further from the set point. This creates an escalating cascade that continues until an external event breaks the loop. Positive feedback is rare in homeostasis but critical in specific biological events:

  • Oxytocin during childbirth: Pressure of the baby's head on the cervix stimulates oxytocin release from the posterior pituitary → stronger uterine contractions → more pressure → more oxytocin. The loop breaks when the baby is delivered
  • Blood clotting cascade: Platelet activation releases chemicals that activate more platelets, amplifying the clot formation at the wound site
  • Depolarisation during an action potential: Opening of Na+ channels causes depolarisation, which opens more Na+ channels (positive feedback during the rising phase). The loop ends when Na+ channels inactivate
  • Fruit ripening: Ethylene released by ripening fruit stimulates more ethylene production in nearby fruit
Positive feedback is not a failure of homeostasis — it serves specific functions where a rapid, amplified response is needed. Always contrast it with negative feedback: negative = stability (opposes change), positive = completion of a process (amplifies change until an endpoint is reached).

7.2 The Nervous System

Nervous system: A rapid, short-lived communication system using electrical impulses transmitted along neurones. It enables rapid coordination of responses to stimuli. The nervous system is divided into: CNS (central nervous system — brain and spinal cord, processes information) and PNS (peripheral nervous system — sensory and motor neurones connecting receptors and effectors to the CNS). The PNS is further divided into the somatic (voluntary, skeletal muscle) and autonomic (involuntary, smooth muscle/glands/cardiac) nervous systems. The autonomic system comprises the sympathetic (fight-or-flight) and parasympathetic (rest-and-digest) divisions.

Key Neurone Features

  • Dendrites/dendrons: Carry impulses towards the cell body; have many branches to receive signals from multiple sources
  • Axon: Carries impulses away from cell body; can be up to 1 m long (e.g. sciatic nerve from spinal cord to foot)
  • Myelin sheath: Formed by Schwann cells wrapping around the axon in concentric layers of lipid-rich membrane. Acts as electrical insulator, forcing the impulse to "jump" between gaps (nodes of Ranvier) — saltatory conduction. Increases speed from ~2 m/s (unmyelinated) to ~120 m/s (myelinated)
  • Nodes of Ranvier: Gaps between Schwann cells (~1 mm apart) where the axon membrane is exposed and contains a high density of voltage-gated Na+ channels
  • Synaptic knob (terminal bouton): Swollen end of axon containing mitochondria (ATP for vesicle transport), synaptic vesicles (containing neurotransmitter), and voltage-gated Ca2+ channels

Resting Potential (−70 mV)

At rest, the neurone membrane is polarised at approximately −70 mV (inside negative relative to outside). This potential difference is established and maintained by:

  • Na+/K+ ATPase pump: Actively transports 3 Na+ out for every 2 K+ in (electrogenic — net export of positive charge). Uses ~40% of neural ATP
  • Potassium leak channels: The membrane is ~50x more permeable to K+ than Na+ at rest, so K+ continually leaks out down its concentration gradient, carrying positive charge out of the cell
  • Large organic anions (A−): Proteins and other negatively charged molecules trapped inside the cell cannot cross the membrane, contributing to the negative internal charge
  • Na+ channels are mostly closed at rest: Voltage-gated Na+ channels remain shut, preventing Na+ influx that would depolarise the membrane

Action Potential

An action potential is a rapid, transient reversal of the membrane potential that propagates along the axon as a nerve impulse. It follows an all-or-nothing principle — if the threshold is reached, the full action potential fires; if not, nothing happens. The strength of stimulus is coded by the frequency of action potentials, not their amplitude.

  1. Resting state: Membrane at −70 mV; Na+ channels closed; K+ leak channels open
  2. Depolarisation: A stimulus (e.g. neurotransmitter binding, pressure) causes some Na+ channels to open; Na+ floods in down its electrochemical gradient; membrane becomes less negative (depolarises)
  3. Threshold (−55 mV): If depolarisation reaches the threshold, voltage-gated Na+ channels open rapidly (positive feedback); massive Na+ influx drives membrane potential to approximately +40 mV
  4. Repolarisation: After ~1 ms, Na+ channels inactivate (distinct from closing — a separate inactivation gate blocks the channel); voltage-gated K+ channels open (with a slight delay); K+ flows out down its concentration gradient; membrane repolarises
  5. Hyperpolarisation (undershoot): K+ channels are slow to close, so excess K+ leaves; membrane briefly becomes more negative than −70 mV (typically −80 to −90 mV)
  6. Resting potential restored: K+ channels close; Na+/K+ ATPase pump re-establishes ion gradients; Na+ channel inactivation gates reset

Refractory Period

The period following an action potential during which the neurone cannot (or is less likely to) fire again:

  • Absolute refractory period (~1 ms): Na+ channels are inactivated and cannot reopen regardless of stimulus strength. No action potential is possible
  • Relative refractory period (~2–4 ms): Some Na+ channels have reset but K+ channels are still open (hyperpolarisation). A stronger-than-normal stimulus is needed to reach threshold

The refractory period is functionally important because it: (1) ensures impulses travel in one direction only (the region just depolarised is refractory), (2) ensures action potentials are discrete, separate events that cannot merge, (3) limits the maximum frequency of impulses (typically 500–1000 per second).

7.3 Synapses

Synapse: The junction between two neurones (or a neurone and an effector cell) across which a nerve impulse is transmitted. Most synapses are chemical synapses using neurotransmitter molecules that diffuse across a ~20 nm gap called the synaptic cleft. The most common excitatory neurotransmitter in the PNS is acetylcholine (ACh); synapses using ACh are called cholinergic synapses.

Cholinergic Synapse Transmission (Step by Step)

  1. Action potential arrives at the presynaptic knob (synaptic terminal)
  2. Depolarisation of the presynaptic membrane opens voltage-gated Ca2+ channels; Ca2+ floods into the synaptic knob down its concentration gradient
  3. The influx of Ca2+ causes synaptic vesicles (each containing ~10,000 ACh molecules) to move towards and fuse with the presynaptic membrane (exocytosis). This process requires the SNARE protein complex
  4. ACh is released into the synaptic cleft by exocytosis
  5. ACh molecules diffuse across the ~20 nm cleft (takes <0.5 ms) and bind to nicotinic ACh receptors (ligand-gated ion channels) on the postsynaptic membrane. Binding is complementary and specific
  6. The receptors are Na+ channels that open when ACh binds; Na+ influx causes depolarisation of the postsynaptic membrane (an excitatory postsynaptic potential, EPSP)
  7. If the EPSP reaches threshold, a new action potential is generated in the postsynaptic neurone
  8. Acetylcholinesterase (AChE) in the synaptic cleft rapidly hydrolyses ACh into choline + ethanoic acid (acetyl). This stops continuous stimulation. Choline is actively reabsorbed into the presynaptic knob and recycled to make new ACh (using acetyl-CoA from mitochondria)
Key reasons why synaptic transmission is important: (1) ensures unidirectional transmission (receptors only on postsynaptic membrane, vesicles only in presynaptic knob), (2) allows integration of signals (summation), (3) allows amplification (one vesicle releases many neurotransmitter molecules), (4) provides a point for modulation by drugs, (5) allows divergence (one neurone connects to many) and convergence (many neurones connect to one).

Summation

A single synaptic vesicle may produce an EPSP too small to reach threshold alone. Summation is the process by which multiple small EPSPs combine to reach threshold:

  • Temporal summation: Multiple action potentials arrive in rapid succession from the same presynaptic neurone, each releasing more neurotransmitter. The EPSPs add up before the previous one has decayed completely. Analogous to repeatedly tapping a drum faster and faster until the vibration builds up
  • Spatial summation: Multiple different presynaptic neurones release neurotransmitter simultaneously onto the same postsynaptic neurone. The combined EPSPs from different synapses sum together to reach threshold

Inhibitory synapses also exist: they release inhibitory neurotransmitters (e.g. GABA, glycine) that open Cl− channels or K+ channels, causing hyperpolarisation (inhibitory postsynaptic potential, IPSP). The postsynaptic neurone integrates all EPSPs and IPSPs to determine whether to fire.

A common exam question involves the secondary messenger model for peptide hormones. Remember the sequence: hormone binds receptor → G-protein activates → adenylyl cyclase converts ATP to cAMP → cAMP activates protein kinases → cascade amplification. One hormone molecule can trigger production of millions of product molecules through this cascade. This is called signal amplification.

7.5 Blood Glucose Regulation

Blood glucose homeostasis: The maintenance of blood glucose concentration at approximately 4–6 mmol/L (fasting) or up to ~8 mmol/L shortly after a meal. This is primarily controlled by the antagonistic hormones insulin (lowers glucose) and glucagon (raises glucose), both secreted by the islets of Langerhans in the pancreas. Glucose is the primary respiratory substrate for cells (especially the brain, which cannot easily use alternatives).

After a Meal (High Blood Glucose)

  1. Beta cells (beta-islets) in the islets of Langerhans detect high blood glucose. The mechanism: glucose enters beta cells via GLUT2 transporters → increased glycolysis and ATP production → ATP-sensitive K+ channels close → cell depolarises → voltage-gated Ca2+ channels open → Ca2+ triggers exocytosis of insulin-containing vesicles
  2. Beta cells secrete insulin (a peptide hormone of 51 amino acids) into the blood
  3. Insulin binds to tyrosine kinase receptors on the surface of target cells (liver, muscle, adipose tissue)
  4. Effects of insulin:
    • Stimulates insertion of GLUT4 glucose transporters into cell membranes of muscle and adipose cells, increasing glucose uptake
    • Activates enzymes for glycogenesis (glucose → glycogen) in liver and muscle cells
    • Increases rate of cellular respiration (glucose used as fuel)
    • Promotes conversion of glucose to fatty acids (lipogenesis) in adipose tissue
    • Stimulates amino acid uptake and protein synthesis
  5. Blood glucose concentration falls back to normal → less insulin secreted (negative feedback)

Between Meals / During Exercise (Low Blood Glucose)

  1. Alpha cells (alpha-islets) detect low blood glucose
  2. Alpha cells secrete glucagon (a peptide hormone of 29 amino acids) into the blood
  3. Glucagon binds to G-protein coupled receptors on liver cell (hepatocyte) membranes, activating the cAMP secondary messenger cascade
  4. Effects of glucagon (primarily on liver):
    • Glycogenolysis: glycogen → glucose (glycogen phosphorylase activated)
    • Gluconeogenesis: amino acids, glycerol, and lactate → glucose
    • Decreased glycogenesis (opposes insulin's effect)
  5. Glucose released from hepatocytes into the blood → blood glucose rises to normal → less glucagon secreted (negative feedback)

Adrenaline also raises blood glucose during stress/exercise (fight-or-flight response) by stimulating glycogenolysis and inhibiting insulin secretion. This is a complementary system to glucagon.

Remember the distinction: glycogenesis (glucose → glycogen, stimulated by insulin), glycogenolysis (glycogen → glucose, stimulated by glucagon), gluconeogenesis (non-carbohydrate sources → glucose, stimulated by glucagon). These are commonly confused in exams. Also note that glucagon acts primarily on the liver (hepatocytes), not muscle cells. Muscle glycogen is used locally and cannot release glucose into the blood.

7.6 Thermoregulation

Humans are endotherms — we generate metabolic heat internally and regulate core body temperature at approximately 37 degrees C (range 36.1–37.8 degrees C). This is optimal for enzyme function. The hypothalamus acts as the thermoregulatory centre, functioning as a biological thermostat. It receives information from:

  • Peripheral thermoreceptors in the skin (detect external temperature changes, providing early warning)
  • Central thermoreceptors in the hypothalamus itself (detect temperature of blood flowing through it, monitoring core temperature)

A very common error is to say that "blood vessels move closer to the surface" during vasodilation. Capillaries do NOT move. It is the arterioles that dilate (or constrict), controlling the volume of blood flowing through the existing superficial capillary network. Always say "more blood flows through capillaries near the skin surface" rather than "blood vessels move to the surface."

7.7 The Kidney and Osmoregulation

Osmoregulation: The control of the water potential of the blood and body fluids. If blood water potential is too low (too concentrated), cells lose water by osmosis and crenate. If too high (too dilute), cells gain water and may lyse. In mammals, osmoregulation is primarily carried out by the kidneys under hormonal control of ADH (antidiuretic hormone / vasopressin) from the posterior pituitary gland.

Kidney Functions

The kidneys have three main functions: (1) excretion of metabolic waste (urea, creatinine, excess ions), (2) osmoregulation (controlling water balance), (3) regulation of blood pH and ion concentrations. Each kidney contains ~1 million nephrons, the functional units.

ADH and the Osmoregulatory Feedback Loop

Scenario: Dehydration (blood water potential decreases / blood too concentrated)

  1. Osmoreceptors in the hypothalamus detect decreased blood water potential (these cells lose water by osmosis and shrink/change shape)
  2. Hypothalamus stimulates the posterior pituitary gland to release more ADH into the blood (ADH is actually synthesised in the hypothalamus and transported down neurosecretory neurones to the posterior pituitary for storage)
  3. ADH travels in the blood to the kidneys and binds to receptors on collecting duct and DCT cells
  4. More aquaporin-2 water channels are inserted into the apical membranes
  5. More water is reabsorbed from the collecting duct by osmosis into the hypertonic medulla, then into the vasa recta (capillaries)
  6. Small volume of concentrated (dark) urine produced
  7. Blood water potential returns to normal → osmoreceptors detect this → less ADH released (negative feedback)

Scenario: Overhydration (blood water potential increases / blood too dilute)

  1. Osmoreceptors detect increased blood water potential (cells gain water by osmosis and swell)
  2. Less ADH released from posterior pituitary
  3. Fewer aquaporins in collecting duct walls → walls less permeable to water
  4. Less water reabsorbed → large volume of dilute (pale) urine produced
  5. Blood water potential returns to normal (negative feedback)
Desert animals like the kangaroo rat have very long loops of Henle that extend deep into the medulla. This creates a steeper concentration gradient, allowing more water to be reabsorbed from the collecting duct. They can produce urine up to 5x more concentrated than their blood. Conversely, aquatic mammals (beavers) have short loops and produce dilute urine. This is a common comparative/adaptation question.

7.8 Muscle Contraction

Sliding filament theory: Muscle contraction occurs when actin (thin) filaments slide over myosin (thick) filaments, shortening the sarcomere. The filaments themselves do not shorten or change length; they increase their degree of overlap. This theory was proposed by Huxley and Hanson in 1954 and is supported by electron microscopy evidence showing changes in banding patterns during contraction.

Structure of Skeletal Muscle (Hierarchical Organisation)

Whole muscle → fascicles (bundles wrapped in connective tissue) → muscle fibres (individual cells; very large, multinucleate, formed by fusion of myoblasts during development) → myofibrils (contractile units within each fibre) → sarcomeres (repeating functional units bounded by Z lines). Key structures:

  • Sarcolemma: Plasma membrane of the muscle fibre, which extends inward as T-tubules (transverse tubules) that carry depolarisation deep into the fibre
  • Sarcoplasmic reticulum (SR): Specialised smooth ER that stores and releases Ca2+ ions; wraps around each myofibril
  • Sarcoplasm: Cytoplasm of the muscle fibre; rich in glycogen (fuel store) and myoglobin (oxygen store, red pigment)
  • Many mitochondria: Clustered between myofibrils to supply ATP for contraction

Mechanism of Contraction (Sliding Filament + Cross-Bridge Cycle)

  1. Nerve impulse arrives at the neuromuscular junction (NMJ); ACh released; depolarisation of the sarcolemma
  2. Depolarisation spreads along the sarcolemma and down the T-tubules into the interior of the muscle fibre
  3. The T-tubule depolarisation triggers the sarcoplasmic reticulum to release Ca2+ ions into the sarcoplasm (via ryanodine receptor channels)
  4. Ca2+ binds to troponin (a regulatory protein on the actin filament); this causes a conformational change in troponin
  5. The shape change in troponin moves tropomyosin (a filamentous protein wound around actin) away from the myosin-binding sites on the actin filament, exposing them
  6. Myosin heads (already energised by previous ATP hydrolysis — ATPase activity of myosin "cocks" the head) bind to the exposed sites on actin, forming actin-myosin cross-bridges
  7. Power stroke: The myosin head pivots (changes angle from ~90 degrees to ~45 degrees), pulling the actin filament towards the centre of the sarcomere. ADP + Pi are released from the myosin head during this stroke
  8. A new ATP molecule binds to the myosin head, causing it to detach from actin (cross-bridge broken)
  9. ATP is hydrolysed by myosin ATPase → myosin head re-cocked to its high-energy conformation, ready for the next cycle
  10. The cycle repeats as long as Ca2+ and ATP are available. Each cycle slides actin ~10 nm. Hundreds of myosin heads work asynchronously to produce smooth contraction

Relaxation

When nerve stimulation stops:

  1. Ca2+ is actively pumped back into the sarcoplasmic reticulum by Ca2+-ATPase (SERCA pumps) — this requires ATP
  2. Troponin returns to its original conformation; tropomyosin re-covers the myosin-binding sites on actin
  3. No further cross-bridges can form; existing ones complete their cycle and detach
  4. The muscle returns to its resting length passively (muscle fibres can only pull, not push; an antagonistic muscle actively contracts to extend the relaxed muscle)
During contraction: I bands and H zones get SHORTER (more overlap); A bands stay the SAME (myosin length unchanged); sarcomere gets shorter (Z lines move closer). This is the most commonly tested aspect of the sliding filament theory. When interpreting electron micrographs: darker regions = more protein overlap; the A band boundary never changes. Also note that ATP is needed for both contraction (power stroke energy) and relaxation (Ca2+ reuptake and cross-bridge detachment). This explains rigor mortis: after death, no ATP is produced, so cross-bridges cannot detach and muscles remain rigid.
