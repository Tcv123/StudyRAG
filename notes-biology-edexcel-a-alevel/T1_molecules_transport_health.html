1.1 Water

Water: A polar inorganic molecule (H2O) that acts as the universal solvent in biological systems. It is essential for metabolic reactions, transport, temperature regulation and as a habitat.

Molecular Structure of Water

Water is a simple molecule consisting of one oxygen atom covalently bonded to two hydrogen atoms. The bond angle is approximately 104.5 degrees. Because oxygen is significantly more electronegative than hydrogen, the shared electrons in each O–H bond are pulled towards the oxygen atom. This creates a permanent dipole: the oxygen carries a partial negative charge (δ−) and each hydrogen carries a partial positive charge (δ+).

This polarity enables water molecules to form hydrogen bonds with each other. Each water molecule can form up to four hydrogen bonds with neighbouring molecules (two through its hydrogen atoms acting as donors, two through its oxygen lone pairs acting as acceptors). Although each individual hydrogen bond is weak (approximately 1/20th the strength of a covalent bond), collectively they give water its remarkable properties.

Hypothermia treatment in hospitals relies on water's high specific heat capacity — warming intravenous fluids before infusion prevents further heat loss. Conversely, athletes use evaporative cooling (sweating) to prevent overheating, exploiting water's high latent heat of vaporisation.

1.2 Carbohydrates

Carbohydrate: An organic molecule containing carbon, hydrogen and oxygen, often in the ratio Cn(H2O)n. They function as energy sources (glucose), energy stores (starch, glycogen) and structural components (cellulose, chitin).

Monosaccharides

Monosaccharides are the simplest carbohydrates — single sugar units that cannot be hydrolysed further. They have the general formula (CH2O)n and are classified by the number of carbon atoms:

  • Trioses (3C) — e.g., glyceraldehyde, dihydroxyacetone phosphate (DHAP). These are key intermediates in glycolysis and the Calvin cycle
  • Pentoses (5C) — e.g., ribose (found in RNA, ATP, NAD, FAD, coenzyme A) and deoxyribose (found in DNA, which lacks the –OH on carbon-2)
  • Hexoses (6C) — e.g., glucose (α and β isomers), fructose, galactose. Glucose is the primary respiratory substrate in most organisms

All monosaccharides are reducing sugars — they can donate electrons to other molecules. This is the basis of the Benedict's test.

Alpha-Glucose vs Beta-Glucose

Glucose exists in two isomeric ring forms. Alpha-glucose (α) has the hydroxyl group (–OH) on carbon-1 positioned below the plane of the ring. Beta-glucose (β) has it above the ring. This seemingly small difference has enormous consequences for the polysaccharides they form: α-glucose produces helical storage molecules (starch, glycogen), while β-glucose produces straight structural chains (cellulose).

Condensation and Hydrolysis

Monosaccharides join together by condensation reactions: the –OH group of one monosaccharide reacts with the –OH of another, releasing a molecule of water (H2O) and forming a glycosidic bond. The reverse process, hydrolysis, uses water to break the glycosidic bond, catalysed by specific enzymes (e.g., maltase, lactase, sucrase). Digestion of carbohydrates is essentially hydrolysis of glycosidic bonds.

A common 6-mark question asks you to compare and contrast starch and cellulose. Remember: both are glucose polymers, but starch uses α-glucose with α-1,4 bonds (coiled) while cellulose uses β-glucose with β-1,4 bonds (straight). This structural difference accounts for their entirely different functions (storage vs structural).

1.3 Lipids

Lipid: A diverse group of hydrophobic (water-insoluble) organic molecules containing carbon, hydrogen and oxygen, with a higher H:O ratio than carbohydrates. Includes triglycerides, phospholipids, cholesterol and waxes. They are not true polymers — they do not form by condensation of repeating monomers in the same way proteins and polysaccharides do.

Triglycerides (Fats and Oils)

Triglycerides are formed from one molecule of glycerol (a 3-carbon alcohol) and three fatty acid chains, joined by ester bonds through three condensation reactions (releasing 3 H2O). They are the main long-term energy storage molecules in animals and plants.

Glycerol + 3 Fatty acids → Triglyceride + 3H2O (condensation / esterification)

Fatty acids consist of a hydrocarbon chain (typically 14–20 carbons long) with a carboxyl group (–COOH) at one end. They can be:

  • Saturated — no C=C double bonds in the hydrocarbon chain; chains are straight and pack tightly together; solid at room temperature (e.g., animal fats: butter, lard). Associated with increased LDL cholesterol and cardiovascular disease risk
  • Monounsaturated — one C=C double bond; introduces a kink in the chain; liquid at room temperature (e.g., olive oil)
  • Polyunsaturated — two or more C=C double bonds; multiple kinks prevent tight packing; oils remain liquid even at low temperatures (e.g., sunflower oil, fish oils). Omega-3 fatty acids (in oily fish) are associated with reduced cardiovascular disease risk

Why Lipids Store More Energy Than Carbohydrates

Lipids release approximately twice as much energy per gram (37 kJ g−1) compared to carbohydrates (16 kJ g−1). This is because lipids have a much higher proportion of hydrogen atoms relative to oxygen atoms — the C–H bonds provide more energy when oxidised. Additionally, lipids are stored in a anhydrous (water-free) state, making them lighter per unit of energy. This makes them ideal for long-distance migrants (birds) and organisms that need to minimise weight.

Phospholipids

Phospholipids are structurally similar to triglycerides, but with one fatty acid replaced by a phosphate group (often with an additional polar group such as choline). This gives them an amphipathic (or amphiphilic) nature:

  • Hydrophilic head — the phosphate group is polar and charged; interacts with water
  • Hydrophobic tails — the two fatty acid chains are non-polar; repelled by water

In aqueous environments, phospholipids spontaneously arrange into a bilayer with hydrophilic heads facing outward (towards water) and hydrophobic tails facing inward (away from water). This is the fundamental structural basis of all biological membranes. One tail is usually saturated (straight) and one unsaturated (kinked), affecting membrane fluidity.

Cholesterol

Cholesterol is a lipid with a distinctive four-ring hydrocarbon structure (a steroid) with a short hydrocarbon tail and a hydroxyl group. In cell membranes, cholesterol molecules sit between phospholipid tails. Cholesterol plays a dual role in regulating membrane fluidity:

  • At high temperatures: cholesterol reduces fluidity by restricting phospholipid movement and making the membrane less permeable to small water-soluble molecules
  • At low temperatures: cholesterol prevents the membrane from becoming too rigid by disrupting regular packing of fatty acid tails, acting as a "fluidity buffer"

Cholesterol is also the precursor for steroid hormones (oestrogen, testosterone, cortisol), bile salts (emulsify fats in digestion) and vitamin D.

Blood cholesterol is transported by lipoproteins. LDL (low-density lipoprotein) carries cholesterol to tissues and can deposit it in arterial walls (increasing atherosclerosis risk). HDL (high-density lipoprotein) transports cholesterol back to the liver for excretion. High LDL:HDL ratio is a risk factor for cardiovascular disease. Statins are drugs that lower LDL by inhibiting HMG-CoA reductase, the enzyme controlling cholesterol synthesis in the liver.

1.4 Proteins

Protein: A polymer of amino acids joined by peptide bonds. Proteins have enormous structural and functional diversity: enzymes, antibodies, transport proteins (haemoglobin, channel proteins), structural proteins (collagen, keratin), hormones (insulin), receptors, motor proteins (actin, myosin) and storage proteins (casein in milk).

Amino Acid Structure

All 20 amino acids share a common core structure: a central alpha carbon (α-C) bonded to four groups:

  • An amino group (–NH2) — acts as a base (can accept H+)
  • A carboxyl group (–COOH) — acts as an acid (can donate H+)
  • A hydrogen atom
  • A variable R group (side chain) — determines the properties of each amino acid (polar, non-polar, charged, aromatic, sulfur-containing)

At physiological pH (~7.4), amino acids exist as zwitterions with the amino group protonated (–NH3+) and the carboxyl group deprotonated (–COO−).

Peptide Bonds and Polypeptides

Amino acids are linked by peptide bonds formed through condensation reactions between the amino group (–NH2) of one amino acid and the carboxyl group (–COOH) of another, releasing water. The bond formed is C–N (with partial double bond character due to resonance). A chain of two amino acids is a dipeptide; many amino acids form a polypeptide. Peptide bonds are broken by hydrolysis (adding water), catalysed by protease enzymes during digestion.

For a 6-mark question on protein structure, make sure you can describe ALL four levels and name the specific bonds at each level. Remember: primary structure (gene-determined sequence) determines all higher levels — changing the primary structure can alter folding and function. Sickle cell anaemia is caused by a single amino acid substitution in the β-globin chain of haemoglobin (glutamic acid → valine at position 6). This changes a hydrophilic amino acid to a hydrophobic one, causing haemoglobin molecules to aggregate in low oxygen conditions, distorting red blood cells into a sickle shape. This demonstrates how primary structure determines function. Heterozygous carriers have sickle cell trait and partial resistance to malaria — an example of heterozygote advantage maintaining a harmful allele in populations.

1.5 Enzymes

Enzyme: A biological catalyst, usually a globular protein, that lowers the activation energy (Ea) of a specific metabolic reaction, greatly increasing the rate of reaction without being consumed or permanently altered. Enzymes typically increase reaction rates by a factor of 106 to 1012.

Enzymes work by providing an alternative reaction pathway with a lower activation energy. They do NOT change the equilibrium position or the overall free energy change (ΔG) of the reaction — they just help the reaction reach equilibrium faster.

Lock and Key Model vs Induced Fit Model

The lock and key model (Emil Fischer, 1894) proposes that the substrate has a shape exactly complementary to the enzyme's active site, like a key fitting into a lock. While this explains enzyme specificity, it is an oversimplification.

The induced fit model (Daniel Koshland, 1958) is more accurate. It proposes that the active site is not a rigid structure but changes shape slightly when the substrate binds, moulding around the substrate to form a tighter, more precise fit. This conformational change:

  • Places strain on bonds within the substrate, weakening them and lowering activation energy
  • Brings catalytic amino acid residues in the active site into optimal positions for catalysis
  • May exclude water from the active site, creating a more favourable chemical environment

Factors Affecting Enzyme Activity

  • Temperature: Increasing temperature increases kinetic energy of molecules, leading to more frequent and energetic enzyme-substrate collisions. Rate roughly doubles for every 10°C rise (Q10 ≈ 2) up to the optimum temperature. Above the optimum, hydrogen bonds and ionic bonds maintaining the tertiary structure break, the active site changes shape (denaturation), and the rate drops sharply to zero. Denaturation is usually permanent (irreversible). Human enzymes typically have optimum ~37°C; thermophilic bacteria (e.g., Thermus aquaticus) have enzymes with optima of 70–80°C
  • pH: Each enzyme has a characteristic optimum pH (e.g., pepsin pH 2, trypsin pH 8, most intracellular enzymes pH 7). Changes in pH alter the ionisation of amino acid R groups, disrupting ionic bonds and hydrogen bonds in the tertiary structure. Extreme pH causes denaturation. The active site shape changes and can no longer bind the substrate
  • Substrate concentration: At low [S], rate increases proportionally (first-order kinetics) as more substrate molecules are available to bind active sites. Eventually, all active sites are occupied (enzyme saturated), and rate reaches Vmax (zero-order kinetics). Further substrate increases have no effect unless more enzyme is added
  • Enzyme concentration: If substrate is in excess, increasing enzyme concentration increases the rate proportionally — more active sites are available for catalysis

Michaelis-Menten equation: v = Vmax × [S] / (Km + [S])

Where Km (Michaelis constant) is the substrate concentration at which the reaction rate is half Vmax. A low Km indicates high enzyme-substrate affinity (the enzyme reaches half its maximum rate at a low substrate concentration).

Drug design exploits enzyme inhibition. Penicillin is a competitive inhibitor of transpeptidase, preventing bacterial cell wall synthesis. HIV protease inhibitors block the viral protease needed to produce functional viral proteins. ACE inhibitors treat hypertension by inhibiting angiotensin-converting enzyme. Understanding Km and Vmax helps pharmacologists design effective drug dosages.

1.6 Cell Membranes

Fluid mosaic model (Singer and Nicolson, 1972): The accepted model of membrane structure describing a phospholipid bilayer with various proteins, cholesterol, and glycolipids/glycoproteins distributed throughout like tiles in a mosaic. The "fluid" aspect refers to the fact that phospholipids and some proteins can move laterally within the bilayer. The "mosaic" refers to the scattered arrangement of different protein molecules.

Factors Affecting Membrane Permeability

Temperature increases cause phospholipids to have more kinetic energy and move more, increasing membrane fluidity and permeability. At very high temperatures (above ~45°C), membrane proteins denature and phospholipids move so much that temporary gaps form, dramatically increasing permeability. Organic solvents (e.g., ethanol, acetone) dissolve lipids in the membrane, disrupting the bilayer structure and increasing permeability.

This can be investigated using beetroot discs: placing them in water at different temperatures and measuring the leakage of the red pigment betacyanin (anthocyanin) using a colorimeter. Higher temperature = more pigment leakage = higher absorbance reading.

When explaining the beetroot experiment, describe the mechanism precisely: high temperatures denature the intrinsic proteins in the tonoplast (vacuole membrane) and cell membrane, and increase phospholipid movement, creating gaps. Betacyanin leaks from the vacuole through the damaged tonoplast, then out through the damaged cell membrane into the surrounding water. This is NOT osmosis — it is increased permeability due to membrane disruption.

The Heart Structure

The heart is a muscular double pump with four chambers. The right side pumps deoxygenated blood to the lungs via the pulmonary artery (pulmonary circulation). The left side pumps oxygenated blood to the body via the aorta (systemic circulation). The left ventricle wall is approximately three times thicker than the right because it must generate much higher pressure to push blood around the entire body, while the right ventricle only needs to push blood to the nearby lungs.

Key structural features:

  • Septum — separates left and right sides, preventing mixing of oxygenated and deoxygenated blood
  • Atrioventricular (AV) valves: tricuspid (right) and bicuspid/mitral (left) — prevent backflow from ventricles into atria during ventricular systole
  • Semilunar valves — in the aorta and pulmonary artery; prevent backflow into ventricles during diastole
  • Tendinous cords (chordae tendineae) — prevent AV valves from inverting under pressure
  • Coronary arteries — branch from the aorta to supply heart muscle (cardiac muscle) with oxygen and glucose for continuous aerobic respiration

The Cardiac Cycle

The cardiac cycle is the sequence of events during one complete heartbeat, lasting approximately 0.8 seconds at rest (75 bpm):

  1. Atrial systole (0.1 s): Both atria contract simultaneously, increasing atrial pressure. Blood is pushed through the open AV valves into the ventricles (topping them up — ventricles are already ~70% full from passive filling during diastole). Semilunar valves remain closed
  2. Ventricular systole (0.3 s): Both ventricles contract from the apex upward. Ventricular pressure rises sharply, causing AV valves to snap shut (producing the first heart sound, "lub"). When ventricular pressure exceeds arterial pressure, semilunar valves open and blood is ejected into the aorta and pulmonary artery
  3. Diastole (0.4 s): All chambers relax. Ventricular pressure drops below arterial pressure; semilunar valves close (producing the second heart sound, "dub"), preventing backflow. Pressure in the atria exceeds ventricular pressure, so AV valves open and blood flows passively from the veins into the atria and then into the ventricles
In cardiac cycle pressure-time graphs, key things to identify: (1) atrial pressure rises slightly during atrial systole, (2) ventricular pressure rises steeply during ventricular systole, (3) the AV valves open when atrial pressure exceeds ventricular pressure and close when ventricular pressure exceeds atrial pressure, (4) semilunar valves open when ventricular pressure exceeds aortic/pulmonary pressure. The volume of blood in the ventricle decreases during systole and increases during diastole.

Atherosclerosis and Cardiovascular Disease (CVD)

Atherosclerosis is the progressive build-up of fatty deposits (atheromas/plaques) within the walls of arteries. The process occurs over decades:

  1. Endothelial damage — caused by high blood pressure, smoking toxins, high LDL cholesterol, or infection. Damage to the endothelial lining triggers an inflammatory response
  2. LDL accumulation — low-density lipoproteins infiltrate the damaged arterial wall and become oxidised. Macrophages engulf the oxidised LDL, becoming foam cells
  3. Plaque formation — foam cells, smooth muscle cells, cholesterol crystals and calcium deposits accumulate beneath the endothelium, forming a fibrous plaque with a lipid-rich core
  4. Narrowing of lumen — the plaque protrudes into the arterial lumen, reducing blood flow and increasing blood pressure
  5. Plaque rupture and thrombosis — if the fibrous cap ruptures, the exposed collagen activates the clotting cascade. Platelets aggregate at the site, forming a thrombus (blood clot)
  6. Consequences — if a coronary artery is blocked: myocardial infarction (heart attack) due to oxygen deprivation of cardiac muscle. If a cerebral artery is blocked: stroke. An embolus is a detached clot that can travel and block a vessel elsewhere

1.9 Gas Exchange in the Lungs

Gas exchange surface: A specialised area where respiratory gases (O2 and CO2) are exchanged between an organism and its environment by diffusion down concentration gradients. All gas exchange surfaces share common adaptations to maximise the rate of diffusion.

Fick's Law of Diffusion

Rate of diffusion ∝ (surface area × concentration difference) / thickness of exchange surface

This relationship explains why gas exchange surfaces are adapted to have a large surface area, maintain a steep concentration gradient, and have a short diffusion distance.

Adaptations of the Mammalian Lung for Efficient Gas Exchange

  • Large surface area (~70 m2) — provided by approximately 300–500 million alveoli (tiny air sacs, each 200–300 μm diameter), which collectively provide a surface area roughly the size of a tennis court
  • Thin barrier (short diffusion distance) — alveolar walls are one cell thick (squamous epithelium, ~0.2 μm); capillary walls are also one cell thick. Total diffusion distance from air to blood is only about 0.5 μm
  • Rich blood supply — each alveolus is surrounded by a dense capillary network. Blood constantly carries away O2 (maintaining low O2 concentration in blood) and delivers CO2 (maintaining high CO2 in blood). This maintains steep concentration gradients for both gases
  • Ventilation — continuous breathing replaces air in the alveoli, maintaining high O2 and low CO2 in the alveolar air space, keeping the concentration gradient steep
  • Moist surface — alveolar lining is covered by a thin film of moisture; gases dissolve before diffusing across. Surfactant (a phospholipid mixture secreted by type II pneumocytes) reduces surface tension, preventing alveoli from collapsing
  • Elastin fibres in alveolar walls allow elastic recoil during expiration

Lung Structure and Airway Hierarchy

Air passes through: trachea → bronchi (left and right) → bronchioles (smooth muscle in walls; can constrict in asthma) → terminal bronchioles → respiratory bronchioles → alveolar ducts → alveoli.

The trachea and bronchi are supported by C-shaped cartilage rings that prevent collapse during inhalation (when internal pressure drops) while allowing the oesophagus behind to expand during swallowing. Goblet cells in the airways secrete mucus that traps inhaled pathogens, dust, and particles. Ciliated epithelial cells beat in coordinated waves (mucociliary escalator) to move the mucus and trapped debris upward towards the throat, where it is swallowed.

Smoking and lung disease: Tar in cigarette smoke destroys cilia and stimulates mucus overproduction, leading to chronic bronchitis (persistent cough). Toxins cause inflammation and destruction of alveolar walls, reducing surface area — this is emphysema (part of COPD). Together, loss of surface area, thickening of diffusion barrier, and impaired ventilation severely reduce gas exchange efficiency. Carcinogens in tar can cause mutations in genes controlling cell division, leading to lung cancer.

Pulmonary Ventilation

Pulmonary ventilation rate (dm3 min−1) = tidal volume (dm3) × breathing rate (breaths min−1)

Inspiration (inhalation):

  • External intercostal muscles contract, pulling the rib cage up and out
  • Diaphragm contracts and flattens (moves downward)
  • Thoracic volume increases, intrapulmonary pressure decreases below atmospheric pressure
  • Air is drawn into the lungs down the pressure gradient (Boyle's law)
  • This is an active process (requires ATP for muscle contraction)

Expiration (exhalation) at rest:

  • External intercostal muscles and diaphragm relax
  • Elastic recoil of lungs and chest wall decreases thoracic volume
  • Intrapulmonary pressure increases above atmospheric pressure
  • Air is forced out of the lungs
  • This is largely a passive process at rest (elastic recoil, no muscle contraction needed)

Forced expiration (during exercise) involves active contraction of internal intercostal muscles (pull ribs down and in) and abdominal muscles (push diaphragm up), increasing the rate and volume of air expelled.

Always refer to Fick's law when answering questions about gas exchange efficiency. For every adaptation you describe, explicitly link it to one of the three factors: surface area, concentration gradient, or diffusion distance. Common mistake: saying "thick walls" instead of "thin walls" or confusing the role of cartilage rings (support, NOT gas exchange).

For the non-reducing sugar test, you MUST: (1) first perform Benedict's test and get a negative result (if positive, reducing sugars are present), (2) THEN hydrolyse with HCl, (3) neutralise with NaHCO3 (essential — Benedict's only works in alkaline conditions), and (4) re-test with Benedict's. If positive only after hydrolysis, the sample contains non-reducing sugars (sucrose). Common mistake: forgetting to state the neutralisation step or not testing with Benedict's first.
For quantitative analysis, use a colorimeter to measure absorbance/transmission of the colour change, then compare to a calibration curve made with known concentrations. This is more objective and accurate than judging colour by eye. Serial dilutions can be used to create the standard curve.
These biochemical tests are used routinely in clinical diagnostics and food science. Urine dipstick tests for glucose (using glucose oxidase, a modified version of the reducing sugar concept) help diagnose diabetes mellitus. The biuret test principle is used in clinical protein assays (Bradford assay, BCA assay). Food labelling regulations require accurate determination of sugar, protein, and fat content in processed foods.
