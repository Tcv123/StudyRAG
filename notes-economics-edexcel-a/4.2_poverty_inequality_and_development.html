4.2 Poverty, Inequality and Development

Measuring Development

Economic Growth vs. Development

Economic growth is an increase in real GDP over time — a quantitative measure of output.
Economic development is a broader, qualitative concept encompassing improvements in living standards, health, education, political freedom, and human well-being. Growth is necessary but not sufficient for development.

Poverty

Absolute vs. Relative Poverty

Absolute poverty — a condition where a person's income is insufficient to meet basic needs for survival (food, shelter, clothing, clean water). The World Bank defines extreme poverty as living on less than $2.15 per day (2022 PPP).

Relative poverty — a condition where a person's income falls below a certain proportion of the average/median income in their society. In the UK, this is typically defined as below 60% of median household income. Relative poverty is a measure of inequality.

Causes of Poverty

  • Low income/wages — minimum wage below living wage, part-time/gig work
  • Unemployment — structural, cyclical, frictional
  • Low human capital — poor education, lack of skills/training
  • Health problems — reduce ability to work, high healthcare costs
  • Discrimination — gender, race, disability affecting employment and wages
  • Housing costs — high rents consuming large share of income
  • Poverty trap — high effective marginal tax rates mean working more barely increases net income
  • Institutional factors — corruption, weak property rights, poor governance

Inequality

The Lorenz Curve

The Lorenz curve shows the cumulative share of income (or wealth) received by each cumulative percentage of the population, ranked from poorest to richest. The further the curve is from the line of perfect equality (45-degree line), the greater the inequality.

Lorenz curves for two countries. Country B has greater inequality (curve further from the line of equality). The Gini coefficient equals Area A / (Area A + Area B).

Gini Coefficient

Gini coefficient — a numerical measure of inequality ranging from 0 (perfect equality) to 1 (perfect inequality). It is calculated as the area between the Lorenz curve and the line of perfect equality, divided by the total area under the line of equality.
Gini coefficient = Area between line of equality and Lorenz curve / Total area under line of equality

Interpreting the Gini coefficient:

  • 0 = perfect equality (everyone has same income)
  • 1 = perfect inequality (one person has all income)
  • Typical values: Scandinavia ~0.25-0.30, UK ~0.35, USA ~0.39, South Africa ~0.63
  • Rising Gini = increasing inequality; falling Gini = decreasing inequality

Limitations of the Gini coefficient:

  • Different Lorenz curves can give the same Gini value
  • Does not show where in the distribution inequality occurs
  • Based on income data which may be inaccurate (tax evasion, informal economy)
  • Does not account for wealth inequality (which is typically much greater)
  • Pre-tax vs. post-tax income can give very different values

Causes and Consequences of Inequality

Causes of income inequality:

  • Differences in human capital (education, skills, experience)
  • Globalisation and technological change (skilled workers gain, unskilled lose)
  • Weakening of trade unions
  • Tax and benefit policy changes
  • Inheritance and wealth concentration
  • Discrimination in labour markets
  • Winner-take-all markets (celebrity, CEO pay)

Consequences of inequality:

  • Social: health problems, crime, lower social mobility, reduced social cohesion
  • Economic: lower aggregate demand (if poor have higher MPC), reduced human capital investment, political instability deterring investment
  • Some argue inequality provides incentives for effort and enterprise (though evidence suggests extreme inequality is harmful)

Other Important Factors

  • Institutional quality — rule of law, property rights, good governance, low corruption are essential preconditions for development
  • Political stability — conflict and instability deter investment and destroy human/physical capital
  • Demographics — rapid population growth can offset GDP gains; demographic dividend from falling birth rates
  • Geography and climate — landlocked countries, disease burden, natural disasters
  • Resource curse — abundance of natural resources can lead to corruption, Dutch disease, and poor diversification

Sustainability

Sustainable development — development that meets the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs. It requires balancing economic growth with environmental protection and social inclusion.

Environmental concerns of growth:

  • Climate change from carbon emissions
  • Deforestation, biodiversity loss
  • Resource depletion (fossil fuels, water, minerals)
  • Pollution (air, water, soil)
  • Environmental Kuznets Curve — suggests pollution rises with early development then falls as countries become richer (but evidence is mixed)

Policies for sustainable development:

  • Carbon taxes and emissions trading schemes
  • Investment in renewable energy
  • International agreements (Paris Agreement)
  • Sustainable agriculture and land management
  • Green technology transfer to developing countries
  • Circular economy initiatives

Comparison of development indicators by comprehensiveness. Multiple measures should be used together for a complete picture.

Exam tip: Development essays require nuanced evaluation. Consider: (1) the difference between growth and development; (2) short-run vs. long-run impacts; (3) the role of institutions and governance; (4) trade-offs between growth and sustainability; (5) whether market-oriented or interventionist approaches are more appropriate depends on the country's specific circumstances. Use real-world country examples to support your arguments.

Government Policies to Address Poverty and Inequality

Redistribution policies:

  • Progressive taxation — higher earners pay a larger proportion of income in tax
  • Transfer payments — benefits, pensions, universal credit
  • National minimum/living wage — floor on wages
  • In-kind provision — free education, NHS healthcare, social housing

Evaluation of redistribution:

  • Progressive taxes may reduce incentives to work/invest (Laffer curve argument)
  • Benefits may create dependency and poverty traps
  • Minimum wage may cause unemployment if set too high
  • Public services improve equality of access but are expensive
  • Trade-off between equality and efficiency (though extreme inequality also harms efficiency)
