1.2 Market Failure and Government Intervention

Market Failure

Market failure occurs when the free market mechanism fails to achieve allocative efficiency — where resources are not allocated to their best possible use from society's perspective. The market outcome does not maximise social welfare.
Allocative efficiency is achieved when price equals marginal cost (P = MC), meaning resources are allocated to produce the goods and services that consumers value most. At the socially optimal output, MSB = MSC.

Types of Market Failure

  • Externalities (positive and negative, in production and consumption)
  • Public goods (non-excludable, non-rival)
  • Merit and demerit goods
  • Information failure (asymmetric and imperfect information)
  • Market power (monopoly, oligopoly)
  • Factor immobility
  • Inequality

Externalities

Externalities are costs or benefits that affect third parties who are not directly involved in the production or consumption of a good or service. They are also called spillover effects or third-party effects.

Key Concepts

  • MPC (Marginal Private Cost) — the cost to the producer of producing one more unit
  • MSC (Marginal Social Cost) — MPC + external costs (MSC = MPC + MEC)
  • MPB (Marginal Private Benefit) — the benefit to the consumer of consuming one more unit
  • MSB (Marginal Social Benefit) — MPB + external benefits (MSB = MPB + MEB)

Negative Externality in Production

When production imposes costs on third parties (e.g., factory pollution affecting local residents). MSC > MPC, meaning the social cost exceeds the private cost.

Negative externality in production: MSC > MPC. The free market overproduces at Qm; the social optimum is Q* where MSC = MSB. The shaded triangle is the welfare (deadweight) loss.

Negative Externality in Consumption

When consumption imposes costs on third parties (e.g., smoking, alcohol, loud music). MSB < MPB, meaning the social benefit is less than the private benefit.

Positive Externality in Consumption

When consumption creates benefits for third parties (e.g., education, vaccination). MSB > MPB, meaning the social benefit exceeds the private benefit. The free market under-produces/under-consumes.

Positive externality in consumption: MSB > MPB. The free market under-consumes at Qm; the social optimum is Q* where MSC = MSB.

Exam tip: Always identify the type of externality carefully. For negative production externalities, MSC is above MPC. For positive consumption externalities, MSB is above MPB. Draw the welfare loss triangle between the market and socially optimal output levels.

Public Goods

Public goods have two characteristics: non-excludability (impossible to prevent non-payers from consuming) and non-rivalry (one person's consumption does not reduce availability for others).

The free rider problem: Because public goods are non-excludable, consumers have no incentive to pay — they can enjoy the good without paying. This means private firms cannot charge for them, so the market fails to provide them. The government must provide public goods financed through taxation.

Quasi-public goods possess some but not all characteristics of pure public goods. They may be partially excludable or partially rival (e.g., roads that can become congested, parks with entry fees).

Merit and Demerit Goods

Merit goods are goods that are under-consumed in a free market because consumers undervalue the private benefits (due to imperfect information) and/or because they generate positive externalities. Examples: education, healthcare, vaccinations.
Demerit goods are goods that are over-consumed in a free market because consumers overvalue the private benefits (due to imperfect information) and/or because they generate negative externalities. Examples: cigarettes, alcohol, gambling.

Merit/demerit goods involve two sources of market failure:

  1. Information failure — consumers do not fully appreciate the benefits/harms
  2. Externalities — third-party effects not reflected in market price

Information Failure

Information failure occurs when economic agents (consumers, producers, government) do not have perfect knowledge, leading to a misallocation of resources.

Types of information failure:

  • Asymmetric information — one party in a transaction has more/better information than the other (e.g., used car market, insurance)
  • Imperfect information — consumers/producers lack full knowledge about costs, benefits, or quality

Consequences:

  • Adverse selection — buyers cannot distinguish quality, driving good products out (Akerlof's lemons problem)
  • Moral hazard — one party takes more risk because another bears the cost (e.g., insurance leading to reckless behaviour)
  • Under/over-consumption of merit/demerit goods

Government Intervention

Indirect Taxes

Taxes on goods/services designed to internalise negative externalities and reduce consumption.

  • Specific tax — fixed amount per unit (shifts supply curve up by parallel amount)
  • Ad valorem tax — percentage of price (supply curve pivots — larger shift at higher prices)

Advantages: Raises revenue, internalises externalities, discourages consumption, can be targeted

Disadvantages: Regressive (hits poor harder), may not reduce consumption much if demand is inelastic, difficult to set at correct level, black markets may develop

Subsidies

Government payments to producers that reduce costs and shift supply right, encouraging production/consumption of goods with positive externalities.

Advantages: Increases consumption of merit goods, reduces costs for consumers, can correct positive externalities

Disadvantages: Opportunity cost of government spending, may lead to over-production, firms may become dependent, difficult to set at correct level

Maximum Prices (Price Ceilings)

Set below the equilibrium price to make goods more affordable (e.g., rent controls, basic food price caps).

Effects: Creates excess demand (shortage), may lead to black markets, reduced quality, reduced investment/supply in the long run.

Minimum Prices (Price Floors)

Set above the equilibrium price to protect producers or discourage consumption (e.g., minimum unit pricing for alcohol, minimum wage, agricultural price supports).

Effects: Creates excess supply (surplus), may lead to government stockpiling, higher consumer prices, potential unemployment (if applied to labour).

Regulation and Legislation

  • Bans or restrictions (e.g., smoking ban in public places)
  • Pollution permits (cap and trade systems)
  • Quality standards and licensing
  • Competition law (preventing monopoly abuse)

State Provision

Government directly provides goods/services — especially public goods and merit goods (NHS, state education, national defence).

Information Provision

Government campaigns to correct information failure (e.g., health warnings, nutritional labelling, advertising bans).

Property Rights (Coase Theorem)

If property rights are clearly defined and transaction costs are low, private bargaining can resolve externalities without government intervention.

Government Failure

Government failure occurs when government intervention leads to a net welfare loss or a more inefficient allocation of resources than the free market would achieve.

Causes of government failure:

  • Unintended consequences — policies produce unexpected negative effects (e.g., rent controls reducing housing supply)
  • Administrative costs — cost of implementing policy may exceed the benefits
  • Information gaps — government may lack the information needed to set policies at the correct level
  • Political short-termism — politicians focus on re-election rather than long-term welfare
  • Regulatory capture — regulators become influenced by the industries they regulate
  • Distortion of market signals — interventions may disrupt the price mechanism
  • Moral hazard — intervention encourages risky behaviour (bank bailouts)
Exam tip: In evaluation, always consider whether government intervention might create government failure. The key question is whether the intervention leads to a more or less efficient allocation than the market would achieve without intervention. Use real-world examples to support your argument.

Cost-Benefit Analysis (CBA)

Cost-Benefit Analysis is a technique used to assess whether a project or policy should be undertaken by comparing the total social costs with the total social benefits, including externalities that are not captured by market prices.

Steps in CBA:

  1. Identify all private and external costs
  2. Identify all private and external benefits
  3. Place a monetary value on each cost and benefit
  4. Discount future costs and benefits to present values
  5. Compare total costs with total benefits — proceed if net social benefit is positive

Limitations of CBA:

  • Difficult to place monetary value on externalities (how much is clean air worth?)
  • Subjective valuations — different groups value costs/benefits differently
  • Difficult to predict future costs and benefits accurately
  • Choice of discount rate affects outcome significantly
  • May be subject to political manipulation
  • Distributional effects ignored — who gains and who loses matters
Exam tip: CBA is an excellent evaluation tool in essays. When discussing any government intervention, consider whether the social benefits exceed the social costs. Acknowledge that CBA is useful but imperfect due to the difficulty of valuing externalities.
