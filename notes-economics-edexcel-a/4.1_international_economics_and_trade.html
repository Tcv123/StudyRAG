4.1 International Economics and Trade

Theories of Trade

Absolute Advantage

Absolute advantage (Adam Smith) — a country has an absolute advantage in producing a good if it can produce it using fewer resources (or more output per unit of resource) than another country.

If each country specialises in the good where it has an absolute advantage and then trades, both countries can consume beyond their individual PPFs.

Comparative Advantage

Comparative advantage (David Ricardo) — a country has a comparative advantage in producing a good if it can produce it at a lower opportunity cost than another country. Even if one country has an absolute advantage in both goods, both can still benefit from specialisation and trade.

Example:

CountryCloth (units/hour)Wine (units/hour)Opportunity cost of 1 ClothOpportunity cost of 1 Wine
England420.5 Wine2 Cloth
Portugal661 Wine1 Cloth

England has a comparative advantage in cloth (lower opportunity cost: 0.5 Wine vs. 1 Wine). Portugal has a comparative advantage in wine (lower opportunity cost: 1 Cloth vs. 2 Cloth). Both benefit from specialising and trading at a mutually beneficial terms of trade between the two opportunity costs.

PPFs for England and Portugal. The different slopes reflect different opportunity costs. England should specialise in cloth (flatter slope for cloth), Portugal in wine.

Limitations of comparative advantage theory:

  • Assumes constant returns to scale and no transport costs
  • Assumes perfect factor mobility within countries but immobility between
  • Ignores exchange rate effects
  • Does not account for externalities or environmental costs
  • Assumes only two countries and two goods (simplified)
  • May not consider dynamic comparative advantage (developing new industries)
  • Terms of trade may not be equally favourable

Free Trade and Protectionism

Benefits of Free Trade

  • Lower prices for consumers through competition and specialisation
  • Greater choice — access to wider variety of goods
  • Economies of scale — firms access larger markets
  • Increased efficiency — competitive pressure drives innovation
  • Higher economic growth — export-led growth strategy
  • Technology transfer — FDI brings new technology and skills

Protectionism

Protectionism involves government policies that restrict international trade to protect domestic industries from foreign competition. Methods include tariffs, quotas, subsidies, and non-tariff barriers.

Types of protectionist measures:

MeasureDescriptionEffect
TariffTax on importsRaises price of imports, increases domestic production, reduces imports, generates government revenue
QuotaPhysical limit on quantity of importsRestricts supply of imports, raises domestic price, no government revenue
Domestic subsidyGovernment payment to domestic producersLowers domestic costs, increases competitiveness without directly taxing imports
Non-tariff barriersRegulations, standards, red tapeIncreases cost/difficulty of importing
EmbargoComplete ban on imports from a countryEliminates all trade with that country

Arguments for protectionism:

  • Infant industry argument — new industries need temporary protection until they achieve economies of scale
  • National security — strategic industries (defence, food, energy) should not depend on imports
  • Protect employment — prevent job losses from cheap imports
  • Prevent dumping — foreign firms selling below cost to destroy domestic competitors
  • Correct a current account deficit — reduce imports
  • Protect against unfair competition — low labour/environmental standards abroad

Arguments against protectionism:

  • Higher prices for consumers — reduced consumer surplus
  • Retaliation from trading partners — trade wars
  • Misallocation of resources — protects inefficient industries
  • Reduces incentive to innovate and improve efficiency
  • Government failure — difficulty choosing which industries to protect
  • Reduces specialisation gains from comparative advantage
Exam tip: Always evaluate protectionism arguments by considering the short-run vs. long-run effects, and whether the benefits to protected industries outweigh the costs to consumers and the wider economy. The infant industry argument is the strongest theoretically but rarely works in practice.

Trading Blocs and Economic Integration

Trading bloc — a group of countries that have agreed to reduce or eliminate trade barriers between members. Levels of integration range from free trade areas to full economic and monetary union.
TypeFeaturesExample
Free Trade AreaNo tariffs between members; each sets own external tariffNAFTA/USMCA
Customs UnionFree trade between members + common external tariff (CET)EU customs union
Single/Common MarketCustoms union + free movement of factors (labour, capital)EU Single Market
Economic and Monetary UnionSingle market + common currency and monetary policyEurozone

Trade creation vs. trade diversion:

  • Trade creation — new trade is created between member states that would not have occurred without the bloc. Welfare improving.
  • Trade diversion — trade is diverted from a more efficient non-member to a less efficient member country due to the CET. Welfare reducing.

A customs union is welfare-enhancing overall if trade creation exceeds trade diversion.

Exchange Rates

Exchange rate — the price of one currency in terms of another. It determines the cost of imports and the revenue from exports, and therefore has a major impact on international competitiveness.

Floating Exchange Rate

Determined by market forces of supply and demand in the foreign exchange market, with no government intervention.

Floating exchange rate: an increase in demand for pounds (e.g., from increased exports or capital inflows) causes the exchange rate to appreciate from ER1 to ER2.

Factors causing exchange rate changes:

  • Interest rate differentials — higher rates attract hot money (capital) inflows, increasing demand for the currency
  • Relative inflation rates — higher inflation reduces competitiveness, reducing demand for exports and the currency
  • Trade balance — surplus increases demand for currency; deficit increases supply
  • FDI flows — inward FDI increases demand for currency
  • Speculation — expected appreciation causes buying, creating self-fulfilling prophecies
  • Quantitative easing — increases money supply, tends to depreciate currency

Fixed Exchange Rate

Government/central bank sets the exchange rate and intervenes to maintain it by buying/selling currency and adjusting interest rates.

Impact of Exchange Rate Changes

Appreciation (stronger currency):

  • Imports cheaper — lower inflation, increased consumer choice
  • Exports more expensive — reduced competitiveness, lower export volumes
  • Current account may worsen
  • Lower cost-push inflation from cheaper imported raw materials

Depreciation (weaker currency):

  • Exports cheaper — improved competitiveness
  • Imports more expensive — imported inflation
  • Current account may improve (Marshall-Lerner condition: |PED(X)| + |PED(M)| > 1)
  • J-curve effect — current account worsens initially before improving

Globalisation

Globalisation is the increasing integration and interdependence of the world's economies through the growth of international trade, investment, capital flows, and migration. It involves the spread of ideas, technology, and culture across borders.

Causes of globalisation:

  • Reduction in trade barriers (WTO, trade agreements)
  • Improvements in transport and communication technology
  • Growth of multinational corporations (MNCs/TNCs)
  • Liberalisation of capital markets
  • Containerisation reducing shipping costs
  • Internet and digital technology

Benefits: Lower prices, greater choice, technology transfer, economic growth, FDI and job creation, specialisation gains

Costs: Job displacement in developed countries, environmental degradation, exploitation of workers in developing countries, cultural homogenisation, increased inequality, tax avoidance by MNCs, financial contagion

Exam tip: Globalisation questions require balanced evaluation. Distinguish between short-run adjustment costs (job losses in declining industries) and long-run benefits (efficiency gains, growth). Consider who gains and who loses — gains may be spread thinly while losses are concentrated in specific industries or regions.
