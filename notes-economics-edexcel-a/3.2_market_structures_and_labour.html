Perfect Competition

Perfect competition is a market structure with many buyers and sellers, homogeneous products, perfect information, no barriers to entry or exit, and no single firm can influence the market price. Each firm is a price taker.

Assumptions:

  • Many small firms, each with negligible market share
  • Identical (homogeneous) products
  • Perfect knowledge for all buyers and sellers
  • Freedom of entry and exit (no barriers)
  • Firms are price takers (perfectly elastic demand curve)
  • No externalities, no transport costs

Perfect competition long-run equilibrium: P = MC = minimum ATC. The firm earns only normal profit. This achieves both allocative efficiency (P = MC) and productive efficiency (minimum ATC).

Short-run: Firms can earn supernormal profit (P > ATC) or make losses (P < ATC).

Long-run adjustment: Supernormal profits attract new entrants, increasing supply, driving price down to P = min ATC. Losses cause firms to exit, reducing supply, driving price up. In long-run equilibrium, only normal profit is earned.

Monopoly

Pure monopoly — a single firm is the sole supplier in the market. In practice, a firm with 25%+ market share can be considered to have monopoly power (under UK competition law).

Sources of monopoly power (barriers to entry):

  • Economies of scale — natural monopoly: one firm can supply at lower cost than two or more
  • Legal barriers — patents, copyrights, licences, regulation
  • Brand loyalty and advertising — huge marketing budgets deter entrants
  • Control of essential resources — exclusive access to raw materials
  • Predatory pricing — temporarily setting price below cost to drive out competitors
  • High sunk costs — costs that cannot be recovered on exit

Monopoly: profit maximisation at MC = MR. Price (Pm) exceeds ATC, generating supernormal profit. Output is lower and price higher than under perfect competition, creating a deadweight loss (DWL).

Oligopoly

Oligopoly is a market structure dominated by a few large firms, each with significant market share. The key feature is interdependence — each firm's actions affect and are influenced by rivals' actions.

Key features:

  • High concentration ratio (e.g., 5-firm concentration ratio > 50%)
  • Interdependence — firms consider rivals' likely reactions
  • High barriers to entry
  • Non-price competition common (advertising, branding, quality)
  • Price rigidity — prices tend to be stable (kinked demand curve theory)

Game Theory and the Prisoner's Dilemma

Firms face a strategic choice: cooperate (collude to keep prices high) or compete (cut prices). The prisoner's dilemma shows why firms may end up competing even though collusion would maximise joint profits — each firm has an incentive to cheat on any agreement.

Collusion

  • Overt (formal) collusion — explicit agreements on prices/output (cartels like OPEC). Illegal in the UK/EU
  • Tacit (informal) collusion — firms follow a price leader without formal agreement. Difficult to prove/prosecute

Factors favouring collusion: Few firms, similar costs, high barriers, stable demand, lack of regulation

Factors against collusion: Many firms, different costs, easy entry, unstable demand, strong regulation

Price Discrimination

Price discrimination occurs when a firm charges different prices to different consumers for the same product, where the price difference is not justified by cost differences.

Conditions for price discrimination: Market power, ability to segment markets, ability to prevent resale, different PED in each segment.

Monopolistic Competition

Many firms, differentiated products, low barriers to entry. Each firm has a small degree of market power due to differentiation. In the long run, entry of new firms competes away supernormal profits, leaving firms earning normal profit but not at minimum ATC (productive inefficiency) and with P > MC (allocative inefficiency).

The Labour Market

Demand for Labour

Demand for labour is a derived demand — firms demand workers not for their own sake but because they produce goods/services that generate revenue. The demand for labour depends on the demand for the final product.
Marginal Revenue Product (MRP) = Marginal Physical Product (MPP) x Marginal Revenue (MR). The MRP curve is the firm's demand curve for labour.

A profit-maximising firm will hire workers up to the point where MRP = wage rate (MC of labour).

Labour market equilibrium in a competitive market. The firm hires L* workers where MRP = W (the wage rate). The MRP curve slopes downward due to diminishing marginal returns.

Factors shifting labour demand:

  • Changes in demand for the final product
  • Changes in productivity (technology, training)
  • Changes in the price of substitute/complementary factors
  • Changes in the price of the final product

Supply of Labour

Factors affecting labour supply to an occupation:

  • Wage rate (higher wages attract more workers)
  • Non-monetary factors (job satisfaction, working conditions, status)
  • Qualifications and training requirements (barriers to entry)
  • Net migration
  • Population and demographics
  • Benefits and tax system (affects participation rates)

Wage Determination

In a competitive labour market, wages are determined by the interaction of labour demand (MRP) and labour supply. Wage differentials exist due to:

  • Differences in skills, qualifications, and human capital
  • Compensating wage differentials (dangerous or unpleasant work)
  • Differences in MRP across occupations
  • Barriers to entry (professional qualifications)
  • Trade union power
  • Discrimination
  • Monopsony power

Monopsony in the Labour Market

Monopsony — a market with a single (or dominant) buyer of labour. The monopsonist can set wages below the competitive equilibrium because workers have limited alternative employers. The marginal cost of labour (MCL) exceeds the average cost (wage) because hiring an additional worker requires raising wages for all workers.

A monopsonist hires where MCL = MRP, but pays the wage on the supply curve (lower). This results in lower employment and lower wages than in a competitive market.

Trade Unions

Trade unions are organisations of workers that collectively bargain with employers over wages, working conditions, and other terms of employment.

Effects of trade unions:

  • Can raise wages above the competitive equilibrium (but may reduce employment)
  • Improve working conditions and job security
  • Counter monopsony power — bilateral monopoly can achieve a more competitive outcome
  • May reduce wage inequality within firms
  • Can cause wage-push inflation if wage rises exceed productivity growth

Discrimination in the Labour Market

Workers may be paid differently based on characteristics unrelated to productivity (gender, race, age, disability). This leads to wage gaps and is both allocatively inefficient and inequitable.

Types: Direct discrimination, indirect discrimination, statistical discrimination (using group averages)

Solutions: Equal pay legislation, anti-discrimination laws, transparency reporting, positive action

Exam tip: When comparing market structures, always focus on price, output, efficiency, and profit outcomes. Use diagrams to support your analysis. For labour market questions, clearly show the MRP = W condition and discuss the impact of imperfections (monopsony, unions) on the competitive outcome.
