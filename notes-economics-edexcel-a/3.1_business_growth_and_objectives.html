3.1 Business Growth and Objectives

Revenue

Total Revenue (TR) = Price × Quantity sold
Average Revenue (AR) = TR / Q = Price (the demand curve is the AR curve)
Marginal Revenue (MR) = Change in TR from selling one additional unit = ΔTR / ΔQ

For a price-taking firm (perfect competition), AR = MR = Price (horizontal demand curve).

For a price-making firm (monopoly, oligopoly), the demand curve slopes downward, so MR falls faster than AR. The MR curve lies below the AR curve and has twice the gradient.

AR and MR curves for a firm with a downward-sloping demand curve. MR has twice the gradient of AR and intersects the quantity axis at half the AR intercept.

Relationship between TR and PED:

  • When demand is elastic (|PED| > 1): MR is positive, TR increases as output rises
  • When demand is unit elastic (|PED| = 1): MR = 0, TR is maximised
  • When demand is inelastic (|PED| < 1): MR is negative, TR falls as output rises

Costs of Production

Short-Run Costs

Fixed Costs (FC) — do not vary with output in the short run (rent, insurance, salaries of permanent staff)
Variable Costs (VC) — vary directly with output (raw materials, energy, piece-rate wages)
Total Cost (TC) = FC + VC
Average Total Cost (ATC or AC) = TC / Q
Average Fixed Cost (AFC) = FC / Q (always falls as output increases)
Average Variable Cost (AVC) = VC / Q
Marginal Cost (MC) = change in TC from producing one more unit = ΔTC / ΔQ

Short-run cost curves. MC crosses both AVC and ATC at their minimum points. The gap between ATC and AVC equals AFC (which falls continuously).

Key relationships:

  • MC crosses AVC at the minimum point of AVC
  • MC crosses ATC at the minimum point of ATC (productive efficiency)
  • When MC < ATC, ATC is falling; when MC > ATC, ATC is rising
  • The U-shape of cost curves is explained by diminishing marginal returns in the short run
Law of Diminishing Marginal Returns: In the short run, as additional units of a variable factor (e.g., labour) are added to a fixed factor (e.g., capital), beyond some point, the marginal product of the variable factor will decline. This causes MC to rise and gives cost curves their U-shape.

Long-Run Costs

In the long run, all factors of production are variable. The long-run average cost (LRAC) curve is the envelope of all short-run ATC curves.

Economies and Diseconomies of Scale

Economies of scale are reductions in long-run average cost as a firm increases its scale of production. They cause the LRAC curve to slope downward.

Internal economies of scale (arise from growth of the firm itself):

TypeExplanation
TechnicalLarger firms can use more efficient, specialised machinery; larger containers carry more per unit of surface area (container principle)
Purchasing/Bulk-buyingLarge orders give bargaining power for discounts from suppliers
ManagerialLarge firms can employ specialist managers, increasing efficiency
FinancialLarge firms access cheaper finance (lower interest rates, more collateral)
MarketingMarketing costs spread over larger output; brand recognition
Risk-bearingLarge firms can diversify products/markets, reducing risk

External economies of scale — arise from growth of the industry/cluster:

  • Skilled labour pool in the area
  • Specialist suppliers and infrastructure
  • Knowledge spillovers between firms
Diseconomies of scale occur when a firm becomes too large, causing LRAC to rise. Causes include: communication difficulties, coordination problems, loss of control, worker alienation, and bureaucracy.

Minimum Efficient Scale (MES) is the lowest level of output at which LRAC is minimised. Industries with a high MES relative to market demand tend to be dominated by a few large firms (natural monopoly tendency).

Business Growth

Internal (Organic) Growth

Growth from within the firm through reinvesting profits, expanding capacity, developing new products, or entering new markets. Slower but lower risk.

External Growth (Mergers and Acquisitions)

TypeDescriptionExample
Horizontal integrationMerger with a firm at the same stage of production in the same industryTwo car manufacturers merging
Vertical integration (forward)Merger with a firm at a later stage of productionBrewery buying a pub chain
Vertical integration (backward)Merger with a firm at an earlier stage of productionSupermarket buying a farm
Conglomerate/Lateral integrationMerger with a firm in a different industryVirgin Group's diverse businesses

Motives for growth: Economies of scale, increased market power, diversification, synergies, asset stripping, managerial motives (empire building).

Constraints on growth: Funding limitations, regulation (CMA/competition authorities), diseconomies of scale, management capacity, market size.

Profit

Normal profit — the minimum profit needed to keep a firm in the industry in the long run. It is the opportunity cost of enterprise. Occurs when TR = TC (economic profit = 0).
Supernormal (abnormal) profit — profit above normal profit. Occurs when TR > TC. Acts as a signal for new firms to enter the market.
Subnormal profit (loss) — below normal profit. TR < TC. Firm should exit in the long run.
Profit = Total Revenue - Total Cost
Profit maximisation occurs where MC = MR

Profit Maximisation

Profit maximisation at MC = MR. Supernormal profit = (AR - ATC) × Q, shown by the shaded rectangle.

Exam tip: The profit-maximising output is always where MC = MR (and MC is rising). The profit per unit is (AR - ATC) at that output level. Total supernormal profit is this profit per unit multiplied by quantity — shown as the rectangle between AR and ATC at the profit-maximising output.

Alternative Business Objectives

Revenue Maximisation

Occurs where MR = 0 (total revenue is at its peak). Output is higher and price is lower than profit maximisation. Rationale: managers may prefer sales/market share growth; can be used as a predatory strategy.

Sales Maximisation (Baumol)

Firm maximises output/sales volume subject to making at least normal profit (the profit constraint: AC = AR). Output is even higher than revenue maximisation. Rationale: managerial prestige from running a large firm, market share dominance.

Satisficing

Satisficing is where firms aim for a satisfactory level of profit rather than the maximum. This satisfies all stakeholders (shareholders, managers, workers) without maximising any single objective. It is linked to the principal-agent problem — managers (agents) may pursue their own objectives rather than maximising profits for shareholders (principals).

Profit maximisation requires perfect information about costs and revenue, which firms rarely have. Always link objectives to market structure.
