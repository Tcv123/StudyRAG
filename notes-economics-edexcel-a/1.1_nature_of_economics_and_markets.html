1.1 Nature of Economics and Markets

The Nature of Economics

Economics is the study of how scarce resources are allocated among competing, unlimited wants. It examines how individuals, firms, and governments make choices about what to produce, how to produce, and for whom to produce.

Scarcity and the Economic Problem

The fundamental economic problem arises because resources (land, labour, capital, enterprise) are finite while human wants are infinite. This means societies must make choices about resource allocation, which inevitably involves trade-offs.

Opportunity cost is the next best alternative foregone when a choice is made. It is the true cost of any decision in economics and applies to individuals, firms, and governments.

Examples of opportunity cost:

  • A student choosing to study economics instead of watching TV — the opportunity cost is the enjoyment from watching TV
  • A government spending on defence rather than healthcare — the opportunity cost is the improved health outcomes
  • A firm investing in new machinery rather than paying dividends — the opportunity cost is the shareholder returns

Production Possibility Frontier (PPF)

The PPF shows the maximum combinations of two goods or services that can be produced with existing resources and technology, assuming full and efficient use of all resources.

Production Possibility Frontier showing efficient, inefficient, and unattainable points. The outward shift represents economic growth.

Key features of the PPF:

  • Points on the curve — productively efficient (all resources fully employed)
  • Points inside the curve — productively inefficient (unemployed resources or inefficiency)
  • Points outside the curve — currently unattainable given existing resources
  • Concave shape — reflects increasing opportunity cost as resources are not perfectly substitutable
  • Outward shift — represents economic growth (more resources or better technology)
Exam tip: When drawing PPF diagrams, always label both axes clearly. If the question asks about opportunity cost, show it as movement along the curve. If asked about economic growth, show an outward shift. A movement from inside to the curve represents improved efficiency, not growth.

Demand and Supply

Demand

Demand is the quantity of a good or service that consumers are willing and able to buy at a given price in a given time period.

The law of demand states that there is an inverse relationship between price and quantity demanded, ceteris paribus (all other things being equal).

Conditions of demand (shift factors):

  • Income — normal goods: demand rises as income rises; inferior goods: demand falls as income rises
  • Price of substitutes — rise in price of substitute increases demand for the good
  • Price of complements — rise in price of complement decreases demand for the good
  • Tastes and preferences — advertising, fashion trends
  • Population — larger population increases market demand
  • Expectations — expected future price rises increase current demand

Supply

Supply is the quantity of a good or service that producers are willing and able to offer for sale at a given price in a given time period.

The law of supply states that there is a positive relationship between price and quantity supplied, ceteris paribus.

Conditions of supply (shift factors):

  • Costs of production — wages, raw materials, rent
  • Technology — improved technology reduces costs, increases supply
  • Indirect taxes — shift supply left (increase costs)
  • Subsidies — shift supply right (reduce costs)
  • Number of firms — more firms increases market supply
  • Weather/natural factors — particularly for agricultural goods

Market Equilibrium

Market equilibrium where demand equals supply at price P* and quantity Q*.

At equilibrium, there is no tendency for the price to change because quantity demanded equals quantity supplied. If price is above equilibrium, there is a surplus (excess supply) which pushes price down. If price is below equilibrium, there is a shortage (excess demand) which pushes price up.

The Price Mechanism

The price mechanism (also called the market mechanism) is the interaction of demand and supply that determines market prices and allocates scarce resources in a free market economy.

The price mechanism performs three key functions:

  1. Rationing function — prices rise when goods are scarce, limiting consumption to those willing and able to pay
  2. Signalling function — prices convey information to buyers and sellers about where resources are needed (high prices signal profitable opportunities)
  3. Incentive function — price changes motivate producers to enter/exit markets and consumers to change behaviour

Consumer and Producer Surplus

Consumer surplus is the difference between the price a consumer is willing to pay and the price they actually pay. It represents the extra utility/satisfaction gained from paying less than the maximum price.
Producer surplus is the difference between the price a producer receives and the minimum price they would be willing to accept. It represents extra revenue above the minimum required to supply the good.

Consumer surplus (CS) is the area below the demand curve and above the market price. Producer surplus (PS) is the area above the supply curve and below the market price.

Exam tip: Always shade and label surplus areas clearly in diagrams. When analysing the impact of taxes, subsidies, or price controls, show how consumer and producer surplus change — this demonstrates strong evaluative skills.

Elasticity

Price Elasticity of Demand (PED)

PED measures the responsiveness of quantity demanded to a change in price.
PED = % change in quantity demanded / % change in price

PED is always negative (due to the inverse relationship) but we often refer to the absolute value.

PED ValueDescriptionRevenue Impact of Price Rise
|PED| > 1Elastic — demand responsive to priceTotal revenue falls
|PED| = 1Unit elasticTotal revenue unchanged
|PED| < 1Inelastic — demand unresponsive to priceTotal revenue rises
PED = 0Perfectly inelastic (vertical demand)Revenue rises proportionally
PED = infinityPerfectly elastic (horizontal demand)Revenue falls to zero

Determinants of PED:

  • Availability of substitutes — more substitutes = more elastic
  • Necessity vs luxury — necessities tend to be inelastic
  • Proportion of income — higher proportion = more elastic
  • Time period — demand more elastic in the long run
  • Addiction/habit — addictive goods tend to be inelastic
  • Brand loyalty — strong loyalty = more inelastic

Income Elasticity of Demand (YED)

YED measures the responsiveness of quantity demanded to a change in income.
YED = % change in quantity demanded / % change in income
YED ValueGood TypeExample
YED > 1Luxury (income elastic)Designer clothes, holidays
0 < YED < 1Necessity (income inelastic)Bread, milk, electricity
YED < 0Inferior goodValue brand foods, bus travel
Exam tip: YED is crucial for firms planning production. During economic growth, firms producing luxury goods (YED > 1) will see demand rise more than proportionally. During recession, demand for inferior goods (YED < 0) rises. This links to business strategy and the economic cycle.

Cross Elasticity of Demand (XED)

XED measures the responsiveness of quantity demanded of good A to a change in the price of good B.
XED = % change in Qd of Good A / % change in price of Good B
XED ValueRelationshipExample
XED > 0 (positive)SubstitutesCoke and Pepsi
XED < 0 (negative)ComplementsPrinters and ink cartridges
XED = 0Unrelated goodsBread and televisions

The larger the absolute value, the stronger the relationship between the two goods. This is important for firms when pricing strategies affect competitors or complementary products.

Price Elasticity of Supply (PES)

PES measures the responsiveness of quantity supplied to a change in price.
PES = % change in quantity supplied / % change in price

PES is always positive (due to the law of supply).

Determinants of PES:

  • Spare capacity — more spare capacity = more elastic supply
  • Availability of stocks — firms with stocks can respond quickly
  • Time period — supply more elastic in the long run
  • Mobility of factors — if factors easily switched = more elastic
  • Nature of the good — agricultural goods have inelastic supply in short run

Applications of Elasticity

Government Policy

  • Indirect taxes on inelastic goods — maximises tax revenue (cigarettes, petrol), but burden falls mainly on consumers
  • Subsidies for elastic goods — larger increase in quantity, better value for money
  • Minimum prices — greater surplus (excess supply) if demand is elastic

Business Decision Making

  • If demand is inelastic, firms can raise prices to increase total revenue
  • If demand is elastic, firms should lower prices to increase total revenue
  • YED helps firms predict how demand changes with the economic cycle
  • XED helps firms understand competitive dynamics and complementary pricing

Tax Incidence

The incidence of an indirect tax depends on the relative elasticities of demand and supply:

  • If demand is more inelastic than supply — consumers bear more of the tax burden
  • If supply is more inelastic than demand — producers bear more of the tax burden
Exam tip: For 25-mark essays on elasticity, always link to real-world examples and evaluate the significance. Consider time lags (elasticity changes over time), the difficulty of measuring elasticity accurately, and how firms/governments use elasticity data in decision-making.
