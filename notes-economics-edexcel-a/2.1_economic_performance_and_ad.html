2.1 Economic Performance and Aggregate Demand

Measures of Economic Performance

Gross Domestic Product (GDP)

GDP (Gross Domestic Product) is the total value of all goods and services produced within a country's borders in a given time period (usually one year). It is the primary measure of national output and economic performance.

Three methods of measuring GDP:

  • Output method — sum of the value added by all industries in the economy
  • Income method — sum of all factor incomes (wages, profits, rent, interest)
  • Expenditure method — sum of all spending on final goods and services (C + I + G + (X - M))

Real vs Nominal GDP:

  • Nominal GDP — measured at current prices (not adjusted for inflation)
  • Real GDP — adjusted for inflation using a base year, giving a true picture of output changes
Real GDP = Nominal GDP / Price Deflator x 100

GDP per capita = GDP / Population. This is a better indicator of living standards as it accounts for population size.

Limitations of GDP as a measure of living standards:

  • Does not account for income distribution (inequality)
  • Excludes the informal/shadow economy and non-marketed output (housework, volunteering)
  • Does not reflect environmental degradation or resource depletion
  • Does not capture quality of life factors (leisure time, health, happiness)
  • Purchasing power differences not reflected (use PPP-adjusted GDP for comparisons)
  • Composition of output matters — military spending vs. education spending

Inflation

Inflation is a sustained increase in the general price level of goods and services over a period of time, reducing the purchasing power of money.

Measurement:

  • CPI (Consumer Price Index) — measures change in price of a representative basket of goods and services. The UK's target measure, with a 2% target set by the government
  • RPI (Retail Price Index) — similar to CPI but includes housing costs (mortgage interest payments). Tends to be higher than CPI

Types of inflation:

TypeCauseDiagram
Demand-pullExcess aggregate demand — "too much money chasing too few goods"AD shifts right
Cost-pushRising costs of production — wages, raw materials, energySRAS shifts left

Consequences of inflation:

  • Reduced purchasing power for those on fixed incomes (pensioners)
  • Menu costs — firms must frequently update prices
  • Shoe leather costs — consumers shop around more for best prices
  • Reduced international competitiveness (exports become more expensive)
  • Uncertainty reduces investment and economic growth
  • Fiscal drag — inflation pushes earners into higher tax brackets
  • Redistribution from savers to borrowers (real value of debt falls)

Deflation is a sustained fall in the general price level. It can be harmful because consumers delay purchases expecting further falls, increasing real debt burden, and firms reduce output and employment.

Unemployment

Unemployment occurs when people who are willing and able to work at the going wage rate cannot find employment. The unemployment rate = (unemployed / labour force) x 100.

Measurement methods:

  • Claimant count — number of people claiming unemployment-related benefits. Easy to measure but excludes those not eligible for benefits
  • ILO/Labour Force Survey — internationally comparable survey-based measure. Includes those actively seeking work in the last 4 weeks

Types of unemployment:

TypeDescriptionSolution
FrictionalShort-term — between jobs, searchingBetter job information, reduced benefits
StructuralSkills mismatch — industry declineRetraining, education, relocation subsidies

Costs of unemployment:

  • Lost output — economy operates inside PPF
  • Government costs — higher benefit spending, lower tax revenue
  • Social costs — poverty, crime, mental health issues, family breakdown
  • Hysteresis — long-term unemployment erodes skills and employability

Balance of Payments

Balance of payments is a record of all financial transactions between a country and the rest of the world over a given period. It consists of the current account, capital account, and financial account.

Current account components:

  • Trade in goods — exports minus imports of physical goods
  • Trade in services — exports minus imports of services (financial, tourism, etc.)
  • Primary income — investment income flows (dividends, interest, wages)
  • Secondary income — transfers (foreign aid, EU contributions, remittances)

The UK has run a persistent current account deficit, mainly due to a large trade deficit in goods.

Causes of a current account deficit:

  • Strong domestic currency making exports expensive and imports cheap
  • High domestic income growth increasing import demand
  • Low productivity and competitiveness
  • Structural decline of manufacturing
  • Relatively high inflation compared to trading partners

Aggregate Demand

Aggregate Demand (AD) is the total demand for goods and services in an economy at a given price level in a given time period. AD = C + I + G + (X - M).

Components of AD

C — Consumption (household spending)

The largest component of AD (around 60-65% in the UK). Determinants include:

  • Disposable income (income after tax)
  • Interest rates (higher rates discourage borrowing and spending)
  • Consumer confidence and expectations
  • Wealth effects (house prices, share prices)
  • Availability of credit
  • Distribution of income

I — Investment (business spending on capital goods)

  • Interest rates (cost of borrowing)
  • Business confidence and expectations of future demand
  • Corporate tax rates
  • Technological change
  • Access to credit and finance
  • Government incentives (capital allowances)

G — Government spending

  • Fiscal policy decisions (austerity vs. expansion)
  • Political priorities and electoral cycle
  • Level of national debt
  • Automatic stabilisers (benefits increase in recession)

(X - M) — Net exports

  • Exchange rate (depreciation boosts exports, appreciation boosts imports)
  • Relative inflation rates
  • Income levels at home and abroad
  • Non-price competitiveness (quality, innovation, branding)
  • Trade policies and agreements

The AD Curve

The AD curve slopes downward. A rightward shift from AD1 to AD2 represents an increase in aggregate demand (e.g., from lower interest rates, increased government spending, or increased consumer confidence).

Why the AD curve slopes downward:

  • Wealth effect — higher prices reduce real wealth, reducing consumption
  • Interest rate effect — higher prices lead to higher interest rates, reducing investment and consumption
  • Trade effect — higher domestic prices make exports less competitive and imports relatively cheaper, reducing net exports

Circular Flow of Income

Simplified circular flow model. National income is in equilibrium when injections (I + G + X) equal withdrawals (S + T + M).

Injections (increase the flow of income):

  • Investment (I) — business spending on capital goods
  • Government spending (G) — public expenditure
  • Exports (X) — foreign spending on domestic goods

Withdrawals/Leakages (reduce the flow of income):

  • Saving (S) — income not spent by households
  • Taxation (T) — income taken by government
  • Imports (M) — domestic spending on foreign goods

The economy is in equilibrium when total injections equal total withdrawals: I + G + X = S + T + M. If injections exceed withdrawals, national income rises; if withdrawals exceed injections, national income falls.

Exam tip: When discussing AD shifts, always specify which component is changing and explain the transmission mechanism. For example: "Lower interest rates reduce the cost of borrowing, encouraging consumption (C) and investment (I), shifting AD to the right." This shows clear chain of reasoning that examiners reward.

The Economic Cycle

The economic (business/trade) cycle refers to fluctuations in real GDP growth over time, consisting of four phases: boom, slowdown/recession, trough/slump, and recovery/expansion.

Phases and characteristics:

PhaseGDP GrowthUnemploymentInflationConfidence
BoomHigh/above trendFalling/lowRisingHigh
SlowdownFallingRisingFallingDeclining
RecessionNegative (2+ quarters)High/risingLow/fallingLow
RecoveryRising/positiveFallingStarting to riseImproving

Output gap: The difference between actual GDP and potential (trend) GDP.

  • Positive output gap — actual GDP exceeds potential GDP, associated with inflationary pressure
  • Negative output gap — actual GDP below potential GDP, associated with unemployment and spare capacity
Exam tip: Link the four macroeconomic objectives (growth, low inflation, low unemployment, healthy balance of payments) to the economic cycle. Explain why trade-offs exist — for example, during a boom, growth and employment are strong but inflation and the current account deficit may worsen. This shows sophisticated understanding of macroeconomic interconnections.
