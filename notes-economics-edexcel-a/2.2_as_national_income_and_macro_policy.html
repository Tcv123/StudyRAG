2.2 Aggregate Supply, National Income and Macroeconomic Policy

Aggregate Supply

Short-Run Aggregate Supply (SRAS)

SRAS shows the total output that firms are willing and able to supply at each price level in the short run, when at least one factor of production (typically capital) is fixed. It slopes upward because higher prices incentivise greater output while some costs (wages, contracts) are sticky.

Shifts of the SRAS curve (changes in costs of production):

  • Changes in wage rates
  • Changes in raw material/energy costs (e.g., oil price shocks)
  • Changes in indirect taxes or subsidies
  • Exchange rate changes (affecting import costs)
  • Changes in productivity

Long-Run Aggregate Supply (LRAS)

LRAS shows the maximum potential output of the economy when all factors of production are fully employed. In the classical model, LRAS is vertical at the full employment level of output (Yfe), meaning changes in the price level do not affect long-run output.

Shifts of LRAS (changes in productive capacity):

  • Technological innovation
  • Increase in quantity/quality of labour (immigration, education)
  • Increase in capital stock (investment)
  • Discovery of new resources
  • Institutional improvements (property rights, deregulation)
  • Entrepreneurship and competition

AD/AS model with SRAS and classical (vertical) LRAS. The LRAS is at full employment output (Yfe). Short-run equilibrium is where AD intersects SRAS.

Keynesian LRAS

The Keynesian view differs from the classical model. The Keynesian LRAS has three sections:

  • Horizontal section — at low output, spare capacity exists. Increased AD raises output without inflation
  • Upward-sloping section — as economy approaches full capacity, bottlenecks cause some inflation alongside output growth
  • Vertical section — at full capacity, no further output increase possible; increased AD only causes inflation

Keynesian LRAS curve showing three distinct regions: spare capacity (horizontal), approaching full employment (upward sloping), and full capacity (vertical).

Exam tip: Know both the classical and Keynesian LRAS models. In evaluation, discuss which model is more appropriate given current economic conditions. If there is significant spare capacity (negative output gap), the Keynesian model suggests fiscal policy can boost output without inflation.

National Income Determination

The Multiplier

The multiplier effect is the process by which an initial injection of spending leads to a larger final increase in national income. This occurs because one person's spending becomes another's income, which is then partly re-spent, creating successive rounds of spending.
Multiplier (k) = 1 / (1 - MPC) = 1 / MPW
Where MPW = MPS + MPT + MPM (marginal propensity to withdraw)

MPC = Marginal Propensity to Consume (proportion of additional income that is spent)

MPS = Marginal Propensity to Save

MPT = Marginal Propensity to Tax

MPM = Marginal Propensity to Import

Example: If MPC = 0.8, then MPW = 0.2, and the multiplier = 1/0.2 = 5. An initial injection of £10bn leads to a final increase in national income of £50bn.

The multiplier process: successive rounds of spending with MPC = 0.8. Each round, 80% is re-spent and 20% leaks out.

Factors affecting the size of the multiplier:

  • Higher MPC (lower savings rate) = larger multiplier
  • Higher tax rates reduce the multiplier (more leaks to taxation)
  • Higher propensity to import reduces the multiplier (spending leaks abroad)
  • Spare capacity — multiplier is larger when there is slack in the economy
  • Consumer and business confidence affect spending patterns

The negative multiplier: Works in reverse — a withdrawal (e.g., government spending cut) leads to a larger final fall in national income.

Macroeconomic Policy

Fiscal Policy

Fiscal policy involves the use of government spending and taxation to influence aggregate demand, output, and employment. It is set by the government (Chancellor/Treasury).

Expansionary fiscal policy (to boost AD during recession):

  • Increase government spending (G rises, AD shifts right)
  • Cut income tax (increases disposable income, C rises)
  • Cut corporation tax (increases post-tax profits, I rises)

Contractionary fiscal policy (to reduce AD during boom):

  • Cut government spending
  • Raise taxes

Automatic stabilisers: Tax receipts and benefit spending automatically adjust with the economic cycle without deliberate policy change. In recession, tax revenue falls and benefit spending rises, partly offsetting the fall in AD.

Monetary Policy

Monetary policy involves the use of interest rates, money supply, and other monetary tools to influence aggregate demand, inflation, and economic activity. In the UK, it is set by the Bank of England's Monetary Policy Committee (MPC), independent of government since 1997.

The Bank of England's primary objective: Maintain price stability (CPI inflation target of 2%), subject to which it supports the government's economic objectives of growth and employment.

Transmission mechanism of interest rate changes:

  1. Bank Rate is changed by the MPC
  2. Commercial banks adjust lending/saving rates
  3. This affects: cost of borrowing, return on savings, mortgage payments, exchange rate
  4. Which in turn affects: consumption, investment, net exports
  5. Leading to changes in: aggregate demand, output, employment, inflation

Quantitative Easing (QE): When interest rates are at or near zero (lower bound), the Bank of England creates new money to buy government bonds from financial institutions. This increases bank reserves, lowers long-term interest rates, and encourages lending and spending.

Advantages of Monetary PolicyDisadvantages of Monetary Policy
Independent from political interferenceTime lags (18-24 months for full effect)
Can be changed quickly (monthly MPC meetings)Blunt instrument — affects whole economy equally
Credibility reduces inflation expectationsLiquidity trap — ineffective at very low interest rates
QE provides additional tool when rates hit zeroQE may increase asset prices/inequality

Supply-Side Policies

Supply-side policies aim to increase the productive capacity of the economy by shifting LRAS to the right. They address the underlying competitiveness and efficiency of the economy, increasing potential output.

Exam tip: In 25-mark essays on macroeconomic policy, evaluate by considering: (1) time lags — demand-side policies work faster but supply-side policies address root causes; (2) the state of the economy — different policies suit different conditions; (3) trade-offs between objectives; (4) the appropriate policy mix. Always consider whether demand-side or supply-side policies (or both) are needed.

Conflicts Between Macroeconomic Objectives

The four main macroeconomic objectives often conflict:

ConflictExplanation
Growth vs. InflationRapid growth may cause demand-pull inflation
Growth vs. Current AccountGrowth increases imports, worsening the deficit
Growth vs. EnvironmentGrowth may deplete resources and cause pollution
Low Unemployment vs. InflationPhillips curve trade-off — low unemployment may cause wage-push inflation
Low Inflation vs. GrowthTight monetary policy to control inflation may slow growth

The Phillips Curve: Suggests an inverse relationship between unemployment and inflation in the short run. However, monetarists argue this trade-off does not exist in the long run — the long-run Phillips curve is vertical at the natural rate of unemployment (NAIRU).

Exam tip: Supply-side policies are often presented as a solution to policy conflicts because they can increase output without causing inflation. However, always evaluate their limitations — long time lags, cost, uncertainty of outcomes, and potential distributional effects.
