2.2 Equations and Inequalities

OCR GCSE Mathematics (J560) — Paper 1, 2 & 3 — Foundation and Higher

2.2.1 Solving Linear Equations

Linear equation: An equation where the highest power of the unknown is 1. The goal is to find the value of the unknown that makes the equation true.

The golden rule: whatever you do to one side, do the same to the other side.

One-Step and Two-Step Equations

Solve $5x - 3 = 17$.

Add 3 to both sides: $5x = 20$
Divide both sides by 5: $x = 4$
Solve $\dfrac{2x+1}{3} = 5$.

Multiply both sides by 3: $2x + 1 = 15$
Subtract 1: $2x = 14$
Divide by 2: $x = 7$

Equations with Unknowns on Both Sides

Collect all variable terms onto one side, then all constants onto the other:

Solve $5x + 2 = 3x + 10$.

Subtract $3x$ from both sides: $2x + 2 = 10$
Subtract 2: $2x = 8$
Divide by 2: $x = 4$
Solve $4(2x - 3) = 6x + 2$.

Expand: $8x - 12 = 6x + 2$
Subtract $6x$: $2x - 12 = 2$
Add 12: $2x = 14$
Divide by 2: $x = 7$
Solve $\dfrac{x+3}{4} = \dfrac{2x-1}{3}$.

Multiply through by 12 (LCM of 4 and 3): $3(x+3) = 4(2x-1)$
Expand: $3x + 9 = 8x - 4$
Collect terms: $9 + 4 = 8x - 3x$, so $13 = 5x$
$x = \dfrac{13}{5} = 2.6$
Always check your answer by substituting back into the original equation. This takes 10 seconds and will catch arithmetic slips.

2.2.2 Forming and Solving Equations

Many real-world and geometry problems require you to write an equation before solving it.

The angles of a triangle are $3x$, $(2x+10)°$, and $(x-4)°$. Find $x$ and the three angles.

Angles in a triangle sum to 180°: $3x + (2x+10) + (x-4) = 180$
Simplify: $6x + 6 = 180$
$6x = 174$, so $x = 29$
Angles: $87°$, $68°$, $25°$. Check: $87+68+25 = 180$ ✓
A rectangle has length $(3x+2)$ cm and width $(x-1)$ cm. Its perimeter is 46 cm. Find its area.

Perimeter: $2(3x+2) + 2(x-1) = 46$
$6x + 4 + 2x - 2 = 46$
$8x + 2 = 46 \Rightarrow 8x = 44 \Rightarrow x = 5.5$
Length $= 3(5.5)+2 = 18.5$ cm, Width $= 4.5$ cm
Area $= 18.5 \times 4.5 = 83.25$ cm²
A bag of sweets contains $n$ sweets. Ali takes 5, then Ben takes half of what remains. 12 sweets are left. How many sweets were in the bag?

After Ali: $n - 5$
After Ben: $\dfrac{n-5}{2} = 12$
$n - 5 = 24 \Rightarrow n = 29$

2.2.3 Linear Inequalities

An inequality shows that one expression is greater than (or less than) another. The solution is a range of values, not a single value.

Symbols: $<$ (less than), $\leq$ (less than or equal to), $>$ (greater than), $\geq$ (greater than or equal to).

Solving Linear Inequalities

Use the same inverse operations as equations, with one critical difference:

When you multiply or divide both sides by a negative number, you must reverse the inequality sign.
Solve $3x - 7 > 5$.

Add 7: $3x > 12$
Divide by 3: $x > 4$
Solve $-2x + 1 \leq 9$.

Subtract 1: $-2x \leq 8$
Divide by $-2$ (reverse sign!): $x \geq -4$

Representing Inequalities on a Number Line

Use an open circle $\circ$ for strict inequalities ($<$ or $>$) and a filled circle $\bullet$ for $\leq$ or $\geq$.

Combined Inequalities

E.g. $-3 < 2x + 1 \leq 7$: solve as two separate inequalities or work on all three parts simultaneously.

Solve $-3 < 2x + 1 \leq 7$ and represent on a number line.

Subtract 1 from all three parts: $-4 < 2x \leq 6$
Divide all three parts by 2: $-2 < x \leq 3$
Integer values: $x \in \{-1, 0, 1, 2, 3\}$
Number line: open circle at $-2$, filled circle at $3$, line between them.
When listing integer solutions, make sure to include the endpoints only if the sign is $\leq$ or $\geq$.

2.2.4 Simultaneous Equations

Two equations in two unknowns — find values of both unknowns that satisfy both equations simultaneously.

Method 1: Elimination

Add or subtract multiples of the equations to eliminate one variable.

Solve: $3x + 2y = 13$ and $5x - 2y = 11$.

Coefficients of $y$ are $+2$ and $-2$, so add the equations:
$8x = 24 \Rightarrow x = 3$
Substitute into equation 1: $9 + 2y = 13 \Rightarrow 2y = 4 \Rightarrow y = 2$
Check in equation 2: $15 - 4 = 11$ ✓
Solve: $2x + 3y = 16$ and $5x - y = 2$.

Multiply equation 2 by 3: $15x - 3y = 6$
Add to equation 1: $17x = 22 \Rightarrow x = \dfrac{22}{17}$
Or — to get integers here, multiply eq 1 by 5 and eq 2 by 2: $10x+15y=80$ and $10x-2y=4$. Subtract: $17y=76 \Rightarrow y=\dfrac{76}{17}$. Messy — but systematic.
Better: multiply eq 2 by 3 to match $y$ coefficient: $15x-3y=6$. Add to eq 1: $17x=22$, so $x=\frac{22}{17}$, and back-substitute for $y$.

Method 2: Substitution

Rearrange one equation to express one variable in terms of the other, then substitute into the second equation.

Solve: $y = 2x - 1$ and $3x + 4y = 18$.

Substitute $y = 2x-1$ into the second equation:
$3x + 4(2x-1) = 18$
$3x + 8x - 4 = 18 \Rightarrow 11x = 22 \Rightarrow x = 2$
$y = 2(2)-1 = 3$
Solution: $x = 2,\ y = 3$

Graphical Method

Plot both equations as straight lines. The solution is the point of intersection of the two lines.

  • Parallel lines (same gradient, different intercept) → no solution.
  • Coincident lines (same equation) → infinitely many solutions.
In elimination, make sure the signs are correct when subtracting — this is the most common error. Write the subtraction out explicitly.

2.2.5 Quadratic Equations

A quadratic equation has the form $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$ and generally has two solutions (roots).

Method 1: Factorising

Rearrange to equal zero, factorise, then set each factor equal to zero:

Solve $x^2 - 5x + 6 = 0$.

Factorise: $(x-2)(x-3) = 0$
Either $x-2=0$ or $x-3=0$
$x = 2$ or $x = 3$
Solve $2x^2 + x - 6 = 0$.

Factorise (AC method: $ac = -12$; factors $-3$ and $4$): $(2x-3)(x+2)=0$
$x = \dfrac{3}{2}$ or $x = -2$

Method 2: The Quadratic Formula

For any quadratic $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$:

$$x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}$$

The expression $b^2 - 4ac$ is called the discriminant:

Value of $b^2 - 4ac$Number of real rootsNature of roots
$b^2 - 4ac > 0$2 distinct real rootsTwo different solutions
$b^2 - 4ac = 0$1 repeated rootOne solution (parabola touches $x$-axis)
$b^2 - 4ac < 0$No real rootsParabola does not cross $x$-axis
Solve $3x^2 - 5x - 2 = 0$ using the quadratic formula.

$a=3,\ b=-5,\ c=-2$
$b^2 - 4ac = 25 - 4(3)(-2) = 25 + 24 = 49$
$x = \dfrac{5 \pm \sqrt{49}}{6} = \dfrac{5 \pm 7}{6}$
$x = \dfrac{12}{6} = 2$ or $x = \dfrac{-2}{6} = -\dfrac{1}{3}$
Solve $x^2 + 3x - 1 = 0$, giving answers to 2 decimal places.

$a=1,\ b=3,\ c=-1$
$b^2-4ac = 9+4 = 13$
$x = \dfrac{-3 \pm \sqrt{13}}{2}$
$x = \dfrac{-3 + 3.6056}{2} = 0.30$ (2 d.p.) or $x = \dfrac{-3 - 3.6056}{2} = -3.30$ (2 d.p.)
The quadratic formula always works, even when factorising seems impossible. Always rearrange to $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$ first. Identify $a$, $b$, $c$ before substituting — include the sign.

Higher — Completing the Square

Write $ax^2 + bx + c$ in the form $a(x+p)^2 + q$. This reveals the vertex of the parabola and is a third method for solving quadratics.

For $x^2 + bx + c$ (coefficient of $x^2$ is 1):

$$x^2 + bx + c = \left(x + \frac{b}{2}\right)^2 - \left(\frac{b}{2}\right)^2 + c$$
Solve $x^2 + 6x + 2 = 0$ by completing the square.

$(x+3)^2 - 9 + 2 = 0$
$(x+3)^2 = 7$
$x + 3 = \pm\sqrt{7}$
$x = -3 \pm \sqrt{7}$
Write $2x^2 - 8x + 5$ in the form $a(x+p)^2 + q$. Hence state the minimum value and the $x$-coordinate where it occurs.

Factor out 2: $2(x^2 - 4x) + 5$
Complete the square inside: $2\big[(x-2)^2 - 4\big] + 5$
Expand: $2(x-2)^2 - 8 + 5 = 2(x-2)^2 - 3$
Minimum value is $-3$ at $x = 2$

2.2.6 Algebraic Proof [Higher]

Higher only

Algebraic proof uses algebra to show that a statement is always true. You must work with general expressions, not specific numbers.

Key vocabulary:
  • Even number: $2n$
  • Odd number: $2n+1$
  • Consecutive integers: $n, n+1, n+2, \ldots$
  • Multiple of $k$: $kn$ (where $n$ is any integer)

Showing Identities

Prove that $(n+3)^2 - (n+1)^2 \equiv 4(n+2)$.

LHS: $(n+3)^2 - (n+1)^2$
$= n^2 + 6n + 9 - (n^2 + 2n + 1)$
$= n^2 + 6n + 9 - n^2 - 2n - 1$
$= 4n + 8 = 4(n+2)$ = RHS ✓

Proving Divisibility

Prove that the sum of any three consecutive integers is always divisible by 3.

Let the three integers be $n$, $n+1$, $n+2$
Sum $= n + (n+1) + (n+2) = 3n + 3 = 3(n+1)$
This is a multiple of 3, so the sum is always divisible by 3. $\square$
Prove that the product of two odd numbers is always odd.

Let the two odd numbers be $2m+1$ and $2n+1$
Product $= (2m+1)(2n+1) = 4mn + 2m + 2n + 1 = 2(2mn+m+n) + 1$
This is in the form $2k + 1$ (where $k = 2mn+m+n$ is an integer), so the product is odd. $\square$

Disproving by Counterexample

To disprove a general claim, you only need to find ONE example where it fails — a counterexample.

Disprove: "The square of any odd number is always 1 more than a multiple of 8."

Wait — actually this is TRUE. $(2n+1)^2 = 4n^2 + 4n + 1 = 4n(n+1) + 1$. Since one of $n$ or $n+1$ is even, $4n(n+1)$ is a multiple of 8, so $(2n+1)^2 \equiv 1 \pmod{8}$.
Alternative disproof example — "The sum of two prime numbers is always even": counterexample is $2 + 3 = 5$ (odd). $\square$
In a proof question, use fully general algebraic expressions. Do not use specific numbers. End with a clear concluding sentence explaining what you have shown.
