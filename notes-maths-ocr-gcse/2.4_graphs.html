2.4.1 Coordinates and Midpoints

A point in 2D is given by its coordinates $(x, y)$, where $x$ is the horizontal displacement from the origin and $y$ is the vertical displacement.

The midpoint of the line segment joining $(x_1, y_1)$ and $(x_2, y_2)$ is: $$M = \left(\frac{x_1+x_2}{2},\ \frac{y_1+y_2}{2}\right)$$ The distance between two points (by Pythagoras): $$d = \sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2 + (y_2-y_1)^2}$$
Find the midpoint and length of the line segment joining $A(1, 3)$ and $B(7, 11)$.

Midpoint: $M = \left(\dfrac{1+7}{2}, \dfrac{3+11}{2}\right) = (4, 7)$
Length: $d = \sqrt{(7-1)^2 + (11-3)^2} = \sqrt{36 + 64} = \sqrt{100} = 10$
The midpoint formula is an average of the two $x$-coordinates and an average of the two $y$-coordinates. You can think of it as "meet in the middle."

2.4.2 Straight Line Graphs

Any straight line can be written in the form $y = mx + c$, where:
  • $m$ = gradient (slope): how steep the line is = $\dfrac{\text{rise}}{\text{run}} = \dfrac{\Delta y}{\Delta x}$
  • $c$ = $y$-intercept: where the line crosses the $y$-axis.

Gradient and Equation of a Straight Line

Find the equation of the line through $(1, 3)$ and $(4, 9)$.

Gradient: $m = \dfrac{9-3}{4-1} = \dfrac{6}{3} = 2$
Using $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$ with point $(1,3)$: $y - 3 = 2(x-1)$
Expand: $y = 2x - 2 + 3 = 2x + 1$
A line has equation $3y - 6x = 12$. State the gradient and $y$-intercept.

Rearrange into $y = mx + c$ form: $3y = 6x + 12 \Rightarrow y = 2x + 4$
Gradient $m = 2$, $y$-intercept $= 4$ (i.e. the line crosses at $(0, 4)$).

Higher — Parallel and Perpendicular Lines

  • Parallel lines have the same gradient: $y = 3x + 1$ and $y = 3x - 5$ are parallel (both have $m=3$).
  • Perpendicular lines have gradients that are negative reciprocals: if line 1 has gradient $m$, then any perpendicular line has gradient $-\dfrac{1}{m}$. Their product is $-1$: $m_1 \times m_2 = -1$.
Find the equation of the line perpendicular to $y = 3x - 2$ that passes through $(6, 1)$.

Gradient of given line $= 3$. Perpendicular gradient $= -\dfrac{1}{3}$.
$y - 1 = -\dfrac{1}{3}(x - 6)$
$y = -\dfrac{1}{3}x + 2 + 1 = -\dfrac{1}{3}x + 3$

2.4.4 Real-Life Graphs

Distance–Time Graphs

  • Gradient of a distance–time graph = speed $\left(\text{speed} = \dfrac{\text{distance}}{\text{time}}\right)$.
  • A horizontal line means the object is stationary.
  • A steeper line means greater speed.
  • A negative gradient means moving back towards the start.

Speed–Time Graphs

  • Gradient of a speed–time graph = acceleration (in m/s²).
  • A horizontal line means constant speed (zero acceleration).
  • Area under the graph = distance travelled.
A car accelerates from 0 to 20 m/s in 5 seconds, travels at 20 m/s for 10 seconds, then decelerates to 0 in 5 seconds. Find the total distance.

The graph is a trapezium with parallel sides 10 s and 20 s, height 20 m/s.
Area = $\dfrac{1}{2}(10 + 20) \times 20 = \dfrac{1}{2} \times 30 \times 20 = 300$ m

Conversion Graphs

Straight-line graphs that convert between units (e.g. miles to km, £ to €). Read off values by locating one axis and reading across to the line, then down/up to the other axis.

On distance–time graphs: steeper = faster. On speed–time graphs: steeper = greater acceleration. Area under speed–time graph = distance — and area under a velocity–time graph includes direction.

2.4.5 Transformations of Graphs [Higher]

Higher only

If $y = f(x)$ is the original graph, the transformed graphs are:

TransformationEquationEffect on graph
Translation up by $a$$y = f(x) + a$Shift the graph $a$ units up
Translation down by $a$$y = f(x) - a$Shift the graph $a$ units down
Translation left by $a$$y = f(x + a)$Shift the graph $a$ units left (counter-intuitive!)
Translation right by $a$$y = f(x - a)$Shift the graph $a$ units right
Reflection in $x$-axis$y = -f(x)$Reflect in the $x$-axis (negate all $y$ values)
Reflection in $y$-axis$y = f(-x)$Reflect in the $y$-axis (negate all $x$ values)
Stretch (vertical), scale factor $a$$y = af(x)$Multiply all $y$-coordinates by $a$
Stretch (horizontal), scale factor $\frac{1}{a}$$y = f(ax)$Multiply all $x$-coordinates by $\frac{1}{a}$ (compress if $a>1$)
Memory aid for horizontal shifts: $y = f(x + a)$ shifts LEFT by $a$ because the same $y$-value now occurs at a smaller $x$-value. Think "inside the bracket does the opposite."
The graph of $y = f(x)$ passes through $(2, 5)$. State the coordinates of the corresponding point on: (a) $y = f(x) + 3$; (b) $y = f(x-4)$; (c) $y = -f(x)$.

(a) $y = f(x)+3$: add 3 to $y$-coordinate: $(2, 8)$
(b) $y = f(x-4)$: add 4 to $x$-coordinate (shift right): $(6, 5)$
(c) $y = -f(x)$: negate $y$-coordinate: $(2, -5)$
Transformation questions often ask you to apply a transformation to a sketch or to state coordinates of key points after transformation. Always think about what happens to a single point before drawing.

2.4.6 Equation of a Circle [Higher]

Higher only

A circle with centre at the origin $(0,0)$ and radius $r$ has equation: $$x^2 + y^2 = r^2$$ This follows directly from Pythagoras' theorem: for any point $(x, y)$ on the circle, the distance to the centre is $\sqrt{x^2+y^2} = r$.
(a) Write the equation of the circle with centre $(0,0)$ and radius 7.
(b) Does the point $(3, \sqrt{40})$ lie on, inside, or outside this circle?

(a) $x^2 + y^2 = 49$
(b) Compute $3^2 + (\sqrt{40})^2 = 9 + 40 = 49$. Since this equals $r^2 = 49$, the point lies on the circle.

Tangent to a Circle at a Point

A tangent to a circle at a point $P$ is perpendicular to the radius $OP$ at that point.
Find the equation of the tangent to the circle $x^2 + y^2 = 25$ at the point $(3, 4)$.

Gradient of radius $OP$: $m_{OP} = \dfrac{4-0}{3-0} = \dfrac{4}{3}$
Gradient of tangent (perpendicular): $m_t = -\dfrac{3}{4}$
Equation using point $(3,4)$: $y - 4 = -\dfrac{3}{4}(x-3)$
$y = -\dfrac{3}{4}x + \dfrac{9}{4} + 4 = -\dfrac{3}{4}x + \dfrac{25}{4}$
Or in integer form: $3x + 4y = 25$
To verify a point is on a circle: substitute its coordinates into the equation and check. To find the tangent: find the gradient of the radius, then take the negative reciprocal.
