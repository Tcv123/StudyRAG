4.1.2 Angles and Parallel Lines

When a transversal crosses two parallel lines, three types of angle pairs are formed. You must know their names, their letter shapes, and whether they are equal or supplementary.

Fig 2 — Parallel lines with transversal: alternate (blue, equal), corresponding (green, equal), co-interior (red, supplementary)

Angle PairLetter ShapeRelationshipReason to write
Alternate anglesZ (or N)Equal"alternate angles, parallel lines"
Corresponding anglesFEqual"corresponding angles, parallel lines"
Co-interior anglesC (or U)Sum = $180°$"co-interior angles, parallel lines"
Always state the reason when finding angles from parallel lines. "Z angles" alone is not sufficient — write "alternate angles, parallel lines".
Lines AB and CD are parallel. A transversal creates an angle of $65°$ with AB. Find the co-interior angle with CD.

Co-interior angles sum to $180°$.
Co-interior angle = $180° - 65° = \mathbf{115°}$
Reason: co-interior angles, AB parallel to CD.

4.1.3 Angles in Triangles and Quadrilaterals

Triangle: angles sum to $180°$
Quadrilateral: angles sum to $360°$

The exterior angle of a triangle equals the sum of the two non-adjacent interior angles (the "remote interior angles").

Fig 3 — Exterior angle of a triangle = sum of the two remote interior angles

In triangle PQR, angle P = $48°$ and angle Q = $67°$. Find the exterior angle at R.

Interior angle R = $180 - 48 - 67 = 65°$
Exterior angle at R = $180 - 65 = 115°$
Or directly: exterior angle = $48 + 67 = \mathbf{115°}$

4.1.4 Angles in Polygons

Sum of interior angles of a polygon with $n$ sides: $$S = (n-2) \times 180°$$ Sum of exterior angles of any convex polygon = $360°$ (always)

For a regular polygon with $n$ sides: $$\text{Each interior angle} = \frac{(n-2) \times 180°}{n}$$ $$\text{Each exterior angle} = \frac{360°}{n}$$

Fig 4 — Regular hexagon: interior angle = $120°$, exterior angle = $60°$, sum of all exterior angles = $360°$

The interior angle of a regular polygon is $156°$. How many sides does it have?

Exterior angle = $180 - 156 = 24°$
$n = 360 \div 24 = \mathbf{15\,\text{sides}}$
If you know the exterior angle, use $n = 360 \div \text{exterior angle}$ — much quicker than using the interior angle formula.

4.1.6 Circle Theorems Higher

Key vocabulary:
  • Chord — a straight line joining two points on the circumference.
  • Arc — part of the circumference.
  • Tangent — a line that touches the circle at exactly one point.
  • Cyclic quadrilateral — a quadrilateral whose four vertices all lie on a circle.
  • Subtend — an arc or chord subtends an angle at a point if the two ends of the arc/chord are joined to that point.
Theorem 1: Angle at the Centre
The angle subtended at the centre of a circle is twice the angle subtended at the circumference by the same arc.
Theorem 2: Angles in the Same Segment
Angles subtended by the same arc (or chord) at the circumference are equal.
Theorem 3: Angle in a Semicircle
The angle subtended by a diameter at the circumference is always $90°$.
Theorem 4: Cyclic Quadrilateral
Opposite angles in a cyclic quadrilateral sum to $180°$ (they are supplementary).
Theorem 5: Tangent–Radius
A tangent to a circle is perpendicular to the radius drawn to the point of tangency.
Theorem 6: Two Tangents from an External Point
Tangent segments from an external point to a circle are equal in length.
Theorem 7: Perpendicular from Centre to Chord
The perpendicular from the centre of a circle to a chord bisects the chord.
Theorem 8: Alternate Segment Theorem (Tangent–Chord Angle)
The angle between a tangent and a chord equals the inscribed angle in the alternate segment (the segment on the other side of the chord).
When tackling circle theorem problems, mark all given angles on the diagram first. Work systematically around the circle stating each reason. Always use the full theorem name as the reason (e.g., "angle in a semicircle = 90°" not just "semicircle").
O is the centre of a circle. Points A, B and C lie on the circumference. Angle AOB = $130°$. Find angle ACB.

Angle at centre = twice angle at circumference (same arc):
$\angle ACB = 130 \div 2 = \mathbf{65°}$
Reason: angle at centre is twice angle at circumference (Theorem 1).
ABCD is a cyclic quadrilateral. Angle ABC = $74°$ and angle BCD = $110°$. Find angles CDA and DAB.

Opposite angles in cyclic quadrilateral sum to $180°$:
$\angle CDA = 180 - 74 = \mathbf{106°}$
$\angle DAB = 180 - 110 = \mathbf{70°}$
