2.2 Children's Written Language Development

Edexcel A-Level English Language (9EN0) — Paper 2 Child Language

Writing is a culturally learned system — unlike speech it requires motor skill, orthographic knowledge, genre awareness and audience positioning. Key frameworks: Kroll\'s stages, Britton\'s functions, Rothery\'s genres, Gentry\'s spelling stages, and Halliday\'s metafunctions applied to text production.

2.2.1 Emergent Writing

Before children can form letters, they produce mark-making — scribbles, shapes, letter-like forms. This shows awareness that writing uses discrete symbols arranged in lines, directionality, word spacing, and a difference between drawing and writing.

2.2.2 Kroll's Four Stages (1981)

Preparation (up to ~6)

Motor control develops for letter formation. Child learns letter-sound links, begins phonetic spelling, writes simple high-frequency words.

Consolidation (7–8)

Writing resembles speech. Coordination with "and", "then"; simple past; first person; limited punctuation; expressive voice dominates.

Differentiation (9–10)

Child distinguishes spoken and written styles. More varied sentence types, subordination with "because", "although". More formal register for appropriate genres.

Integration (teenage onwards)

Flexible control of a range of styles adapted to audience, purpose and genre. Varied sentence openings, non-finite clauses, complex noun phrases, modality, evaluation, paragraphing, skilled graphology.

2.2.3 Britton's Three Functions (1970)

FunctionDescriptionExamples
ExpressiveSpeech on paper — personal voice, uneditedDiaries, letters to friends
TransactionalGets something done in the worldLetters to institutions, reports, essays
PoeticCrafted — attentive to form, sound, patternPoems, stories, stylised narrative

Early writing is predominantly expressive and gradually develops into transactional and poetic forms through schooling.

2.2.4 Rothery's Four Genres

GenreStructureExample
RecountOrientation → events chronologically → optional reorientation"At the weekend I went to…"
ReportGeneral statement → aspects → concluding comment"Penguins are birds…"
NarrativeOrientation → complication → resolution"One day the boy saw a dragon…"
Observation / commentIdentification of event + evaluative remark"We saw a robin. It was pretty."

The Sydney School argued explicit genre teaching is a matter of equity: children from literacy-rich homes enter school already familiar with these structures.

2.2.5 Gentry\'s Spelling Stages

StageFeatureExample
Pre-communicativeLetters without sound-letter mapping"PLKMS" for "dog"
Semi-phoneticSome sound-letter links (mostly consonants)"DG" for "dog"
PhoneticSystematic sound-by-sound spelling"cum" for "come"

ConventionalConsistent standard spellingMature orthography

Spelling errors are linguistic evidence — "phonetic" mis-spellings show sound-letter mapping internalised.

2.2.6 Grammar, Punctuation and Cohesion

Early writers typically:

  • Rely on coordination ("and", "then", "so").
  • Omit or inconsistently use full stops.
  • Lack paragraphing and broader cohesion.
  • Use simple past throughout narratives.
  • Include first-person perspective typical of speech.

Development introduces: subordination, non-finite clauses, complex noun phrases, cohesive devices beyond "then", modality and evaluation.

2.2.7 Interaction with Spoken Development

Writing builds on speech but is not reducible to it. Early writing often shows spoken features (coordination, first-person, run-on structure). As children advance they learn to:

  • Depersonalise — third-person reports, passive constructions.
  • Plan and edit — revisit and improve.
  • Address a distant reader — compensating for absent shared context.
  • Use written-only conventions — punctuation, paragraphing, headings.

2.2.8 Scaffolding Writing Pedagogy

Drawing on Vygotsky (ZPD) and Bruner (scaffolding):

  1. Modelled writing — teacher composes aloud.
  2. Shared writing — class suggests, teacher drafts.
  3. Guided writing — small-group support.
  4. Independent writing — with success criteria.

Genre-based pedagogy adds the teaching and learning cycle: deconstruction → joint construction → independent construction.

2.2.9 Digital Writing

Research (Crystal, Tagg, Wood) suggests children switch between digital and standard registers — adding registers rather than replacing standard writing. Texters with better phonological awareness tend to produce more creative abbreviations.

A Year 6 narrative opening: "Darkness fell across the city, silent but for a single, flickering streetlight. Mia had been waiting." Features: fronted adverbial, complex noun phrase, past perfect, minor-sentence fragment. Across frameworks: Kroll — integration stage; Britton — poetic function dominant; Rothery — narrative genre; Halliday — textual metafunction strong. Top-band answers name feature, framework, stage and reader effect.

2.2.10 How to Analyse a Child's Writing

  1. Identify audience, purpose, genre.
  2. Place on Kroll\'s stages with evidence.
  3. Identify dominant Britton function.
  4. Map the Rothery genre structure.
  5. Analyse at linguistic levels — lexis, grammar, graphology, cohesion.
  6. Discuss errors as evidence (spelling stage, virtuous errors).
  7. Integrate a theoretical frame — Vygotsky\'s ZPD for teaching implications, Halliday for metafunctions.
Always link features to development. "Coordination with \'and\' appears repeatedly — typical of Kroll\'s consolidation stage, where writing follows speech patterns. A target is varied subordination, which would move the writer towards differentiation." That sentence integrates feature, framework, stage and pedagogy.
