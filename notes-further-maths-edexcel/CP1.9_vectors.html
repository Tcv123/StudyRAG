CP1.9: Vectors in 3D

1. 3D Vector Basics

A 3D vector is written as \(\mathbf{a} = \begin{pmatrix}a_1\\a_2\\a_3\end{pmatrix} = a_1\mathbf{i} + a_2\mathbf{j} + a_3\mathbf{k}\)

Magnitude: \(|\mathbf{a}| = \sqrt{a_1^2 + a_2^2 + a_3^2}\)
Unit vector: \(\hat{\mathbf{a}} = \dfrac{\mathbf{a}}{|\mathbf{a}|}\)

Direction Cosines

The direction cosines of a vector \(\mathbf{a} = (a_1, a_2, a_3)\) are:

$$\cos\alpha = \frac{a_1}{|\mathbf{a}|}, \quad \cos\beta = \frac{a_2}{|\mathbf{a}|}, \quad \cos\gamma = \frac{a_3}{|\mathbf{a}|}, \quad \text{where } \cos^2\alpha + \cos^2\beta + \cos^2\gamma = 1$$

2. Scalar (Dot) Product

Geometric definition: $$\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = |\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|\cos\theta$$ where \(\theta\) is the angle between the vectors \((0 \leq \theta \leq \pi)\).

Component form: $$\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = a_1 b_1 + a_2 b_2 + a_3 b_3$$

Key Applications

  • Testing perpendicularity: \(\mathbf{a} \perp \mathbf{b} \iff \mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = 0\)
  • Finding angle: \(\cos\theta = \dfrac{\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}}{|\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|}\)
  • Projection of \(\mathbf{a}\) onto \(\mathbf{b}\): \(\dfrac{\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}}{|\mathbf{b}|}\)

Properties

  • \(\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = \mathbf{b} \cdot \mathbf{a}\) (commutative)
  • \(\mathbf{a} \cdot (\mathbf{b} + \mathbf{c}) = \mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} + \mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{c}\) (distributive)
  • \(\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{a} = |\mathbf{a}|^2\)

3. Cross (Vector) Product

Geometric definition: $$\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b} = |\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|\sin\theta\,\hat{\mathbf{n}}$$ where \(\theta\) is the angle between them and \(\hat{\mathbf{n}}\) is the unit vector perpendicular to both, following the right-hand rule.

Component form (determinant expansion): $$\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b} = \begin{vmatrix}\mathbf{i}&\mathbf{j}&\mathbf{k}\\a_1&a_2&a_3\\b_1&b_2&b_3\end{vmatrix} = \mathbf{i}(a_2 b_3 - a_3 b_2) - \mathbf{j}(a_1 b_3 - a_3 b_1) + \mathbf{k}(a_1 b_2 - a_2 b_1)$$

Key Properties of Cross Product

  • \(\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b} = -(\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{a})\) (anti-commutative)
  • \(\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{a} = \mathbf{0}\)
  • \(\mathbf{a} \parallel \mathbf{b} \iff \mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b} = \mathbf{0}\)
  • \(\mathbf{i} \times \mathbf{j} = \mathbf{k}\), \(\mathbf{j} \times \mathbf{k} = \mathbf{i}\), \(\mathbf{k} \times \mathbf{i} = \mathbf{j}\)

Area Applications

$$\text{Area of parallelogram} = |\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b}|$$ $$\text{Area of triangle} = \frac{1}{2}|\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b}|$$
Example: Find \(\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b}\) and hence the area of the triangle with vertices \(A, B, C\) where \(\overrightarrow{AB} = \begin{pmatrix}1\\2\\-1\end{pmatrix}\) and \(\overrightarrow{AC} = \begin{pmatrix}3\\0\\2\end{pmatrix}\).

$$\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b} = \begin{vmatrix}\mathbf{i}&\mathbf{j}&\mathbf{k}\\1&2&-1\\3&0&2\end{vmatrix} = \mathbf{i}(4-0) - \mathbf{j}(2+3) + \mathbf{k}(0-6) = \begin{pmatrix}4\\-5\\-6\end{pmatrix}$$ \(|\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b}| = \sqrt{16+25+36} = \sqrt{77}\)
Area of triangle \(= \frac{1}{2}\sqrt{77}\)

4. Equation of a Line in 3D

A line through point \(A\) with position vector \(\mathbf{a}\) and direction vector \(\mathbf{b}\) has equation:

Vector form: \(\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{a} + \lambda\mathbf{b}\), where \(\lambda \in \mathbb{R}\) is a parameter.

Parametric form: $$x = a_1 + \lambda b_1, \quad y = a_2 + \lambda b_2, \quad z = a_3 + \lambda b_3$$ Cartesian (symmetric) form: $$\frac{x - a_1}{b_1} = \frac{y - a_2}{b_2} = \frac{z - a_3}{b_3} = \lambda$$ (provided all components of \(\mathbf{b}\) are non-zero)
Example: Write the line through \(A = (2,-1,3)\) and \(B = (5,1,-2)\) in all three forms.

Direction vector: \(\overrightarrow{AB} = \begin{pmatrix}3\\2\\-5\end{pmatrix}\)

Vector form: \(\mathbf{r} = \begin{pmatrix}2\\-1\\3\end{pmatrix} + \lambda\begin{pmatrix}3\\2\\-5\end{pmatrix}\)

Parametric: \(x = 2+3\lambda,\; y = -1+2\lambda,\; z = 3-5\lambda\)

Cartesian: \(\dfrac{x-2}{3} = \dfrac{y+1}{2} = \dfrac{z-3}{-5}\)

5. Equation of a Plane

A plane with normal vector \(\mathbf{n} = \begin{pmatrix}a\\b\\c\end{pmatrix}\) passing through point \(A\) with position vector \(\mathbf{a}\):

Scalar product form: \(\mathbf{r} \cdot \mathbf{n} = \mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{n} = d\)

Cartesian form: \(ax + by + cz = d\)

Parametric form: \(\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{a} + \lambda\mathbf{b} + \mu\mathbf{c}\) where \(\mathbf{b}\) and \(\mathbf{c}\) are two non-parallel direction vectors in the plane.
Example: Find the equation of the plane through \(A(1,0,2)\), \(B(3,1,0)\), \(C(0,2,1)\).

Find two direction vectors: \(\overrightarrow{AB} = \begin{pmatrix}2\\1\\-2\end{pmatrix}\), \(\overrightarrow{AC} = \begin{pmatrix}-1\\2\\-1\end{pmatrix}\)

Normal: \(\mathbf{n} = \overrightarrow{AB} \times \overrightarrow{AC} = \begin{vmatrix}\mathbf{i}&\mathbf{j}&\mathbf{k}\\2&1&-2\\-1&2&-1\end{vmatrix}\)
\(= \mathbf{i}(-1+4) - \mathbf{j}(-2-2) + \mathbf{k}(4+1) = \begin{pmatrix}3\\4\\5\end{pmatrix}\)

\(d = \mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{n} = 1(3) + 0(4) + 2(5) = 13\)
Plane: \(3x + 4y + 5z = 13\)

6. Angles Between Lines and Planes

Angle betweenFormulaNotes
Two lines (direction \(\mathbf{b}_1, \mathbf{b}_2\))\(\cos\theta = \dfrac{|\mathbf{b}_1 \cdot \mathbf{b}_2|}{|\mathbf{b}_1||\mathbf{b}_2|}\)Use \(|\cdot|\) to get acute angle
Two planes (normals \(\mathbf{n}_1, \mathbf{n}_2\))\(\cos\theta = \dfrac{|\mathbf{n}_1 \cdot \mathbf{n}_2|}{|\mathbf{n}_1||\mathbf{n}_2|}\)Use \(|\cdot|\) to get acute angle
Line (direction \(\mathbf{b}\)) and plane (normal \(\mathbf{n}\))\(\sin\theta = \dfrac{|\mathbf{b} \cdot \mathbf{n}|}{|\mathbf{b}||\mathbf{n}|}\)Note: \(\sin\), not \(\cos\)
Exam tip: For the angle between a line and a plane, the formula uses \(\sin\) (because the line makes an angle \(\theta\) with the plane but \(90°-\theta\) with the normal). This is the most common error in this section.

7. Intersections

Line–Line Intersection (or Skew)

For two lines \(\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{a} + \lambda\mathbf{b}\) and \(\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{c} + \mu\mathbf{d}\):

  1. Set the \(x\), \(y\), \(z\) components equal to get three equations in \(\lambda\) and \(\mu\)
  2. Solve two of the equations for \(\lambda\) and \(\mu\)
  3. Check if these values satisfy the third equation
  4. If yes: lines intersect (substitute to find point). If no: lines are skew or parallel

Parallel lines: Direction vectors are parallel (\(\mathbf{b} = k\mathbf{d}\) for some scalar \(k\)).

Skew lines: Not parallel and do not intersect.

Line–Plane Intersection

Substitute the parametric equations of the line into the plane equation to find \(\lambda\), then find the point.

Example: Find where \(\mathbf{r} = \begin{pmatrix}1\\2\\-1\end{pmatrix} + \lambda\begin{pmatrix}2\\1\\3\end{pmatrix}\) meets the plane \(x - 2y + z = 5\).

Substitute parametric form: \((1+2\lambda) - 2(2+\lambda) + (-1+3\lambda) = 5\)
\(1+2\lambda - 4 - 2\lambda - 1 + 3\lambda = 5\)
\(3\lambda - 4 = 5 \Rightarrow \lambda = 3\)

Point: \(x = 7, y = 5, z = 8\). Intersection at \((7, 5, 8)\).

Plane–Plane Intersection

Two non-parallel planes intersect in a line. Find the direction vector using the cross product of the two normals. Then find a specific point on the intersection (set one variable to 0 and solve the system of two equations).

8. Distance Formulae

Distance from point \(P\) to plane \(\mathbf{r} \cdot \mathbf{n} = d\): $$\text{Distance} = \frac{|\mathbf{p} \cdot \mathbf{n} - d|}{|\mathbf{n}|}$$ where \(\mathbf{p}\) is the position vector of \(P\).

In Cartesian form (plane \(ax+by+cz = d\), point \((x_0, y_0, z_0)\)): $$\text{Distance} = \frac{|ax_0 + by_0 + cz_0 - d|}{\sqrt{a^2+b^2+c^2}}$$
Distance from point \(A\) to line \(\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{b} + \lambda\mathbf{d}\): $$\text{Distance} = \frac{|\overrightarrow{BA} \times \mathbf{d}|}{|\mathbf{d}|}$$ where \(B\) is any point on the line.
Shortest distance between two skew lines \(\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{a} + \lambda\mathbf{b}\) and \(\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{c} + \mu\mathbf{d}\): $$\text{Distance} = \frac{|(\mathbf{c} - \mathbf{a}) \cdot (\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{d})|}{|\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{d}|}$$
Example: Find the shortest distance between the skew lines
\(l_1: \mathbf{r} = \begin{pmatrix}1\\0\\-1\end{pmatrix} + \lambda\begin{pmatrix}2\\1\\0\end{pmatrix}\) and \(l_2: \mathbf{r} = \begin{pmatrix}0\\2\\3\end{pmatrix} + \mu\begin{pmatrix}1\\-1\\2\end{pmatrix}\)

\(\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{d} = \begin{pmatrix}2\\1\\0\end{pmatrix} \times \begin{pmatrix}1\\-1\\2\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}1\cdot2-0\cdot(-1)\\0\cdot1-2\cdot2\\2\cdot(-1)-1\cdot1\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}2\\-4\\-3\end{pmatrix}\)

\(|\mathbf{b}\times\mathbf{d}| = \sqrt{4+16+9} = \sqrt{29}\)

\(\mathbf{c} - \mathbf{a} = \begin{pmatrix}-1\\2\\4\end{pmatrix}\)

\((\mathbf{c}-\mathbf{a})\cdot(\mathbf{b}\times\mathbf{d}) = -2 - 8 - 12 = -22\)

Shortest distance: \(\dfrac{|-22|}{\sqrt{29}} = \dfrac{22}{\sqrt{29}} = \dfrac{22\sqrt{29}}{29}\)

Common exam mistakes in vectors:
  • Line–plane angle: Must use \(\sin\theta\) with the NORMAL, not \(\cos\theta\).
  • Skew vs parallel: Always check both conditions. Skew means not parallel AND not intersecting.
  • Normal to plane from parametric form: Take the cross product of the two direction vectors.
  • Shortest distance: Always verify the lines are actually skew before applying the formula.
  • Exact answers: Leave distances in surd form unless told to give a decimal.
