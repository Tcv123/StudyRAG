Topic 2: Cells and Control

Edexcel GCSE (9-1) Biology (1BI0) — Paper 1

2.1 The Cell Cycle

The cell cycle is the series of stages that a cell passes through each time it divides. It consists of a long growth stage called interphase, followed by a short division stage: mitosis (nuclear division) and cytokinesis (division of the cytoplasm).

All body (somatic) cells of multicellular organisms divide by mitosis to produce two genetically identical daughter cells. Before a cell can divide, it must grow, copy all of its DNA, and duplicate its organelles. This preparation happens during interphase, which is much longer than the actual division stages — a typical human cell spends 90% or more of its life in interphase.

Stages of the Cell Cycle

StageWhat happens
G1 (Gap 1)The cell grows larger. Organelles such as mitochondria and ribosomes are produced in large numbers. Proteins required for DNA synthesis are made.
S (Synthesis)DNA is replicated: each chromosome is copied so that it now consists of two identical strands joined at the centromere (sister chromatids).
G2 (Gap 2)The cell continues to grow. More organelles are made and proteins needed for cell division (e.g. spindle fibres) are synthesised. The cell checks the DNA for errors.
MitosisThe nucleus divides. The duplicated chromosomes separate into two identical sets.
CytokinesisThe cytoplasm splits, forming two new daughter cells, each with a full set of chromosomes and organelles.
Interphase is NOT "resting". It is the most active part of the cycle — the cell is growing, copying DNA and making organelles. Don't write that cells "rest" between divisions. Use the word interphase.

2.2 Mitosis

Mitosis is a type of cell division in which a parent cell divides to produce two genetically identical daughter cells, each with the same number of chromosomes as the parent (diploid in humans — 46 chromosomes).

Mitosis itself has four stages, remembered by the mnemonic PMAT: Prophase, Metaphase, Anaphase, Telophase. After telophase the cytoplasm divides (cytokinesis) to produce the two daughter cells.

Stages of Mitosis

StageWhat happens
ProphaseChromosomes (already replicated in S phase) condense and become visible as X-shaped structures, each made of two sister chromatids joined at the centromere. The nuclear membrane breaks down. Spindle fibres form from the centrioles at opposite poles of the cell.
MetaphaseChromosomes line up single file across the centre (equator) of the cell. Spindle fibres attach to each centromere.
AnaphaseSpindle fibres contract, pulling sister chromatids apart. One chromatid from each pair is pulled to each pole of the cell.
TelophaseChromatids reach the poles and uncoil back into chromatin. A new nuclear membrane forms around each set. The cell now has two identical nuclei.
CytokinesisThe cytoplasm splits in two. In animal cells the membrane pinches inwards (cleavage furrow); in plant cells a new cell wall forms across the middle (cell plate).
A common exam question is to identify a mitosis stage from a diagram. Key clues: chromosomes lined up in the middle = metaphase. Chromatids being pulled to opposite poles = anaphase. Two nuclei forming = telophase. Learn these visual markers.

2.3 Purposes of Mitosis

Mitosis produces genetically identical diploid cells. Multicellular organisms rely on mitosis for three main purposes:

  • Growth: an organism grows by producing more cells. A baby is made up of far more cells than a fertilised egg.
  • Repair: damaged tissues are replaced by new identical cells (e.g. skin cells dividing to close a wound).
  • Asexual reproduction: some organisms (e.g. bacteria, strawberry runners, hydra) produce offspring from a single parent using mitosis. The offspring are clones — genetically identical to the parent.

Because mitosis produces genetically identical cells, all the cells in your body (apart from gametes) have the same DNA as the fertilised egg you developed from. Differences between cell types come from which genes are switched on or off — not from the cells having different DNA.

2.4 Cancer — Uncontrolled Mitosis

Cancer is a disease caused by uncontrolled mitosis. Changes (mutations) to genes that normally control the cell cycle cause cells to divide repeatedly, producing a mass of cells called a tumour.

Under normal conditions, cells divide only when more cells are needed, and the cell cycle has many checkpoints that stop division if DNA is damaged. In cancer, these checks fail — cells divide when they shouldn't, and may ignore signals telling them to die. A tumour is the result.

Type of tumourFeatures
BenignGrows in one place; does not invade other tissues. Usually harmless unless it presses on an organ. Can often be removed by surgery.
MalignantInvades surrounding tissue. Cells can break off, travel in the blood/lymph and start secondary tumours elsewhere — a process called metastasis. This is what we mean by cancer.

Risk factors for cancer include: smoking (lung cancer), UV radiation (skin cancer), ionising radiation, viruses (e.g. HPV causes cervical cancer), diet, alcohol and inherited genetic factors. Treatments include surgery, chemotherapy and radiotherapy — all of which target rapidly dividing cells.

2.5 Growth in Animals and Plants

Animals and plants both grow — but the way they grow is very different.

Growth in Animals

  • Animals grow mainly by cell division (mitosis), especially during embryonic development and childhood.
  • Once an adult size is reached, most cell division is for repair and replacement of worn-out cells, not for further growth.
  • Animals have determinate growth: they stop growing at maturity.
  • Cells differentiate early in development. Most adult animal cells are specialised and cannot change into another cell type.

Growth in Plants

  • Plants grow throughout their lives (indeterminate growth).
  • Growth happens by three processes at regions called meristems:
    • Cell division — mitosis at the meristem produces new cells.
    • Cell elongation — the new cells absorb water into the vacuole and grow much longer. This is how roots push into soil and shoots grow upwards.
    • Cell differentiation — cells then differentiate into specialised cells (xylem, phloem, root hair cells, etc.).
  • Meristems are found at the tips of roots and shoots (apical meristems) and in the cambium of stems.
Compare and contrast questions: animals grow by division only; plants grow by division, elongation and differentiation. Animals grow everywhere; plants grow only at meristems. Animals have determinate growth; plants are indeterminate.

2.6 Measuring Growth in Humans — Percentile Charts

A baby's growth is monitored using percentile charts. Mass, length (or height) and head circumference are measured regularly and plotted against age.

  • A percentile curve shows the typical range for a population. For example, the 50th percentile is the median — half of babies are above this line and half below.
  • A baby who consistently plots along the 25th percentile is smaller than most, but still healthy — what matters is whether they follow a percentile.
  • A sudden jump across percentile lines (up or down) may indicate a problem: poor feeding, illness, or overfeeding.

2.7 Stem Cells

A stem cell is an undifferentiated cell that can divide repeatedly by mitosis to produce more stem cells, and can differentiate (become specialised) into various types of cell.

Types of Stem Cell

TypeSourcePotential
Embryonic stem cellsEarly human embryo (5–7 days old); obtained from spare embryos from IVFCan differentiate into any type of cell in the body (pluripotent); very versatile
Adult stem cellsAdult tissues, especially bone marrowCan only become a limited number of cell types (e.g. blood cells)
Plant meristem cellsMeristems at tips of roots, shoots and in cambiumCan differentiate into any type of plant cell (xylem, phloem, root hair cells, etc.) throughout life

Embryonic stem cells are described as pluripotent — they can become almost any body cell. Very early embryonic cells (first few divisions) are totipotent and can become any cell, including the placenta. Adult stem cells are multipotent — they can only form a few cell types, those related to the tissue they were taken from.

2.8 Uses, Risks and Ethics of Stem Cells

Medical Uses

  • Type 1 diabetes: stem cells could be used to grow new insulin-producing beta cells for the pancreas.
  • Paralysis caused by spinal cord damage: stem cells may be used to grow new nerve cells to reconnect severed nerves.
  • Leukaemia (blood cancer): bone marrow transplants replace the patient's faulty bone marrow stem cells with healthy donor stem cells that produce healthy blood cells.
  • Burns: stem cells can grow new skin.
  • Macular degeneration: new retinal cells could be grown to restore sight.

Risks

  • Rejection: if the stem cells come from another person, the patient's immune system may attack them. Patients often need immunosuppressant drugs.
  • Tumour formation: if the stem cells continue to divide uncontrollably, they may form a tumour.
  • Infection: during the procedure, viruses might be transferred with the stem cells.

Ethical Issues

Using embryonic stem cells is controversial because it involves destroying a human embryo. Many people believe an embryo has the same right to life as a person. Others argue that the embryos used are spare embryos from IVF that would otherwise be destroyed, and that saving lives justifies their use. Adult stem cells and stem cells from umbilical cord blood are less controversial because they do not involve destroying an embryo.

"Evaluate the use of stem cells" questions expect balanced answers: one or two benefits (e.g. could treat paralysis, could produce insulin for diabetics) plus one or two issues (rejection, tumour formation, ethics of embryo destruction) and a supported conclusion.

2.9 The Nervous System — Overview

The nervous system allows organisms to respond quickly to changes in their environment. It consists of two parts:

SystemIncludesRole
Central nervous system (CNS)Brain & spinal cordProcesses information from receptors and coordinates a response via motor neurones
Peripheral nervous system (PNS)All other nerves (sensory and motor neurones) that connect the CNS to the rest of the bodyCarries signals between receptors, the CNS, and effectors (muscles/glands)

A typical nervous response follows this pathway: stimulus → receptor → sensory neurone → CNS (relay neurone) → motor neurone → effector → response. The CNS makes sense of the information and decides what to do.

2.10 Neurones

A neurone is a specialised nerve cell that carries electrical impulses. Neurones have a cell body, dendrites (receive impulses) and an axon (transmits impulses away).

Three Types of Neurone

NeuroneRoleStructure
Sensory neuroneCarries impulses from receptors (skin, eyes, etc.) to the CNSLong dendron and short axon; cell body sticks out on a branch
Relay (interneurone)Connects sensory and motor neurones inside the CNSShort; found in brain and spinal cord
Motor neuroneCarries impulses from CNS to an effector (muscle or gland)Short dendrites and a long axon ending in motor end plates on the muscle

Adaptations of Neurones

  • Long: to carry messages over long distances (up to 1 m in humans).
  • Myelin sheath: a layer of fatty material (made by Schwann cells) that insulates the axon and greatly speeds up the impulse.
  • Nodes of Ranvier: gaps in the myelin sheath where the impulse "jumps" from node to node (saltatory conduction), making transmission even faster.
  • Axon terminals (synaptic knobs): branches at the end of the axon that connect to many other cells, allowing the impulse to influence multiple targets.
  • Many mitochondria: provide ATP for active transport of ions to reset the neurone after an impulse.

2.11 Synapses

A synapse is a junction between two neurones (or between a neurone and an effector). The two cells are separated by a small gap called the synaptic cleft.

Electrical impulses cannot cross the synaptic cleft directly. Instead, the signal is carried across by chemicals called neurotransmitters:

  1. An impulse arrives at the end of the first neurone (the pre-synaptic neurone).
  2. This triggers vesicles in the axon terminal to fuse with the membrane and release neurotransmitter molecules into the synaptic cleft.
  3. The neurotransmitters diffuse across the cleft.
  4. They bind to specific receptors on the next (post-synaptic) neurone. Only neurotransmitters with the right shape can fit the receptors.
  5. This triggers a new electrical impulse in the next neurone.
  6. The neurotransmitter is then broken down or reabsorbed, so the signal stops.
Why is the synapse one-way only? Because vesicles containing neurotransmitters are only on the pre-synaptic side and receptors are only on the post-synaptic side. The signal cannot travel backwards.

2.12 The Reflex Arc

A reflex action is an automatic, rapid response to a stimulus that does not involve conscious thought. The pathway is called a reflex arc.

Reflex actions protect the body from harm — e.g. pulling your hand away from a hot plate, blinking when something comes near your eye, the knee-jerk reflex. Because the impulse goes to the spinal cord (not the brain), the response is much faster than a conscious reaction.

The Reflex Arc Pathway

stimulus → receptor → sensory neurone → relay neurone (in spinal cord) → motor neurone → effector → response

  1. Stimulus (e.g. hot plate) is detected by a receptor in the skin (temperature/pain receptor).
  2. A sensory neurone carries the impulse along a nerve to the spinal cord.
  3. In the spinal cord, a relay neurone passes the impulse across to a motor neurone (via synapses).
  4. The motor neurone carries the impulse to a muscle in the arm.
  5. The muscle (effector) contracts and pulls the hand away — the response.
Why are reflexes fast? Two reasons: (1) The impulse does NOT go to the brain — it takes the shortcut through the spinal cord. (2) Only two synapses are involved (sensory → relay, relay → motor), and synapses are the slow step. Don't just write "fast" — explain why.

2.13 The Brain (Higher Tier)

The brain is the control centre of the nervous system. It is made of billions of interconnected neurones. Different regions carry out different functions.

RegionFunction
Cerebrum (cerebral hemispheres)The largest part of the brain. Controls conscious thought, memory, language, intelligence, reasoning, and voluntary movement. Divided into left and right hemispheres.
CerebellumFound at the back of the brain, under the cerebrum. Coordinates balance, posture and fine muscle movements.
Medulla (oblongata)At the base of the brain, connected to the spinal cord. Controls unconscious activities such as heart rate and breathing rate.
HypothalamusRegulates body temperature and water balance; controls the pituitary gland which releases hormones.
Pituitary gland"Master gland" — releases many hormones that control other glands in the body.

2.14 Investigating Brain Function

The brain is delicate, encased in the skull and protected by the blood-brain barrier. Three main methods are used to study it:

MethodHow it worksWhat it shows
Studying patients with brain damageIf an area is damaged (by stroke, tumour, injury) and the patient loses an ability, scientists infer that the area controls that abilityThe function of brain regions (e.g. damage to cerebellum → loss of balance)
CT scan (Computed Tomography)X-rays from many angles build a 3D image of the brain's structurePhysical structure — tumours, bleeding, damaged areas
PET scan (Positron Emission Tomography)A radioactive tracer (e.g. radioactive glucose) is injected; active brain cells take it up. Emitted radiation is detected to show which regions are activeWhich regions are active during different activities (thinking, moving, listening)
MRIUses strong magnetic fields and radio waves to produce detailed images of soft tissueHigh-detail structural images; can show activity (fMRI)

Why Brain Disorders are Difficult to Treat

  • Delicate tissue: brain tissue is easily damaged by surgery. Damaged neurones do not easily regrow.
  • Complex structure: each region has different functions and is densely interconnected. Damage to a small area can affect many processes.
  • Blood-brain barrier: the walls of the capillaries in the brain are very selective about which substances enter. Many drugs cannot cross it, so treating conditions with drugs is difficult.
  • Access: the brain is surrounded by the skull, making it hard to reach without major surgery.

2.15 The Eye

The eye is a sense organ that detects light. Light entering the eye is focused onto the retina, which contains photoreceptor cells that send impulses to the brain via the optic nerve.

Parts of the Eye

PartFunction
CorneaTransparent front of the eye. Refracts (bends) most of the light entering the eye.
PupilHole in the centre of the iris that lets light through.
IrisColoured ring of muscle around the pupil. Controls pupil size to regulate how much light enters (bright: circular muscles contract → pupil small; dim: radial muscles contract → pupil large).
LensTransparent, flexible structure that fine-focuses light onto the retina. Changes shape (accommodation).
Ciliary musclesRing of muscles that change the shape of the lens.
Suspensory ligamentsConnect the ciliary muscles to the lens; transmit tension to flatten the lens.
RetinaLight-sensitive layer at the back of the eye. Contains rods (sensitive to dim light, no colour) and cones (colour, need bright light; three types: red, green, blue).
FoveaCentral region of the retina packed with cones; gives sharpest colour vision.
Optic nerveCarries impulses from the retina to the brain.
ScleraTough white outer layer that protects the eye and keeps its shape.
Blind spotPoint where the optic nerve leaves the eye — no photoreceptors here.

2.16 How the Eye Focuses — Accommodation

Accommodation is the process by which the eye changes the shape of the lens to focus on objects at different distances.

When you switch your gaze between a distant tree and a book in your hand, your eye must change how much it bends the light. The cornea does most of the bending, but the lens provides the fine focus by changing shape.

Distant object (far)Near object (close)
Ciliary musclesRelaxContract
Suspensory ligamentsPulled tightSlacken
LensPulled thin and flatBecomes thick and rounded
Light refraction by lensLess bending (light already nearly parallel)More bending (light rays diverging strongly)
Common mistake: students say "the lens contracts" — the lens doesn't contract, only muscles do. Be precise: "ciliary muscles contract, suspensory ligaments slacken, lens becomes thicker/fatter."

2.17 Eye Defects and Correction

Eye defects happen when light does not focus properly onto the retina. The commonest are long-sightedness, short-sightedness, cataracts and colour blindness.

Long-Sightedness (Hyperopia)

  • Problem: distant objects are clear, but near objects look blurred.
  • Cause: the eyeball is too short, or the lens is too thin/cannot become fat enough. Light from near objects is focused behind the retina.
  • Correction: wear glasses with a convex (converging) lens, which bends light rays inward before they enter the eye so they focus on the retina.

Short-Sightedness (Myopia)

  • Problem: near objects are clear, but distant objects look blurred.
  • Cause: the eyeball is too long, or the lens is too thick/cannot become thin enough. Light from distant objects is focused in front of the retina.
  • Correction: wear glasses with a concave (diverging) lens, which spreads light rays outward before they enter the eye so they focus on the retina.

Cataracts

  • Problem: the lens becomes cloudy. Vision is blurred, colours fade, and bright lights cause glare.
  • Cause: proteins in the lens clump together, often due to ageing or UV damage.
  • Correction: the cloudy lens is surgically removed and replaced with an artificial lens.

Colour Blindness

  • Problem: the person cannot distinguish certain colours — most commonly red and green.
  • Cause: one or more types of cone cell in the retina are missing or not working. Usually inherited (more common in males as it is linked to the X chromosome).
  • Correction: no cure. Special tinted lenses can help some people distinguish colours more easily.
Mnemonic: MYopia = MY eye is too long (light meets short of retina). Hyperopia = eyeball short (light overshoots). Remember concave = myopia, convex = hyperopia.
