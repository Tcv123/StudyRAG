Topic 8: Exchange and Transport in Animals

Edexcel GCSE (9-1) Biology (1BI0) — Paper 2

All organisms must take in useful substances (oxygen, glucose, water, ions) and get rid of waste products (carbon dioxide, urea). In a single-celled organism these substances can simply diffuse in and out across the cell surface membrane. But as organisms get bigger, the distances become too great and the surface area is not big enough. This topic explains why multicellular animals need dedicated exchange surfaces (like the alveoli in the lungs) and transport systems (the blood and the heart), and how these structures are adapted for their functions.

8.1 Surface-Area-to-Volume Ratio (SA:V)

The surface-area-to-volume ratio (SA:V) is the surface area of an object divided by its volume. As an object grows larger, its volume increases faster than its surface area, so SA:V decreases.

For a single-celled organism like an amoeba, the cell is small enough that oxygen can diffuse in and CO₂ can diffuse out across the cell surface fast enough to meet its needs. But as organisms get bigger, there is simply not enough surface for diffusion to supply every cell. A large animal therefore needs specialised exchange surfaces (lungs, intestine) to take substances in, and a transport system (blood and heart) to carry them around.

Worked example: cube SA:V

For a cube of side length L: surface area = 6 × L², volume = L³, so SA:V = 6/L.

Notice how doubling the side length (from 1 to 2) halves the SA:V. A small organism therefore has a much larger SA:V than a large organism of the same shape.

Always write SA:V as a ratio (e.g. "6:1") or as a number with "per unit volume". If asked "compare", say both values and state which is bigger and by how much.

8.2 Why Multicellular Organisms Need Transport Systems

In a small organism, every cell is close enough to the surface for simple diffusion to supply oxygen and remove waste. In a large animal two problems arise:

  • Diffusion distances are too large — it would take far too long for oxygen to diffuse from the skin to a muscle cell deep inside the body.
  • SA:V is too small — the outer surface is not large enough to supply the enormous number of inner cells.
  • Energy demands are high — active tissues (muscle, brain) need a constant, rapid supply of oxygen and glucose, which diffusion alone cannot deliver.

Multicellular animals solve these problems with two linked adaptations:

  1. Dedicated exchange surfaces (e.g. lungs, small intestine) where substances move between the body and the environment.
  2. A mass transport system (the circulatory system) that carries substances between exchange surfaces and every cell in the body.

8.3 Adaptations of Exchange Surfaces

All efficient exchange surfaces share the same four features, because each of them increases the rate of diffusion (Fick's law: rate ∝ surface area × concentration difference / distance).

When asked "explain how this is adapted for gas exchange", always link each feature to an effect on diffusion rate. Don't just list features — say why they matter.

8.4 Respiration

Respiration is a series of chemical reactions in cells that release energy from glucose. The energy is stored temporarily in a molecule called ATP, which powers all the energy-requiring processes in the cell. Respiration is not breathing — it happens inside every living cell.

Aerobic respiration (with oxygen)

Occurs in the mitochondria. Requires oxygen. Releases a large amount of energy per glucose molecule.

glucose + oxygen  →  carbon dioxide + water  (+ energy/ATP)

C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6 O₂  →  6 CO₂ + 6 H₂O

Anaerobic respiration in humans (no oxygen)

Happens in muscle cells during vigorous exercise when oxygen cannot be supplied fast enough. Glucose is only partially broken down, so much less energy is released.

glucose  →  lactic acid  (+ a little energy)

Lactic acid builds up and lowers the pH of the muscle, causing fatigue and cramp. The oxygen needed after exercise to break down the lactic acid is called the oxygen debt — it is why you keep breathing heavily after you stop running.

Anaerobic respiration in yeast (fermentation)

Yeast is a single-celled fungus. In the absence of oxygen, it carries out fermentation:

glucose  →  ethanol + carbon dioxide  (+ a little energy)

This reaction is used in brewing (the ethanol produces alcohol) and breadmaking (the CO₂ bubbles make the dough rise; the ethanol evaporates during baking).

Learn the symbol equation for aerobic respiration by heart — it often carries a whole 2 marks on its own. Balance check: 6 C + 12 H + 18 O on each side.

8.5 Uses of Energy from Respiration

The energy released by respiration is used for:

  • Muscle contraction — movement, posture, heartbeat.
  • Active transport — moving ions/molecules against a concentration gradient (e.g. mineral ion uptake in root hair cells, glucose reabsorption in kidneys).
  • Synthesis of large molecules — building proteins from amino acids, DNA replication, making starch/glycogen.
  • Keeping warm — mammals and birds release heat from respiration to maintain a constant body temperature.
  • Cell division (mitosis) — growth and repair require large amounts of energy.

8.6 Investigating Respiration Rate

Respiration rate can be measured by either the uptake of oxygen or the release of carbon dioxide.

Method 1 — Limewater (detecting CO₂)

Breathed-out air is bubbled through limewater. CO₂ turns limewater from clear to milky/cloudy. Comparing the speed at which the limewater turns cloudy between rest and after exercise shows that exercise increases the rate of respiration and therefore CO₂ production.

Method 2 — Respirometer (measuring O₂ uptake)

  1. Place a small organism (e.g. germinating peas or woodlice) in a sealed test tube containing soda lime (absorbs all CO₂ produced).
  2. Connect a capillary tube containing a coloured liquid drop.
  3. As the organism respires, oxygen is taken up but the CO₂ is absorbed, so the total gas volume falls and the drop moves towards the organism.
  4. The distance moved per minute is a measure of the rate of oxygen consumption (and therefore respiration).

Control variables: temperature (use a water bath), mass of organisms, type of organism. Control experiment: a tube with dead/boiled organisms, to rule out any changes caused by temperature or air pressure.

8.7 The Human Gas Exchange System

Air is drawn in through the nose and mouth, passes down the trachea, which divides into two bronchi (one per lung), then into smaller bronchioles, ending in millions of tiny air sacs called alveoli where gas exchange with the blood takes place.

The route of air is: mouth/nose → trachea → bronchi → bronchioles → alveoli.

The trachea is held open by C-shaped rings of cartilage. The walls of the bronchioles contain smooth muscle. The ribs form a protective cage; the intercostal muscles run between the ribs. The diaphragm is a dome of muscle below the lungs separating the thorax from the abdomen.

8.8 Adaptations of Alveoli for Gas Exchange

Each lung contains around 300 million alveoli, giving a combined surface area of about 70–100 m² — roughly the size of a tennis court. Alveoli are exceptionally well-adapted exchange surfaces:

AdaptationHow it helps gas exchange
Huge total surface area (~70 m²)More O₂ and CO₂ can diffuse per second
Walls are only one cell thickShort diffusion distance between air and blood
Capillary walls are also one cell thickBlood and air are separated by only two flat cells
Dense capillary networkKeeps concentration gradient steep (constant O₂ removal, CO₂ supply)
Moist inner liningOxygen dissolves so it can diffuse through the membrane
Ventilated by breathingFresh oxygen-rich air constantly replaces used air
"Why are alveoli moist?" Answer: so that oxygen can dissolve in the water lining before diffusing across the alveolar wall into the blood. Dry membranes cannot exchange gases efficiently.

8.9 Ventilation — Inhalation and Exhalation

Ventilation is the movement of air in and out of the lungs. It is produced by changes in the volume and pressure of the thorax, which follow the principle that when volume increases, pressure falls — and air flows from high to low pressure.

Inhalation (breathing in)

  • The diaphragm contracts and flattens downward.
  • The external intercostal muscles contract, pulling the ribs up and out.
  • The volume of the thorax increases.
  • The pressure inside the thorax decreases (below atmospheric).
  • Air flows into the lungs down the pressure gradient.

Exhalation (breathing out)

  • The diaphragm relaxes and rises back into its dome shape.
  • The external intercostal muscles relax; the ribs fall down and in under gravity (and under elastic recoil of the lung tissue).
  • The volume of the thorax decreases.
  • The pressure inside rises (above atmospheric).
  • Air flows out of the lungs.
A common trap: writing "the lungs suck air in". They don't — air is pushed in by atmospheric pressure when thorax pressure drops below it. Describe ventilation in terms of volume → pressure → flow.

Blood is a tissue made up of four components: three types of cell (and cell fragments) suspended in a yellow liquid called plasma.

8.11 Red Blood Cells

Red blood cells (erythrocytes) are highly specialised for transporting oxygen. They are made in the bone marrow and live about 120 days.

AdaptationExplanation
Biconcave disc shapeLarge surface area to volume ratio for faster diffusion of oxygen; flexible enough to squeeze through narrow capillaries
No nucleusMore room to pack in haemoglobin; more oxygen carried per cell
Contain haemoglobinIron-containing protein that binds O₂ reversibly
Small and flexibleCan bend to pass single-file through capillaries, bringing O₂ close to every cell

In the lungs (high O₂): haemoglobin + O₂ → oxyhaemoglobin. In respiring tissues (low O₂): oxyhaemoglobin → haemoglobin + O₂. This reversible reaction allows red blood cells to pick up oxygen where it is plentiful and release it where it is needed.

8.12 White Blood Cells

White blood cells defend the body against pathogens (bacteria, viruses, fungi). Unlike red blood cells they have a nucleus. The two main types are phagocytes and lymphocytes (covered in more detail in Topic 5).

  • Phagocytes — engulf and digest pathogens by a process called phagocytosis. They have a multi-lobed nucleus and many lysosomes.
  • Lymphocytes — produce antibodies that bind specifically to a pathogen's antigens. This marks pathogens for destruction and produces immunity (memory cells). Some lymphocytes produce antitoxins to neutralise bacterial toxins.

8.13 Platelets

Platelets are small fragments of cells (they have no nucleus). When a blood vessel is damaged, platelets stick to the wound and release chemicals that trigger a cascade of reactions producing the fibrous protein fibrin. Fibrin forms a mesh of threads that traps red blood cells to form a clot. The clot:

  • Prevents excessive blood loss.
  • Stops pathogens from entering the body.
  • Forms a scab under which the skin can heal.

People who lack clotting factors (e.g. those with haemophilia) bleed for longer because clots do not form normally.

8.14 Plasma

Plasma is the straw-coloured liquid that makes up about 55% of blood. It is ~90% water with many substances dissolved in it. Plasma transports:

  • Blood cells (red, white, platelets) — held in suspension.
  • Dissolved nutrients — glucose, amino acids, fatty acids from the small intestine to the tissues.
  • Carbon dioxide — from respiring tissues to the lungs (mostly as dissolved hydrogencarbonate ions).
  • Hormones — chemical messengers from glands to target organs.
  • Urea — from the liver to the kidneys for excretion.
  • Heat — moved from active organs (e.g. muscles, liver) to cooler parts of the body.
  • Antibodies and blood proteins.

8.15 Blood Vessels

Blood travels through three main types of vessel: arteries (carry blood away from the heart), veins (carry blood to the heart), and capillaries (thin exchange vessels that link the two). Each is adapted to its function.

Remember: Arteries carry blood Away from the heart. Veins return to the heart (think "v" pointing back). The only exceptions: the pulmonary artery carries deoxygenated blood, and the pulmonary vein carries oxygenated blood.

8.16 Double Circulation

Mammals have a double circulatory system — blood passes through the heart twice for each complete trip around the body. The heart contains two pumps side by side.

  • Pulmonary circulation — carries deoxygenated blood from the right side of the heart to the lungs and returns oxygenated blood to the left side.
  • Systemic circulation — carries oxygenated blood from the left side of the heart to the body and returns deoxygenated blood to the right side.

Advantage: after being oxygenated in the lungs the blood returns to the heart and is pumped again. This means the blood going to the body leaves the heart at high pressure, so it can flow quickly to every tissue. A single circulation (as in fish) cannot do this because blood loses pressure at the gills. Double circulation is essential for supporting the high metabolic rates of mammals and birds.

8.17 The Structure of the Heart

The heart is a muscular pump made of cardiac muscle. It has four chambers: two upper atria (thin-walled, receive blood) and two lower ventricles (thick-walled, pump blood out). A thick muscular wall called the septum separates the right and left sides, preventing oxygenated and deoxygenated blood from mixing.

Valves (one-way)

  • Tricuspid valve — between right atrium and right ventricle (3 flaps).
  • Bicuspid (mitral) valve — between left atrium and left ventricle (2 flaps).
  • Semilunar valves — one in the pulmonary artery and one in the aorta, preventing backflow of blood into the ventricles after contraction.

Blood flow through the heart

body  →  vena cava  →  right atrium  →  tricuspid valve  →  right ventricle
        →  (semilunar valve)  →  pulmonary artery  →  LUNGS
LUNGS  →  pulmonary vein  →  left atrium  →  bicuspid valve  →  left ventricle
        →  (semilunar valve)  →  aorta  →  BODY

8.18 Why the Left Ventricle Wall Is Thicker

The right ventricle pumps blood only to the lungs — a short distance away. The left ventricle pumps blood all the way around the body, against more resistance, to reach every cell.

The left ventricle therefore needs a much thicker, more muscular wall so that it can generate a higher pressure when it contracts. The right ventricle wall is thinner because a high pressure would damage the delicate capillaries in the lungs.

Common mark-scheme phrase: "The left ventricle has a thicker muscular wall so that it can contract with more force, generating a higher pressure to pump blood all around the body."

8.19 Cardiac Output

Cardiac output is the volume of blood pumped by the heart (by one ventricle) per minute.
Stroke volume is the volume of blood pumped out per beat.
Heart rate is the number of beats per minute. 
Cardiac output (cm³ min⁻¹)  =  stroke volume (cm³)  ×  heart rate (beats min⁻¹)

Worked example

A person has a stroke volume of 70 cm³ and a heart rate of 72 beats per minute. Calculate their cardiac output.

cardiac output = 70 × 72 = 5040 cm³ min⁻¹ (about 5 litres per minute)

During exercise, both the heart rate and stroke volume rise, so cardiac output can increase to 25 litres min⁻¹ or more. This delivers oxygen and glucose to active muscles and removes CO₂ and lactic acid more quickly.
