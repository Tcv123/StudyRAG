Topic 7: Animal Coordination, Control and Homeostasis

Edexcel GCSE (9-1) Biology (1BI0) — Paper 2

Your body must coordinate its activities so that cells, tissues and organs work together. Two systems do this: the nervous system (covered in Topic 2) which uses fast electrical impulses, and the endocrine system which uses chemical messengers called hormones. Topic 7 focuses on the endocrine system and how it contributes to homeostasis — the maintenance of constant internal conditions (blood glucose, body temperature, water balance).

Homeostasis is the regulation of the internal conditions of a cell or organism to maintain optimum conditions for function, in response to internal and external changes. It typically involves negative feedback: a change from the set point triggers responses that restore the set point.

7.1 The Endocrine System

The endocrine system is a collection of glands that secrete chemical messengers called hormones directly into the blood. Blood carries the hormone to target organs, where it produces an effect by binding to specific receptors on target cells.

Key features of hormonal communication:

  • Slow to take effect — blood has to carry the hormone.
  • Long-lasting — hormones persist in the blood until broken down (often by the liver).
  • Wide-reaching — a single hormone can affect many organs at once.
  • Specific — only cells with the correct receptor respond.

If an exam asks you to compare nervous and hormonal control, always cover: (1) signal type, (2) speed, (3) duration, (4) how precisely targeted. Use the word "target organ" when describing hormone action.

Adrenaline — the Fight-or-Flight Hormone

Adrenaline is released by the adrenal glands when the body is under stress (fear, excitement, danger). Its effects prepare the body for rapid action:

  • Heart rate increases — more oxygen and glucose to muscles per minute.
  • Breathing rate increases — more oxygen taken in.
  • Pupils dilate — more light enters the eye, better vision.
  • Blood vessels to muscles dilate, those to gut constrict — blood is redirected to muscles.
  • Liver releases glucose from glycogen — more respiratory substrate for muscles.
  • Sweating — prepares to lose heat from extra respiration.
Adrenaline effects and vital statistics (heart rate, breathing, pupil size) are popular 4- and 6-mark questions. Always link each effect back to "more oxygen and glucose reach the muscles faster" so they can respire and power action.

Thyroxine and Negative Feedback

Thyroxine from the thyroid controls the basal metabolic rate — the rate at which cells respire and use energy at rest. Its release is itself controlled by negative feedback:

  1. Pituitary releases TSH (thyroid-stimulating hormone).
  2. TSH stimulates the thyroid to release thyroxine.
  3. Rising blood thyroxine feeds back on the pituitary to inhibit TSH.
  4. If thyroxine falls, inhibition weakens and TSH rises again.

7.4 Blood Glucose Regulation

Blood glucose must be kept close to a set point (~5 mmol dm−³ when fasting, ~7 mmol dm−³ after a meal). If it falls too low, cells — especially brain cells — cannot respire enough; if it rises too high it damages tissues. The pancreas monitors blood glucose and responds by releasing either insulin or glucagon.

ConditionHormone releasedTargetEffect on glucose
Blood glucose too high (after a meal)Insulin (from β-cells)Liver & muscle cellsGlucose taken up from blood; stored as glycogen → blood glucose falls
Blood glucose too low (between meals, exercise)Glucagon (from α-cells)Liver cellsGlycogen broken down to glucose; glucose released into blood → blood glucose rises
Don't muddle up the words — glucose (the sugar in blood), glycogen (storage polymer in liver/muscle), glucagon (the hormone that raises blood glucose). Spell each one correctly!

7.5 Diabetes

Diabetes is a condition in which blood glucose is not properly regulated, so it rises dangerously high after meals (hyperglycaemia) and cannot be brought back to normal.

FeatureType 1 diabetesType 2 diabetes
CauseAutoimmune destruction of β-cells in the pancreas — little or no insulin producedBody cells become resistant to insulin; pancreas may also produce less
Age of onsetUsually childhood/adolescenceUsually adulthood (risk rises with age)
Risk factorsGenetic predisposition; not linked to lifestyleObesity, poor diet, inactivity, ethnicity, family history
TreatmentRegular insulin injections; monitor blood glucose; carbohydrate-controlled diet; exerciseDiet control (low sugar, low refined carbs), exercise, weight loss; later, oral drugs (e.g. metformin) or insulin
Cure?No cure — lifelongOften controlled / reversed by lifestyle changes in early stages

A healthy person's blood glucose rises after a meal but is brought back to the normal range within about 2 hours by insulin. In untreated Type 1 diabetes, blood glucose rises much higher after a meal and stays elevated for much longer because no insulin is available to trigger uptake. Excess glucose spills into the urine (glycosuria) and draws water out by osmosis, causing dehydration and thirst.

A classic 6-mark question: "Explain the difference in blood glucose between a healthy person and someone with untreated Type 1 diabetes after a meal." Walk through: meal → glucose absorbed → healthy person secretes insulin → glucose taken up by liver/muscle → returns to set point. Type 1: no insulin, glucose stays high.

7.6 The Menstrual Cycle

The menstrual cycle is a roughly 28-day cycle controlled by hormones from the pituitary gland and the ovaries. It prepares the body for pregnancy each month. Four main phases:

DaysPhaseWhat happens
1–5MenstruationUterus lining (endometrium) breaks down and is shed if there is no pregnancy
5–14Follicular phaseFSH stimulates a follicle in the ovary to develop. The follicle secretes oestrogen, which rebuilds the uterus lining
~14OvulationA surge of LH (triggered by high oestrogen) causes the follicle to burst and release an egg
14–28Luteal phaseThe empty follicle (now called the corpus luteum) secretes progesterone, which maintains the uterus lining. If no fertilisation occurs, progesterone falls and menstruation begins again

A common 4-mark question asks you to describe the sequence: "FSH → follicle & oestrogen → high oestrogen triggers LH → LH triggers ovulation → corpus luteum secretes progesterone → progesterone maintains lining." Learning this loop gets you most menstrual cycle marks.

Advantages and Disadvantages

  • Advantages: very effective (>99% when used correctly), reversible, long-lasting options available.
  • Disadvantages: do not protect against STIs, can have side effects (headaches, weight change, mood changes, small increase in blood clot risk with combined pill), user has to remember to take the pill daily.

7.10 Infertility Treatments

If a couple cannot conceive, hormonal or assisted reproductive treatments can help.

TreatmentHow it worksNotes
ClomifeneBlocks oestrogen's negative feedback on the pituitary → pituitary releases more FSH and LH → stimulates ovulationUsed when a woman does not ovulate regularly; can cause multiple ovulations (twins/triplets)

Ethical Issues with IVF

  • Unused embryos are usually frozen or discarded — some people believe an embryo has the same moral status as a child.
  • Multiple births (twins/triplets) carry risks for mother and babies.
  • Cost and availability on the NHS are limited — is it fair that some can pay privately?
  • Concerns about screening embryos (pre-implantation genetic diagnosis) leading to "designer babies".
  • Religious objections to fertilisation occurring outside the body.

7.11 ADH and Water Balance (Osmoregulation)

Osmoregulation is the control of water and ion concentration in the body's cells and blood. Water is gained through drinking and food; it is lost in urine, sweat, breath and faeces. The body must balance these so that the blood plasma has a constant water potential — if it became too dilute or too concentrated, cells could swell and burst or shrink and lose function by osmosis.

ADH (antidiuretic hormone) is released from the pituitary gland when the blood is too concentrated. It acts on the kidney tubules (especially the collecting duct and distal tubule), making them more permeable to water so more water is reabsorbed into the blood. The result: less, more concentrated urine.
TriggerADH levelKidney responseUrine produced
Blood too concentrated (exercise, sweating, low water intake, salty food)HighCollecting duct very permeable — lots of water reabsorbedSmall volume, concentrated (dark yellow)
Blood too dilute (drinking a lot)LowCollecting duct less permeable — less water reabsorbedLarge volume, dilute (pale / clear)

7.12 The Kidney

The kidneys are bean-shaped organs in the lower back that filter blood to remove waste (urea), regulate water balance and adjust ion concentrations. Each kidney contains about a million filtering units called nephrons.

Kidney structure

  • Cortex — dark outer region where most filtration occurs (contains glomeruli and Bowman's capsules).
  • Medulla — inner region where loops of Henle and collecting ducts sit (concentrating urine).
  • Pelvis — central cavity where urine collects before leaving down the ureter.
  • Renal artery brings blood in; renal vein takes cleaned blood out; ureter carries urine to the bladder.

The nephron — two key processes

  1. Ultrafiltration (in Bowman's capsule/glomerulus): high pressure in the glomerular capillaries forces water, glucose, ions, urea and other small molecules out of the blood into the Bowman's capsule. Blood cells and large proteins stay in the blood.
  2. Selective reabsorption (mainly in the proximal convoluted tubule): all the glucose, most of the water and useful ions are reabsorbed back into the blood. Urea is not reabsorbed.

The loop of Henle creates a salt gradient in the medulla so that water can be reabsorbed from the collecting duct. ADH controls how permeable the collecting duct is — the final step in adjusting urine concentration.

For a "what is reabsorbed?" question: all glucose, most water, useful ions are reabsorbed (selective reabsorption). Urea is NOT reabsorbed — that's how we excrete it. Blood cells and big proteins never leave the blood in the first place because they are too large to pass through the filter.

7.13 Urea Formation

Your body cannot store excess amino acids or protein. Instead, the liver breaks down surplus amino acids in a process called deamination:

  1. The amine group (−NH₂) is removed from each surplus amino acid in the liver.
  2. The amine group is converted to ammonia (NH₃), which is very toxic.
  3. Ammonia is quickly combined with CO₂ to form urea, which is much less toxic but still must be removed.
  4. Urea travels in the blood to the kidneys, where it is filtered out in ultrafiltration and excreted in the urine.
  5. The remaining carbon-containing part of the amino acid is converted to glucose or fat and used for respiration or stored.
Deamination: the removal of the amine group from an amino acid in the liver, producing ammonia (toxic), which is then converted to urea (less toxic) for excretion by the kidneys.

7.14 Kidney Failure Treatments

If both kidneys fail (because of diabetes, high blood pressure, genetic disease, infection), urea builds up in the blood and ion/water balance is lost. Two treatments are available:

1. Kidney Dialysis

A dialysis machine acts as an artificial kidney. The patient's blood flows along one side of a partially permeable membrane; dialysis fluid flows on the other side in the opposite direction.

  • Dialysis fluid contains the same concentration of glucose and mineral ions as normal blood, so these do not diffuse out (no concentration gradient).
  • It contains no urea, so urea diffuses out of the blood into the dialysis fluid down its concentration gradient.
  • Excess ions and water also leave the blood, matching normal blood composition.

Patients usually need dialysis 3 times a week, for around 4 hours each session. It is life-saving but time-consuming and restricts diet.

2. Kidney Transplant

A kidney from a donor (often a close relative or a dead donor) is surgically placed in the patient. The donated kidney takes over full function, ideally for many years.

TreatmentAdvantagesDisadvantages
DialysisAvailable quickly; no surgery; no rejectionTime-consuming (~12 hours/week); restricted diet; risk of infection at tube sites; not a cure
TransplantLong-term cure; free diet; better quality of lifeShortage of donors — long waiting list; surgery risks; must take immunosuppressants for life to prevent rejection; immunosuppressants increase infection risk
Dialysis fluid has the same salt and glucose concentration as blood so these are NOT lost. Only waste (urea) and excess water/salts move out — down concentration gradients. This is a favourite question.

7.15 Thermoregulation (Control of Body Temperature)

Core body temperature must be kept close to 37 °C because enzymes have a narrow optimum. The hypothalamus in the brain monitors blood temperature and also receives input from temperature receptors in the skin. It coordinates responses through the nervous system (mostly) and hormones.

Too Hot

  • Sweating — sweat glands release sweat onto the skin; evaporation of water removes heat (it takes a lot of energy to evaporate water).
  • Vasodilation — arterioles supplying skin capillaries widen, so more blood flows close to the surface → more heat lost by radiation. Skin appears red.
  • Hairs lie flat — erector muscles relax; no insulating air layer is trapped.
  • Behavioural: removing clothing, drinking cold water, moving to shade.

Too Cold

  • Shivering — rapid involuntary muscle contractions; respiration in the contracting muscles releases heat.
  • Vasoconstriction — arterioles supplying skin capillaries narrow; less blood flows near the surface → less heat lost. Skin appears pale.
  • Hairs stand up — erector muscles contract; hairs trap an insulating layer of air. (Not very effective in humans, but visible as "goosebumps".)
  • Reduced sweating.
  • Behavioural: putting on clothes, curling up, drinking hot drinks.
A common trap: vasodilation does NOT mean blood vessels move closer to the skin. The capillaries are always where they are; the arterioles widen so more blood flows through the capillaries near the surface. Write "arterioles dilate so more blood flows through capillaries near the skin surface".
