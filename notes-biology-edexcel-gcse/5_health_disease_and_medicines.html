Topic 5: Health, Disease and the Development of Medicines

Edexcel GCSE (9-1) Biology (1BI0) — Paper 2

5.1 What is Health?

The World Health Organisation (WHO) defines health as "a state of complete physical, mental and social wellbeing and not merely the absence of disease or infirmity."

Health is more than simply being free of illness. It has three components, all of which must be reasonably good for a person to be truly healthy:

  • Physical wellbeing — bodies functioning normally; absence of injury or disease.
  • Mental wellbeing — ability to think clearly, cope with stress, handle emotions.
  • Social wellbeing — positive relationships, sense of belonging, ability to interact with others.

Disease

A disease is a condition that impairs the normal functioning of the body. Diseases are split into two broad categories:
CategoryCauseSpread?Examples
Communicable (infectious)Caused by a pathogen — a microorganism that invades the body (bacteria, viruses, fungi, protists).Yes — passes from one organism to another (direct contact, water, air, vectors, body fluids).Cholera, TB, malaria, HIV, flu, measles, chalara ash dieback.
Non-communicableCaused by genetics, lifestyle, age or environment; not caused by a pathogen.No — cannot be passed between individuals.Cardiovascular disease, most cancers, type 2 diabetes, asthma, obesity-related conditions.

One disease increasing the risk of another

Diseases do not occur in isolation. One illness can make a person more likely to suffer from another:

  • A compromised immune system (e.g. HIV infection, which destroys lymphocytes) leaves the body vulnerable to infections that a healthy immune system would clear easily — TB, pneumonia, certain cancers.
  • Some viruses trigger cancers: HPV (cervical cancer), hepatitis B/C (liver cancer), Epstein–Barr virus (certain lymphomas).
  • Long-term immune responses to pathogens can trigger allergies or autoimmune problems.
  • Severe physical illness affects mental health (depression/anxiety), which in turn can worsen recovery and impair immune function.
A common 2-mark question asks you to give the WHO definition of health and to give one example of how one disease can increase the risk of another. Use a named pathogen (e.g. HIV weakens the immune system so a person is more likely to catch TB).

5.3 Viruses

Viruses are not alive in the usual sense — they are not made of cells, do not respire, do not grow and cannot reproduce on their own. They can only replicate inside a living host cell.

Structure of a virus

  • Genetic material — either DNA or RNA, carrying instructions to make new viruses.
  • Protein coat (capsid) — surrounds and protects the genetic material.
  • Some viruses have an outer envelope of lipid (taken from the host cell membrane) studded with proteins used to attach to host cells.

The lytic cycle vs the lysogenic cycle

Once a virus enters a host cell, it can reproduce in two ways. In the lytic cycle the virus immediately makes copies of itself and bursts the host cell open. In the lysogenic cycle the viral genetic material is inserted into the host's DNA and lies dormant for many cell divisions, until triggered to enter the lytic cycle later.

StageLytic cycleLysogenic cycle
1. AttachmentVirus attaches to receptors on host cell surface.Same.
2. EntryViral genetic material is injected into host cell.Same.
3. SynthesisHost cell machinery forced to copy viral DNA/RNA and make viral proteins.Viral DNA is integrated into host chromosome (a provirus) and replicated each time the host divides.
4. AssemblyNew viruses self-assemble from the copied parts.Dormant — no new viruses made.
5. ReleaseHost cell lyses (bursts) releasing new viruses; host dies.Under stress (UV, chemicals, poor host health) the provirus activates and the cycle switches to lytic.
Lytic = lysis (cell bursts, virus reproduces immediately). Lysogenic = integrated into host DNA, stays hidden. Herpes and HIV both have lysogenic stages — this is why these infections are very hard to get rid of.

5.4 Communicable Diseases — The Edexcel Case Studies

You must know the following named diseases: the pathogen type, how it spreads, its main symptoms, and how it can be prevented or treated.

Cholera

Pathogen: Vibrio cholerae (bacterium).
Transmission: consumption of food or water contaminated by faeces containing the bacterium — common where there is poor sanitation.
Symptoms: severe watery diarrhoea (the bacterium releases a toxin that makes gut cells pump water out), dehydration — can be fatal in hours.
Prevention: clean drinking water, sewage treatment, hand-washing, oral rehydration therapy (ORT) for treatment.

Tuberculosis (TB)

Pathogen: Mycobacterium tuberculosis (bacterium).
Transmission: airborne droplets when an infected person coughs or sneezes.
Symptoms: persistent cough (sometimes with blood), chest pain, fever, weight loss ("consumption"); primarily damages the lungs but can spread.
Prevention/treatment: BCG vaccine in high-risk areas, good ventilation, avoiding crowded indoor spaces, long course of antibiotics (6 months). Risk groups include people with HIV.

Chalara Ash Dieback

Pathogen: Hymenoscyphus fraxineus (fungus).
Transmission: spores carried in the wind; can also spread via movement of infected saplings between countries.
Symptoms (in ash trees): leaf loss, dark brown/black lesions on bark, crown dieback, eventual tree death.
Prevention: restricting movement of ash trees, removing and burning infected trees, researching resistant varieties.

Malaria

Pathogen: Plasmodium (protist).
Transmission: bite of an infected female Anopheles mosquito — the mosquito is the vector.
Symptoms: recurring fever, chills, sweating, anaemia; liver damage; can be fatal.
Prevention: insecticide-treated bed nets, indoor insecticide spraying, removing standing water where mosquitoes breed, antimalarial drugs for travellers.

HIV / AIDS

Pathogen: Human Immunodeficiency Virus (HIV).
Transmission: exchange of body fluids — unprotected sex, sharing needles, mother to baby (in pregnancy, birth or breastfeeding), infected blood transfusions.
Symptoms: initial flu-like illness; virus attacks T-lymphocytes (white blood cells); after years, the immune system is so weakened that the person develops AIDS and cannot fight off other infections (e.g. TB, pneumonia).
Prevention/treatment: condoms, clean needles, screening of blood donations, antiretroviral (ART) drugs keep viral load low but cannot cure.

Stomach Ulcers — Helicobacter pylori

Pathogen: Helicobacter pylori (bacterium).
Transmission: oral–oral or faecal–oral spread (saliva, contaminated food/water).
Symptoms: stomach pain, nausea, indigestion; bacterium burrows into the stomach lining, producing ammonia which damages cells and leads to an ulcer (also a cancer risk factor).
Treatment: combination antibiotics plus a proton-pump inhibitor to reduce stomach acid.

Ebola

Pathogen: Ebola virus.
Transmission: direct contact with the body fluids (blood, vomit, saliva) of an infected person or animal; can remain infectious in a body after death.
Symptoms: severe haemorrhagic fever — fever, severe internal and external bleeding, organ failure; high mortality (~50%).
Prevention: isolation of cases, protective equipment for carers, safe burial practices, experimental vaccines.

5.5 The Malaria Life Cycle

Plasmodium is a single-celled protist with a complex life cycle that requires two hosts: a human and a female Anopheles mosquito. The mosquito is the vector — an organism that carries the pathogen between hosts but is not itself severely harmed.

  1. An infected female Anopheles mosquito bites a human to take a blood meal. Her saliva contains Plasmodium cells, which enter the human's blood.
  2. Plasmodium travels to the liver, multiplies there for about a week, then returns to the blood.
  3. It invades red blood cells, multiplies inside them and makes the cells burst — causing the classic fever pattern and anaemia of malaria.
  4. Another mosquito bites the infected person, taking in Plasmodium cells with the blood.
  5. Inside the mosquito's gut, Plasmodium reproduces sexually and migrates to the salivary glands, ready to infect the next human.
Plasmodium 1. Infected mosquito bites human; injects Plasmodium in saliva 2. Liver: Plasmodium multiplies 3. Invades red blood cells → burst 5. Reproduces in gut Moves to salivary glands
When asked how malaria is prevented, mention preventing the vector not just treating the patient — insecticide-treated nets, draining stagnant water (where larvae grow), indoor spraying, and avoiding mosquito bites with clothing/repellent.

5.6 Plant Defences Against Pathogens

Plants are attacked by bacteria, viruses, fungi and protists too — and they cannot run away or mount an immune response like animals. Instead, plants use a combination of physical barriers and chemical defences.

Physical defences

  • Waxy cuticle — a waterproof layer on leaves that stops pathogens landing on the surface from entering.
  • Cellulose cell walls — a tough physical barrier around every plant cell that most pathogens cannot cross.
  • Bark/dead cells on stems — a thick outer layer of dead, lignified cells that pathogens cannot easily penetrate; old bark flakes off, taking pathogens with it.
  • Stomatal closure — stomata (pores for gas exchange) can close when a pathogen is detected to stop it entering.

Chemical defences

  • Antibacterial / antifungal compounds — many plants make chemicals that kill microbes (e.g. mint contains menthol; witch hazel contains tannins).
  • Poisons that deter herbivores — nicotine (tobacco), caffeine (coffee, tea), pyrethrum (chrysanthemums) — all evolved to poison insect pests that would otherwise damage the plant and open it to infection.

Identifying plant diseases

Plant pathologists use three main approaches to diagnose a sick plant:

  1. Observation — visible symptoms such as leaf spots, wilting, stunted growth, discolouration, unusual growths (galls), rotting or "dieback" of branches.
  2. Laboratory testing — take a tissue sample, look under a microscope for the pathogen, or use DNA tests (PCR) and monoclonal antibody kits to identify the exact species.
  3. Distribution analysis — map where infected plants occur. A disease spreading through adjacent plants suggests a contact/vector pathogen; clusters downwind suggest spores in air; patchy distribution may indicate soil-borne pathogens.

5.7 Sexually Transmitted Infections (STIs)

A sexually transmitted infection (STI) is a communicable disease passed mainly through sexual contact — the exchange of body fluids during intercourse.

HIV

The Human Immunodeficiency Virus was covered in 5.4. It infects and gradually destroys T-helper lymphocytes, which normally coordinate the immune response. As T-helper numbers fall, the immune system loses its ability to fight off other infections. The person develops AIDS (Acquired Immune Deficiency Syndrome) and is vulnerable to opportunistic infections and cancers.

Chlamydia

Chlamydia trachomatis is actually a bacterium, but it behaves unusually — it can only reproduce inside host cells, like a virus.

  • Transmission: unprotected vaginal, anal or oral sex with an infected partner; also from mother to baby during birth.
  • Symptoms: often asymptomatic (especially in women) — which is why it spreads so widely. When symptoms appear they include pain on urinating, unusual discharge, pelvic pain.
  • Consequences: if untreated can cause pelvic inflammatory disease and infertility.
  • Treatment: antibiotics (it is a bacterium, so antibiotics work).

Preventing STIs

  • Condoms — a physical barrier that prevents exchange of body fluids; the single most effective method alongside abstinence.
  • Screening and testing — routine tests allow early diagnosis and treatment before transmission to partners.
  • Monogamy / reducing partner numbers — reduces exposure.
  • Abstinence — the only 100% effective method.
  • Contact tracing — when someone is diagnosed, past partners are informed so they can be tested.
  • Education — especially of young people about risks and methods of prevention.

5.8 The Human Body's Non-Specific Defences

Before a pathogen ever reaches the bloodstream, the body has a battery of physical and chemical barriers designed to stop it getting in. These are non-specific — they defend against any pathogen rather than a specific one.

5.9 The Immune System

If pathogens get past the outer defences, the immune system — largely the white blood cells — takes over. There are two main types of white blood cell on the specification: phagocytes and lymphocytes.

Phagocytes — non-specific "eaters"

Phagocytosis is the process by which a phagocyte engulfs and digests a pathogen.

  1. The phagocyte recognises foreign chemicals on the pathogen's surface (non-specific).
  2. It flows round the pathogen and engulfs it, taking it inside a vesicle called a phagosome.
  3. Lysosomes inside the phagocyte fuse with the vesicle and release enzymes that digest the pathogen.
  4. Harmless breakdown products are released or displayed on the surface.

Lymphocytes — specific "targeters"

Lymphocytes are the specific part of the immune system. Every pathogen has unique chemical markers on its surface called antigens. Lymphocytes produce antibodies — Y-shaped proteins with binding sites that exactly fit the shape of one specific antigen, like a key in a lock.

  1. A lymphocyte recognises an antigen on the pathogen.
  2. It divides rapidly (clonal expansion) to make a clone of cells that all make the same antibody.
  3. Antibodies bind to antigens, causing pathogens to clump together (agglutination), or marking them for phagocytes, or neutralising their toxins.
  4. Some lymphocytes remain afterwards as memory cells (see 5.10).
Antibodies are specific — each antibody only fits one antigen. This is why the immune system must produce a different antibody for every pathogen, and why vaccines are disease-specific.

5.10 The Immune Response — Primary vs Secondary

The body responds differently the first time it meets a pathogen (primary response) compared with later exposures (secondary response). The secondary response is the basis of long-term immunity and of how vaccines work.

FeaturePrimary response (1st exposure)Secondary response (subsequent)

In an exam, label both axes (time, antibody concentration), mark the two exposure arrows, and show the secondary curve as higher, steeper and starting sooner than the primary. Forgetting any of these loses marks.

5.11 Vaccines and Herd Immunity

A vaccine contains dead or weakened (inactivated) forms of a pathogen, or its antigens alone, introduced to stimulate an immune response without causing the disease.

How a vaccine works

  1. Weakened/dead pathogen (or its antigens) is injected into the body.
  2. The antigens trigger a primary immune response: lymphocytes produce antibodies and, crucially, memory cells.
  3. If the live pathogen later enters the body, memory cells recognise the antigen immediately.
  4. A fast, large secondary response destroys the pathogen before the person becomes ill. The person is immune.

Herd immunity

When a high proportion of a population is vaccinated, the pathogen cannot find enough susceptible hosts to spread. People who cannot be vaccinated (newborns, immune-compromised people, the very elderly) are protected indirectly — this is herd immunity. If vaccination rates fall below the threshold (around 90–95% for measles), outbreaks return.

Smallpox is the only human disease to be eradicated — a global vaccination programme ended natural transmission in 1977. It shows what vaccines are capable of. Measles, polio and rubella all fell dramatically after mass vaccination but have rebounded in areas where uptake has dropped.

5.12 Antibiotics and Resistance

Antibiotics are chemicals that kill bacteria or stop them reproducing, without harming human cells (e.g. penicillin, amoxicillin, tetracycline). They do not work against viruses.

Antibiotics target features unique to bacteria — for example, penicillin blocks the building of bacterial cell walls, which human cells don't have. Viruses have no cell wall and reproduce inside our own cells, so antibiotics cannot reach them. Colds, flu and COVID are viral — antibiotics do nothing.

Antibiotic resistance

Overuse and misuse of antibiotics has led to the evolution of antibiotic-resistant bacteria — a serious public health crisis.

  1. A random mutation arises in a bacterial gene that happens to give some resistance to an antibiotic.
  2. When the antibiotic is given, susceptible bacteria die but resistant ones survive — this is natural selection.
  3. The resistant bacteria reproduce (every 20 minutes in some species) and pass the resistance gene to their descendants. They can also share it with other bacteria via plasmids.
  4. Over time, an increasing proportion of the population is resistant; the antibiotic becomes useless.
MRSA (methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus) has evolved resistance to most common antibiotics and causes hard-to-treat infections in hospitals. New "last-resort" antibiotics such as vancomycin are being lost to resistance too.

Slowing the evolution of resistance

  • Doctors should only prescribe antibiotics when needed (not for viral infections).
  • Patients must complete the full course — stopping early leaves the more resistant bacteria alive.
  • Rotate antibiotics; use narrow-spectrum where possible.
  • Control use of antibiotics in farming (where they are fed to livestock to promote growth).
  • Improve hygiene to reduce the number of infections in the first place.
  • Research new antibiotics.

5.13 Developing New Drugs

A new medicine goes through a long, staged development process to make sure it works and is safe. This typically takes 10–15 years and costs hundreds of millions of pounds.

StageWhat happensPurpose
1. Preclinical — cells & tissuesDrug tested on cells and tissue cultures in the lab.Does it have the intended effect? Is it cytotoxic?
2. Preclinical — animalsTested on live animals (e.g. rats).Check for toxicity and see how it is processed in a whole body.
3. Clinical trial — healthy volunteersLow doses given to healthy humans, increased gradually.Check for safety and side effects in humans; determine safe dose.
4. Clinical trial — patientsGiven to people with the disease; double-blind, placebo-controlled, random allocation.Is the drug actually effective at treating the disease? What is the optimum dose?
5. Peer review & publicationResults examined by independent scientists before publication.Checks methods, statistics and conclusions. Prevents flawed or false claims.
6. Regulatory approvale.g. MHRA (UK), FDA (USA) — agency licenses the drug.Independent regulator decides if benefits outweigh risks.
7. Post-marketing surveillanceOngoing monitoring after release.Catches rare side effects only visible in very large populations.

Placebos and double-blind trials

  • A placebo is a dummy treatment (e.g. a sugar pill) that looks identical to the real drug but has no active ingredient. It controls for the placebo effect — people can feel better just because they believe they've been treated.
  • In a single-blind trial, patients don't know whether they got the drug or placebo.
  • In a double-blind trial, neither patients nor the doctors assessing them know, so expectations cannot bias the measurements. The trial code is only broken after the data is collected.
Spec favourite: "Why must new drugs be tested on healthy volunteers before patients?" → to check for safety and side effects in humans before testing effectiveness in people with the disease.

5.14 Monoclonal Antibodies (Higher Tier)

A monoclonal antibody is a pure population of identical antibodies, all specific to one antigen. They are made from a single clone of B-lymphocytes.

How they are made — hybridoma cells

B-lymphocytes make antibodies but don't divide in culture. Tumour cells divide indefinitely but don't make antibodies. By fusing the two you get a hybridoma cell that does both — making unlimited identical antibodies.

  1. Inject a mouse with the target antigen — its immune system makes B-lymphocytes that produce the wanted antibody.
  2. Extract B-lymphocytes from the mouse's spleen.
  3. Fuse them with tumour cells (myelomas) to form hybridoma cells.
  4. Screen hybridomas to find the one making the right antibody; clone it.
  5. Grow the clone in large cultures — they produce unlimited quantities of a single (monoclonal) antibody.

Uses of monoclonal antibodies

  • Pregnancy tests — detect the hormone hCG (human chorionic gonadotropin) in urine. Antibodies in the test strip bind hCG and change colour.
  • Diagnosis — e.g. detecting antigens of particular pathogens or cancer markers in blood.
  • Targeted drug delivery for cancer — a toxin or radioactive substance is attached to an antibody that binds only to tumour cells, so the drug kills cancer cells while sparing healthy cells.
  • Research — locating specific molecules inside cells.
Don't confuse monoclonal antibodies (pure, all identical) with normal antibodies produced in an immune response (polyclonal — from many lymphocyte clones, directed at many different antigens).

5.15 Non-Communicable Diseases (NCDs)

NCDs are not caused by pathogens and cannot be spread from person to person. They tend to develop slowly and are strongly influenced by lifestyle and genetic risk factors.

Cardiovascular disease (CVD)

Coronary heart disease (CHD) is the commonest form of CVD. The coronary arteries supply the heart muscle with oxygenated blood. Fatty deposits called atheromas (plaques) build up inside these arteries, narrowing them and reducing blood flow. If blood flow is blocked completely (e.g. by a clot forming on a plaque), part of the heart muscle dies — a heart attack.

Risk factors: smoking, high saturated-fat diet, high cholesterol, high blood pressure, obesity, lack of exercise, heavy alcohol use, stress, age, genetics, type 2 diabetes.

Cancer

Cancer is a disease caused by uncontrolled cell division. Cells grow into a lump called a tumour.
Type of tumourBehaviour
BenignCells stay in one place inside a capsule; do not invade other tissue; rarely life-threatening (though can press on organs).
Malignant (cancerous)Cells invade surrounding tissue and can break off, enter the blood and form new (secondary) tumours elsewhere — metastasis.

Risk factors: smoking (lung, throat, bladder cancers), UV radiation (skin cancer), ionising radiation, asbestos and other carcinogens, certain viruses (HPV → cervical cancer; HBV → liver cancer), obesity, alcohol, and inherited mutations (e.g. BRCA1/2 increases breast cancer risk).

Obesity

Obesity is excess body fat, measured by:

"Apple-shaped" fat around the belly (high ratio) is more dangerous to the heart than "pear-shaped" fat on hips/thighs.

Effects of alcohol

  • Liver: long-term drinking causes fatty liver → alcoholic hepatitis (inflammation) → cirrhosis (scarring). A scarred liver cannot detoxify, make proteins or store glycogen; eventually fatal.
  • Brain: impaired judgement and reaction times in the short term; long-term use damages brain cells, causing memory loss, mood problems and dementia-like conditions.
  • Drinking during pregnancy → fetal alcohol syndrome (developmental/facial abnormalities).

Effects of smoking

  • Lungs: tar damages cilia so mucus (and pathogens) build up → smoker's cough, bronchitis; tar contains carcinogens → lung cancer; breakdown of alveoli walls → emphysema (shortness of breath).
  • Circulation: nicotine raises heart rate and blood pressure; carbon monoxide binds irreversibly to haemoglobin, reducing oxygen carriage; chemicals damage artery linings → faster build-up of atheromas → heart attack and stroke risk.
  • In pregnancy: low birth weight, increased miscarriage/stillbirth.

5.17 Risk Factors, Correlation and Causation

A risk factor is anything that increases the probability of developing a disease. Risk factors can be modifiable (diet, smoking, exercise) or non-modifiable (age, sex, genetics).

Reading data on risk

Epidemiologists study huge populations to pick out patterns. They express risk in several ways:

  • Incidence — number of new cases in a year per 100 000 people.
  • Prevalence — number of people currently with the disease.
  • Relative risk — how much more likely you are to develop the disease if exposed (e.g. "smokers are 20× more likely to get lung cancer than non-smokers").

Correlation vs causation

A correlation is a statistical link between two variables. Causation is when one variable actually causes the other. Correlation does not prove causation.

  • Ice-cream sales and drownings are correlated — but neither causes the other. The real cause is hot weather.
  • Smoking and lung cancer were correlated for decades before causation was finally proven by controlled biological studies showing tar causes DNA damage in airway cells.

To go from correlation to causation, scientists look for:

  1. A plausible biological mechanism (how could A cause B?).
  2. A dose–response relationship (more A = more B).
  3. Consistency across multiple large studies.
  4. Experimental evidence — e.g. animal studies or natural experiments (e.g. smoking rates falling, lung cancer rates falling a decade later).
Many NCDs are strongly linked to preventable lifestyle factors. This is why public-health campaigns target smoking, diet, alcohol and exercise — modest changes across a population can prevent huge numbers of heart attacks, strokes and cancers.
