3.1 Sexual vs Asexual Reproduction

Sexual reproduction involves the fusion of male and female gametes (sex cells). Each parent contributes half of the offspring's genetic material, producing offspring that are genetically different from both parents and from one another.

Asexual reproduction involves only one parent and no gametes. The offspring are produced by mitosis and are genetically identical clones of the parent.

Reproduction is the production of new individuals of the same species. Organisms have evolved two very different strategies for doing this. Sexual reproduction mixes the genetic material of two parents, producing variation that is vital for evolution and adaptation. Asexual reproduction produces identical copies quickly without needing a mate — efficient when conditions are stable and favourable.

Gametes and Fertilisation

In animals, the male gamete is the sperm and the female gamete is the egg (ovum). In flowering plants, pollen nuclei fertilise the egg cell inside the ovule. Gametes are haploid — they contain only one copy of each chromosome. When a sperm fuses with an egg at fertilisation, the resulting zygote is diploid: it has two copies of each chromosome, one from each parent. In humans this means 23 chromosomes from each gamete combine to give the normal body-cell count of 46.

Know the numbers: human body cells have 46 chromosomes (23 pairs — diploid, 2n). Human gametes have 23 chromosomes (haploid, n). After fertilisation 23 + 23 = 46 restores the diploid number.

Organisms That Use Both

Some organisms can switch between sexual and asexual reproduction depending on conditions.

  • Malaria parasite (Plasmodium) — reproduces asexually inside a human host (fast multiplication), then sexually inside a mosquito.
  • Fungi — spread by asexual spores when food is plentiful, but reproduce sexually to create variation under stress.
  • Strawberry plants — produce flowers (sexual) and runners (asexual clones).
  • Daphnia (water fleas) — reproduce asexually in good conditions, sexually when conditions deteriorate.
If a question asks for the advantage of using both: asexual reproduction produces many offspring rapidly when the environment is favourable; sexual reproduction produces variation to survive when conditions change.

3.2 Meiosis

Meiosis is a type of cell division that produces four genetically different haploid gametes from one diploid parent cell. It involves two rounds of division following a single round of DNA replication.

Meiosis only happens in the reproductive organs (testes, ovaries, anthers, ovaries of a plant). It is essential for sexual reproduction because it halves the chromosome number and shuffles the genetic material, so that fertilisation can restore the diploid number and produce a unique individual.

Overview of the Stages

Before meiosis begins, the DNA is replicated during interphase, so every chromosome is copied into two identical sister chromatids (as in mitosis). Then two divisions follow:

  1. Meiosis I — homologous chromosomes (one from mum, one from dad) pair up and then separate. This halves the chromosome number (diploid → haploid). Crossing over occurs between pairs, swapping sections of DNA.
  2. Meiosis II — the sister chromatids are separated (like mitosis). This produces four haploid daughter cells.

Why Meiosis Creates Genetic Variation

  • Independent assortment — homologous pairs line up randomly, so each gamete gets a random mixture of maternal and paternal chromosomes.
  • Crossing over — at the start of meiosis I, chromatids of homologous pairs swap sections of DNA, creating new combinations of alleles.
  • Random fertilisation — any sperm can fertilise any egg, adding a further layer of variation.
Remember: meiosis = many cells, more different. Mitosis = two identical cells. You do NOT need to learn the detailed stages of meiosis (prophase I, metaphase I, etc.) at GCSE — just the outcome: one diploid cell → four haploid, genetically different gametes.

3.3 DNA Structure

DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid) is the chemical that stores genetic information. It is made of two strands wound around each other to form a double helix. Each strand is a polymer of smaller units called nucleotides.

DNA is found in the nucleus of every eukaryotic cell, organised into chromosomes. Every living organism uses DNA (with the exception of some viruses that use RNA). The molecular structure of DNA was worked out in 1953 by James Watson and Francis Crick, using X-ray diffraction images produced by Rosalind Franklin and Maurice Wilkins.

Nucleotides

Each nucleotide has three parts:

  • A sugar (deoxyribose)
  • A phosphate group
  • A base — one of four: Adenine (A), Thymine (T), Cytosine (C), Guanine (G)

The sugars and phosphates link alternately to form the backbone of each strand. The bases stick out sideways from the backbone and pair up with bases on the opposite strand.

Complementary Base Pairing

The two strands are held together by hydrogen bonds between specific base pairs:

BasePairs with
Adenine (A)Thymine (T)
Cytosine (C)Guanine (G)

A pairs only with T; C pairs only with G. This means if you know the sequence of one strand, you automatically know the other. For example, if one strand reads ATTGCA, the complementary strand must read TAACGT.

Remember the pairing with "Apples in the Tree, Cars in the Garage" — A-T and C-G. The pairs are complementary, not identical.

Chromosomes, Genes and DNA

DNA is very long — if unwound, one human cell contains about 2 metres of DNA. It is coiled up tightly around proteins (histones) to form chromosomes.

  • A chromosome is a single long molecule of DNA wrapped around proteins.
  • A gene is a section of DNA on a chromosome that codes for a particular protein.
  • An allele is a different version of the same gene (e.g. the gene for eye colour has alleles for brown, blue, green).

3.4 The Genome

The genome of an organism is the entire set of its genetic material — all of its DNA.
A gene is a section of DNA that codes for a specific protein (or sequence of amino acids).

The human genome is about 3.2 billion base pairs long, organised onto 23 pairs of chromosomes, and contains roughly 20,000 genes. Most of the DNA does not code for proteins — these non-coding regions regulate when genes are switched on or off.

The Human Genome Project

The Human Genome Project (1990–2003) was an international research project that sequenced all 3.2 billion base pairs of the human genome. It has had far-reaching consequences for medicine and biology.

3.5 Genotype and Phenotype

Genotype = the combination of alleles an organism has for a gene (e.g. BB, Bb, bb).
Phenotype = the observable physical characteristic produced by the genotype (e.g. brown eyes, blue eyes).

Key Vocabulary

TermMeaning
AlleleA version of a gene. Humans have two alleles for each gene — one on each chromosome of a pair.
HomozygousThe two alleles are the same, e.g. BB or bb.
HeterozygousThe two alleles are different, e.g. Bb.
DominantAn allele that is always expressed if present. Shown with a capital letter (B).
RecessiveAn allele that is only expressed if the organism has two copies of it (homozygous). Shown with a lowercase letter (b).

For a gene with a dominant allele B and a recessive allele b:

  • BB — homozygous dominant — phenotype: dominant trait
  • Bb — heterozygous — phenotype: dominant trait (the B masks the b)
  • bb — homozygous recessive — phenotype: recessive trait
Always use the same letter for both alleles of a gene, with capital = dominant and lowercase = recessive. Using B and b (not B and n) makes it clear they are two alleles of the same gene.

3.6 Monohybrid Inheritance and Punnett Squares

Monohybrid inheritance is the inheritance of a single characteristic controlled by one gene.
A Punnett square is a diagram used to predict the possible offspring of a genetic cross.

To build a Punnett square:

  1. Write the possible gametes of one parent along the top.
  2. Write the possible gametes of the other parent down the side.
  3. Combine the row and column alleles in each box to give the possible offspring genotypes.
  4. Work out the phenotype ratio from the offspring.

Example 1: BB × bb (Homozygous Dominant × Homozygous Recessive)

A homozygous brown-eyed parent (BB) crosses with a homozygous blue-eyed parent (bb). B = brown (dominant), b = blue (recessive).

BB
bBbBb
bBbBb

Results: 100% Bb (heterozygous). All offspring have brown eyes but carry the blue allele. Phenotype ratio: all brown eyes (4:0).

Example 2: Bb × Bb (Heterozygous × Heterozygous)

Two brown-eyed parents who both carry the blue allele.

Bb
BBBBb
bBbbb

Results: 1 BB : 2 Bb : 1 bb. Phenotype ratio: 3 brown : 1 blue. There is a 25% (1 in 4) chance of a blue-eyed child.

Example 3: Bb × bb (Heterozygous × Homozygous Recessive — "Test Cross")

A brown-eyed carrier parent crosses with a blue-eyed parent.

Bb
bBbbb
bBbbb

Results: 2 Bb : 2 bb. Phenotype ratio: 1 brown : 1 blue. There is a 50% chance of a blue-eyed child.

Example 4: BB × Bb

BB
BBBBB
bBbBb

Results: 2 BB : 2 Bb. Phenotype ratio: all brown eyes. No blue-eyed offspring possible.

Always state the phenotype ratio after doing the Punnett square — a common mistake is just listing genotypes. If the question asks for probability, express it as a percentage or fraction (e.g. 25%, 1/4).

3.7 Pedigree Diagrams

A pedigree diagram is a family tree that shows how a genetic trait is inherited across generations.

Conventions

  • Circles = female; squares = male
  • Shaded = affected (has the trait/disorder); unshaded = unaffected
  • A horizontal line between two people = a mating/couple
  • A vertical line going down = children of that couple
  • Half-shaded = known carrier (heterozygous for a recessive disorder)

Interpreting a Pedigree

Key things to check when deducing the inheritance pattern of a trait:

  • Does it skip generations? If yes, it is likely recessive (carriers pass it on invisibly).
  • Do two unaffected parents have an affected child? The trait must be recessive (both parents are heterozygous carriers).
  • Does every affected person have an affected parent? Likely dominant.
  • Is it mostly males? Might be X-linked recessive (e.g. red-green colour blindness, haemophilia).
If two unaffected parents produce an affected child, the disorder must be recessive. Both parents must be heterozygous carriers (Aa × Aa).

Worked Example: Cystic Fibrosis

Cystic fibrosis is caused by a recessive allele (f). Both parents are heterozygous carriers (Ff). What is the probability that their child will have cystic fibrosis?

Ff
FFFFf
fFfff

Genotypes: 1 FF : 2 Ff : 1 ff. Only ff individuals have CF.

  • Probability of unaffected (FF or Ff): 3/4 = 75%
  • Probability of carrier (Ff): 2/4 = 50%
  • Probability of having CF (ff): 1/4 = 25%

Worked Example: Polydactyly

Polydactyly (extra fingers/toes) is caused by a dominant allele (P). If one parent is heterozygous (Pp) and the other is unaffected (pp), what is the probability their child has polydactyly?

Pp
pPppp
pPppp

Phenotype ratio: 1 polydactyly : 1 unaffected — 50% chance of polydactyly.

Probabilities do NOT guarantee numbers. "25% chance" means each child has a 1 in 4 chance; it does NOT mean exactly 1 in every 4 children will have the disorder. Small families may not match the predicted ratio at all.

3.9 Sex Determination in Humans

In humans, sex is determined by the 23rd pair of chromosomes, called the sex chromosomes.
XX = female
XY = male

The Y chromosome carries a gene (SRY) that triggers the development of male sex characteristics in the embryo. Without a Y, the default pathway is female. All the other 22 pairs are called autosomes and are the same in males and females.

Sex Inheritance Punnett Square

Females (XX) can only produce eggs containing X. Males (XY) produce sperm containing either X or Y in roughly equal numbers. The sex of the baby is therefore determined by the father's sperm.

XY
XXX (♀)XY (♂)
XXX (♀)XY (♂)

Results: 2 XX : 2 XY = 1 female : 1 male. So there is a 50% chance of a boy and a 50% chance of a girl at each pregnancy.

The father determines the sex of the baby, not the mother — because he provides either X or Y, while the mother always provides X. Historically this was often misunderstood, sometimes with tragic consequences for women.

3.10 Genetic Disorders

A genetic disorder is a condition caused by a faulty allele inherited from one or both parents.

Cystic Fibrosis (Recessive)

  • Caused by a recessive allele of the CFTR gene on chromosome 7.
  • The faulty CFTR protein normally moves chloride ions across cell membranes. When it fails, the mucus lining airways, the pancreas and the reproductive tract becomes thick and sticky.
  • Symptoms: chest infections (mucus traps bacteria in lungs); breathing difficulty; poor digestion (mucus blocks pancreatic ducts so enzymes can't reach the gut); reduced fertility.
  • Inheritance: must have two faulty copies (ff) to show the disorder. Carriers (Ff) are unaffected but can pass it on.
  • In the UK, about 1 in 25 people are carriers.

Polydactyly (Dominant)

  • Caused by a dominant allele — only one copy needed to show the condition.
  • Causes extra fingers or toes (usually harmless, often removed in infancy if inconvenient).
  • An affected parent has a 50% chance of passing it on to each child (if heterozygous).
  • Because it's dominant, the disorder does not skip generations in a family tree.

Embryo Screening (Pre-implantation Genetic Diagnosis)

During IVF, embryos can be tested for genetic disorders before being implanted. One cell is removed from a 2–3 day old embryo and its DNA is analysed. Only embryos free of the tested disorders are implanted.

Benefits of Embryo Screening

  • Prevents the birth of children with serious, life-shortening disorders.
  • Reduces suffering of the child and stress on the family.
  • Saves the NHS money in the long term.
  • Laws stop it being abused (in the UK, screening is only allowed for specific serious conditions).

Ethical Objections

  • Some view it as a step towards "designer babies" — choosing traits like eye colour, intelligence or gender.
  • It implies that people living with these conditions are less valued; they would never have been born under this system.
  • Embryos that are not selected are destroyed — some religious and ethical traditions view this as destroying a human life.
  • The procedure is expensive and not available to everyone equally.
  • Screening is never 100% accurate — errors could reject healthy embryos or allow disorders through.
In an evaluate question about embryo screening, always give BOTH benefits and ethical objections, and finish with a reasoned conclusion that states your view and why.

3.11 Variation

Variation is the differences between individuals of the same species. It can be caused by genes, the environment, or a combination of both.

Genetic Variation

Inherited differences in DNA. Caused by:

  • Mutations — random changes in DNA that introduce new alleles.
  • Meiosis — independent assortment and crossing over.
  • Sexual reproduction — random fertilisation combines alleles from two parents.

Examples of purely genetic characteristics: ABO blood group, earlobe shape (attached or free), natural hair colour, sex.

Environmental Variation

Differences caused by the environment — not inherited (usually). Includes:

  • Diet and nutrition (affects growth, body mass)
  • Climate (skin darkens with sun exposure)
  • Lifestyle (exercise, smoking)
  • Disease or accident (scars, amputation)
  • Language spoken / culture / accent

Both Genetic and Environmental

Most human characteristics are controlled by a combination of genes AND environment. Examples:

  • Height: determined by many genes, but also affected by childhood nutrition.
  • Body mass: genes set a tendency; diet and exercise determine the actual weight.
  • Skin colour: genes determine base tone; UV exposure affects darkness.
  • Intelligence test scores: genes and upbringing/education both contribute.

If you see a bell-shaped graph or data that can take any value in a range, it's continuous variation — controlled by many genes and affected by environment. If it falls into clear categories, it's discontinuous — usually one gene.

3.12 Mutations

A mutation is a random change in the base sequence of DNA. Mutations occur continuously in every cell, and they are the ultimate source of all new genetic variation.

Effects of Mutations

Most mutations have no effect on the phenotype. Some are harmful; a few are beneficial.

Type of effectExplanationExample
Neutral (most)The change is in non-coding DNA or does not alter the protein (silent mutation); phenotype unchanged.A base change that codes for the same amino acid (the genetic code is degenerate).
HarmfulThe protein produced is faulty or missing; the organism's function is impaired.CFTR mutation → cystic fibrosis; mutation in a proto-oncogene → cancer.
Beneficial (rare)The new protein has an improved function or gives some advantage.Lactase persistence in adults; sickle-cell allele confers malaria resistance in heterozygotes.

How Mutations Cause Variation

A mutation in a gamete can be passed on to offspring, introducing a new allele into the population. Natural selection then acts on this new allele: if it improves survival or reproduction, it will become more common over generations. If it is harmful, it will usually be eliminated.

Causes of Mutations

  • Errors during DNA replication (copying mistakes).
  • Mutagens — agents that increase mutation rate, e.g. UV light, X-rays, tobacco smoke, some chemicals.

Effects on the Phenotype

A mutation in a gene may:

  • Change a single amino acid in the protein — may affect protein shape and function.
  • Introduce a stop signal early — the protein is truncated and non-functional.
  • Shift the reading frame (insertion or deletion) — most of the protein becomes nonsense.
  • Have no effect (silent mutation or in a non-coding region).
Key examination phrase: most mutations are neutral. A common mistake is to say most mutations are harmful. Only a small proportion have any phenotypic effect at all, and of those, most are harmful but a few are beneficial.

Mutations in Non-Coding DNA

Not all DNA codes for proteins. Some non-coding regions control when and how much a gene is expressed. A mutation here can affect the amount of protein made, which can still alter the phenotype — even without changing the protein itself.

Summary of Topic 3: Sexual reproduction uses meiosis to produce genetically varied haploid gametes. DNA stores information in a double helix; genes code for proteins; the full set is the genome. Alleles determine traits; Punnett squares predict offspring ratios. Sex is determined by X and Y chromosomes. Genetic disorders like cystic fibrosis (recessive) and polydactyly (dominant) can be screened for in embryos — a technology with both medical benefits and ethical concerns. Variation is continuous or discontinuous, caused by genes, environment, or both. Mutations — random changes in DNA — are the source of all new alleles.
