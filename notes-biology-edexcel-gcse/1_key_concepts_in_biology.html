1.1 Microscopes and Magnification

A microscope is an instrument used to view objects that are too small to be seen with the naked eye. It works by using lenses (in a light microscope) or electron beams (in an electron microscope) to produce a magnified image of a specimen.

The development of microscopes transformed biology. In the 1600s, Robert Hooke used an early light microscope to observe cork cells — the first time anyone had seen cells. Antonie van Leeuwenhoek improved lenses and observed bacteria ("animalcules") for the first time. As microscope technology improved, scientists were able to see finer and finer detail: first cells, then organelles, then the internal structure of organelles themselves. The key idea is that as resolution improves, we can see more detail.

Magnification and Resolution

TermDefinitionKey point
MagnificationHow many times larger an image appears compared to the actual size of the specimenA higher magnification makes the image bigger but does not necessarily make it clearer.
ResolutionThe shortest distance between two points that can still be distinguished as separateHigher resolution = more detail visible. Limited by the wavelength of the radiation used (light vs. electrons).

Increasing magnification without improving resolution just produces a bigger blurry image. Electron microscopes have much higher resolution than light microscopes because electrons have a much shorter wavelength than visible light, allowing them to resolve much smaller structures.

A common exam question: "Why can electron microscopes see smaller structures than light microscopes?" Answer: electrons have a much shorter wavelength than light, giving a higher resolution, so smaller structures can be resolved as separate.

Units of Measurement in Biology

Biologists work at a huge range of scales, so you must be comfortable converting between millimetres (mm), micrometres (µm) and nanometres (nm).

UnitSymbolRelation to metreTypical use
millimetremm1 × 10−3 mSmall organisms, ruler measurements
micrometreµm1 × 10−6 mCells (most 10–100 µm), organelles
nanometrenm1 × 10−9 mRibosomes, viruses, membranes

Conversions: 1 mm = 1000 µm, 1 µm = 1000 nm, 1 mm = 1,000,000 nm.

The Magnification Equation

Magnification = Image size ÷ Actual size
Rearranged: Image size = magnification × actual size, and Actual size = image size ÷ magnification.

Always ensure image size and actual size are in the same units before dividing. Convert to micrometres if the question gives mixed units.

Worked example 1
  A plant cell appears 40 mm wide in an image taken at ×400 magnification.
  What is the actual width of the cell in µm?

  Image size = 40 mm = 40,000 µm
  Actual = image / magnification = 40,000 / 400 = 100 µm

Worked example 2
  A mitochondrion is 2 µm long. How long does it appear on an
  image taken at ×50,000?

  Image = magnification × actual = 50,000 × 2 = 100,000 µm = 100 mm
Always check units! A wrong answer by a factor of 1000 is almost always a unit conversion error (mm vs µm). Show your working clearly — the final answer is usually only worth 1 mark, but correctly rearranging the equation earns additional marks.

1.2 Animal, Plant and Bacterial Cells

All living organisms are made of cells. There are two main types: eukaryotic cells (animal, plant, fungi, protists) which have a true nucleus and membrane-bound organelles, and prokaryotic cells (bacteria) which do not.

"What do animal and plant cells have in common?" Answer: nucleus, cell membrane, cytoplasm, mitochondria, ribosomes. Plant cells also have cell wall, chloroplasts, and a permanent vacuole. Don't say "plant cells have more organelles" — be specific.

1.4 Specialised Cells

Multicellular organisms contain many types of cell. Each type is specialised — its structure is adapted to carry out a particular function efficiently. This is called the structure-function relationship.

Sperm Cell

  • Function: male gamete — carries genetic material to fertilise an egg.
  • Long tail (flagellum): allows the sperm to swim towards the egg.
  • Many mitochondria in the middle section: release energy from respiration for tail movement.
  • Acrosome (at the head): contains digestive enzymes that break through the egg's outer membrane.
  • Haploid nucleus: carries 23 chromosomes so that fertilisation restores the full 46.
  • Streamlined head: reduces resistance while swimming.

Egg Cell (Ovum)

  • Function: female gamete — carries genetic material and provides nutrients for the early embryo.
  • Large cytoplasm full of nutrients (yolk): supplies the embryo with energy before implantation.
  • Jelly-like outer coat (zona pellucida): changes chemically after fertilisation so that only one sperm can enter — prevents polyspermy.
  • Haploid nucleus: contains 23 chromosomes.

Ciliated Epithelial Cell

  • Function: lines surfaces such as the trachea, bronchi, and fallopian tubes.
  • Cilia on the surface: tiny hair-like projections that beat in a rhythmic wave to move mucus (with trapped dust/bacteria) out of the lungs, or an egg down the fallopian tube.
  • Many mitochondria: provide energy for the continuous beating of the cilia.
For specialised cell questions, always link structure to function. Don't just say "sperm has a tail"; say "the tail allows the sperm to swim to the egg." Don't just list features — explain the adaptation.

1.5 Enzymes — Lock-and-Key Model

An enzyme is a biological catalyst — a protein that speeds up a chemical reaction without being used up. Enzymes are essential for life because reactions in cells would otherwise be far too slow at body temperature.

Every enzyme has a region called the active site. Only molecules with a matching shape (the substrate) can fit into the active site. This is called the lock-and-key model: the enzyme is the lock and the substrate is the key — only the correct key fits.

  1. Substrate collides with the enzyme and fits into the active site, forming an enzyme–substrate complex.
  2. The reaction occurs — the substrate is broken down (or built up) into product(s).
  3. Product leaves the active site. The enzyme is unchanged and can be reused.

Because the shape of the active site is so specific, each enzyme catalyses only one reaction — this is called enzyme specificity.

1.6 Factors Affecting Enzyme Activity

Three main factors affect how fast an enzyme catalyses a reaction: temperature, pH, and substrate concentration.

Effect of Temperature

  • At low temperatures: molecules have little kinetic energy, so fewer successful collisions — rate is low.
  • As temperature rises: molecules move faster, more collisions per second — rate increases.
  • At the optimum temperature (around 37°C for human enzymes): rate is at its maximum.
  • Above the optimum: bonds holding the enzyme's 3D shape break. The active site changes shape so the substrate no longer fits — the enzyme is denatured. Denaturation is permanent.

Effect of pH

  • Each enzyme has an optimum pH. Most human enzymes work best around pH 7, but pepsin (in the stomach) works best at pH 2, and enzymes in the small intestine work best around pH 8.
  • Above or below the optimum pH, the H+ and OH− ions disrupt bonds holding the enzyme together, changing the active site's shape and denaturing the enzyme.

Effect of Substrate Concentration

  • As substrate concentration increases, the rate of reaction increases because more collisions between substrate and enzyme occur per second.
  • At high substrate concentration, all enzyme active sites are occupied — the enzyme is the limiting factor. Adding more substrate has no effect; the graph levels off (plateaus).
  • Adding more enzyme at this point would increase the rate again.
Never say an enzyme is "killed" by high temperature — enzymes aren't alive! The correct term is denatured: the 3D shape is permanently changed so the substrate no longer fits. Denaturation is irreversible.

1.8 Calculating Rate of Reaction

Rate of reaction = amount of substrate used (or product formed) ÷ time taken
Units depend on the measurement used — cm3/s, g/min, mol/s, etc. 
Worked example
  In an experiment, 36 cm³ of oxygen is produced by catalase
  in 60 seconds. Calculate the rate.

  Rate = 36 / 60 = 0.6 cm³/s

Worked example 2
  A starch solution is completely digested by amylase in 4 minutes.
  The starting amount was 20 mg.

  Rate = 20 / 4 = 5 mg/min

1.10 Uses of Energy in the Body

Energy released from glucose during respiration is used for many processes:

  • Metabolism — building up larger molecules (e.g. proteins from amino acids, starch from glucose).
  • Movement — muscle contraction requires ATP (energy).
  • Keeping warm — maintaining body temperature at 37°C (especially in mammals and birds).
  • Making molecules — synthesising enzymes, hormones, DNA, and other biological molecules.
  • Active transport — moving substances against a concentration gradient across membranes.
  • Cell division — growth, repair, and reproduction.

1.11 Transport Across Cell Membranes

Cells must constantly exchange substances with their surroundings — taking in oxygen, glucose, water and ions, and getting rid of carbon dioxide and urea. There are three main transport processes:

Diffusion

Diffusion is the net movement of particles from a region of high concentration to low concentration, down the concentration gradient. It is a passive process — no energy from respiration is required.

Examples in biology: oxygen diffusing from alveoli into blood; carbon dioxide diffusing from cells into blood; urea diffusing from cells into blood.

Factors Affecting Rate of Diffusion (Fick's Law)

Rate of diffusion ∝ (surface area × concentration difference) ÷ distance
  • Surface area: larger surface area = more particles can move across at once = faster rate (e.g. alveoli are folded into tiny sacs to increase SA).
  • Concentration difference (gradient): steeper gradient = greater net movement = faster rate.
  • Distance (membrane thickness): shorter distance = faster rate (e.g. alveolus walls are just one cell thick).
  • Temperature: higher temperature = particles have more kinetic energy = faster rate.

Osmosis

Osmosis is the movement of water molecules from a region of higher water concentration (dilute solution) to lower water concentration (concentrated solution), across a partially permeable membrane. It is passive.
SolutionMeaningEffect on animal cellEffect on plant cell
HypotonicLess solute (more water) than the cellWater enters; cell swells and may burst (lysis)Water enters vacuole; cell becomes turgid (firm) — cell wall prevents bursting
IsotonicEqual solute/water to the cellNo net movement; cell unchangedNo net movement; cell normal
HypertonicMore solute (less water) than the cellWater leaves; cell shrinks and shrivels (crenated)Water leaves vacuole; cell becomes plasmolysed — membrane pulls away from cell wall

Active Transport

Active transport is the movement of particles against a concentration gradient (from low to high concentration) across a membrane. It requires energy from respiration (ATP).

Examples:

  • Root hair cells absorb mineral ions (e.g. nitrate, magnesium) from the soil even when the soil has a lower concentration than the cell.
  • Glucose is absorbed from the intestine into blood after the gradient has reversed (once blood glucose is higher than gut glucose).
  • Sodium-potassium pumps in nerve cells maintain ion gradients essential for electrical signals.
The three transport processes differ in three key ways: (1) What moves — any particle (diffusion), water only (osmosis), specific ions/molecules (active transport). (2) Direction — down the gradient (diffusion, osmosis) vs against it (active transport). (3) Energy — passive (diffusion, osmosis) vs requires ATP (active transport). Always state which process is which in exam answers.
