⚗ PAG1: Moles Determination

Apparatus

  • Gas volume: Conical flask, gas syringe (100 cm³), delivery tube, balance, measuring cylinder, Mg ribbon, dilute HCl
  • Water of crystallisation: Crucible, pipe-clay triangle, tripod, Bunsen burner, balance (to 0.01 g), hydrated salt (e.g., CuSO₄·5H₂O)

Method (A — Molar Gas Volume)

  1. Weigh a known length of Mg ribbon accurately.
  2. Place excess dilute HCl in a conical flask connected to a gas syringe.
  3. Add the Mg ribbon, quickly seal, and collect the H₂ gas.
  4. Record the final volume of gas at room temperature and pressure.
  5. Calculate: n(Mg) = mass/Aᵣ. Since Mg + 2HCl → MgCl₂ + H₂, n(H₂) = n(Mg).
  6. Molar gas volume = V(gas) / n(H₂).

Method (B — Water of Crystallisation)

  1. Weigh a clean, dry crucible. Add hydrated salt and weigh again.
  2. Heat gently at first, then more strongly. Allow to cool. Weigh.
  3. Reheat, cool, and reweigh until constant mass is achieved (to ensure all water is driven off).
  4. Mass of water = mass before - mass after. Mass of anhydrous salt = final mass - crucible mass.
  5. Calculate n(water) and n(salt). The ratio gives x in the formula MX·xH₂O.

Safety

  • H₂ gas is flammable — no naked flames near gas syringe method.
  • Hot crucible — use tongs; place on heatproof mat.
  • Dilute HCl is irritant — goggles.

Analysis

Gas volume: Molar gas volume ≈ 24,000 cm³ mol⁻¹ at room temperature and pressure.

Water of crystallisation: Calculate the integer ratio to find x. For CuSO₄·xH₂O, expect x = 5.

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Gas escaping before bung fittedSystematicSeal quickly; use excess acid
Gas syringe frictionSystematicTap gently; lubricate
Not heating to constant massSystematicReheat and reweigh until mass is constant
Salt spitting out of crucibleSystematicHeat gently at first; use a lid
Reabsorption of water on coolingSystematicCool in a desiccator
Exam Tip: "Heating to constant mass" means heating, cooling, weighing, reheating, and reweighing until the mass doesn't change (difference < 0.01 g). This confirms all water of crystallisation has been removed. Always cool in a desiccator to prevent reabsorption of moisture.
Worked Example — Water of Crystallisation:

Mass of crucible = 15.20 g. Mass of crucible + hydrated salt = 20.18 g. Mass after heating to constant mass = 18.38 g.

Mass of water lost = 20.18 − 18.38 = 1.80 g → n(H2O) = 1.80 / 18.0 = 0.100 mol
Mass of anhydrous salt = 18.38 − 15.20 = 3.18 g → n(CuSO4) = 3.18 / 159.6 = 0.0199 mol
Ratio = 0.100 / 0.0199 = 5.02 ≈ 5, so formula is CuSO4·5H2O

⚗ PAG2: Acid-Base and Redox Titrations

Apparatus

  • Burette (50 cm³), pipette (25.0 cm³), conical flasks
  • White tile, indicators (for acid-base)
  • Acid-base: Standard acid/alkali solutions
  • Redox: KMnO₄ solution, acidified Fe²⁺ solution (Mohr's salt + H₂SO₄)

Method (Redox — KMnO₄/Fe²⁺)

  1. Pipette 25.0 cm³ of acidified Fe²⁺ solution into a conical flask.
  2. Fill the burette with standardised KMnO₄ solution.
  3. Add KMnO₄ slowly, swirling. The purple colour is decolourised as MnO₄⁻ is reduced to Mn²⁺.
  4. End point: the first permanent pale pink colour that persists for 30 seconds (no indicator needed — KMnO₄ is self-indicating).
  5. Record the titre. Repeat for concordant results.
  6. MnO₄⁻ + 5Fe²⁺ + 8H⁺ → Mn²⁺ + 5Fe³⁺ + 4H₂O (1:5 ratio).

Safety

  • KMnO₄ stains skin and is an oxidiser — wear gloves.
  • Sulfuric acid is corrosive — goggles.
  • Use H₂SO₄ (not HCl or HNO₃) to acidify — HCl would be oxidised by KMnO₄, HNO₃ is itself an oxidiser.

Analysis

Calculate: n(KMnO₄) = c × V(titre). n(Fe²⁺) = 5 × n(KMnO₄). Then [Fe²⁺] = n/V.

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Overshooting end pointSystematicAdd drop-wise near end; swirl between drops
KMnO₄ staining burette (hard to read)SystematicRinse well; read from top of meniscus (KMnO₄ is opaque)
Not using H₂SO₄ as the acidSystematicAlways use sulfuric acid for KMnO₄ titrations
Fe²⁺ oxidised by air before titrationSystematicUse freshly prepared solution; keep acidified
Exam Tip: For KMnO₄ titrations, read the burette from the TOP of the meniscus (not the bottom) because the solution is so dark you cannot see through it. No indicator is needed — KMnO₄ is self-indicating. The end point is a permanent faint pink colour.
Worked Example — Redox Titration:

25.0 cm³ of Fe²⁺ solution required 23.60 cm³ of 0.0200 mol dm⁻³ KMnO₄.

n(KMnO₄) = 0.0200 × 23.60/1000 = 4.72 × 10⁻⁴ mol
From equation: n(Fe²⁺) = 5 × n(KMnO₄) = 5 × 4.72 × 10⁻⁴ = 2.36 × 10⁻³ mol
[Fe²⁺] = 2.36 × 10⁻³ / 0.0250 = 0.0944 mol dm⁻³

⚗ PAG3: Enthalpy Changes by Calorimetry

Apparatus

  • Polystyrene cup with lid
  • Thermometer (±0.1 °C) or data logger
  • Measuring cylinder or pipette
  • Stopwatch
  • Reagents for Hess's law cycle (e.g., NaOH + HCl direct, and via NaOH + NH₄Cl then NH₃ + HCl)

Method

  1. Measure a known volume of solution into the polystyrene cup.
  2. Record temperature every minute for 3 minutes (baseline).
  3. Add the second reagent at the 4th minute. Stir and record temperature for 6+ more minutes.
  4. Plot temperature vs. time. Extrapolate to find the true ΔT.
  5. Calculate q = mcΔT. Then ΔH = -q/n.
  6. For Hess's Law: Perform two separate experiments to measure two enthalpy changes in a cycle. Use Hess's Law to calculate the third (unmeasurable) enthalpy change.

Safety

  • Acids and alkalis — corrosive, wear goggles.
  • Exothermic reactions — cup may become warm.

Analysis

Plot: Temperature vs. time with graphical extrapolation.

Hess's Law: ΔH₁ = ΔH₂ + ΔH₃ (or appropriate cycle). Compare calculated value with literature.

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Heat loss to surroundingsSystematicLid, insulation, graphical extrapolation
Assuming c = 4.18 and density = 1SystematicAcknowledged approximation
Heat absorbed by calorimeterSystematicDetermine calorimeter constant
Incomplete mixingRandomStir thoroughly; use magnetic stirrer
Exam Tip: Hess's Law: enthalpy change for a reaction is independent of the route taken, provided initial and final conditions are the same. You can measure ΔH(formation) indirectly by measuring ΔH(combustion) of reactants and products. Draw a clear energy cycle in your answer. Always use graphical extrapolation of your temperature-time plot to correct for heat loss — this significantly improves accuracy.

⚗ PAG4: Rates of Reaction

Apparatus

  • Conical flask, gas syringe, delivery tube, bung
  • Stopwatch
  • Measuring cylinders
  • CaCO₃ (marble chips) and dilute HCl (for concentration investigation)
  • Na₂S₂O₃ and HCl (for temperature investigation)
  • Water baths at various temperatures

Method

  1. Continuous monitoring (concentration): Set up flask + gas syringe. Use different concentrations of HCl (same excess CaCO₃). Record volume of CO₂ every 30s. Plot volume vs. time for each concentration.
  2. Effect of temperature: Use Na₂S₂O₃ + HCl disappearing cross method at different temperatures. Record time for cross to disappear. Rate = 1/t.
  3. Control variables: keep total volume constant (for concentration); keep volumes constant (for temperature).

Safety

  • HCl is irritant — goggles.
  • SO₂ from thiosulfate reaction — ventilated area.
  • Hot water baths — care with burns.

Analysis

Concentration: Plot volume vs. time curves. Initial gradient = initial rate. Plot initial rate vs. [HCl] to find order.

Temperature: Plot 1/t vs. T for exponential curve, or ln(1/t) vs. 1/T for Arrhenius plot. Gradient = -Eₐ/R.

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Gas escaping before bung fittedSystematicSeal quickly
Subjective cross disappearanceRandomSame observer; colorimeter
Temperature not maintainedSystematicUse thermostatted water bath
Uneven marble chip sizesRandomUse similar size/mass
Exam Tip: When drawing tangents on a volume-time curve, the tangent at t = 0 gives the initial rate. For the concentration investigation, if you use excess CaCO₃ with different [HCl], the initial rate changes but the final gas volume is the same (limited by the same mass of solid). For the disappearing cross experiment, rate = 1/t — this is an approximation of initial rate assuming the reaction is monitored over the same extent each time.

⚗ PAG5: Qualitative Tests for Ions

Apparatus

  • Test tubes, nichrome wire, Bunsen burner
  • NaOH(aq), NH₃(aq), AgNO₃(aq), BaCl₂(aq)
  • Dilute HNO₃, dilute HCl
  • Limewater, litmus paper

Method

  1. Flame tests: Clean wire in HCl, dip in sample, observe colour.
  2. Cation precipitate tests: Add NaOH or NH₃ to sample. Record precipitate colour and behaviour in excess.
  3. Halide tests: Acidify with HNO₃, add AgNO₃. White (Cl⁻), cream (Br⁻), yellow (I⁻).
  4. Sulfate test: Acidify with HNO₃, add BaCl₂. White ppt = SO₄²⁻.
  5. Carbonate test: Add HCl. Gas through limewater — milky = CO₃²⁻.
  6. Ammonium test: Add NaOH, warm gently. Test gas with damp red litmus — turns blue = NH₄⁺.

Safety

  • AgNO₃ stains — gloves. BaCl₂ is toxic.
  • Conc. HCl in fume cupboard.
  • Goggles throughout.

Analysis

Record: Systematic table of observations for each test and each unknown ion.

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Contaminated wire/glasswareSystematicClean thoroughly before each test
Na⁺ masking other flame coloursSystematicView through blue cobalt glass
Not acidifying before AgNO₃/BaCl₂SystematicAlways acidify with HNO₃ first
Similar precipitate coloursRandomCompare with known standards
Exam Tip: Al(OH)₃ dissolves in excess NaOH (amphoteric) but not in excess NH₃. Cu(OH)₂ dissolves in excess NH₃ (forms deep blue [Cu(NH₃)₄]²⁺) but not in excess NaOH. This is the key distinguishing test in exams. Always acidify with dilute HNO₃ before adding AgNO₃ (halide test) or BaCl₂ (sulfate test) to prevent false positives from carbonates or sulfites.

⚗ PAG6: Organic Preparation and Purification

Apparatus

  • Round-bottomed flask, anti-bumping granules
  • Reflux condenser (vertical), distillation condenser (angled)
  • Separating funnel
  • Buchner funnel, filter flask, vacuum pump (for solids)
  • Drying agent (anhydrous MgSO₄ or Na₂SO₄)
  • Melting point apparatus (solids) or thermometer (liquids)

Method

  1. Reflux: Heat reaction mixture with a vertical condenser to ensure complete reaction without losing volatile components.
  2. Distillation: Rearrange for distillation. Collect product at the correct boiling point range.
  3. Liquid purification: Use separating funnel to wash with Na₂CO₃ (removes acids) and water. Dry with anhydrous drying agent. Filter. Redistil.
  4. Solid purification (recrystallisation): Dissolve in minimum hot solvent, hot filter, cool slowly, Buchner filter, wash with cold solvent, dry.
  5. Test purity: melting point (solids) or boiling point (liquids).

Safety

  • Flammable organics — no naked flames; electric heating.
  • Fume cupboard for volatile substances.
  • Never heat sealed apparatus.
  • Vent separating funnel to release pressure.

Analysis

Calculate: Percentage yield = (actual/theoretical) × 100. Atom economy = (Mᵣ of desired product / sum of Mᵣ of all products) × 100.

Purity: Sharp melting/boiling point matching literature value = pure.

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Product lost during transfersSystematicMinimise transfers; rinse equipment
Incomplete reactionSystematicReflux for longer; use excess reagent
Impure product (broad mp/bp)SystematicRepeat recrystallisation/distillation
Wet product (lowers mp)SystematicDry thoroughly; use desiccator
Exam Tip: A pure solid has a sharp melting point matching the data book value. Impurities lower and broaden the melting point. For organic liquids, purity is confirmed by a sharp boiling point. Always explain each purification step and why it is necessary. Common yield-reducing factors: incomplete reaction, loss during transfer, loss during washing/filtration, side reactions.
Worked Example — Percentage Yield:

A student prepared aspirin from 2.00 g of salicylic acid. The theoretical yield is 2.61 g. The actual yield after purification was 1.85 g.

Percentage yield = (1.85 / 2.61) × 100 = 70.9%

This is a typical yield for a lab preparation. Losses occur during transfer, filtration, washing, and recrystallisation.

⚗ PAG7: Electrochemical Cells

Apparatus

  • Beakers, metal electrodes (Zn, Cu, Fe, Mg, etc.)
  • 1.0 mol dm⁻³ metal salt solutions
  • Salt bridge (KNO₃), high-resistance voltmeter
  • Connecting wires, crocodile clips, emery paper

Method

  1. Clean electrodes with emery paper.
  2. Set up half-cells in separate beakers with their metal ion solutions.
  3. Connect with a salt bridge and wires to voltmeter.
  4. Record EMF and polarity.
  5. Investigate: different metal pairs, effect of concentration, effect of temperature.
  6. Compare with E°(cell) = E°(cathode) - E°(anode).

Safety

  • Metal salt solutions — wear gloves and goggles.
  • Dispose of solutions correctly.

Analysis

Calculate: E°(cell) = E°(right) - E°(left). Compare measured and theoretical.

Investigate: How changing [ion] affects EMF (Nernst equation qualitatively).

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Oxide on electrodesSystematicClean immediately before use
Non-standard conditionsSystematicRecord T; use 1.0 mol dm⁻³ solutions
Salt bridge resistanceSystematicFresh salt bridge; good contact
Voltmeter drawing currentSystematicHigh-resistance voltmeter
Exam Tip: Electrons flow from the more negative electrode (anode, oxidation) to the more positive electrode (cathode, reduction) through the external circuit. Ions flow through the salt bridge to maintain electrical neutrality. The salt bridge does not allow electron flow. Remember: AN OX (anode = oxidation), RED CAT (reduction = cathode).

⚗ PAG8: pH Measurement and Buffer Solutions

Apparatus

  • Calibrated pH meter or pH probe
  • Buffer solutions: weak acid (e.g., CH₃COOH) + its conjugate base (CH₃COONa)
  • Dilute HCl and NaOH (for testing buffer action)
  • Measuring cylinders, beakers
  • Distilled water (as control)

Method

  1. Calibrate the pH meter using standard buffer solutions.
  2. Prepare a buffer solution by mixing known volumes of weak acid and its sodium salt.
  3. Measure the initial pH of the buffer.
  4. Add a small measured volume of dilute HCl. Record the new pH.
  5. Prepare a fresh buffer and add the same volume of NaOH. Record the pH.
  6. Repeat steps 4-5 with distilled water as a control.
  7. Compare: the buffer pH barely changes, while the water pH changes dramatically.
  8. Extension: Prepare buffers with different ratios of [HA]:[A⁻] and verify that pH = pKₐ + log([A⁻]/[HA]).

Safety

  • Weak acids are irritants — goggles.
  • NaOH is corrosive — gloves.
  • pH probes are fragile — handle carefully.

Analysis

Calculate: Expected pH using Henderson-Hasselbalch: pH = pKₐ + log([A⁻]/[HA]). Compare with measured pH.

Buffer capacity: Number of moles of H⁺/OH⁻ needed to change pH by 1 unit.

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
pH meter not calibratedSystematicCalibrate with at least 2 standard buffers
Inaccurate volumes of HA and A⁻RandomUse pipettes for precise volumes
CO₂ from air dissolving in solutionsSystematicUse freshly prepared solutions; work quickly
Temperature effects on pHSystematicRecord temperature; work at constant T
Exam Tip: A buffer resists pH change because: when H⁺ is added, A⁻ reacts with it (A⁻ + H⁺ → HA); when OH⁻ is added, HA reacts with it (HA + OH⁻ → A⁻ + H₂O). The buffer works best when [HA] ≈ [A⁻] (i.e., pH ≈ pKₐ). Maximum buffer capacity is at the half-equivalence point of a titration.

⚗ PAG9: Transition Metal Chemistry

Apparatus

  • Colorimeter with appropriate colour filter, cuvettes
  • Standard solutions of transition metal complex (various known concentrations)
  • Test tubes for ligand substitution reactions
  • Solutions: CuSO₄, NH₃(aq), NaOH(aq), HCl, EDTA

Method

  1. Colorimetry: Prepare a series of standard solutions of known concentration.
  2. Select the complementary colour filter on the colorimeter (e.g., red/orange filter for blue Cu²⁺ solutions).
  3. Zero the colorimeter using distilled water as the blank.
  4. Measure the absorbance of each standard. Plot a calibration curve: absorbance vs. concentration.
  5. Measure the absorbance of the unknown solution. Read the concentration from the calibration graph.
  6. Ligand substitution: Add NH₃ dropwise to CuSO₄(aq). Observe colour change from pale blue to deep blue as NH₃ replaces H₂O ligands. Record observations for each drop added.

Safety

  • NH₃ has irritant fumes — work in well-ventilated area.
  • Transition metal salts can be harmful — wear gloves.
  • Handle cuvettes by the top (fingerprints affect readings).

Analysis

Plot: Calibration curve of absorbance vs. [Cu²⁺]. Should be linear (Beer-Lambert Law: A = εcl).

Calculate: Unknown concentration by interpolation from the calibration graph.

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Fingerprints on cuvetteSystematicHandle by top; wipe with lens tissue
Wrong filter selectedSystematicUse complementary colour filter; determine λmax
Air bubbles in cuvetteRandomTap gently to dislodge; fill carefully
Dilution errors in standard solutionsRandomUse volumetric flasks and pipettes
Exam Tip: The filter used in the colorimeter must be the complementary colour to the solution being measured. A blue solution absorbs orange/red light, so use an orange filter. The relationship A = εcl (Beer-Lambert Law) is only valid at low concentrations — at high concentrations, the calibration curve deviates from linearity. Always include a blank (distilled water) to zero the instrument.

⚗ PAG10: Organic Analysis

Apparatus

  • Melting point apparatus, capillary tubes
  • Test tubes, water bath
  • Reagents: Br₂(aq), 2,4-DNP, Tollens', Na₂CO₃, K₂Cr₂O₇/H⁺, I₂/NaOH
  • Spectroscopic data (mass spec, IR, NMR — provided in exams)

Method

  1. Measure the melting point of the unknown solid. Compare with literature values.
  2. Perform functional group tests systematically (see PAG5/RP6 methods).
  3. Record all positive and negative results in a table.
  4. Combine lab results with spectroscopic data to identify the compound:
    • Mass spectrum: molecular ion peak gives Mᵣ; fragmentation pattern gives structural clues.
    • IR spectrum: broad O-H at 2500-3300 cm⁻¹ (carboxylic acid), sharp C=O at ~1700 cm⁻¹, broad O-H at 3200-3550 cm⁻¹ (alcohol).
    • NMR: number of peaks = number of unique H/C environments. Integration ratio = relative number of H. Splitting pattern (n+1 rule).

Safety

  • All standard organic test precautions apply.
  • K₂Cr₂O₇ is carcinogenic — gloves, fume cupboard.
  • Tollens' — prepare fresh, dispose immediately.

Analysis

Combine: Melting point, functional group tests, mass spec (Mᵣ), IR (bonds), NMR (structure) to fully identify the compound.

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Impure sample (broad mp)SystematicPurify first by recrystallisation
False positive/negative in testsSystematicRun positive and negative controls alongside
Heating too fast for melting pointSystematicHeat slowly near expected mp
Contaminated NMR solventSystematicUse pure deuterated solvent
Exam Tip: In combined analytical questions, start with the mass spectrum (molecular ion gives Mᵣ and molecular formula), then check IR for key functional groups, and finally use NMR to determine the exact structure. The number of ¹H NMR peaks tells you the number of different hydrogen environments; integration gives the ratio; splitting (n+1 rule) tells you adjacent hydrogens. Always calculate the degree of unsaturation (IHD) from the molecular formula — IHD = 4 strongly suggests a benzene ring.

⚗ PAG11: Chromatography

Apparatus

  • TLC plates (silica or alumina on plastic/glass backing)
  • Developing chamber (beaker with lid/cling film)
  • Capillary tubes (for spotting), pencil, ruler
  • Suitable solvent or solvent mixture (mobile phase)
  • UV lamp or iodine vapour (for visualising colourless spots)

Method

  1. Draw a faint pencil line (origin) about 1 cm from the bottom of the TLC plate.
  2. Spot samples onto the origin using capillary tubes. Keep spots small and evenly spaced. Include known reference compounds.
  3. Pour solvent into the developing chamber to a depth of about 0.5 cm (below the origin line). Allow atmosphere to saturate.
  4. Place the plate in the chamber. Ensure the origin line is above the solvent level.
  5. Cover and allow the solvent to rise up the plate by capillary action.
  6. When the solvent front nears the top (~1 cm from edge), remove the plate and immediately mark the solvent front with pencil.
  7. If spots are colourless, visualise under UV lamp or in iodine vapour.
  8. Measure distances from origin to centre of each spot and to the solvent front.
  9. Calculate Rf = distance by spot / distance by solvent front.

Safety

  • Many solvents are flammable and volatile — no flames; fume cupboard.
  • UV lamp — do not look directly at the UV light.
  • Iodine vapour is irritant — use in fume cupboard.

Analysis

Calculate: Rf values for each spot. Compare with reference values or known standards run alongside.

Identify: Components with the same Rf as known compounds (in the same solvent system) are likely the same substance.

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Spots too large (poor resolution)SystematicApply small, concentrated spots; dry between applications
Solvent level above origin lineSystematicCheck depth before inserting plate
Solvent front not marked immediatelyRandomMark immediately on removal — solvent evaporates quickly
Chamber not saturated with vapourSystematicLine chamber with filter paper; allow time to saturate
Using ink pen instead of pencilSystematicAlways use pencil — ink will dissolve in the solvent
Exam Tip: Rᶠ values are only valid if compared under identical conditions (same solvent, temperature, plate type). A substance is identified if its Rᶠ matches a known reference run alongside it in the same experiment. Two spots from an unknown sample at the origin means the unknown contains at least two components. TLC is a form of adsorption chromatography — polar substances adsorb more strongly to the polar silica stationary phase and have lower Rᶠ values. Non-polar substances travel further (higher Rᶠ).

⚗ PAG12: Investigate Equilibria

Apparatus

  • Test tubes, water baths (ice and hot)
  • CoCl₂ solution, conc. HCl, distilled water
  • For Kc: Sealed tubes with ethanoic acid + ethanol + catalyst (H₂SO₄), NaOH for titration, burette, pipette, indicator

Method

  1. Le Chatelier's (qualitative): Prepare cobalt equilibrium mixture (purple). Add HCl → blue. Add water → pink. Heat → blue. Cool → pink.
  2. Kc determination (quantitative): Seal mixtures of ethanoic acid and ethanol with catalyst in tubes. Leave at constant temperature until equilibrium (several days or heat to speed up).
  3. Titrate the equilibrium mixture with standardised NaOH to find moles of acid remaining.
  4. Use ICE table: calculate moles of each species at equilibrium.
  5. Calculate Kc = [products]/[reactants].

Safety

  • CoCl₂ is toxic — gloves.
  • Conc. HCl — fume cupboard.
  • Conc. H₂SO₄ catalyst — very corrosive.

Analysis

Qualitative: Record colour changes and link to Le Chatelier's principle.

Quantitative: ICE table to calculate equilibrium concentrations. Kc = [CH₃COOC₂H₅][H₂O] / [CH₃COOH][C₂H₅OH].

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Equilibrium not reachedSystematicAllow sufficient time; use catalyst; check with repeat
Temperature not constantSystematicUse thermostatted water bath
Titrating disturbs equilibriumSystematicQuick titration; acknowledged limitation
Catalyst (H₂SO₄) also titrated by NaOHSystematicRun a blank with just H₂SO₄ to subtract; account for in calculation
Exam Tip: For the esterification K⁾: remember to subtract the moles of H₂SO₄ catalyst from the titre (it also reacts with NaOH). The value of K⁾ for this reaction is approximately 4 at room temperature. A catalyst does not change K⁾ or the equilibrium position — it only speeds up attainment of equilibrium. When describing Le Chatelier's shifts, always state: (1) what change was made, (2) which direction the equilibrium shifts, (3) why (to counteract the change), and (4) what observable effect this has (colour change).
Worked Example — K⁾ from Esterification:

1.00 mol ethanoic acid and 1.00 mol ethanol were sealed with H₂SO₄ catalyst. At equilibrium, titration with NaOH showed 0.333 mol acid remaining (after subtracting the catalyst contribution).

Moles reacted = 1.00 − 0.333 = 0.667 mol. From stoichiometry, moles of ester = 0.667, moles of water = 0.667, moles of alcohol = 1.00 − 0.667 = 0.333.

K⁾ = (0.667 × 0.667) / (0.333 × 0.333) = 0.445 / 0.111 = 4.01 (no units — moles cancel)
