5.1 How Fast? — Rate Equations

Rate Equations and Orders

Rate equation: An experimentally determined expression that relates the rate of a reaction to the concentrations of reactants raised to certain powers (orders). For a reaction A + B → products: Rate = k[A]m[B]n, where m and n are the orders with respect to A and B, and k is the rate constant. The rate equation can only be determined from experimental data, not from the balanced equation.
Order of reaction: The power to which the concentration of a reactant is raised in the rate equation. The overall order is the sum of the individual orders (m + n). Orders are always small whole numbers (0, 1, or 2) at A-level.
  • Zero order: Changing the concentration has no effect on rate. Rate vs [A] graph is a horizontal line. Concentration-time graph is a straight line with negative gradient.
  • First order: Rate is directly proportional to concentration. Rate vs [A] gives a straight line through the origin. The concentration-time graph shows exponential decay with a constant half-life — this is the key test for first order.
  • Second order: Rate is proportional to concentration squared. Rate vs [A] gives an upward curve; Rate vs [A]2 gives a straight line through the origin. Half-life increases as reaction proceeds.

Determining Orders from Experimental Data

Using the initial rates method: compare experiments where only one concentration changes at a time while all others are held constant.

  • If doubling [A] has no effect on rate → zero order in A
  • If doubling [A] doubles the rate → first order in A
  • If doubling [A] quadruples the rate → second order in A

More generally: if [A] is multiplied by factor x, and rate changes by factor xn, then the order with respect to A is n.

The Rate Constant k

Rate constant (k): The proportionality constant in the rate equation. Its value is independent of concentration but increases with temperature (an increase in temperature always increases k). A larger k means a faster reaction at that temperature.

Units of k depend on the overall order:

The Arrhenius Equation

k = Ae−Ea/RT

Where A = the pre-exponential (Arrhenius) factor (related to collision frequency and orientation), Ea = activation energy (J mol−1), R = 8.314 J K−1 mol−1, T = temperature (K). Taking natural logarithms gives a linear form:

ln k = ln A − Ea/RT

A plot of ln k vs 1/T gives a straight line with gradient = −Ea/R and y-intercept = ln A. This allows Ea to be determined graphically: Ea = −gradient × R.

Exam Tip: When calculating Ea from the Arrhenius equation, ensure T is in kelvin and Ea comes out in J mol−1 (not kJ mol−1). Divide by 1000 at the end if the question asks for kJ. When reading the gradient from a graph, use a large triangle and show coordinates clearly. The gradient will be negative (downward slope); multiply by −R to get a positive Ea.

Rate-Determining Step and Reaction Mechanisms

Rate-determining step (RDS): The slowest step in a multi-step reaction mechanism. It acts as a bottleneck, controlling the overall rate. The species that appear in the rate equation are those involved in or before the rate-determining step.

The rate equation provides evidence for the mechanism. If the rate equation is Rate = k[A][B], then both A and B are involved in (or before) the RDS. If a reactant does not appear in the rate equation, it is involved in a faster step after the RDS.

Worked Example — Linking Rate Equation to Mechanism:

The reaction between propanone and iodine in acid solution has the rate equation: Rate = k[CH3COCH3][H+]

This tells us: (1) the reaction is first order in propanone and first order in H+, (2) iodine does NOT appear in the rate equation so it is not involved in the RDS, (3) the RDS involves one molecule of propanone and one H+ ion, (4) iodine must react in a fast step after the RDS.

5.2 How Far? — Equilibrium Constants

Kc Calculations

Use an ICE (Initial, Change, Equilibrium) table to determine equilibrium concentrations, then substitute into the Kc expression. Remember to convert amounts (moles) to concentrations (mol dm−3) by dividing by the volume of the container in dm3.

Kp — Equilibrium Constant in Terms of Partial Pressures

Partial pressure: The pressure exerted by an individual gas in a mixture. It is the contribution that gas makes to the total pressure. It equals the mole fraction of that gas multiplied by the total pressure.
pA = xA × Ptotal     where xA = moles of A / total moles of gas

For the reaction: aA(g) + bB(g) ⇌ cC(g) + dD(g)

Kp = (pC)c(pD)d / (pA)a(pB)b

Kp, like Kc, is only affected by temperature. Include units for Kp (typically in terms of the pressure unit used, e.g. Pa, kPa, or atm). Only gaseous species are included in Kp expressions.

Worked Example — Calculating Kp:

N2O4(g) ⇌ 2NO2(g). At equilibrium, the mole fractions are: x(N2O4) = 0.30, x(NO2) = 0.70. Total pressure = 100 kPa.

p(N2O4) = 0.30 × 100 = 30 kPa
p(NO2) = 0.70 × 100 = 70 kPa

Kp = p(NO2)2 / p(N2O4) = 702 / 30 = 4900 / 30 = 163 kPa

5.3 Acids, Bases and Buffers

Bronsted-Lowry Theory

Bronsted-Lowry acid: A proton (H+) donor.
Bronsted-Lowry base: A proton (H+) acceptor.
A conjugate acid-base pair differs by one proton.

pH Calculations

pH = −log10[H+]      [H+] = 10−pH

Strong acids fully dissociate, so [H+] = concentration of a monobasic acid. For dibasic strong acids like H2SO4, [H+] = 2 × concentration.

Strong bases: Use Kw to find [H+]:

Kw = [H+][OH−] = 1.00 × 10−14 mol2 dm−6 at 25 °C
Worked Example — pH of a Strong Base:

Calculate the pH of 0.050 mol dm−3 NaOH at 25 °C.

NaOH fully dissociates: [OH−] = 0.050 mol dm−3
[H+] = Kw / [OH−] = 1.00 × 10−14 / 0.050 = 2.00 × 10−13 mol dm−3
pH = −log(2.00 × 10−13) = 12.7

Weak Acids and Ka

Weak acid: An acid that only partially dissociates in aqueous solution, establishing an equilibrium. The vast majority of molecules remain undissociated. Examples: CH3COOH, H2CO3, HF.
Ka = [H+][A−] / [HA]

Two key assumptions for weak acid pH calculations (state these in exams for full marks):

  1. [H+] = [A−] because they are produced in equal amounts from the dissociation and the contribution of H+ from water is negligible.
  2. [HA]equilibrium ≈ [HA]initial because dissociation is so small that the change in [HA] is negligible.
Ka ≈ [H+]2 / [HA]initial     so     [H+] = √(Ka × [HA])
Exam Tip: Always state the two assumptions when performing weak acid calculations. Examiners frequently award marks specifically for these. Also remember: pKa = −log Ka, and a smaller Ka (or larger pKa) means a weaker acid.

Buffer Solutions

Buffer solution: A solution that resists changes in pH when small amounts of acid or base are added. An acidic buffer (pH < 7) consists of a weak acid and its conjugate base (salt), e.g. CH3COOH + CH3COONa. A basic buffer (pH > 7) consists of a weak base and its conjugate acid.

How an acidic buffer works:

  • When H+ is added: The conjugate base (A−, present in large excess from the salt) reacts with the added H+: A− + H+ → HA. This removes the excess H+, so pH barely changes.
  • When OH− is added: The weak acid (HA, present in large reserve) reacts with OH−: HA + OH− → A− + H2O. This removes the excess OH−, so pH barely changes.

The buffer works because it contains large reserves of both HA and A−, which can neutralise added acid or base respectively.

To calculate the pH of a buffer:

[H+] = Ka × [HA] / [A−]

The half-equivalence point (or half-neutralisation point) is where exactly half the acid has been neutralised. At this point, [HA] = [A−], so [H+] = Ka, meaning pH = pKa. This is the point of maximum buffering capacity and can be used to determine Ka from a titration curve.

Exam Tip: An indicator is suitable if its colour change range (pKIn ± 1) falls within the steep vertical section of the titration curve. For weak acid + weak base titrations, the curve has no steep section, so no indicator works — use a pH meter instead.

5.4 Enthalpy, Entropy and Free Energy

Lattice Enthalpy

Lattice enthalpy of formation: The enthalpy change when one mole of an ionic lattice is formed from its gaseous ions under standard conditions. It is always exothermic (negative). Alternatively defined as lattice enthalpy of dissociation (endothermic, positive) — check which definition the exam board uses.

Lattice enthalpy becomes more exothermic (stronger lattice) with:

  • Smaller ionic radii — ions are closer together, so electrostatic attraction is stronger
  • Higher ionic charges — greater charge means stronger electrostatic attraction

For example, MgO has a much more exothermic lattice enthalpy than NaCl because Mg2+ and O2− have higher charges and smaller radii than Na+ and Cl−.

A Born-Haber cycle applies Hess's Law to calculate the lattice enthalpy of an ionic compound when it cannot be measured directly. The cycle includes: enthalpies of atomisation, ionisation energies (for the metal), electron affinities (for the non-metal), and enthalpy of formation. The lattice enthalpy is the unknown that completes the cycle.

Enthalpy of Hydration and Solution

ΔsolH = −ΔlattH + ΣΔhydH

For a salt to dissolve, the energy released by hydration of the gaseous ions (ΔhydH, always exothermic) must compensate for the energy needed to break apart the lattice (−ΔlattH, endothermic). Enthalpy of hydration is more exothermic for ions with a higher charge density (smaller radius or higher charge), because they attract water molecules more strongly.

Entropy

Entropy (S): A quantitative measure of the disorder (or number of possible arrangements of particles) in a system. Units: J K−1 mol−1. Higher entropy = more disorder = more ways to arrange the particles.

Entropy increases when:

  • A solid melts to a liquid, or a liquid boils to a gas (gas >> liquid > solid)
  • A solid dissolves in a solvent (particles become dispersed)
  • The number of gaseous moles increases in a reaction
  • Temperature increases (particles have more energy and more possible arrangements)
ΔSsystem = ΣS(products) − ΣS(reactants)

Gibbs Free Energy

ΔG = ΔH − TΔS

A reaction is thermodynamically feasible (spontaneous) when ΔG < 0. However, even if ΔG is negative, the reaction may still not occur at an observable rate if the activation energy is too high (kinetic barrier). Feasibility tells us whether a reaction can happen, not whether it will happen quickly.

Exam Tip: When using ΔG = ΔH − TΔS, ensure consistent units. If ΔH is in kJ mol−1 and ΔS is in J K−1 mol−1, convert ΔS to kJ (divide by 1000) or ΔH to J (multiply by 1000) before calculating. Temperature must be in kelvin. To find the minimum temperature for feasibility, set ΔG = 0 and solve: T = ΔH / ΔS.

5.5 Redox and Electrode Potentials

Standard Electrode Potential

Standard electrode potential (E°): The EMF (voltage) of a half-cell measured under standard conditions (298 K, 100 kPa, 1.00 mol dm−3 solutions) relative to the standard hydrogen electrode (SHE), which is assigned a value of exactly 0.00 V.
cell = +0.34−(−0.76) = +1.10 V

Electrochemical Cells

E°cell = E°cathode − E°anode = E°(more positive) − E°(more negative)

A positive E°cell indicates the reaction is thermodynamically feasible under standard conditions. The more positive half-cell undergoes reduction (cathode); the more negative half-cell undergoes oxidation (anode). Electrons flow from anode to cathode through the external circuit. The salt bridge completes the circuit by allowing ions to flow between the half-cells, maintaining electrical neutrality.

Limitations of E° predictions: A positive E°cell does not guarantee the reaction will actually occur at an observable rate because: (1) the activation energy may be too high (kinetic barrier), (2) conditions may not be standard, (3) E° values apply to standard conditions only — non-standard concentrations, temperatures, or pressures will shift the value.

Fuel Cells

Fuel cell: An electrochemical cell that converts the chemical energy of a fuel (e.g. hydrogen) directly into electrical energy, as long as fuel and oxidant are continuously supplied. Unlike batteries, fuel cells do not run down or need recharging.

In a hydrogen fuel cell:

  • Anode: H2(g) → 2H+(aq) + 2e− (oxidation)
  • Cathode: O2(g) + 4H+(aq) + 4e− → 2H2O(l) (reduction)
  • Overall: 2H2(g) + O2(g) → 2H2O(l)

Advantages: only product is water (zero emissions at point of use), higher theoretical efficiency than combustion engines (not limited by Carnot cycle), quiet operation.

Challenges: hydrogen production is currently energy-intensive (often from steam reforming of methane, which produces CO2), storage requires high pressure (700 bar) or cryogenic temperatures, distribution infrastructure is limited, platinum catalyst is expensive and scarce.

5.6 Transition Elements

Transition element: A d-block element that forms at least one stable ion with an incomplete d sub-shell. This definition excludes scandium (Sc3+ is [Ar] 3d0 — empty d shell) and zinc (Zn2+ is [Ar] 3d10 — full d shell).

Properties of Transition Elements

  • Variable oxidation states: Due to the similar energies of the 3d and 4s sub-shells, transition metals can lose different numbers of electrons to form ions with different charges (e.g. Fe2+ and Fe3+, Cu+ and Cu2+, Mn2+ to Mn7+). This allows them to participate in redox reactions.
  • Formation of coloured complexes: Caused by d-d transitions. When ligands surround a transition metal ion, the d orbitals split into two energy levels. Electrons absorb visible light of a specific wavelength and transition between these levels. The colour observed is complementary to the colour absorbed. If the d sub-shell is empty (d0) or full (d10), no d-d transition is possible and the compound is white or colourless.
  • Catalytic activity: Transition metals act as catalysts due to their ability to change oxidation state (providing alternative pathways) and provide a surface for adsorption of reactants. Examples: Fe in the Haber process, V2O5 in the Contact process, MnO2 in decomposition of H2O2, Ni in hydrogenation.
  • Formation of complex ions with ligands (see below).

Complex Ions

Complex ion: A central metal ion surrounded by ligands. A ligand is a molecule or ion that donates a lone pair of electrons to the central metal ion to form a dative (coordinate) bond. The coordination number is the total number of dative bonds to the central metal ion.

Monodentate: A ligand that donates one lone pair (forms one dative bond). Bidentate: Donates two lone pairs from two different atoms. Hexadentate (polydentate): Donates six lone pairs, e.g. EDTA wraps around the central ion, forming 6 bonds.

Ligand Substitution

Ligands can be exchanged for other ligands. This may change the colour, coordination number, and shape of the complex:

  • [Cu(H2O)6]2+ (pale blue, octahedral) + 4Cl− ⇌ [CuCl4]2− (yellow-green, tetrahedral) + 6H2O — coordination number changes from 6 to 4 because Cl− is larger than H2O
  • [Cu(H2O)6]2+ (pale blue) + 4NH3 → [Cu(NH3)4(H2O)2]2+ (deep blue) + 4H2O — partial substitution only (4 of 6 water molecules replaced), coordination number stays at 6, shape stays octahedral

The chelate effect: complexes with polydentate ligands (e.g. EDTA, en) are more stable than those with equivalent monodentate ligands because the substitution reaction has a positive ΔS (more particles are released than consumed), making ΔG more negative.

Colour of Complexes

The colour depends on: (1) the identity of the central metal ion, (2) its oxidation state, (3) the identity of the ligands (different ligands cause different degrees of splitting), and (4) the coordination number/geometry. Any change in these factors can change the colour.

Exam Tip: When explaining why transition metal complexes are coloured, you must mention all three points: (1) d orbital splitting by ligands, (2) d-d electron transitions absorbing specific wavelengths of visible light, (3) the transmitted/reflected colour is complementary to the absorbed colour. If the d sub-shell is empty (d0, e.g. Sc3+) or full (d10, e.g. Zn2+, Cu+), no d-d transition is possible and the compound is white or colourless.
