3.8 Nuclear Physics

1. Radioactive Radiation

Radioactive decay: The spontaneous and random emission of radiation from an unstable nucleus. Spontaneous means it is not affected by external conditions (temperature, pressure, etc.). Random means it is impossible to predict which nucleus will decay next.

Nuclear Equations

Alpha decay:

AZX → A−4Z−2Y + 42α

Beta-minus decay:

AZX → AZ+1Y + 0−1β + νe

Gamma emission: No change in A or Z. The nucleus loses energy by emitting a gamma photon. Often follows alpha or beta decay (the daughter nucleus is left in an excited state).

In all nuclear equations: mass number (A) and atomic number (Z) are conserved on both sides.

Exam Tip: The existence of the neutrino was proposed to explain the continuous energy spectrum of beta particles. If only two particles were produced, conservation of energy and momentum would require a fixed kinetic energy for the electron. The variable energy implies a third particle (the antineutrino) carries away the remaining energy.

2. Radioactive Decay and Half-Life

Activity (A): The rate of decay (number of disintegrations per second). A = λN. Units: Bq (becquerels). 1 Bq = 1 decay per second.
Decay constant (λ): The probability of a given nucleus decaying per unit time. Units: s-1.
Half-life (t½): The time taken for the number of radioactive nuclei (or the activity) to halve.
λ = ln 2 / t½ = 0.693 / t½

Exponential Decay Equations

N = N0e−λt
A = A0e−λt

where N0 = initial number of undecayed nuclei, A0 = initial activity, t = time.

Graphical Methods

  • A plot of N (or A) against t gives an exponential decay curve.
  • A plot of ln N against t gives a straight line with gradient −λ and y-intercept ln N0.
  • Half-life can be read from the decay curve: the time for the value to drop to half.
Exam Tip: After n half-lives, the fraction remaining is (1/2)n. So after 3 half-lives, 1/8 of the original nuclei remain. You can use this for quick calculations without needing the exponential formula.

3. Nuclear Radius

Experiments (electron diffraction, alpha scattering) show that nuclear radius depends on mass number:

r = r0A1/3
  • r = nuclear radius (m)
  • r0 = constant ≈ 1.2–1.4 fm (1 fm = 10-15 m)
  • A = mass number (nucleon number)

Nuclear Density

Since mass = Au (where u = atomic mass unit) and volume = (4/3)πr3 = (4/3)πr03A:

ρ = Au / [(4/3)πr03A] = 3u / (4πr03)

The A cancels, meaning nuclear density is approximately constant for all nuclei: ρ ≈ 1017 kg m-3. This is evidence that nucleons are tightly packed with a fixed separation.

Exam Tip: Electron diffraction is used to measure nuclear radius because electrons are leptons (no strong force interaction with nucleons) and at high energies their de Broglie wavelength is comparable to nuclear size (~10-15 m).

4. Mass Defect and Binding Energy

Mass defect (Δm): The difference between the total mass of the individual (separated) nucleons and the actual mass of the nucleus. Δm = [Zmp + (A−Z)mn] − mnucleus
Binding energy: The energy required to completely separate all the nucleons in a nucleus. Equivalently, the energy released when individual nucleons come together to form the nucleus. Related to mass defect by E = Δmc2.

Mass-Energy Equivalence

E = mc2
  • E = energy (J)
  • m = mass (kg)
  • c = speed of light = 3.00 × 108 m s-1

1 u = 1.661 × 10-27 kg = 931.5 MeV/c2

Binding Energy per Nucleon

Binding energy per nucleon = total binding energy / A

This is a measure of the stability of a nucleus. Higher binding energy per nucleon = more stable.

The Binding Energy Curve

Key features of the binding energy per nucleon vs mass number graph:

  • The peak is at iron-56 (and nickel-62), with binding energy per nucleon ≈ 8.8 MeV. Iron-56 is the most stable nucleus.
  • Light nuclei (A < 56): binding energy per nucleon increases with A. These nuclei can release energy by fusion (combining to form heavier nuclei).
  • Heavy nuclei (A > 56): binding energy per nucleon decreases with A. These nuclei can release energy by fission (splitting into lighter nuclei).
  • Both fusion and fission move nuclei towards the peak (higher binding energy per nucleon), releasing energy equal to the increase in total binding energy.
Exam Tip: Energy released = change in total binding energy = (total BE of products) − (total BE of reactants). If this is positive, energy is released. You can also calculate it from the mass defect: energy released = Δm × c2.

5. Nuclear Fission

Nuclear fission: The splitting of a heavy nucleus (e.g. uranium-235, plutonium-239) into two smaller daughter nuclei of roughly equal mass, accompanied by the release of neutrons and a large amount of energy.

Induced Fission

A thermal (slow) neutron is absorbed by a heavy nucleus (e.g. U-235), forming an unstable compound nucleus (U-236), which then splits:

23592U + 10n → 23692U → 14156Ba + 9236Kr + 310n + energy

(This is just one possible fission pathway; many different product pairs are possible.)

Chain Reaction

Each fission event releases 2–3 neutrons. These can go on to cause further fission events, creating a chain reaction.

  • Uncontrolled chain reaction: Every neutron causes further fission. Leads to an exponential increase in energy release (nuclear weapon).
  • Controlled chain reaction: On average, exactly one neutron per fission goes on to cause another fission. Steady power output (nuclear reactor).

Nuclear Reactor Components

ComponentFunction
Fuel rodsContain fissile material (enriched uranium or plutonium)
ModeratorSlows down fast neutrons to thermal speeds (increases probability of absorption). Usually water or graphite.
Control rodsAbsorb excess neutrons to control the rate of the chain reaction. Made of boron or cadmium. Inserted further to slow the reaction, withdrawn to speed it up.
CoolantTransfers thermal energy from the reactor core to heat exchangers (to generate steam). Often water or CO2.
ShieldingThick concrete and steel to absorb radiation and protect workers.

6. Nuclear Fusion

Nuclear fusion: The joining (fusing) of two light nuclei to form a heavier nucleus, with a release of energy (because the products have higher binding energy per nucleon than the reactants).

Example (deuterium-tritium fusion):

21H + 31H → 42He + 10n + 17.6 MeV

Conditions for Fusion

  • Extremely high temperature (~107–108 K) is needed to give nuclei enough kinetic energy to overcome the electrostatic (Coulomb) repulsion between the positive charges.
  • High density/pressure to increase the probability of collisions.
  • Sufficient confinement time to keep the plasma contained long enough for fusion to occur.

At these temperatures, matter exists as a plasma (a gas of fully ionised atoms). The main challenge for fusion power is confining this plasma (using magnetic confinement in a tokamak or inertial confinement using lasers).

Fusion in Stars

Stars are powered by nuclear fusion. In main sequence stars like the Sun, hydrogen nuclei fuse to form helium (the proton-proton chain). The enormous gravitational pressure in the core provides the necessary conditions. The star is in equilibrium: gravitational collapse is balanced by radiation pressure from fusion.

Exam Tip: Fusion releases more energy per kilogram of fuel than fission. However, achieving the conditions for controlled fusion on Earth remains an enormous engineering challenge. Both fission and fusion release energy because the products have a higher binding energy per nucleon than the reactants.

⚗ Required Practical 12: Investigation of the Inverse-Square Law for Gamma Radiation

Apparatus

  • Sealed gamma source (e.g. cobalt-60 or caesium-137) in a source holder
  • Geiger-Müller (GM) tube and counter (or ratemeter)
  • Metre ruler or tape measure
  • Retort stand and clamp (to hold the source and/or GM tube)
  • Stopwatch (for timed counts)
  • Lead absorber set (to verify the source emits gamma)

Method

  1. Background count: Before setting up the source, measure the background count rate. Run the counter for at least 3 minutes (or take 3 separate 1-minute counts) with no source present. Calculate the mean background count rate Cbg.
  2. Place the gamma source in its holder at one end of the ruler. Place the GM tube at a known distance d from the source. Align the tube window to face the source directly.
  3. Count the number of counts N in a fixed time (e.g. 60 seconds). Calculate the count rate C = N/t.
  4. Subtract the background: corrected count rate = C − Cbg.
  5. Move the GM tube further away. Repeat for at least 6–8 different distances (e.g. from 5 cm to 50 cm in steps of 5 cm).
  6. Plot corrected count rate (y-axis) against 1/d² (x-axis). A straight line through the origin confirms the inverse-square law.
  7. Alternatively, plot log(C) vs log(d). The gradient should be −2.

Safety

  • Radioactive source safety is critical:
  • Handle the source with long tongs — never with bare hands.
  • Keep the source in a lead-lined container when not in use.
  • Point the source away from people.
  • Maximise distance from the source (inverse-square law reduces exposure).
  • Minimise time of exposure — take readings efficiently.
  • Only a teacher or authorised person should handle the source.
  • Wash hands after the experiment.
  • Record the source in the school’s radioactive materials register.

Analysis

For a point source emitting gamma radiation uniformly, the intensity (and hence count rate) follows the inverse-square law:

I ∝ 1/d²    ⇒    C = k/d²

Plot corrected C vs 1/d²: a straight line through the origin confirms the inverse-square law, with gradient k related to the source activity and detector efficiency.

If the line does not pass through the origin, the effective centre of the source may not be at the measured position — introduce a correction d0 so that C = k/(d + d0)².

Sources of Error & Improvements

Exam Tip: Always subtract background count rate before plotting. The randomness of radioactive decay means count rates fluctuate — this is why you take long counting times and repeat measurements. If the graph of C vs 1/d² does not pass through the origin, this indicates a systematic error in d (the effective centre of the source is not at the point you measured from). You can find d0 by plotting √(1/C) vs d, which should be linear with an x-intercept of −d0.
