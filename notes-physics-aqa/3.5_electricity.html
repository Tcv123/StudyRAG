3.5 Electricity

1. Charge, Current, and Potential Difference

Electric current (I): The rate of flow of charge. I = Q / t. Units: A (amperes).
Charge (Q): Q = It. Units: C (coulombs). The charge of one electron = 1.60 × 10-19 C.

Conventional current flows from positive to negative (opposite to the direction of electron flow).

Potential difference (V): The energy transferred per unit charge passing between two points. V = W / Q. Units: V (volts). 1 V = 1 J C-1.

2. Resistance

Resistance (R): The opposition to the flow of current. R = V / I. Units: Ω (ohms).

Ohm's Law

Ohm's Law: The current through a conductor is directly proportional to the potential difference across it, provided physical conditions (e.g. temperature) remain constant. Such a conductor is called an ohmic conductor.

Exam Tip: Be careful with thermistor types. AQA specifies NTC (Negative Temperature Coefficient) thermistors, where resistance falls as temperature rises. This is because more charge carriers are released at higher temperatures.

3. Resistivity

ρ = RA / L
  • ρ = resistivity (Ω m) — a property of the material
  • R = resistance (Ω)
  • A = cross-sectional area (m2)
  • L = length (m)

Good conductors (e.g. copper) have very low resistivity (~10-8 Ω m). Insulators (e.g. rubber) have very high resistivity (~1013 Ω m).

Effect of Temperature on Metals

For metals, resistance increases with temperature. As temperature rises, positive metal ions vibrate more, so conduction electrons collide more frequently, reducing their drift velocity. The number of charge carriers stays roughly constant.

Superconductors

Superconductor: A material whose resistivity drops to zero below a critical temperature (transition temperature). Current flows without any energy loss.

Applications: MRI scanners, particle accelerators, maglev trains, power transmission without losses.

4. EMF and Internal Resistance

Electromotive force (EMF, ε): The energy transferred per unit charge by the source in driving charge around a complete circuit. Units: V. It is NOT a force.
Internal resistance (r): The resistance of the source itself. Some energy is dissipated inside the source as heat.
ε = I(R + r) = V + Ir
ε = IR + Ir
  • ε = EMF of the source (V)
  • I = current (A)
  • R = external (load) resistance (Ω)
  • r = internal resistance (Ω)
  • V = terminal PD (= IR, the PD across the external resistance)
  • Ir = "lost volts" (energy wasted inside the source)

The terminal PD (V) is always less than the EMF when current flows, because of the voltage drop across the internal resistance.

Exam Tip: To find ε and r experimentally, plot V against I. The y-intercept gives ε and the gradient gives −r (since V = ε − Ir).

5. Kirchhoff's Laws

Kirchhoff's First Law (junction rule): The total current entering a junction equals the total current leaving the junction. This is based on conservation of charge.
ΣIin = ΣIout
Kirchhoff's Second Law (loop rule): The sum of EMFs around any closed loop equals the sum of the PDs around that loop. This is based on conservation of energy.
Σε = ΣIR

6. Series and Parallel Circuits

Series

  • Same current through all components: Itotal = I1 = I2 = ...
  • PDs add up: Vtotal = V1 + V2 + ...
  • Resistances add: Rtotal = R1 + R2 + ...

Parallel

  • Same PD across each branch: Vtotal = V1 = V2 = ...
  • Currents add up: Itotal = I1 + I2 + ...
  • Reciprocal of resistances add: 1/Rtotal = 1/R1 + 1/R2 + ...
Exam Tip: For two resistors in parallel, use Rtotal = (R1 × R2) / (R1 + R2). The total resistance is always less than the smallest individual resistance.

7. Potential Dividers

A potential divider consists of two (or more) resistors in series connected across a supply voltage. The output voltage is taken across one of the resistors.

Vout = Vin × R2 / (R1 + R2)

Sensor Circuits

Potential dividers with variable resistors (thermistors, LDRs) can be used as sensor circuits:

  • Thermistor in a potential divider: As temperature rises, thermistor resistance falls, so Vout across the thermistor falls (and Vout across the fixed resistor rises).
  • LDR in a potential divider: As light intensity increases, LDR resistance falls, so Vout across the LDR falls.
Exam Tip: Think about which resistor Vout is measured across. If Vout is across the thermistor, then Vout decreases when temperature increases (because the thermistor resistance decreases). If Vout is across the fixed resistor, Vout increases.

8. Electrical Power

P = IV

Using V = IR, we can derive:

P = I2R = V2/R

Energy Transferred

E = Pt = IVt = I2Rt = V2t/R

Units: J (joules). Sometimes expressed in kWh for domestic energy calculations (1 kWh = 3.6 × 106 J).

Exam Tip: Choose the power formula based on what you know. If you know I and R but not V, use P = I2R. If you know V and R but not I, use P = V2/R. This avoids unnecessary intermediate calculations.
