3.7 Fields and Their Consequences

1. Gravitational Fields

Gravitational field: A region of space in which a mass experiences a force. All objects with mass create a gravitational field. Gravity is always attractive.

Newton's Law of Gravitation

F = −GMm / r2
  • F = gravitational force (N) — the negative sign indicates attraction
  • G = gravitational constant = 6.67 × 10-11 N m2 kg-2
  • M, m = masses of the two objects (kg)
  • r = distance between centres of mass (m)

Gravitational Field Strength

Gravitational field strength (g): The force per unit mass at a point in a gravitational field. g = F/m. Units: N kg-1 (or m s-2).

Radial field (point or spherical mass):

g = GM / r2

Field strength follows an inverse square law. Field lines point radially inward towards the mass.

Uniform field: Near the Earth's surface, g is approximately constant at 9.81 N kg-1. Field lines are parallel and equally spaced.

Gravitational Potential

Gravitational potential (V): The work done per unit mass in bringing a small test mass from infinity to that point. V is always negative (since gravity is attractive and V = 0 at infinity).
V = −GM / r

Gravitational potential energy:

Ep = mV = −GMm / r

The relationship between g and V: g = −dV/dr (the field strength is the negative gradient of the potential).

Orbits and Satellites

For a circular orbit, the gravitational force provides the centripetal force:

GMm/r2 = mv2/r
v = √(GM/r)
T2 = (4π2/GM) r3

This last equation is Kepler's Third Law: T2 is proportional to r3.

Escape Velocity

The minimum velocity needed for an object to escape a gravitational field (reach infinity with zero KE):

vesc = √(2GM/r)
Exam Tip: A geostationary orbit has T = 24 hours, orbits above the equator, and moves in the same direction as Earth's rotation. Using Kepler's Third Law, the orbital radius is approximately 42,200 km from Earth's centre.

2. Electric Fields

Electric field: A region of space in which a charged particle experiences a force. Field lines go from positive to negative. Like charges repel; unlike charges attract.

Coulomb's Law

F = kQ1Q2 / r2 = Q1Q2 / (4πε0r2)
  • k = 1/(4πε0) = 8.99 × 109 N m2 C-2
  • ε0 = permittivity of free space = 8.85 × 10-12 F m-1
  • F is positive (repulsion) for like charges, negative (attraction) for unlike charges

Electric Field Strength

Electric field strength (E): The force per unit positive charge at a point. E = F/Q. Units: N C-1 or V m-1.

Radial field (point charge):

E = kQ / r2 = Q / (4πε0r2)

Uniform field (between parallel plates):

E = V / d

where V = potential difference between the plates and d = plate separation. The field is uniform (constant E) between the plates.

Electric Potential

Electric potential (V): The work done per unit positive charge in bringing a small test charge from infinity to that point.
V = kQ / r = Q / (4πε0r)

V is positive for a positive charge and negative for a negative charge. E = −dV/dr.

3. Capacitance

Capacitance (C): The charge stored per unit potential difference across a capacitor. C = Q/V. Units: F (farads). 1 F = 1 C V-1.

Parallel Plate Capacitor

C = ε0εrA / d
  • ε0 = permittivity of free space
  • εr = relative permittivity (dielectric constant) of the material between the plates
  • A = area of overlap of the plates (m2)
  • d = separation of the plates (m)

Capacitors in Series and Parallel

ArrangementRule
Series1/Ctotal = 1/C1 + 1/C2 + ... (total capacitance decreases)
ParallelCtotal = C1 + C2 + ... (total capacitance increases)
Exam Tip: Capacitors in series/parallel combine the OPPOSITE way to resistors. Capacitors in series use the reciprocal rule (like resistors in parallel). Capacitors in parallel add directly (like resistors in series).

Energy Stored in a Capacitor

E = ½QV = ½CV2 = ½Q2/C

This is the area under a Q-V graph (a straight line through the origin with gradient C).

Charging and Discharging

When a capacitor charges or discharges through a resistor, the process is exponential:

Discharging:

Q = Q0e−t/RC
V = V0e−t/RC
I = I0e−t/RC

Charging:

Q = Q0(1 − e−t/RC)
V = V0(1 − e−t/RC)

Current still decays exponentially during charging: I = I0e−t/RC

Time Constant

τ = RC

The time constant is the time taken for the charge (or PD or current) to fall to 1/e (≈ 37%) of its initial value during discharge. After 5τ, the capacitor is considered fully discharged (<1% remaining).

Exam Tip: To find τ from a graph, read off the time when the value drops to 37% of the initial value. Alternatively, plot ln(Q) against t — the gradient is −1/RC.

4. Magnetic Fields

Magnetic flux density (B): A measure of the strength of a magnetic field. Units: T (tesla). 1 T = 1 kg s-2 A-1.

Force on a Current-Carrying Conductor

F = BIL sinθ
  • F = force (N)
  • B = magnetic flux density (T)
  • I = current (A)
  • L = length of conductor in the field (m)
  • θ = angle between the current direction and the field direction

Direction given by Fleming's left-hand rule (thuMb = Motion, First finger = Field, seCond finger = Current).

Force on a Moving Charge

F = BQv sinθ

A charged particle moving perpendicular to a magnetic field follows a circular path (the magnetic force provides centripetal force):

BQv = mv2/r   ⇒   r = mv / (BQ)

Magnetic Flux and Flux Linkage

Magnetic flux (Φ): The product of the magnetic flux density and the area perpendicular to the field. Φ = BA cosθ. Units: Wb (webers). 1 Wb = 1 T m2.
Flux linkage: For a coil of N turns, flux linkage = NΦ = BAN cosθ. Units: Wb (or Wb turns).

Electromagnetic Induction

Faraday's Law: The magnitude of the induced EMF is equal to the rate of change of flux linkage.
ε = −d(NΦ)/dt = −N dΦ/dt
Lenz's Law: The direction of the induced EMF (and hence the induced current) is such that it opposes the change in flux that caused it. This is the reason for the negative sign in Faraday's Law. It is a consequence of conservation of energy.

EMF in a Rotating Coil

A coil rotating in a uniform magnetic field generates a sinusoidal EMF:

ε = BANω sin(ωt)

Maximum EMF: ε0 = BANω (when the coil is parallel to the field).

Transformers

Ns/Np = Vs/Vp

For an ideal transformer (100% efficient): VpIp = VsIs

  • Step-up transformer: Ns > Np, Vs > Vp
  • Step-down transformer: Ns < Np, Vs < Vp

Energy losses in real transformers are due to: resistance in coils (I2R heating), eddy currents in the core (reduced by lamination), magnetic hysteresis, flux leakage.

Exam Tip: High-voltage transmission reduces power loss in cables. Ploss = I2R. By stepping up the voltage, the current is reduced (since P = IV is constant), so I2R losses are greatly reduced. This is why the National Grid operates at very high voltages (up to 400 kV).

⚗ Required Practical 9: Investigation of the Charge and Discharge of Capacitors

0 Charge Discharge R Resistor C Capacitor Voltmeter / Datalogger ln(V) vs t ln(V) t grad = −1/RC τ = RC (time constant)

Apparatus

  • Capacitor (e.g. 470 μF or 1000 μF electrolytic)
  • Resistor (e.g. 10 kΩ or 100 kΩ — chosen to give a time constant of several seconds)
  • DC power supply (e.g. 6 V)
  • Two-way switch (DPDT) for charge/discharge selection
  • Voltmeter or datalogger with voltage sensor (across the capacitor)
  • Stopwatch (if not using a datalogger)
  • Connecting leads

Method

  1. Connect the circuit: DC supply → two-way switch → resistor R → capacitor C in series. Connect the voltmeter (or datalogger) across the capacitor.
  2. Charging: Set the switch to the charge position. The capacitor charges through R. If using a datalogger, record V across C at regular time intervals. If using a voltmeter and stopwatch, record V every 5–10 seconds.
  3. Discharging: Once fully charged (V = V0), switch to the discharge position. Record V at regular time intervals as the capacitor discharges through R.
  4. Record data until V has dropped to less than 10% of V0 (at least 5 time constants).
  5. Plot ln(V) vs t for the discharge. This should be a straight line with gradient = −1/RC.
  6. Calculate the time constant τ = RC and compare with the theoretical value from the component ratings.

Safety

  • Use low voltages (6 V or less).
  • Ensure electrolytic capacitors are connected with the correct polarity (+ to +).
  • Discharge the capacitor fully before handling the circuit.
  • Large capacitors can store significant charge — avoid touching terminals.

Analysis

During discharge: V = V0e−t/RC. Taking natural logs:

ln(V) = ln(V0) − t/RC

Plot ln(V) vs t: straight line with y-intercept = ln(V0) and gradient = −1/RC.

Therefore RC = −1/gradient. The time constant τ = RC is the time for V to fall to V0/e ≈ 0.37V0.

⚗ Required Practical 10: Investigation of the Force on a Current-Carrying Conductor in a Magnetic Field

Apparatus

  • Strong horseshoe magnet (or two flat magnets with a yoke)
  • Stiff copper wire (straight section of known length L)
  • Top-pan balance (reading to 0.01 g or 0.001 g)
  • Variable DC power supply
  • Ammeter (in series with the wire)
  • Retort stands and clamps to support the wire frame above the magnet
  • Connecting leads

Method

  1. Place the magnet on the top-pan balance. Zero (tare) the balance.
  2. Clamp a rigid wire frame so the horizontal wire section sits in the gap between the poles of the magnet, perpendicular to the magnetic field.
  3. Connect the wire in series with the ammeter and variable DC supply.
  4. Switch on and set a current I. Read the apparent mass change Δm on the balance. The force on the wire is F = BIL; by Newton's third law, an equal and opposite force acts on the magnet, changing the balance reading.
  5. Calculate the force: F = Δm × g (where g = 9.81 m s-2).
  6. Vary the current in steps (e.g. 0.5 A increments). Record Δm and hence F at each current.
  7. Plot F (y-axis) vs I (x-axis). The gradient = BL, so B = gradient / L.

Safety

  • Keep currents moderate to avoid overheating the wire.
  • Switch off between readings.
  • Strong magnets can pinch fingers — handle with care.
  • Keep magnets away from electronic devices and credit cards.

Analysis

The force on a current-carrying wire in a magnetic field:

F = BIL sinθ

With the wire perpendicular to B (θ = 90°, sinθ = 1): F = BIL.

Plot F vs I: straight line through the origin with gradient = BL. Therefore B = gradient / L.

The balance reading changes because the reaction force (Newton’s 3rd law) acts on the magnet.

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement

Non-uniform magnetic field across the wire lengthSystematicUse a strong magnet with a wide, uniform field region; keep the wire centred in the gap

Exam Tip: The balance method exploits Newton's third law. The motor effect pushes the wire up/down; the equal and opposite force pushes the magnet down/up, changing the balance reading. Remember: F = BIL applies when I is perpendicular to B. If the wire is at angle θ to B, use F = BIL sinθ.

⚗ Required Practical 11: Investigation of Characteristics of an AC Supply Using an Oscilloscope

Apparatus

  • Oscilloscope (CRO or digital)
  • AC signal generator or low-voltage AC supply
  • Connecting leads (BNC to crocodile clips or banana plugs)

Method

  1. Connect the AC supply to the Y-input of the oscilloscope.
  2. Adjust the time base (s/div) until a clear, stable sine wave is displayed (1–3 complete cycles visible on screen).
  3. Adjust the Y-gain (V/div) so the wave fills most of the screen vertically.
  4. Measure the peak voltage V0: count the number of divisions from the centre line (zero) to the peak and multiply by the Y-gain setting.
  5. Measure the time period T: count the number of divisions for one complete cycle and multiply by the time base setting.
  6. Calculate the frequency: f = 1/T.
  7. Calculate Vrms = V0 / √2.
  8. Vary the frequency or amplitude on the signal generator and repeat measurements to verify.

Safety

  • Use a low-voltage AC supply (e.g. signal generator output, typically < 10 V).
  • Do not connect the oscilloscope directly to mains voltage.
  • Ensure all connections are secure.

Analysis

From the oscilloscope trace:

  • V0 (peak voltage) = number of vertical divisions from 0 to peak × Y-gain (V/div)
  • T (period) = number of horizontal divisions for one cycle × time base (s/div)
  • f = 1/T
  • Vrms = V0 / √2 ≈ 0.707 V0

For UK mains: V0 ≈ 325 V, Vrms = 230 V, f = 50 Hz, T = 20 ms.

Exam Tip: Vrms is the DC equivalent voltage that would deliver the same power. For a sinusoidal AC supply: Vrms = V0/√2 and Irms = I0/√2. The oscilloscope shows V0 (peak), not Vrms. A multimeter set to AC reads Vrms. Always state which you are using.
