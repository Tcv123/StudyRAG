3.3 Waves

1. Progressive Waves

Progressive wave: A wave that transfers energy from one place to another without transferring matter. The oscillations of particles (or fields) propagate outward from the source.

Wave Properties

  • Amplitude (A): Maximum displacement of a particle from its equilibrium position. Related to intensity (I ∝ A2).
  • Wavelength (λ): The minimum distance between two points in phase (e.g. crest to crest).
  • Frequency (f): Number of complete oscillations per second. Units: Hz (s-1).
  • Period (T): Time for one complete oscillation. T = 1/f.

The Wave Equation

v = fλ

where v = wave speed (m s-1), f = frequency (Hz), λ = wavelength (m).

Phase Difference

Phase difference: The fraction of a cycle by which one wave leads or lags another. Measured in degrees (0°–360°) or radians (0–2π).
  • In phase: Phase difference = 0 (or 2π, 4π, ...). Points at the same displacement, moving in the same direction.
  • Antiphase (completely out of phase): Phase difference = π (or 180°). Opposite displacements.
Phase difference = (2π / λ) × path difference

2. Superposition and Interference

Principle of superposition: When two or more waves meet at a point, the resultant displacement is the vector sum of the individual displacements.

Interference

  • Constructive interference: Waves arrive in phase. Resultant amplitude = sum of amplitudes. Path difference = nλ (n = 0, 1, 2, ...)
  • Destructive interference: Waves arrive in antiphase. Resultant amplitude = difference of amplitudes. Path difference = (n + ½)λ

Coherence

Coherent sources: Two sources that emit waves with a constant phase difference and the same frequency. Coherence is required to observe a stable interference pattern.
Exam Tip: Coherence does NOT mean the waves are in phase — it means the phase difference is constant over time. Two coherent sources could have a fixed phase difference of, say, π/3.

3. Young's Double Slit Experiment

Monochromatic, coherent light passes through two narrow slits separated by distance d. An interference pattern of bright and dark fringes is observed on a screen at distance D.

w = λD / d
  • w = fringe spacing (m)
  • λ = wavelength (m)
  • D = distance from slits to screen (m)
  • d = slit separation (m)

This experiment provided strong evidence for the wave nature of light. White light produces a central white fringe with coloured fringes either side (red fringes are wider because λred > λblue).

4. Diffraction Gratings

A diffraction grating has many equally-spaced slits (typically hundreds per mm). It produces sharp, bright maxima at specific angles.

d sinθ = nλ
  • d = slit spacing (distance between adjacent slits) = 1 / (number of slits per metre)
  • θ = angle of diffraction from the central maximum
  • n = order of the maximum (0, 1, 2, ...)
  • λ = wavelength

The maximum number of orders visible is when sinθ ≤ 1, so nmax = d / λ (rounded down to the nearest integer).

Exam Tip: If given "600 lines per mm", then d = 1 / (600 × 103) = 1.67 × 10-6 m. Do NOT forget the unit conversion.

Gratings vs Double Slit

  • Gratings produce much sharper, brighter maxima than the double slit.
  • More slits → sharper peaks and more precise wavelength measurement.
  • Applications: spectroscopy, measuring wavelengths, analysing starlight.

5. Stationary (Standing) Waves

Stationary wave: Formed by the superposition of two progressive waves of the same frequency and amplitude, travelling in opposite directions. Energy is stored, not transferred.

Nodes and Antinodes

  • Node: A point of zero displacement (always stationary). Formed by destructive interference.
  • Antinode: A point of maximum displacement. Formed by constructive interference.
  • The distance between adjacent nodes (or adjacent antinodes) = λ/2.

Stationary Waves in Pipes

Closed pipe (one end closed): Node at the closed end, antinode at the open end. Only odd harmonics are present (1st, 3rd, 5th...). Fundamental: λ = 4L.

Open pipe (both ends open): Antinodes at both ends. All harmonics present. Fundamental: λ = 2L.

6. Refraction

Refraction: The change in direction of a wave as it passes from one medium to another, caused by a change in speed.

Snell's Law

n1 sinθ1 = n2 sinθ2

where n is the refractive index of the medium and θ is the angle to the normal.

n = c / v

where c = speed of light in a vacuum, v = speed of light in the medium.

When light goes from a less dense to a more dense medium (e.g. air to glass), it slows down and bends towards the normal (θ2 < θ1).

Total Internal Reflection (TIR)

Total internal reflection: Occurs when light travelling in a denser medium hits the boundary with a less dense medium at an angle greater than the critical angle. All light is reflected back into the denser medium.

Conditions for TIR:

  1. Light must travel from a more optically dense medium to a less optically dense medium (n1 > n2).
  2. The angle of incidence must exceed the critical angle (θ > θc).
sinθc = n2 / n1

For a medium surrounded by air (n2 ≈ 1): sinθc = 1/n

Applications: Optical fibres (telecommunications, endoscopes), prisms in binoculars.

Exam Tip: In optical fibres, the core has a higher refractive index than the cladding. The cladding protects the core, prevents signal loss by maintaining TIR, and prevents crossover of signals between adjacent fibres.

7. Polarisation

Polarisation: The restriction of the oscillations of a transverse wave to a single plane. Only transverse waves can be polarised (this proves light is a transverse wave).
  • Unpolarised light: Oscillations occur in all planes perpendicular to the direction of travel.
  • Plane-polarised light: Oscillations occur in only one plane.
  • A polarising filter (Polaroid) transmits only oscillations in one plane.

Two Polarising Filters

If two polarising filters are aligned, maximum light passes through. If they are crossed (at 90°), no light passes through. At an angle θ between them, the transmitted intensity follows Malus's Law:

I = I0 cos2θ

Applications: Polaroid sunglasses (reduce glare from reflected light), LCD screens, stress analysis of transparent materials, confirming transverse nature of electromagnetic waves.

Exam Tip: Longitudinal waves (e.g. sound) cannot be polarised because their oscillations are already in one direction (parallel to wave travel). Polarisation is exclusive proof of transverse wave nature.
