4. Random and Systematic Errors

Random error: An unpredictable variation in measurements that causes readings to scatter about the true value. Each measurement is equally likely to be above or below the true value. Reduced by repeating measurements and averaging.
Systematic error: A consistent error in the same direction every time a measurement is taken. It shifts all readings by the same amount (or proportion) away from the true value. It is NOT reduced by averaging.

Examples of random errors: fluctuations in readings due to electrical noise, varying reaction times, slight differences in positioning instruments.

Examples of systematic errors: zero error on a balance or micrometer, consistently reading a meniscus from the wrong angle (parallax), a miscalibrated instrument.

5. Precision and Accuracy

Precision: How close repeated measurements are to each other. High precision means low spread (small random error). Related to the resolution of the instrument.
Accuracy: How close a measurement is to the true value. High accuracy means low systematic error.

A measurement can be precise but not accurate (e.g. consistently reading 10.3 when the true value is 10.0) or accurate but not precise (e.g. readings scattered around the true value with large spread).

Exam Tip: Repeating measurements improves precision (reduces the effect of random error) but does NOT improve accuracy. To improve accuracy, you must eliminate systematic errors (e.g. recalibrate the instrument).

6. Uncertainties

6.1 Absolute Uncertainty

The absolute uncertainty is the margin of error in a measurement, expressed in the same units as the measurement.

  • For a single reading with an analogue scale: uncertainty = ± half the smallest division.
  • For a single reading with a digital instrument: uncertainty = ± the last digit.
  • For repeated readings: uncertainty = range / 2 (i.e. half the range of the data set).

6.2 Percentage (Fractional) Uncertainty

Percentage uncertainty = (absolute uncertainty / measured value) × 100%

6.3 Combining Uncertainties

OperationRule
Addition or Subtraction
(e.g. y = a + b or y = a − b)		Add the absolute uncertainties:
Δy = Δa + Δb
Multiplication or Division
(e.g. y = ab or y = a/b)		Add the percentage uncertainties:
%Δy = %Δa + %Δb
Raising to a power
(e.g. y = an)		Multiply the percentage uncertainty by the power:
%Δy = n × %Δa
Exam Tip: When subtracting two similar values (e.g. a temperature change), the percentage uncertainty can become very large. This is why it is better to measure a large change where possible.

6.4 Uncertainty in a Mean

When you calculate a mean from repeated measurements:

Uncertainty in the mean = range / 2

where range = maximum value − minimum value.

6.5 Uncertainties from Graphs

When determining a gradient from a straight-line graph:

  • Draw the line of best fit.
  • Draw the steepest and shallowest lines that still pass through the error bars.
  • Uncertainty in gradient = (steepest gradient − shallowest gradient) / 2.
  • The same approach applies to the y-intercept.

7. Significant Figures

The number of significant figures in a result reflects the precision of the measurement.

  • All non-zero digits are significant: 237 has 3 s.f.
  • Zeros between non-zero digits are significant: 1002 has 4 s.f.
  • Leading zeros are NOT significant: 0.0045 has 2 s.f.
  • Trailing zeros after a decimal point ARE significant: 2.30 has 3 s.f.
Exam Tip: Your final answer should be given to the same number of significant figures as the data with the fewest significant figures used in the calculation. Do NOT round intermediate steps.
