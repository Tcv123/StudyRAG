3.1 Measurements and Their Errors

1. SI Base Units

The International System of Units (SI) defines seven base quantities, each with a base unit. These form the foundation of all physical measurement. Every other unit in physics can be derived from combinations of these seven.

Base QuantitySI Base UnitSymbol
Masskilogramkg

Electric currentampereA

Note that the kilogram is the only base unit with a prefix. The base unit of mass is not the gram. This matters when converting: 1 g = 10-3 kg, not 1 kg = 10-3 g.

2. Derived Units

Derived units are combinations of base units. They can always be expressed in terms of kg, m, s, A, K, mol. Being able to derive the base units of any quantity is essential for checking equations.

How to Derive Base Units

Start from a defining equation and substitute until only base units remain.

Worked Example: Show that the volt has base units kg m2 s-3 A-1.

V = W / Q (energy per unit charge)
W is in joules: J = N m = (kg m s-2)(m) = kg m2 s-2
Q is in coulombs: C = A s
So V = kg m2 s-2 / (A s) = kg m2 s-3 A-1

Homogeneity of Units (Dimensional Analysis)

Homogeneity: An equation is homogeneous if the units on the left-hand side are identical to the units on the right-hand side. Every valid physics equation must be homogeneous, though a homogeneous equation is not necessarily correct (it could have a wrong numerical factor or missing terms).
Worked Example: Check whether Ek = ½mv2 is homogeneous.

LHS: Ek is in joules = kg m2 s-2
RHS: ½ is dimensionless, m is in kg, v2 is in (m s-1)2 = m2 s-2
So RHS = kg × m2 s-2 = kg m2 s-2
LHS = RHS, so the equation is homogeneous.
Exam Tip: Dimensional analysis can help you spot errors in your working or eliminate wrong answers in multiple choice. If the units don't match on both sides, the equation (or your rearrangement of it) must be wrong. However, it cannot detect missing dimensionless constants like ½ or 2π.

4. Random and Systematic Errors

Random error: An unpredictable variation in measurements that causes readings to scatter about the true value. Each measurement is equally likely to be above or below the true value. Reduced by repeating measurements and averaging.
Systematic error: A consistent error in the same direction every time a measurement is taken. It shifts all readings by the same amount (or proportion) away from the true value. It is NOT reduced by averaging.
FeatureRandom ErrorSystematic Error
Effect on readingsScatter about the true valueAll shifted in the same direction
Effect on meanMean approaches true value as repeats increaseMean is offset from true value regardless of repeats
How to detectSpread in repeated readingsCompare with a known accurate value
How to reduceRepeat and average; use more sensitive instrumentsRecalibrate instruments; correct technique; check zero error
ExamplesFluctuations from electrical noise, varying reaction times, slight positioning differencesZero error on a balance, parallax from consistent wrong angle, miscalibrated instrument
Zero error: A specific type of systematic error where an instrument gives a non-zero reading when it should read zero. Before taking measurements, always check and record any zero error, then subtract it from all subsequent readings.

Real-world application: In medical physics, systematic errors in radiation dosimetry equipment could lead to patients receiving incorrect doses. Regular calibration against national standards is essential. Random errors in blood pressure measurements are reduced by taking multiple readings and averaging.

5. Precision, Accuracy, and Resolution

Precision: How close repeated measurements are to each other. High precision means low spread (small random error). Related to the resolution of the instrument.
Accuracy: How close a measurement is to the true value. High accuracy means low systematic error.
Resolution: The smallest change in a quantity that an instrument can detect. For example, a ruler with mm markings has a resolution of 1 mm; a micrometer has a resolution of 0.01 mm.
Repeatability: Whether the same person using the same equipment gets the same results. Reproducibility: Whether different people using different equipment get the same results.

A measurement can be precise but not accurate (e.g. consistently reading 10.3 when the true value is 10.0) or accurate but not precise (e.g. readings scattered around the true value with large spread).

Exam Tip: Repeating measurements improves precision (reduces the effect of random error) but does NOT improve accuracy. To improve accuracy, you must eliminate systematic errors (e.g. recalibrate the instrument). These are different concepts — do not confuse them.

6. Uncertainties

6.1 Absolute Uncertainty

The absolute uncertainty is the margin of error in a measurement, expressed in the same units as the measurement.

  • For a single reading with an analogue scale: uncertainty = ± half the smallest division.
  • For a single reading with a digital instrument: uncertainty = ± the last digit (the resolution).
  • For repeated readings: uncertainty = range / 2 (i.e. half the range of the data set).
Worked Example: A ruler with 1 mm divisions is used to measure a length. The reading is 245 mm.

Uncertainty = ± half the smallest division = ± 0.5 mm
Length = 245.0 ± 0.5 mm = 0.2450 ± 0.0005 m
Worked Example: Five measurements of a time period give: 2.31, 2.28, 2.34, 2.30, 2.32 s.

Mean = (2.31 + 2.28 + 2.34 + 2.30 + 2.32) / 5 = 2.310 s
Range = 2.34 - 2.28 = 0.06 s
Uncertainty = range / 2 = 0.03 s
Result: T = 2.31 ± 0.03 s

6.2 Percentage (Fractional) Uncertainty

Percentage uncertainty = (absolute uncertainty / measured value) × 100%
Worked Example: A diameter is measured as 5.30 ± 0.05 mm. Find the percentage uncertainty.

Percentage uncertainty = (0.05 / 5.30) × 100% = 0.94%

Worked Example: Calculate the resistivity and its uncertainty given:
R = 4.50 ± 0.05 Ω, L = 0.800 ± 0.001 m, d = 0.28 ± 0.01 mm

A = π(d/2)2 = π(0.14 × 10-3)2 = 6.16 × 10-8 m2
ρ = RA/L = (4.50)(6.16 × 10-8) / 0.800 = 3.47 × 10-7 Ω m

Now for the uncertainty:
%ΔR = (0.05/4.50) × 100 = 1.11%
%ΔL = (0.001/0.800) × 100 = 0.125%
%Δd = (0.01/0.28) × 100 = 3.57%
%ΔA = 2 × %Δd = 2 × 3.57% = 7.14% (because A depends on d2)

%Δρ = %ΔR + %ΔA + %ΔL = 1.11 + 7.14 + 0.125 = 8.4%
Δρ = 0.084 × 3.47 × 10-7 = 0.29 × 10-7 Ω m
ρ = (3.5 ± 0.3) × 10-7 Ω m

Note: The diameter has the largest percentage uncertainty, so it dominates the final uncertainty.
Exam Tip: When subtracting two similar values (e.g. a temperature change of 3 °C from readings of 22 °C and 25 °C), the percentage uncertainty can become very large even though the absolute uncertainties are small. This is why it is better to measure a large change where possible. For example, measure the time for 10 oscillations rather than 1.

6.4 Uncertainty in a Mean

When you calculate a mean from repeated measurements:

Uncertainty in the mean = range / 2

where range = maximum value − minimum value. If this is smaller than the instrument resolution, use the instrument resolution instead.

6.5 Uncertainties from Graphs

When determining a gradient from a straight-line graph:

  1. Draw the line of best fit through the data points.
  2. Draw the steepest acceptable line that still passes through the error bars.
  3. Draw the shallowest acceptable line that still passes through the error bars.
  4. Uncertainty in gradient = (steepest gradient − shallowest gradient) / 2.
  5. The same approach applies to the y-intercept.
Worked Example: Reading gradients from a graph:

Line of best fit gradient = (260 - 48) / (430 - 80) = 0.606
Steepest gradient = (255 - 35) / (430 - 80) = 0.629
Shallowest gradient = (270 - 62) / (430 - 80) = 0.594

Uncertainty in gradient = (0.629 - 0.594) / 2 = ± 0.018
Gradient = 0.606 ± 0.018 (or 0.61 ± 0.02 to 2 s.f.)
Exam Tip: When plotting a graph with a y-intercept that has physical meaning (e.g. EMF from a V vs I graph), calculate the y-intercept uncertainty from the steepest and shallowest lines too. Use two large, well-separated points on each line to calculate the gradient — not the data points themselves.

7. Significant Figures

The number of significant figures in a result reflects the precision of the measurement.

  • All non-zero digits are significant: 237 has 3 s.f.
  • Zeros between non-zero digits are significant: 1002 has 4 s.f.
  • Leading zeros are NOT significant: 0.0045 has 2 s.f.
  • Trailing zeros after a decimal point ARE significant: 2.30 has 3 s.f.
  • Trailing zeros without a decimal point are ambiguous: 1200 could be 2, 3, or 4 s.f. (use standard form to be clear: 1.2 × 103 for 2 s.f.)

Rules for Calculations

  • Multiplication/Division: Round to the same number of significant figures as the value with the fewest s.f.
  • Addition/Subtraction: Round to the same number of decimal places as the value with the fewest d.p.
  • Never round intermediate steps — only round the final answer.
Worked Example: Calculate the kinetic energy of a 2.5 kg object moving at 3.42 m s-1.

Ek = ½mv2 = ½ × 2.5 × (3.42)2 = ½ × 2.5 × 11.6964 = 14.6205 J

The value with fewest s.f. is 2.5 (2 s.f.), so Ek = 15 J (2 s.f.)
Exam Tip: Your final answer should be given to the same number of significant figures as the data with the fewest significant figures used in the calculation. Do NOT round intermediate steps. If a question says "give your answer to 3 significant figures," follow that instruction even if the data would suggest otherwise.

8. Orders of Magnitude and Estimation

Order of magnitude: The power of ten closest to a quantity. Used for rough comparisons and sanity checks. Two quantities are of the same order of magnitude if one is no more than about 10 times larger than the other.

Estimation Strategy

When making an estimate:

  1. Identify the relevant physics equation or principle.
  2. Estimate each quantity to 1 significant figure or the nearest order of magnitude.
  3. Perform the calculation, showing working clearly.
  4. Give your answer to 1 s.f. or as an order of magnitude.
  5. Sense-check your answer — does it seem reasonable?
Worked Example: Estimate the number of atoms in a human body.

Mass of a person ≈ 70 kg
Human body is mostly water (H2O), with molar mass ≈ 18 g/mol = 0.018 kg/mol
Number of moles ≈ 70 / 0.018 ≈ 4000 mol
Each molecule of water has 3 atoms
Number of atoms ≈ 3 × 4000 × 6 × 1023 ≈ 7 × 1027
Order of magnitude: 1028 atoms
Worked Example: Estimate the power output of a person running up stairs.

Mass ≈ 70 kg, height of one floor ≈ 3 m, time to climb ≈ 5 s
Work done = mgh = 70 × 10 × 3 = 2100 J
Power = W/t = 2100 / 5 ≈ 400 W (order of magnitude 102–103 W)
Exam Tip: Estimation questions often appear on Paper 1 (worth 2-4 marks). They expect you to use sensible approximations and give your answer to 1 significant figure or as an order of magnitude. Show your working clearly — marks are given for your method even if your estimate differs from the mark scheme. Common estimation targets: number of molecules, energy costs, sizes of objects, forces in everyday situations.
