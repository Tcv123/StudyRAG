3.6 Further Mechanics and Thermal Physics

1. Circular Motion

Uniform circular motion: Motion in a circle at constant speed. Although the speed is constant, the velocity is continuously changing (because the direction changes), so the object is accelerating.

Key Quantities

QuantitySymbolEquationUnits
Angular velocityωω = 2πf = 2π/T = v/rrad s-1
Linear speedvv = ωr = 2πr/Tm s-1
Centripetal accelerationaa = v2/r = ω2rm s-2
Centripetal forceFF = mv2/r = mω2rN

The centripetal acceleration (and force) is always directed towards the centre of the circle. It is perpendicular to the velocity at all times.

Exam Tip: Centripetal force is NOT an extra force. It is the resultant of real forces (gravity, tension, friction, normal contact) that happen to point towards the centre. Never label "centripetal force" as a separate force on a free body diagram.

2. Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM)

Simple harmonic motion: Oscillatory motion in which the acceleration is always directed towards a fixed equilibrium position and is proportional to the displacement from that position.
a = −ω2x

The negative sign indicates the acceleration is always in the opposite direction to the displacement (i.e. towards the equilibrium).

SHM Equations

QuantityEquation
Displacementx = A cos(ωt)   (if starting at max displacement)
x = A sin(ωt)   (if starting at equilibrium)
Velocityv = −Aω sin(ωt)
v = ±ω√(A2 − x2)
Maximum velocityvmax = Aω   (at x = 0, the equilibrium)
Maximum accelerationamax = Aω2   (at x = ±A, the extremes)

Energy in SHM

  • At the equilibrium (x = 0): KE is maximum, PE is zero.
  • At the extremes (x = ±A): KE is zero, PE is maximum.
  • Total energy = KE + PE = constant (in the absence of damping).
  • Etotal = ½mω2A2

Time Periods of SHM Systems

Mass-spring system:

T = 2π√(m/k)

T depends on mass and spring constant. It does NOT depend on amplitude.

Simple pendulum:

T = 2π√(L/g)

T depends on length and gravitational field strength. It does NOT depend on mass or amplitude (for small angles).

Exam Tip: In SHM, the period is independent of amplitude. This is a defining feature. If an oscillator has a period that changes with amplitude, it is NOT SHM.

3. Damping

Damping: The reduction in amplitude of an oscillation over time due to energy being transferred to the surroundings (usually as thermal energy due to frictional forces).
Type of DampingDescription
Light dampingAmplitude decreases gradually over many oscillations. The frequency is hardly affected. Example: pendulum in air.
Heavy dampingAmplitude decreases rapidly. The system returns to equilibrium slowly without oscillating (or with very few oscillations). Example: door closer.
Critical dampingThe system returns to equilibrium in the shortest possible time without oscillating. Example: car suspension, needle on a measuring instrument.

Forced Oscillations and Resonance

Forced oscillation: An oscillation caused by an external periodic driving force.
Natural frequency (f0): The frequency at which a system oscillates freely when displaced and released.
Resonance: Occurs when the driving frequency equals the natural frequency of the system. At resonance, maximum energy is transferred from the driver to the oscillating system, resulting in maximum amplitude.

Effects of Damping on Resonance

  • Increased damping reduces the maximum amplitude at resonance.
  • Increased damping broadens the resonance peak.
  • Increased damping shifts the resonance frequency slightly below f0.

Examples of resonance: Tuning a radio (electrical resonance), Tacoma Narrows Bridge collapse (mechanical resonance), microwave heating (molecular resonance of water).

4. Thermal Physics

Internal Energy

Internal energy: The sum of the randomly distributed kinetic energies and potential energies of all the molecules in a system. For an ideal gas, internal energy is purely kinetic (no intermolecular forces, so no PE).

A change in internal energy can result from:

  • Doing work on or by the system
  • Heating or cooling the system

Specific Heat Capacity

Q = mcΔθ
  • Q = energy transferred (J)
  • m = mass (kg)
  • c = specific heat capacity (J kg-1 K-1) — the energy required to raise the temperature of 1 kg of the substance by 1 K
  • Δθ = change in temperature (K or °C)

Specific Latent Heat

Q = mL
  • L = specific latent heat (J kg-1) — the energy required to change the state of 1 kg of a substance without changing its temperature
  • Specific latent heat of fusion (Lf): for solid ↔ liquid transitions
  • Specific latent heat of vaporisation (Lv): for liquid ↔ gas transitions

During a change of state, temperature remains constant because the energy supplied is used to break (or form) intermolecular bonds, changing potential energy without changing kinetic energy.

Exam Tip: In a heating curve (temperature vs time), the flat sections represent changes of state. The gradient of the sloped sections depends on specific heat capacity. A lower gradient means a higher specific heat capacity (for the same power input and mass).

5. Gas Laws and the Ideal Gas

The Three Gas Laws

LawStatementEquationConditions
Boyle's LawPressure is inversely proportional to volumepV = constantConstant temperature and mass
Charles's LawVolume is directly proportional to absolute temperatureV/T = constantConstant pressure and mass
Pressure LawPressure is directly proportional to absolute temperaturep/T = constantConstant volume and mass

These combine into:

pV / T = constant   or   p1V1/T1 = p2V2/T2

Ideal Gas Equation

pV = nRT = NkT
  • p = pressure (Pa)
  • V = volume (m3)
  • n = number of moles
  • R = molar gas constant = 8.31 J mol-1 K-1
  • N = number of molecules
  • k = Boltzmann constant = 1.38 × 10-23 J K-1
  • T = absolute temperature (K)

Note: N = nNA and k = R/NA, where NA = 6.02 × 1023 mol-1 (Avogadro's number).

Exam Tip: Always convert temperature to kelvin (K = °C + 273) and volume to m3 (1 litre = 10-3 m3) before using the ideal gas equation.

6. Kinetic Theory of Gases

Assumptions of an Ideal Gas

  • The gas contains a very large number of molecules in random motion.
  • The volume of the molecules is negligible compared to the volume of the container.
  • Collisions between molecules and between molecules and the walls are perfectly elastic.
  • The time of a collision is negligible compared to the time between collisions.
  • There are no intermolecular forces except during collisions.

Kinetic Theory Equation

pV = ⅓Nmc2

where c2 is the mean square speed of the molecules.

Root Mean Square Speed

crms = √c2

The rms speed is NOT the same as the mean speed. It gives more weight to faster molecules.

Molecular Kinetic Energy

½mc2 = &frac32;kT

The average translational kinetic energy of a gas molecule is directly proportional to the absolute temperature. This connects the microscopic (molecular KE) to the macroscopic (temperature).

Exam Tip: The Boltzmann constant k links microscopic energy to temperature. If a question asks about energy per molecule, use &frac32;kT. If it asks about energy per mole, use &frac32;RT.

⚗ Required Practical 7: Investigation into Simple Harmonic Motion (Mass-Spring & Pendulum)

Apparatus

(a) Mass-Spring:

  • Helical spring (known spring constant k, or to be determined)
  • Slotted masses and hanger
  • Retort stand, boss, and clamp
  • Metre ruler (with fiducial marker at equilibrium)
  • Stopwatch (resolution 0.01 s)

(b) Simple Pendulum:

  • String (at least 1 m, light and inextensible)
  • Pendulum bob (small, dense sphere)
  • Clamp, boss, and retort stand
  • Metre ruler or tape measure
  • Protractor (to check angle < 10°)
  • Stopwatch

Method

(a) Mass-Spring:

  1. Hang the spring from the clamp. Attach a known mass m and allow it to reach equilibrium. Mark the equilibrium position with a fiducial marker on the ruler.
  2. Pull the mass down a small distance (small amplitude, < 5 cm) and release. Time 10 complete oscillations with the stopwatch. Divide by 10 to get T.
  3. Repeat 3 times and calculate a mean value of T at each mass.
  4. Change the mass in steps (e.g. 50 g increments). Record T for each mass.
  5. Plot T² (y-axis) vs m (x-axis). The gradient = 4π²/k, so k = 4π²/gradient.

(b) Simple Pendulum:

  1. Set up the pendulum with a measured length L (from the pivot point to the centre of the bob).
  2. Displace the bob by a small angle (< 10°) and release. Time 10 complete oscillations. Divide by 10 to get T.
  3. Repeat 3 times and calculate a mean T.
  4. Change L in steps (e.g. 0.1 m from 0.2 m to 1.0 m). Record T at each length.
  5. Plot T² (y-axis) vs L (x-axis). The gradient = 4π²/g, so g = 4π²/gradient.

Safety

  • Ensure the retort stand is clamped securely to the bench to prevent toppling.
  • Place a cushion beneath hanging masses in case the spring breaks.
  • Keep small amplitudes to ensure SHM conditions are met and to avoid large swings.
  • Stand clear of the swinging pendulum bob.

Analysis

Mass-Spring: T = 2π√(m/k), so T² = (4π²/k)m.

T² = (4π² / k) × m

Gradient of T² vs m graph = 4π²/k. Calculate k and compare with the manufacturer's value.

Simple Pendulum: T = 2π√(L/g), so T² = (4π²/g)L.

T² = (4π² / g) × L

Gradient of T² vs L graph = 4π²/g. Calculate g and compare with 9.81 m s-2.

Sources of Error & Improvements

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Human reaction time when using a stopwatch (~0.2 s)RandomTime 10 (or more) oscillations and divide; use a light gate or motion sensor
Amplitude too large — motion is not truly SHMSystematicUse small amplitudes (< 10° for pendulum, small extension for spring)
Pendulum length measured to the wrong point on the bobSystematicMeasure to the centre of the bob; use a small, dense bob
Spring may oscillate in a non-vertical mode (wobbling)RandomGuide the mass vertically; use a card or guide tube
Counting error (miscounting oscillations)RandomUse a fiducial marker; start counting from zero when the mass passes the marker
Effective mass of the spring itself contributes to the periodSystematicUse a light spring; the y-intercept of the T² vs m graph accounts for the spring's effective mass (⅓ of spring mass)
Exam Tip: Always time at least 10 oscillations and divide to reduce the percentage uncertainty from reaction time. Start counting from zero (not one) as the mass passes the fiducial marker. Plotting T² (not T) gives a straight-line graph, making it easier to determine the gradient and hence k or g.

⚗ Required Practical 8: Investigation of Boyle's Law and Charles's Law

Apparatus

(a) Boyle's Law:

  • Gas syringe (sealed) with oil plug to prevent gas leaks
  • Bourdon pressure gauge or digital pressure sensor
  • Clamp and stand

(b) Charles's Law:

  • Sealed capillary tube containing a trapped air column (sealed by a drop of oil or concentrated sulfuric acid)
  • Water bath (beaker of water)
  • Thermometer (−10 °C to 110 °C) or temperature sensor
  • Bunsen burner or electric hot plate
  • Ruler (to measure the length of the gas column)
  • Stirrer

Method

(a) Boyle's Law (constant temperature):

  1. Record the initial volume V of trapped gas from the syringe scale and the pressure P from the gauge.
  2. Slowly push the plunger in to reduce the volume. Wait for the temperature to equalise (so the process is isothermal), then record V and P.
  3. Repeat for several different volumes, both increasing and decreasing the volume.
  4. Plot P (y-axis) vs 1/V (x-axis). A straight line through the origin confirms Boyle's Law: P ∝ 1/V (at constant T).
  5. Alternatively plot PV vs P — a horizontal line confirms PV = constant.

(b) Charles's Law (constant pressure):

  1. Place the capillary tube (with trapped gas column) in the water bath. Record the initial temperature T and the length L of the gas column (L ∝ V since the tube has uniform cross-section).
  2. Heat the water slowly, stirring continuously to ensure a uniform temperature. At regular temperature intervals (e.g. every 10 °C), record T and L.
  3. Continue until the water is near boiling. Then allow to cool and take readings on the way down to check for consistency.
  4. Convert temperatures to kelvin: T(K) = T(°C) + 273.15.
  5. Plot V (or L) against T (in kelvin). A straight line through the origin (when extrapolated) confirms Charles's Law: V ∝ T at constant P. The x-intercept should be near −273 °C (absolute zero).

Safety

  • Take care with hot water — use tongs or a clamp to handle the capillary tube.
  • If using concentrated sulfuric acid as a plug, handle with extreme care (wear gloves and goggles).
  • Do not heat above 100 °C — the tube may crack or the plug may be expelled.
  • Ensure the gas syringe is clamped securely to prevent it falling.
  • For Boyle's law, do not compress the gas too quickly (risk of syringe popping).

Analysis

Boyle's Law: PV = constant at constant T. Plot P vs 1/V → straight line through origin, gradient = PV = nRT.

Charles's Law: V/T = constant at constant P. Plot V vs T(K) → straight line through origin (or extrapolates to 0 V at 0 K).

PV = nRT

Sources of Error & Improvements

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Temperature not uniform in the water bathSystematicStir the water continuously; allow time to equilibrate before reading
Gas leaks past the oil plug or syringe sealSystematicUse a good seal; check for leaks by seeing if readings are consistent when repeated
Friction in the syringe makes pressure readings inaccurateSystematicTap the syringe gently before reading; use a low-friction syringe
Gas may not behave ideally at high pressures or extreme temperaturesSystematicKeep within moderate pressure and temperature ranges
Reading the gas column length is impreciseRandomUse a magnifying glass; read from the same angle to avoid parallax
Exam Tip: For Boyle's law, plot P vs 1/V (not P vs V, which gives a curve). For Charles's law, always convert to kelvin before plotting. Extrapolating the V vs T graph to V = 0 should give an x-intercept near −273 °C, providing an estimate of absolute zero.
