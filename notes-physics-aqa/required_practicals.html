⚗ RP1: Stationary Waves on a String

Apparatus

  • Signal generator and vibrator
  • String (e.g. nylon thread)
  • Pulley and bench clamp
  • Slotted masses and hanger
  • Metre ruler
  • Wooden bridge

Method

  1. Set up the vibrator attached to a signal generator at one end of a string. Pass the string over a pulley with a hanging mass to provide tension.
  2. Turn on the signal generator and slowly increase the frequency until a clear stationary wave with one loop (fundamental) forms.
  3. Record the frequency and measure the length of the vibrating section (L) using a metre ruler.
  4. Increase frequency to find harmonics (2 loops, 3 loops, etc.). Record frequency and number of loops each time.
  5. Vary the tension by changing the hanging mass. Repeat measurements.
  6. Vary mass per unit length by using different string types.

Safety

  • Use a safety screen in case the string snaps
  • Ensure masses cannot fall on feet — use a soft landing pad
  • Keep signal generator frequencies below harmful levels

Analysis

For the fundamental: f = 1/(2L) × √(T/μ)

Plot f vs 1/L — expect a straight line through the origin. Gradient = (1/2)√(T/μ).

Alternatively, plot f vs √T keeping L constant — straight line through origin.

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Difficult to judge exact resonance pointRandomLook for maximum amplitude; use a paper rider to detect resonance
String may not vibrate in a perfect planeRandomEnsure vibrator is firmly clamped and aligned
Ruler parallax when measuring LRandomUse a metre ruler with mm divisions; read at eye level
Mass hanger has its own massSystematicInclude mass of hanger in total tension calculation
Exam Tip: Remember that nodes are points of zero displacement and antinodes are points of maximum displacement. The fundamental has exactly one antinode. The wavelength of the stationary wave equals 2L for the fundamental mode.

⚗ RP2: Young's Double Slit & Diffraction Grating

Apparatus

  • Laser (or white light + single slit + colour filter)
  • Double slit (known slit separation d)
  • Diffraction grating (known line spacing d)
  • Screen (white card or wall)
  • Metre ruler and/or vernier callipers
  • Tape measure for slit-to-screen distance D

Method (Double Slit)

  1. Darken the room. Direct the laser beam through the double slit onto a distant screen.
  2. Observe the interference pattern of bright and dark fringes on the screen.
  3. Measure the distance across several fringe spacings (e.g. 10 fringes) and divide to get fringe spacing w.
  4. Measure the slit-to-screen distance D with a tape measure.
  5. Calculate wavelength: λ = wd/D.

Method (Diffraction Grating)

  1. Direct the laser through the diffraction grating onto a screen.
  2. Measure the angle θ to each order maximum using tanθ = x/D, where x is the distance from the central maximum.
  3. Calculate wavelength: d sinθ = nλ.

Safety

  • Never look directly into the laser beam — Class 2 laser safety
  • Display "Laser in use" warning sign
  • Remove reflective objects (watches, jewellery)

Analysis

Double slit: λ = wd / D

Diffraction grating: d sinθ = nλ

The grating gives sharper, more widely spaced maxima — more precise wavelength determination.

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Fringes not sharp; hard to locate centresRandomUse a diffraction grating for sharper maxima
Measurement of D inaccurateRandomUse a long D (>1 m) to reduce percentage uncertainty
Ruler not aligned with fringe patternSystematicEnsure ruler is parallel to fringe spacing direction
Laser beam not perpendicular to slitSystematicAlign carefully; use adjustable stand
Exam Tip: The diffraction grating is more accurate than the double slit because the maxima are sharper and more widely spaced, reducing percentage uncertainty in angle measurement. Always measure across multiple fringes and divide.

⚗ RP3: Determination of g by Free-Fall

Apparatus

  • Electromagnet connected to a switch and power supply
  • Steel ball bearing
  • Trapdoor switch connected to electronic timer
  • Metre ruler (or 2 m ruler)
  • Clamp stand and boss

Method

  1. Clamp the electromagnet at a measured height s above the trapdoor.
  2. Place the steel ball on the electromagnet. When the switch is released, the timer starts and the ball falls.
  3. The ball hits the trapdoor, which opens and stops the timer. Record time t.
  4. Repeat 3 times at each height and take the mean t.
  5. Vary the height s (at least 6 different values from 0.2 m to 1.0 m).
  6. Plot s vs t². The gradient equals g/2, so g = 2 × gradient.

Safety

  • Place a soft pad/cushion below the trapdoor to catch the ball
  • Ensure clamp stand is stable and won't topple
  • Keep feet clear of falling ball

Analysis

Using s = ½gt² (since initial velocity u = 0):

s = (g/2) t²

Plot s (y-axis) vs t² (x-axis). Gradient = g/2, so g = 2 × gradient. Expected value: ~9.81 m s².

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Ball may not release cleanly from electromagnet (residual magnetism)Systematic (makes t too large, g too small)Use a non-magnetic ball release mechanism
Reaction time in starting/stopping timerRandomUse electronic timer triggered by switch release and trapdoor
Height measurement uncertaintyRandomUse a 2 m ruler; measure from bottom of ball to trapdoor
Air resistance on ballSystematic (g appears smaller)Use a dense, small ball to minimise air resistance
Exam Tip: The y-intercept of the s vs t² graph should be zero. A non-zero intercept suggests a systematic error such as residual magnetism causing a delayed release. Always check that u = 0 by ensuring the ball starts from rest.

⚗ RP4: Young Modulus of a Wire

Apparatus

  • Long thin wire (copper or steel, ~2 m)
  • Micrometer screw gauge (to measure wire diameter)
  • Vernier scale or travelling microscope (to measure extension)
  • Slotted masses and hanger
  • Metre ruler or tape measure (for original length)
  • G-clamp and wooden blocks
  • Safety goggles

Method

  1. Measure the diameter of the wire at several points along its length using a micrometer. Take at least 6 readings in different orientations. Calculate mean diameter, then cross-sectional area A = πd²/4.
  2. Measure the original length L of the wire from the clamp to the marker using a tape measure.
  3. Add masses in steps (e.g. 100 g increments). After each addition, measure the extension using the vernier scale.
  4. Record force F = mg and corresponding extension ΔL.
  5. Continue up to a sensible maximum load (below the elastic limit).
  6. Unload and check the wire returns to original length (elastic behaviour).

Safety

  • Wear safety goggles — wire may snap under tension
  • Place a cushion or sand tray beneath the masses
  • Do not exceed the elastic limit of the wire

Analysis

Plot stress (σ = F/A) vs strain (ε = ΔL/L).

Young modulus E = stress / strain = gradient of linear region

Alternatively, plot F vs ΔL; gradient = EA/L, so E = (gradient × L)/A.

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Wire diameter varies along lengthRandomTake multiple readings at different positions and orientations; use mean
Wire may not be straight initially (kinks)SystematicApply a small initial load to straighten the wire before measuring
Difficulty reading small extensionsRandomUse a vernier scale or travelling microscope for precision
Thermal expansion of wireSystematicUse a reference wire to compensate for temperature changes
Exam Tip: Use a long, thin wire to maximise the extension for a given force, making measurements easier and reducing percentage uncertainty. The percentage uncertainty in diameter is often the largest source of error because d is squared in the area calculation.

⚗ RP5: Resistivity of a Wire

Apparatus

  • Constantan wire (approx. 1 m, taped to a ruler)
  • Ammeter (or multimeter on A range)
  • Voltmeter (or multimeter on V range)
  • Power supply or cell with switch
  • Micrometer screw gauge
  • Metre ruler
  • Crocodile clips

Method

  1. Measure the diameter of the wire using a micrometer at several points. Calculate mean d and cross-sectional area A = πd²/4.
  2. Connect the circuit: cell → switch → ammeter → wire (in series); voltmeter in parallel across the wire.
  3. Use crocodile clips to select a length L of wire (start with ~0.1 m).
  4. Close the switch briefly, read V and I. Calculate R = V/I.
  5. Repeat for at least 6 different lengths (0.1 to 1.0 m).
  6. Plot R vs L. Gradient = ρ/A, so ρ = gradient × A.

Safety

  • Switch off between readings to prevent the wire heating up
  • Do not touch the wire when current is flowing — it may get hot
  • Use low voltage supply (< 6 V)

Analysis

Since R = ρL/A:

R = (ρ/A) × L

Plot R (y-axis) vs L (x-axis). Gradient = ρ/A. Therefore ρ = gradient × A.

Expected resistivity of constantan: ~4.9 × 10-7 Ω m.

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Wire heats up, changing resistanceSystematicSwitch off between readings; use low current
Contact resistance at crocodile clipsSystematicClean contacts; ensure firm connection
Diameter varies along wireRandomMeasure at multiple points and take mean
Length measurement error at clip positionRandomTape wire to ruler; align clips carefully
Exam Tip: Keep the current small and switch off between readings to avoid heating the wire. Constantan is used because its resistivity barely changes with temperature. The biggest uncertainty usually comes from the diameter measurement since it is squared in the area formula.

⚗ RP6: EMF and Internal Resistance

Apparatus

  • Cell (e.g. 1.5 V AA battery or similar)
  • Variable resistor (rheostat)
  • Ammeter
  • Voltmeter
  • Switch
  • Connecting wires

Method

  1. Connect the cell, ammeter, variable resistor, and switch in series. Connect the voltmeter in parallel across the cell terminals.
  2. Close the switch and record V and I for one resistance setting.
  3. Change the variable resistor to a new setting. Record new V and I.
  4. Repeat for at least 6 different resistance values, giving a range of currents.
  5. Open the switch between readings to prevent the cell from running down.
  6. Plot V vs I.

Safety

  • Do not short-circuit the cell (minimum resistance always present)
  • Switch off between readings to preserve cell life
  • Handle cells carefully — do not attempt to recharge non-rechargeable cells

Analysis

From V = ε − Ir:

Plot V (y-axis) vs I (x-axis). This gives a straight line with:

  • y-intercept = ε (EMF of the cell)
  • gradient = −r (negative of internal resistance)
Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Cell runs down during experimentSystematic (EMF decreases)Work quickly; switch off between readings
Ammeter has its own resistanceSystematicUse a digital ammeter with very low resistance
Voltmeter draws some currentSystematicUse a high-resistance voltmeter (digital preferred)
Fluctuating readingsRandomTake multiple readings; wait for readings to stabilise
Exam Tip: When V = 0, all the EMF is dropped across the internal resistance (short circuit). The x-intercept of the V vs I graph gives the maximum current I = ε/r. Internal resistance is typically very small (0.5–2 Ω for a standard cell).

⚗ RP7: SHM — Mass-Spring and Pendulum

Apparatus

  • Mass-spring: Helical spring, slotted masses, clamp stand, stopwatch, ruler
  • Pendulum: String, bob (small dense mass), protractor, clamp stand, stopwatch, ruler
  • Optional: light gate and datalogger for more precise timing

Method (Mass-Spring)

  1. Hang a known mass m from the spring. Pull it down by a small displacement and release.
  2. Time 10 complete oscillations. Divide by 10 to get period T.
  3. Repeat for at least 6 different masses.
  4. Plot T² vs m. Gradient = 4π²/k, so k = 4π²/gradient.

Method (Pendulum)

  1. Measure the length L from the pivot to the centre of the bob.
  2. Displace the bob by a small angle (< 10°) and release.
  3. Time 10 complete oscillations. Divide by 10 to get T.
  4. Repeat for at least 6 different lengths.
  5. Plot T² vs L. Gradient = 4π²/g, so g = 4π²/gradient.

Safety

  • Ensure clamp stand is stable (use a G-clamp or heavy base)
  • Keep clear of swinging masses
  • Do not overstretch the spring

Analysis

Mass-spring: T = 2π√(m/k) ⇒ T² = (4π²/k)m

Pendulum: T = 2π√(L/g) ⇒ T² = (4π²/g)L

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Miscounting oscillationsRandomCount from the same point each time (e.g. equilibrium)
Large amplitude invalidates SHM (pendulum)SystematicKeep angle below 10°
Spring may oscillate in a non-vertical planeRandomUse a guide tube or release carefully from rest
Reaction time with stopwatchRandomTime 10+ oscillations; use fiducial marker
Exam Tip: Always time at least 10 oscillations and divide by 10 to reduce percentage uncertainty in T. For the pendulum, the period is independent of mass and amplitude (for small angles). For the mass-spring, T does not depend on g.

⚗ RP8: Boyle's Law and Charles's Law

Apparatus

  • Gas syringe with sealed end (or Boyle's law apparatus with oil column)
  • Bourdon pressure gauge
  • Thermometer
  • Water bath (for Charles's law)
  • Ruler (if using a capillary tube method)

Method (Boyle's Law)

  1. Set up the sealed syringe connected to a pressure gauge.
  2. Record the initial volume V (from syringe scale) and pressure P (from gauge).
  3. Slowly push the piston inward to reduce the volume. Wait for temperature to equilibrate.
  4. Record new V and P. Repeat for at least 6 volume settings.
  5. Plot P vs 1/V. Expect a straight line through the origin, confirming PV = constant.

Method (Charles's Law)

  1. Trap a column of air in a sealed capillary tube (with an oil plug or similar).
  2. Place the tube in a water bath. Heat slowly and record temperature and volume (length of air column) at regular intervals.
  3. Plot V vs T (in kelvin). Expect a straight line; extrapolate back to V = 0 to find absolute zero.

Safety

  • Do not over-pressurise the syringe — risk of bursting
  • Handle hot water carefully (Charles's law); use a thermometer, not your hand
  • Use a safety screen if using pressurised apparatus

Analysis

Boyle's law: P ∝ 1/V at constant T — plot P vs 1/V (straight line through origin)

Charles's law: V ∝ T at constant P — plot V vs T/K (straight line; x-intercept gives absolute zero ≈ −273 °C)

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Gas temperature changes when compressedSystematicCompress slowly; wait for thermal equilibrium
Friction in syringe pistonSystematicLubricate piston; tap gently to free it
Leaks in apparatusSystematicCheck all seals before starting; use Vaseline on joints
Reading pressure gauge at angleRandom (parallax)Read gauge at eye level
Exam Tip: For Boyle's law, plotting P vs 1/V is better than PV vs V because a straight line is easier to verify than a horizontal line. For Charles's law, always convert temperatures to kelvin before plotting.

⚗ RP9: Capacitor Charge and Discharge

Apparatus

  • Capacitor (e.g. 1000 μF electrolytic)
  • Resistor (e.g. 10 kΩ — to give a convenient time constant)
  • DC power supply
  • Voltmeter or datalogger (to record voltage across capacitor)
  • Stopwatch (if not using datalogger)
  • Two-way switch (to toggle between charge and discharge)

Method

  1. Connect the capacitor, resistor, and power supply in series with a two-way switch.
  2. Connect a voltmeter (or datalogger) across the capacitor.
  3. Charge: flip the switch to connect the supply. Record V vs t as the capacitor charges.
  4. Discharge: flip the switch to disconnect the supply and connect the capacitor to discharge through R. Record V vs t.
  5. Take readings every 5–10 seconds (or use a datalogger for continuous recording).
  6. Plot ln(V) vs t for the discharge curve.

Safety

  • Ensure the capacitor is connected with correct polarity (electrolytic capacitors)
  • Discharge the capacitor fully before handling the circuit
  • Use low voltages (< 12 V)

Analysis

During discharge: V = V0 e−t/RC

Taking natural log: ln V = ln V0 − t/RC

Plot ln(V) vs t. Gradient = −1/RC. The time constant τ = RC is the time for V to fall to V0/e (≈ 37% of initial).

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Voltmeter draws current, affecting dischargeSystematicUse a high-impedance voltmeter or datalogger
Timing errors with manual stopwatchRandomUse a datalogger for continuous, automatic recording
Capacitor may have leakage currentSystematicUse a high-quality capacitor; check for self-discharge
Temperature changes affect resistanceSystematicConduct experiment in stable temperature environment
Exam Tip: The time constant τ = RC. After 1τ, V drops to 37% of V0. After 5τ, the capacitor is considered fully discharged (< 1% of V0). Choose R and C so that τ is a convenient value (e.g. 10–30 seconds) for easy measurement.

⚗ RP10: Force on a Current-Carrying Wire

Apparatus

  • U-shaped magnet (or horseshoe magnet on iron yoke)
  • Stiff copper wire shaped into a rectangular "cradle"
  • Top-pan balance (reading to 0.01 g)
  • DC power supply (variable current)
  • Ammeter
  • Connecting wires and crocodile clips
  • Ruler (to measure length of wire in the field)

Method

  1. Place the magnet assembly on the top-pan balance. Zero the balance.
  2. Position the wire "cradle" so the horizontal section sits in the magnetic field between the poles.
  3. Connect the wire to the DC supply and ammeter in series.
  4. Switch on the supply at a low current. Record the current I and the apparent mass change Δm on the balance.
  5. Calculate force: F = Δm × g.
  6. Repeat for at least 6 different currents.
  7. Measure the length L of wire in the field with a ruler.
  8. Plot F vs I.

Safety

  • Keep current low to avoid overheating the wire
  • Do not leave the current on for long periods
  • Handle magnets carefully near electronic equipment

Analysis

From F = BIL:

Plot F (y-axis) vs I (x-axis). Gradient = BL, so B = gradient / L.

Expect a straight line through the origin.

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Balance is sensitive to vibrations and draughtsRandomUse a draught shield; place balance on a stable surface
Magnetic field not uniform across wire lengthSystematicUse strong, flat-faced magnets; keep wire in centre of field
Wire not perpendicular to fieldSystematicAlign wire carefully; use a set square
Wire heats up at high currentsSystematicUse short bursts of current; allow cooling between readings
Exam Tip: The force on the wire is equal and opposite to the force on the magnet (Newton's third law). This is why the balance reading changes. By convention, F = BIL applies when the wire, field, and force are mutually perpendicular (use Fleming's left-hand rule).

⚗ RP11: AC Supply with Oscilloscope

Apparatus

  • Signal generator (AC supply)
  • Cathode ray oscilloscope (CRO) or digital oscilloscope
  • Connecting leads (BNC cables if needed)

Method

  1. Connect the signal generator output to the Y-input of the oscilloscope.
  2. Adjust the time-base and Y-gain (volts/div) to display a clear, stable waveform.
  3. Measure the peak voltage V0: count the number of divisions from the centre line to the peak and multiply by the Y-gain setting (volts/div).
  4. Measure the period T: count the number of divisions for one complete cycle and multiply by the time-base setting (time/div).
  5. Calculate frequency: f = 1/T.
  6. Calculate Vrms = V0 / √2.
  7. Vary the signal generator frequency and repeat measurements to verify.

Safety

  • Use low voltage signals (< 12 V)
  • Ensure oscilloscope is properly grounded
  • Do not connect oscilloscope to mains voltage directly

Analysis

From the oscilloscope trace:

V0 = (number of div from centre to peak) × (volts/div setting)

T = (number of div for one cycle) × (time/div setting)

f = 1/T     Vrms = V0/√2 ≈ 0.707 V0

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Difficulty reading exact peak positionRandomAdjust Y-gain so waveform fills most of the screen
Time-base calibration inaccuracySystematicCalibrate with a known frequency source
Trace thickness makes precise reading hardRandomUse a digital oscilloscope with cursor measurement
AC frequency drift from signal generatorRandomUse a stable, high-quality signal generator
Exam Tip: The Vrms is the DC equivalent voltage that would deliver the same power. Mains electricity in the UK is quoted as 230 V rms, meaning V0 = 230√2 ≈ 325 V. Always check which value (peak or rms) a question is asking for.

⚗ RP12: Inverse-Square Law for Gamma Radiation

Apparatus

  • Gamma source (e.g. cobalt-60 or caesium-137, sealed)
  • Geiger-Müller (GM) tube and counter/ratemeter
  • Metre ruler
  • Source holder and clamp
  • Lead absorbers (optional, for background shielding)

Method

  1. First, measure the background count rate (no source present). Count for at least 60 seconds and calculate counts per second.
  2. Place the gamma source at a measured distance d from the GM tube window.
  3. Record the count rate over a fixed time (e.g. 60 s). Subtract background count rate to get corrected count rate C.
  4. Repeat for at least 6 different distances (e.g. 5 cm to 50 cm).
  5. At each distance, take at least 3 readings and use the mean.
  6. Plot corrected count rate C vs 1/d².

Safety

  • Handle the source with long tongs — never with bare hands
  • Keep maximum distance from the source when not measuring
  • Point the source away from people
  • Minimise exposure time; store the source in a lead-lined container
  • Follow ALARA principle (As Low As Reasonably Achievable)

Analysis

Intensity follows the inverse-square law: I ∝ 1/d²

Plot corrected count rate C vs 1/d². Expect a straight line through the origin.

Alternatively, plot ln(C) vs ln(d). Gradient should be −2 (confirming inverse-square relationship).

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Background radiation fluctuatesRandomMeasure background multiple times; count for longer periods
Radioactive decay is randomRandomCount for longer periods (60+ seconds); take repeats
Distance measured to wrong part of GM tubeSystematicMeasure to the centre of the GM tube window
Source not a point sourceSystematicUse larger distances (d >> source size) where point-source approximation is better
Exam Tip: Always subtract the background count rate before plotting. The inverse-square law applies to point sources in a vacuum with no absorption. In practice, air absorption is negligible for gamma radiation over lab distances. The corrected count rate is proportional to intensity, not equal to it.
