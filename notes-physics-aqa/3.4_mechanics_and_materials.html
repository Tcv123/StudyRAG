3.4 Mechanics and Materials

1. Scalars and Vectors

Scalar: A quantity with magnitude only (e.g. mass, temperature, speed, energy, distance, time, power).
Vector: A quantity with both magnitude and direction (e.g. force, velocity, displacement, acceleration, momentum, weight).

Resolving Vectors

Any vector can be resolved into two perpendicular components:

  • Horizontal component: Fx = F cosθ
  • Vertical component: Fy = F sinθ

where θ is the angle between the vector and the horizontal.

Adding Vectors

Vectors are added tip-to-tail. The resultant is found by:

  • Scale drawing and measurement, or
  • Resolving into components: Rx = ΣFx, Ry = ΣFy, then R = √(Rx2 + Ry2), θ = tan-1(Ry/Rx)

2. Moments

Moment: The turning effect of a force about a pivot. Moment = F × d, where d is the perpendicular distance from the line of action of the force to the pivot. Units: N m.

Principle of Moments

For a body in rotational equilibrium, the sum of clockwise moments about any point equals the sum of anticlockwise moments about that same point.

Couples

A couple is a pair of equal and opposite forces whose lines of action do not coincide. A couple produces a pure turning effect (torque) with no resultant force.

Torque of a couple = F × d

where d is the perpendicular distance between the lines of action of the forces.

Equilibrium

For a body to be in static equilibrium:

  • The resultant force in any direction is zero (no linear acceleration).
  • The resultant moment about any point is zero (no rotational acceleration).

3. Kinematics (SUVAT)

Displacement (s): Vector distance in a specified direction.
Velocity (v): Rate of change of displacement.
Acceleration (a): Rate of change of velocity.

SUVAT Equations (constant acceleration only)

EquationMissing Variable
v = u + ats
s = ut + ½at2v
s = vt − ½at2u
v2 = u2 + 2ast
s = ½(u + v)ta

where s = displacement, u = initial velocity, v = final velocity, a = acceleration, t = time.

Free Fall

An object in free fall near the Earth's surface has acceleration a = g = 9.81 m s-2 (downward). Air resistance is neglected.

Projectile Motion

A projectile has two independent components of motion:

  • Horizontal: Constant velocity (no horizontal acceleration, ignoring air resistance). sx = vxt.
  • Vertical: Constant acceleration due to gravity (a = g = 9.81 m s-2 downward). Use SUVAT equations.
Exam Tip: For projectiles, always resolve the initial velocity into horizontal (u cosθ) and vertical (u sinθ) components, then treat each direction separately. Time is the link between the two.

4. Newton's Laws of Motion

Newton's First Law: An object remains at rest or continues to move at constant velocity unless acted on by a resultant external force.
Newton's Second Law: The rate of change of momentum of an object is directly proportional to the resultant force acting on it and takes place in the direction of that force. F = ma (for constant mass).
Newton's Third Law: If object A exerts a force on object B, then object B exerts an equal and opposite force on object A. The two forces act on different objects, are of the same type, and exist simultaneously.
Exam Tip: Newton's third law pairs must be: (1) equal in magnitude, (2) opposite in direction, (3) the same type of force, (4) acting on different objects. Weight and normal contact force are NOT a Newton's third law pair (both act on the same object).

5. Momentum and Impulse

p = mv

Momentum is a vector quantity. Units: kg m s-1 or N s.

Conservation of Momentum

Conservation of linear momentum: In a system where no external resultant force acts, the total momentum before an event equals the total momentum after the event.

Impulse

Impulse = FΔt = Δp = mv − mu

Impulse equals the change in momentum. On a force-time graph, impulse is the area under the curve.

6. Work, Energy, and Power

Work Done

W = Fd cosθ

where θ is the angle between the force and the displacement. Units: J (joules).

If the force is perpendicular to the displacement, no work is done (cos 90° = 0).

Kinetic Energy and Gravitational Potential Energy

Ek = ½mv2
ΔEp = mgΔh

Conservation of Energy

Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transferred between stores. In the absence of friction/air resistance, loss in GPE = gain in KE (and vice versa).

Power

P = W / t = ΔE / t
P = Fv

Units: W (watts) = J s-1.

Efficiency

Efficiency = useful output energy (or power) / total input energy (or power)

Often expressed as a percentage. Always less than 100% in real systems.

7. Density

ρ = m / V

Units: kg m-3. Density is a property of the material, not the object.

8. Hooke's Law and Springs

Hooke's Law: The extension of a spring is directly proportional to the applied force, provided the limit of proportionality is not exceeded.
F = kx
  • F = force (N)
  • k = spring constant (N m-1) — a measure of stiffness
  • x = extension (m)

Elastic Potential Energy

Ee = ½Fx = ½kx2

This is the area under the force-extension graph (which is a straight line up to the limit of proportionality).

Springs in Series and Parallel

ArrangementCombined Spring Constant
Series1/ktotal = 1/k1 + 1/k2 (softer overall)
Parallelktotal = k1 + k2 (stiffer overall)

Loading and Unloading

  • Below the elastic limit: the material returns to its original length when the force is removed. No permanent deformation.
  • Beyond the elastic limit: permanent (plastic) deformation occurs. The material does not fully return to its original length.
  • For a rubber band, the loading and unloading curves do not follow the same path (hysteresis). The area between the curves represents energy dissipated as heat.

9. Stress, Strain, and Young's Modulus

Tensile stress (σ): Force per unit cross-sectional area. σ = F / A. Units: Pa (N m-2).
Tensile strain (ε): Extension per unit original length. ε = ΔL / L. No units (dimensionless).
Young's modulus (E): The ratio of stress to strain within the proportional region. E = σ / ε = (FL) / (AΔL). Units: Pa.

Stress-Strain Graphs

FeatureDescription
Limit of proportionalityBeyond this point, stress is no longer proportional to strain (Hooke's law no longer applies)
Elastic limitBeyond this point, permanent deformation occurs on unloading
Yield pointA small increase in stress causes a large increase in strain (material begins to flow)
Ultimate tensile stress (UTS)The maximum stress a material can withstand before breaking
Fracture pointThe stress at which the material breaks

Material Behaviour

Material TypeCharacteristicsExample
DuctileLarge plastic deformation before fracture. Can be drawn into wires. Shows a clear yield point.Copper, mild steel
BrittleLittle or no plastic deformation. Fractures suddenly without warning. Stress-strain graph is steep and straight until it snaps.Glass, ceramics, cast iron
PolymericVery large strains possible. Behaviour depends on type (e.g. rubber shows large elastic strain).Rubber, polythene
Exam Tip: The area under a stress-strain graph represents energy stored per unit volume. For a force-extension graph, the area represents energy stored (or work done). Know the difference between the two graph types.
Exam Tip: Young's modulus is found from the gradient of the linear (proportional) region of a stress-strain graph, NOT a force-extension graph. E = stress / strain = gradient of the linear region.

⚗ Required Practical 3: Determination of g by Free-Fall

Apparatus

  • Electromagnet connected to a low-voltage supply
  • Steel ball bearing
  • Trapdoor switch (or light gates as an alternative)
  • Electronic timer (millisecond precision)
  • Metre ruler or tape measure
  • Retort stand and clamp
  • Cushion or sand tray (to catch the ball)

Method

  1. Set up the electromagnet at a measured height h above the trapdoor. The timer is connected so it starts when the electromagnet is switched off (releasing the ball) and stops when the ball hits the trapdoor.
  2. Switch off the electromagnet to release the ball. Record the time t on the electronic timer.
  3. Repeat the drop 3–5 times at this height and calculate a mean value of t.
  4. Change the height h (e.g. in steps of 0.1 m from 0.2 m to 1.0 m). Repeat the timing at each height.
  5. Measure h from the bottom of the ball bearing to the top of the trapdoor using the metre ruler.
  6. Plot a graph of h (y-axis) against t² (x-axis). The gradient = g/2, so g = 2 × gradient.

Safety

  • Place a cushion or sand tray beneath the trapdoor to catch the falling ball and prevent it bouncing away.
  • Keep feet clear of the falling ball.
  • Ensure the retort stand is stable (use a heavy base or clamp to the bench).
  • Use a low-voltage supply for the electromagnet.

Analysis

Using s = ut + ½at² with u = 0 (released from rest):

h = ½gt²

Therefore h = (g/2)t². A graph of h vs t² is a straight line through the origin with gradient = g/2.

Alternatively, plot 2h vs t² to get gradient = g directly.

The accepted value of g = 9.81 m s-2. Calculate percentage difference to assess accuracy.

Sources of Error & Improvements

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Residual magnetism — ball may not release instantly when current is switched offSystematic (makes t too long, g too low)Use a non-magnetic ball with a mechanical release mechanism; or use light gates instead
Reaction time in measuring h (ruler parallax)RandomUse a set square against the ruler; take repeated measurements
Air resistance on the ballSystematic (makes g appear lower)Use a small, dense ball to minimise air resistance; keep h relatively short
Timing uncertainty (especially at short heights where t is small)RandomUse larger heights to increase t; repeat measurements and average
Height measurement from ball centre to trapdoor surfaceSystematicMeasure consistently from the bottom of the ball to the top of the trapdoor
Exam Tip: The electromagnet introduces a systematic error because of residual magnetism. This delays the release, making measured times too long and giving a g value lower than 9.81 m s-2. Light gates eliminate this because timing starts only when the ball breaks the first beam.

⚗ Required Practical 4: Determination of Young Modulus

Apparatus

  • Long, thin copper or steel wire (at least 2 m)
  • Reference wire of the same material (hung alongside the test wire)
  • Vernier scale or travelling microscope (to measure extension)
  • Micrometer screw gauge (to measure wire diameter)
  • Metre ruler or tape measure (to measure original length L)
  • Slotted masses and hanger
  • Pulley, G-clamp, and wooden blocks
  • Safety goggles

Method

  1. Clamp both wires to the same rigid support (ceiling beam or top of a tall frame). The reference wire carries a small fixed mass to keep it taut. The test wire passes over a pulley with a mass hanger.
  2. Measure the original length L of the test wire from the clamp to the vernier scale marker using a tape measure.
  3. Measure the diameter d of the wire using a micrometer screw gauge at several points along the wire and at different orientations. Calculate a mean diameter and then the cross-sectional area A = π(d/2)².
  4. Record the initial reading on the vernier scale (with just the hanger, no added masses).
  5. Add masses in equal increments (e.g. 100 g). After each addition, wait for the wire to stop oscillating, then read the new vernier scale position. The extension e = new reading − initial reading.
  6. Continue adding masses up to a safe maximum (do not exceed the elastic limit).
  7. Unload the masses in reverse order and record the vernier readings again (to check for permanent deformation).
  8. Plot a graph of stress (σ = F/A) vs strain (ε = e/L). The gradient of the linear region = Young modulus E.

Safety

  • Wear safety goggles — the wire is under high tension and may snap.
  • Place a cushion or box beneath the masses to catch them if the wire breaks.
  • Do not exceed the elastic limit of the wire (stop adding masses if the wire does not return to its original length on unloading).
  • Stand to the side of the wire, not directly beneath it.
  • Ensure the bench clamp and support are secure.

Analysis

Stress σ = F/A = mg / π(d/2)². Strain ε = e/L.

E = stress / strain = σ / ε = FL / Ae

Plot stress (y-axis) vs strain (x-axis). The gradient of the straight-line section = Young modulus E. Typical values: copper ~ 130 GPa, steel ~ 200 GPa.

Alternatively, plot force F vs extension e. Then E = gradient × L / A.

Sources of Error & Improvements

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Wire diameter varies along its lengthRandomMeasure diameter at several positions and orientations; take a mean
Parallax error reading the vernier scaleRandomUse a travelling microscope for greater precision
Wire may not be initially straight (kinks)SystematicPre-load the wire with a small mass before recording initial length
Thermal expansion if temperature changesSystematicUse a reference wire to compensate for temperature effects
Extension is very small (fractions of a mm)RandomUse a long wire (2+ m) to maximise extension for a given stress; use the vernier scale
Wire may go past elastic limit without noticingSystematicUnload after each measurement to check the wire returns to original length
Exam Tip: The reference wire compensates for thermal expansion and building vibrations. If both wires expand equally due to temperature change, the vernier reading is unaffected. The biggest source of uncertainty is usually the diameter measurement — since area depends on d², a small error in d causes a large error in A and hence in E.
