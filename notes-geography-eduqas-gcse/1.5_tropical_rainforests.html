1.5 Tropical Rainforests

1. Location and Climate

Tropical rainforests are found between the Tropic of Cancer (23.5°N) and Tropic of Capricorn (23.5°S), with the densest concentration within 5° either side of the equator. Their location is driven by the Inter-Tropical Convergence Zone (ITCZ) — a belt of low pressure where trade winds meet, causing warm, moist air to rise, cool, and produce intense rainfall.

3. Nutrient Cycling

The tropical rainforest has a rapid nutrient cycle, driven by the hot, wet climate. Despite the lush vegetation, the soils are actually thin and nutrient-poor (latosols/oxisols) — almost all nutrients are locked up in living biomass (plants and animals).

How the Nutrient Cycle Works

Dead leaves and plant matter (litter) fall to the forest floor. The warm, moist conditions mean decomposers (fungi and bacteria) break down this litter extremely quickly — sometimes within weeks. Nutrients are released into the thin topsoil and immediately taken up by the shallow root systems of trees. The cycle is therefore almost closed — nutrients flow rapidly from litter to decomposers to soil to plants and back again.

Additional Nuance: The Role of Mycorrhizal Networks

A critical but often overlooked component of the nutrient cycle is the mycorrhizal fungal network — a web of fungal threads (hyphae) that connects the roots of different trees underground. These fungi form mutually beneficial (mutualistic) relationships with tree roots: the tree provides the fungus with sugars produced by photosynthesis, and in return the fungus dramatically extends the tree's ability to absorb phosphorus, nitrogen, and water from the soil. This means nutrients can be transferred between trees through the fungal network, making the cycle even more efficient and resilient. In old-growth rainforest, up to 90% of nutrient uptake may be mediated by mycorrhizal fungi.

Why Leaching is Such a Threat

The intense rainfall (2,000+ mm/year) constantly percolates through the soil, dissolving and washing nutrients downward in a process called leaching. This is why the nutrient cycle must be so fast — any nutrients that are not taken up almost immediately by roots or fungi are lost to deeper layers and the water table. In intact forest, the canopy intercepts much of the rainfall and roots recapture leached nutrients before they are permanently lost. When the forest is cleared, this safety net disappears entirely.

Buttress Roots: Large, plank-like roots that extend from the base of tall trees. They spread the enormous weight of the tree over a wider area (since soils are shallow and unable to support deep taproots) and also allow the tree to access nutrients near the surface, where decomposition deposits them.
Laterisation: The process by which, following deforestation, the hot sun bakes the exposed, nutrient-depleted soil into a hard, iron-rich crust (laterite). This crust is almost impermeable to water and impossible to cultivate. It can form within a few years of forest clearance and is essentially irreversible on human timescales.
Exam Tip: A key misunderstanding is that rainforest soils are fertile. They are NOT — they are thin and poor. If the forest is cleared, heavy rain leaches away remaining nutrients quickly (laterisation), leaving hard, brick-like soil useless for farming. This is why slash-and-burn farmers must move on after only 2–3 years — the soils are exhausted almost immediately.
Exam Tip — Nutrient Cycle Comparisons: If asked to compare the rainforest nutrient cycle to a temperate deciduous forest, the key differences are: (1) speed — decomposition is far faster in the tropics due to heat and moisture; (2) storage — almost all nutrients are in biomass in the rainforest, whereas temperate soils hold more nutrients; (3) leaching risk — far greater in the rainforest due to higher rainfall intensity.

4. The Water Cycle in the Rainforest

The tropical rainforest plays an extraordinary role in the local and global water cycle. The forest does not simply receive rainfall — it actively generates its own rainfall through evapotranspiration, creating a self-sustaining feedback loop between vegetation and climate.

Key Processes

Interception: The process by which rainfall is caught by the leaves, branches, and bark of vegetation before it reaches the ground. In a tropical rainforest, the dense multi-layer canopy can intercept 20–40% of all precipitation. This water either evaporates back into the atmosphere directly (interception loss) or drips slowly to the ground as throughfall, greatly reducing the erosive impact of heavy rain on the soil surface.
Evapotranspiration (EVT): The combined process of evaporation (water evaporating from soil and leaf surfaces) and transpiration (water released through the stomata of leaves as a by-product of photosynthesis). In the Amazon, trees release approximately 20 billion tonnes of water vapour into the atmosphere every single day — more than the Amazon River discharges into the Atlantic Ocean. This moisture rises, cools, condenses, and falls again as rainfall — a process sometimes called the "flying rivers" or "forest-rainfall feedback."

The Forest-Rainfall Feedback Loop

One of the most important — and alarming — aspects of the rainforest water cycle is the forest-rainfall feedback. The Amazon generates roughly half of its own rainfall internally through evapotranspiration. Moisture evaporated from the western Amazon is carried by wind, condenses, falls as rain further east, evaporates again, and is carried still further — creating a "flying river" of moisture that travels thousands of kilometres inland. This means:

  • The rainforest is not simply responding to climate — it is creating the climate it needs to survive.
  • Deforestation at a regional scale interrupts this cycle. Less forest means less evapotranspiration, meaning less moisture in the atmosphere, meaning less rainfall — meaning even more forest dies in a positive feedback (vicious cycle).
  • Research by Antonio Nobre (INPA) suggests that the Amazon generates "flying rivers" carrying up to 20 billion tonnes of water per day westward — more than the Amazon River itself. Cities like São Paulo receive much of their water from this invisible aerial river.

Interception in Detail

The dense multi-layered canopy acts as a giant sponge, cushioning the impact of intense tropical rainfall. Key effects include:

  • Reduced soil erosion: Rain drops are slowed by leaves and branches before reaching the forest floor, reducing their kinetic energy and the ability to dislodge soil particles.
  • Regulated river flow: Water reaches rivers more slowly (as throughfall and stemflow rather than direct surface runoff), reducing flood risk downstream.
  • Moisture recycling: Intercepted water that evaporates directly from leaf surfaces (without entering the soil) feeds local cloud formation and convectional rainfall — keeping the water cycle local and continuous.
Exam Tip — Water Cycle Links to Deforestation: When asked about the effects of deforestation, always include the impact on the water cycle. Without tree cover: (1) interception is lost so rainfall hits bare soil directly, causing erosion and surface runoff; (2) evapotranspiration falls, reducing moisture in the atmosphere and regional rainfall; (3) rivers flood more frequently and more severely. This also links directly to soil degradation and the nutrient cycle.

5. Biodiversity

The tropical rainforest is the most biodiverse biome on Earth, home to over 50% of the world's species while covering only about 6% of the land surface.

Why is Biodiversity so High?

  • Stable climate: Constant warmth and rainfall all year round allows year-round growth and reproduction — no harsh winter or dry season to kill off species.
  • Multiple niches: The layered structure creates dozens of distinct microhabitats — different species can specialise without competing directly.
  • Long evolutionary time: Rainforests have existed for ~60–100 million years without major ice-age disruption, allowing slow accumulation of species through evolution and speciation.
  • High productivity: Intense photosynthesis produces vast amounts of food energy supporting complex food webs.

Interdependence

Species in the rainforest are highly interdependent — the loss of one species can trigger cascading effects throughout the ecosystem. This is sometimes called an ecological cascade or trophic cascade. Key examples:

  • Brazil nut tree (Bertholletia excelsa) — a three-species dependency: The Brazil nut tree can only be pollinated by the large-bodied orchid bee (genus Eulaema), because only this bee is strong enough to lift the heavy petal lid of the flower. The orchid bee itself depends on specific orchid species for a chemical it uses in mating displays — meaning no orchids equals no bees equals no Brazil nuts. Once pollinated, the hard woody seed pod can only be opened by the agouti, a large rodent with teeth strong enough to gnaw through the shell. The agouti buries seeds it does not immediately eat — and many of these are never retrieved, becoming new trees. Remove any one of these three species and the whole chain collapses.
  • Fig trees and fig wasps: Every species of fig tree (over 750 species globally) is pollinated by one or a very small number of dedicated wasp species. The wasp lays eggs inside the fig, the young wasps disperse pollen as they leave. The relationship is so tight that extinction of the wasp means extinction of the fig, and with it the loss of a key food source for dozens of bird and mammal species that depend on fig fruits.
  • Jaguars as apex predators (trophic cascade): The jaguar controls populations of herbivores including peccaries (wild pigs), capybaras, and deer. Where jaguars have been removed, herbivore populations explode, leading to overgrazing of understorey vegetation and a collapse in plant diversity. This then affects insects, birds, and other species that depend on those plants — demonstrating how removing a single apex predator can restructure an entire ecosystem.
  • Epiphytes and pond-in-the-canopy ecosystems: Bromeliads (relatives of the pineapple) collect rainwater in their leaf rosettes, forming miniature ponds up to 3 litres in volume high in the canopy. These tiny pools support entire independent ecosystems — specialised mosquito larvae, aquatic beetles, poison dart frog tadpoles, and bacteria found nowhere else. A single large bromeliad may support over 250 animal species. This illustrates how one plant species can create an entire new habitat layer.
  • Dung beetles and forest regeneration: Dung beetles bury animal droppings in the soil, dispersing seeds and aerating the soil. Research in the Amazon showed that when large mammals (which produce dung) are removed by hunting, dung beetle populations collapse, seed dispersal is drastically reduced, and forest regeneration slows significantly. This is an invisible form of biodiversity collapse.
  • Strangler figs and forest succession: Strangler figs germinate high in the canopy as epiphytes, then slowly send roots down to the ground, eventually engulfing and killing the host tree. The dead host leaves a hollow space that becomes critical shelter for bats, insects, and small mammals — turning a process of apparent destruction into habitat creation.
Exam Tip — Interdependence in Extended Answers: For 6–8 mark questions on biodiversity, do not just list species. Pick one or two specific interdependence examples (e.g. the Brazil nut tree chain, or the jaguar trophic cascade) and explain the mechanism clearly: what depends on what, and what happens if the chain is broken. This demonstrates the depth of understanding that separates high from low band answers.

8. Deforestation

Deforestation: The permanent removal of trees from a forested area, converting land to non-forest use. The global rate is approximately 10 million hectares per year (equivalent to losing a football pitch every second).

Consequences of Deforestation — in Detail

1. Biodiversity Loss
Habitat destruction is the single greatest driver of species extinction globally. The IUCN estimates that around 137 species are lost every day in the Amazon alone — approximately one species every ten minutes. Because of the high interdependence of rainforest species, the extinction of one species can trigger a cascade of secondary extinctions. An estimated 40% of Amazonian tree species are now threatened with extinction as a result of deforestation and climate change combined (2015 Science study). Many species are lost before they are even discovered or described by science.

2. Soil Erosion and Degradation
Without root systems binding the soil and leaf litter cushioning the impact of rain, intense tropical downpours dislodge and wash away topsoil at rates up to 100 times faster than in intact forest. The exposed laterite soil rapidly loses structure and fertility. Within 2–3 agricultural cycles, the land becomes essentially unusable for farming — yet it may take centuries for natural forest to regrow on degraded land. The United Nations Environment Programme estimates that approximately 2 billion hectares of formerly forested land worldwide are now degraded.

3. Disruption of the Water Cycle
Trees recycle 50–80% of rainfall through evapotranspiration. When a large area is deforested, regional rainfall can decline significantly. A 2021 study found that deforested areas of the Amazon receive up to 25% less rainfall than adjacent forested areas. This creates a positive feedback: less forest = less rain = more drought stress on remaining trees = more forest die-off. Cities far from the Amazon, including São Paulo (population 22 million), receive significant proportions of their rainfall from Amazonian "flying rivers" — meaning deforestation in Brazil creates water shortages in cities hundreds of kilometres away.

4. Climate Change — Carbon Release
Tropical forests store approximately 250 billion tonnes of carbon in their biomass and soils — equivalent to about 25 years of current global CO₂ emissions from fossil fuels. When forest is burned or cleared, this carbon is released as CO₂ and methane. Deforestation and land-use change currently account for approximately 10–15% of annual global greenhouse gas emissions, making it the third largest source of emissions after energy and agriculture. Crucially, a degraded Amazon could transition from a carbon sink (absorbing CO₂) to a carbon source (emitting CO₂) — research published in 2021 in Nature showed that the south-eastern Amazon is already emitting more carbon than it absorbs, due to a combination of deforestation and climate-driven drought.

5. Displacement and Rights of Indigenous People
Approximately 300 uncontacted or recently contacted indigenous tribes live in the Amazon, totalling over 1 million people. Deforestation destroys not only their physical environment but their culture, traditional knowledge (including plant-based medicines), and survival systems. The Yanomami crisis of 2022–23, when 20,000 illegal gold miners (garimpeiros) invaded Yanomami territory, resulted in widespread malaria, mercury poisoning, malnutrition, and a dramatic rise in child mortality. The Brazilian health authority declared a public health emergency in January 2023.

6. Loss of Medicinal Potential
An estimated 25% of all modern pharmaceutical drugs are derived from or inspired by rainforest plants. The muscle relaxant curare (used in anaesthesia) comes from Amazonian plants; quinine (malaria treatment) from Andean cinchona bark; and vincristine (childhood leukaemia treatment) from the rosy periwinkle of Madagascar. Given that less than 1% of known rainforest plant species have been scientifically tested for medicinal properties, the pharmaceutical potential that is being permanently destroyed is incalculable.

Exam Tip — Using Statistics Effectively: In 6–8 mark questions, examiners reward specific factual support. Always try to include at least one statistic per consequence you describe. Do not invent statistics — use the ones you have memorised (137 species/day; 25% less rainfall in deforested areas; 250 billion tonnes carbon; 25% of medicines from rainforest plants). Quoting a study or named scientist (e.g. "research published in Nature, 2021") adds further credibility.

9. Case Studies

Case Study A: The Amazon Rainforest, Brazil

Location: South America, spanning 9 countries. Approximately 60% lies within Brazil. The Amazon River drains the basin — the world's largest river by discharge volume.

Size: ~5.5 million km² of tropical rainforest — roughly the size of Western Europe.

Biodiversity: Home to an estimated 10% of all Earth's species: 40,000 plant species, 1,300 bird species, 3,000 fish species, 430 mammal species.

Rate of loss: Approximately 17% of the forest has been cleared since the 1970s. Peak deforestation was in 2004 (~27,000 km²/year). After falling following policy intervention (Forest Code), it rose again after 2018 — under Bolsonaro's government (2019–2022), deforestation reached ~11,000 km²/year. Under Lula (from 2023), deforestation dropped significantly again — by approximately 50% in 2023 compared to the Bolsonaro peak.

Main causes in the Amazon: Cattle ranching (~65%), soya cultivation (~25%), logging, mining (particularly illegal gold mining in indigenous territories), road building.

Tipping Point: Scientists (particularly Carlos Nobre and Thomas Lovejoy) warn that if 20–25% of the Amazon is deforested, the forest may reach a 'tipping point' — reduced evapotranspiration causes regional drying that prevents forest regrowth. As of 2024, approximately 17–20% has been cleared and a further ~17% is degraded. Seasonal drought events driven by climate change have worsened — in 2023, an extreme drought reduced water levels on the Amazon River to historic lows, stranding river communities and killing thousands of river dolphins.

Indigenous impacts: The Yanomami people (35,000 individuals) suffered a major humanitarian crisis in 2022–23 when 20,000 illegal gold miners (garimpeiros) invaded their territory, spreading malaria and mercury poisoning. Child mortality among Yanomami children under 5 was four times the Brazilian national average in 2022.

Significance: The Amazon stores approximately 150–200 billion tonnes of carbon. Its loss would accelerate global climate change significantly and is considered by scientists to be one of the most critical planetary tipping points.

Case Study B: Borneo (SE Asia) — Deforestation for Palm Oil

Location: Borneo is the world's third largest island, divided between Indonesia (Kalimantan), Malaysia (Sabah and Sarawak), and Brunei. It lies between 4°N and 4°S of the equator, in the heart of the Indo-Malayan rainforest region.

Size of original forest: Borneo once had approximately 740,000 km² of rainforest. It is home to some of the oldest rainforest on Earth — estimated at 130 million years old, significantly older than the Amazon.

Rate of loss: Between 1973 and 2015, Borneo lost approximately 30% of its forest cover — roughly 100,000 km² (the equivalent of England and Wales combined). Between 2000 and 2012 alone, the island lost 8.6 million hectares of forest — one of the fastest deforestation rates on Earth at the time. The primary driver has been palm oil plantations, which now cover over 6 million hectares in Malaysian and Indonesian Borneo combined.

Main causes:

  • Palm oil: Used in ~50% of all supermarket products globally (biscuits, shampoo, margarine, cosmetics). Palm oil is the highest-yielding vegetable oil and the cheapest to produce, making it extremely profitable to grow. Vast areas of peatland rainforest have been drained and burned to make way for plantations.
  • Commercial logging: Tropical hardwoods (meranti, teak) exported primarily to Japan, China, and Europe. Both legal and illegal logging have been major drivers since the 1960s.
  • Mining: Coal and bauxite mining in Kalimantan (Indonesian Borneo).
  • Transmigration: The Indonesian government's policy of relocating people from densely populated Java to Borneo, placing pressure on land from subsistence farming.

Ecological consequences:

  • Orangutan: The Bornean orangutan (Pongo pygmaeus) lost over 100,000 individuals between 1999 and 2015 — a 25% decline — primarily due to habitat loss and hunting. It is now classified as Critically Endangered (IUCN). Orangutans need large contiguous forest territories and cannot survive in plantation monocultures.
  • Pygmy elephant and Sumatran rhino: Both critically endangered, with small fragmented populations increasingly unable to maintain viable breeding populations.
  • Peat fires: Borneo's peatlands (carbon-rich waterlogged soils up to 20m deep) have been drained for plantations. When drained peat dries out, it becomes highly flammable. The 1997–98 El Niño-driven fires in Indonesia burned approximately 9.7 million hectares of peat, releasing an estimated 2.57 billion tonnes of CO₂ in a single event — equivalent to 40% of global fossil fuel emissions for that year. Similar large-scale fires occurred in 2015 and 2019.
  • River pollution: Palm oil mill effluent (POME) has severely polluted rivers in Sabah and Kalimantan, with dissolved oxygen levels in some rivers falling to near zero, wiping out freshwater fish populations.

Responses: The Roundtable on Sustainable Palm Oil (RSPO) was established in 2004 to certify sustainably produced palm oil. By 2023, approximately 19% of global palm oil was RSPO-certified. Critics argue the RSPO's standards are insufficiently strong and enforcement is weak. Some countries (Norway, UK) have committed to "deforestation-free" supply chains. Consumer pressure has led some companies (Unilever, Nestle) to commit to sourcing only certified palm oil — but compliance is difficult to verify across complex global supply chains.

Comparison with Amazon: Borneo's deforestation is driven more by a single global commodity (palm oil) than the Amazon's multiple drivers (cattle, soya, logging, mining). This potentially makes it easier to address through supply chain certification — but the global scale of palm oil use makes systemic change extremely challenging. Borneo's forests are also unique — they share very few species with the Amazon and are irreplaceable even if the Amazon were fully protected.

Exam Tip — Using Two Case Studies: If asked to "use a case study to support your answer," one well-chosen example with specific facts scores higher than a vague general statement. If the question says "with reference to examples," you can briefly compare both case studies — e.g. "In the Amazon, the main driver is cattle ranching (~65% of clearance), whereas in Borneo, palm oil for global consumer goods is the primary driver, affecting over 6 million hectares." Contrast shows geographical understanding of scale and variation.

10. Climate Regulation — Global Importance of Rainforests

Rainforests are not merely local or regional ecosystems — they perform critical services for the entire planet's climate system. Their global importance extends far beyond the countries in which they are physically located.

Carbon Storage and the Carbon Sink Function

Tropical rainforests together store approximately 250 billion tonnes of carbon — roughly equivalent to 25 years of current global CO₂ emissions from all fossil fuel burning combined. This carbon is held in:

  • Living biomass (above ground: trunks, branches, leaves; below ground: roots)
  • Dead wood (fallen logs, bark, litter layer)
  • Soil organic matter (particularly in peat-rich soils like those of Borneo)

When trees photosynthesise, they absorb CO₂ from the atmosphere and lock it into woody tissue — acting as a carbon sink. Intact tropical forests currently absorb a net 1.1–1.4 billion tonnes of carbon per year, significantly offsetting global emissions. However, this sink is weakening: a 2021 study in Nature found that heavily deforested and degraded parts of the Amazon (particularly in the south-east) have flipped to become net carbon sources, emitting more than they absorb. If this transition continues across the whole Amazon system, it would dramatically accelerate global warming.

Albedo and Surface Temperature Regulation

Rainforest canopies are relatively dark and absorb more solar radiation than bare or agricultural land. Counterintuitively, forest also causes significant evaporative cooling — the enormous volumes of water transpired by leaves absorb latent heat, cooling the local air temperature. Studies comparing deforested and forested areas in the tropics show that deforested land is on average 1–3°C warmer than equivalent forested land. At a regional scale, large-scale deforestation warms regional temperatures, worsening drought conditions and potentially affecting atmospheric circulation patterns.

Influence on Global Weather Patterns

The Amazon acts as a major driver of atmospheric circulation. The enormous amounts of water vapour and heat released by evapotranspiration drive convection currents that influence the position and strength of the jet stream and inter-tropical weather systems. Climate modelling studies suggest that complete loss of the Amazon would significantly alter rainfall patterns as far away as the Midwestern United States and parts of Europe — demonstrating that the rainforest's climate services are genuinely global in reach, not merely regional.

Biodiversity as a Climate Stabiliser

High biodiversity itself contributes to ecosystem resilience and stability. A more diverse forest is better able to withstand and recover from disturbances such as drought, disease, or insect outbreaks. As climate change intensifies, maintaining high biodiversity in the rainforest may be critical to its ability to continue performing its climate regulation functions — a further reason why biodiversity loss and climate change are interlinked crises, not separate problems.

Carbon Sink: A natural system that absorbs more carbon (as CO₂) from the atmosphere than it releases. Intact tropical rainforests are major carbon sinks. When deforested, they become carbon sources — releasing stored carbon as CO₂.
Ecosystem Services: The benefits that healthy ecosystems provide to people, free of charge. For rainforests, key services include: carbon storage (climate regulation), water cycle regulation, biodiversity (gene pool for medicine and agriculture), local rainfall generation, and soil protection. These services are estimated to be worth trillions of dollars globally if costed — yet they are provided by nature without payment.
Exam Tip — Why Should Wealthy Countries Care? A common exam question asks why people in the UK should care about deforestation in Brazil or Indonesia. Key arguments: (1) CO₂ released anywhere affects global climate equally; (2) UK consumers drive demand for beef, soya, and palm oil that cause deforestation; (3) Rainforest biodiversity may hold future medical cures; (4) atmospheric circulation means Amazonian deforestation may affect European weather. This demonstrates understanding of globalisation, interdependence, and global responsibility.

Deeper Evaluation of Key Strategies

Indigenous Land Rights — the strongest evidence: Research comparing deforestation rates across different land tenure types in the Amazon consistently shows that indigenous territories have dramatically lower deforestation rates than equivalent non-protected forest or even formally designated national parks. A 2019 study in PNAS found that indigenous lands in Brazil had deforestation rates on average 11 times lower than non-protected areas. Indigenous communities have the greatest incentive to protect their territories and have accumulated millennia of ecological knowledge about sustainable forest use. Yet these rights are frequently contested by governments and extractive industries — and under Bolsonaro, demarcation of new indigenous territories was halted entirely, directly contributing to increased deforestation.

REDD+ — promising but flawed: REDD+ (Reducing Emissions from Deforestation and Forest Degradation) is the most ambitious international mechanism, theoretically capable of channelling hundreds of billions of dollars from wealthy countries into forest protection. However, evaluation reveals significant weaknesses: (1) additionality — is the forest only protected because of REDD+ money, or would it have survived anyway? (2) permanence — carbon offsets from protected forest can be undermined if the forest is later burned; (3) leakage — protecting forest in one area may simply displace deforestation elsewhere; (4) a 2023 investigation found that over 90% of rainforest carbon credits issued by one major certifier (Verra) were "phantom credits" that did not represent real emissions reductions. This does not invalidate REDD+ entirely, but shows that robust monitoring and governance are essential.

Ecotourism — effective but limited scale: Costa Rica remains the most cited success story: forest cover recovered from 21% (1987) to over 52% (2023) and ecotourism contributes over $3 billion/year to GDP. However, Costa Rica is a small, stable, middle-income country with good governance. Attempting to replicate this in the DRC (Congo Basin) — a larger, lower-income country with fragile governance and ongoing conflict — is far more challenging. Ecotourism also inherently requires visitor access, which limits its applicability to the most remote and ecologically valuable areas of the Amazon or Congo.

Consumer action and supply chains: Since approximately 60–70% of Amazon deforestation is driven by beef and soya production for global markets, consumer and corporate behaviour in importing countries can be powerful. The UK's Environment Act 2021 contains provisions requiring large companies to ensure their supply chains are free from illegal deforestation — one of the first such laws globally. The EU Deforestation Regulation (EUDR, effective 2024–25) goes further, requiring companies to prove their products (beef, soya, palm oil, coffee, cocoa, rubber, timber) did not cause deforestation before they can be sold in the EU. These laws, if well-enforced, could significantly reduce the economic incentive for deforestation by cutting off market access for destructive producers.

Case Study: Ecotourism in Costa Rica
Costa Rica has transformed from one of the world's highest deforestation rates in the 1970s to a global leader in conservation. Today, 26% of its land area is protected. Ecotourism brings in over $3 billion per year and employs 13% of the workforce. The government's Payment for Ecosystem Services (PES) scheme pays landowners to keep forest standing. Forest cover has recovered from 21% (1987) to over 52% (2023). This demonstrates that economic incentives aligned with conservation can be more effective than protection alone.

12. Exam-Level Evaluation

Exam Tip — Evaluating Management Strategies: Questions asking you to evaluate management strategies require you to go beyond description. Structure your answer using:
  • What the strategy does (brief)
  • Why it is effective (with evidence/example)
  • Why it might be limited or ineffective (e.g. cost, enforcement, scale, political will)
  • Overall judgement — which approaches are most/least effective and WHY
Generally, strategies that create economic value for local communities (ecotourism, indigenous rights, PES) tend to be more sustainable than top-down protection alone, because communities have an active incentive to protect the forest.
Key Argument for 8+ Mark Questions: The most effective management combines multiple approaches — local (indigenous rights + ecotourism), national (legal frameworks + enforcement), and international (REDD+, debt swaps, CITES, supply chain laws). No single strategy is sufficient. The underlying driver — the global demand for beef, soya, and timber — also needs to be addressed through consumer choices and trade agreements.

Development and globalisation (Component 2) explains why countries like Brazil and Indonesia prioritise economic exploitation over conservation. Natural hazards (Component 1) — peat fires in Borneo are intensified by El Niño droughts. Pulling in these cross-topic links in 6–8 mark answers demonstrates the highest level of geographical thinking.
