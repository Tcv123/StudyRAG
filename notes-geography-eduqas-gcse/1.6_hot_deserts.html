1.6 Hot Deserts

1. Location and Climate

Hot deserts are found predominantly at approximately 30° North and 30° South of the equator, in belts corresponding to the subtropical high pressure zones. They also occur in rain shadows (sheltered side of mountain ranges) and deep continental interiors far from moisture sources.

Desert Definition: An area receiving less than 250 mm of rainfall per year. Hot deserts experience extreme heat by day and cold at night due to the absence of cloud cover and low humidity.

2. Causes of Desert Climate

The location of hot deserts at ~30°N/S is explained by the Hadley Cell — a large atmospheric circulation pattern driven by the sun heating the equator.

Other Causes of Desert Climate

CauseMechanismExample
Subtropical high pressureHadley cell — dry air descends at 30°N/S, warms adiabatically, inhibits cloud formation and rainfallSahara, Arabian, Thar deserts
Rain shadow effectMoist air rises over mountains, deposits rain on windward side; descends dry and warm on leeward sideAtacama Desert (behind the Andes); Patagonian Desert (behind Andes)
Distance from sea (continentality)Air masses lose moisture as they travel inland; no maritime moisture sourceGobi Desert (Central Asia)
Cold ocean currentsCold water chills overlying air, preventing it from rising and forming rain clouds; stable cold air = no convectionAtacama — cooled by the Humboldt Current; Namib — Benguela Current

3. Desert Weathering Processes

Weathering breaks rock down in situ (without moving it). In deserts, mechanical (physical) weathering dominates because of the extreme temperature ranges and the absence of vegetation roots. Chemical weathering also occurs, despite the apparent lack of water, whenever thin moisture films are present.

Mechanical (Physical) Weathering

Mechanical weathering: The physical breakdown of rock into smaller fragments without any change to the rock's chemical composition. The rock splits or flakes but remains chemically identical to the parent rock.

Chemical Weathering

Although deserts are dry, chemical weathering is not absent. Dew, fog, and rare rain provide enough moisture to drive chemical reactions, particularly at night or in coastal deserts where fog is frequent (e.g. the Atacama receives significant fog moisture from the Pacific — called "camanchaca").

Exam Tip — Weathering: Many students confuse weathering and erosion. Weathering = breakdown in situ (no movement). Erosion = wearing away AND transporting material. A question asking "explain how a desert landform is created" usually requires you to describe BOTH the weathering that weakens/breaks down rock AND the erosion/transportation that removes material to create the landform's shape.

4. Desert Landscapes and Landforms

Despite the popular image of endless sand dunes, only about 25% of the Sahara is erg (sand desert). Most desert landscapes are dominated by bare rock, gravel plains, and distinctive erosional landforms shaped by wind (aeolian processes) and rare but intense water action.

Exam Tip — Landform Questions: For any landform, structure your answer around: (1) the type of rock and its resistance, (2) the process that erodes/deposits material, (3) the sequence of events that creates the shape. Landforms like mesas/buttes are excellent for showing understanding of how differential erosion (hard rock protects soft rock) creates distinctive shapes. Inselbergs and pediments often appear in 6-mark questions asking you to describe and explain formation.

5. Water Action in Deserts

Water is paradoxically one of the most important agents shaping desert landscapes, despite deserts being defined by lack of rainfall. When rain does fall, it tends to arrive as intense, short-duration convectional rainstorms. Because desert soils are poorly developed, lack vegetation, and are often sealed by a hard surface crust, infiltration is very low — most water runs off immediately as surface flow.

Flash Floods and Wadis

Wadi: A dry river channel (or valley) found in desert environments. Most of the year they are completely dry, but during and after rare rainstorms they fill rapidly with fast-moving, sediment-laden floodwater. The word is Arabic and is used across the Middle East and North Africa. Equivalent terms: "arroyo" (SW USA/Mexico), "nullah" (South Asia).

Flash floods in wadis are among the most dangerous natural hazards in desert regions. Key characteristics:

  • Speed of onset: Floodwaters can arrive with little or no warning — a storm 50 km away in mountains can send a wall of water down a normally dry wadi within minutes. A person in a wadi may hear a roaring sound before seeing the flood.
  • Sediment load: Flash floods carry enormous amounts of sediment — the water appears brown or red and has a consistency almost like wet concrete at peak flow. Boulders are transported that would never move in normal river flow (high competence due to high velocity).
  • Duration: Floods typically last only a few hours, then the water drains away rapidly or evaporates, leaving behind thick deposits of sand and gravel on the wadi floor.
  • Erosional power: Repeated flash floods carve the characteristic flat-bottomed, steep-sided wadi profile. The flat floor results from lateral erosion and deposition during floods; the steep sides result from undercutting and collapse.

Alluvial Fans — Formation and Significance

When a wadi exits a confined mountain valley and reaches an open flat basin or plain, the flow loses velocity dramatically. The sudden change spreads water across a wide area. Sediment drops out of suspension in a characteristic fan shape:

  • Apex (top, near canyon mouth): Coarse material — boulders, cobbles, coarse gravel. Deposited first as velocity drops rapidly.
  • Mid-fan: Medium gravel and sand. Water has slowed further and spread out.
  • Toe (outer edge of fan): Fine silt and clay; may pond into temporary desert lakes (playas).

Where multiple alluvial fans merge along a mountain front, they coalesce into a continuous sloping apron called a bajada. Alluvial fans are significant because they can contain freshwater aquifers (water soaks into the fan gravels during floods) and are often used as settlement sites in desert regions.

Desert Lakes — Playas

Playa: A flat desert basin that occasionally fills with shallow water after flash floods or heavy rain, then dries out to leave a flat, salt-encrusted surface. Also called a "sabkha" (Arabian Peninsula), "chott" (North Africa), or "salt flat." Formed in enclosed basins where water collects but cannot drain — evaporation concentrates minerals, leaving behind evaporite deposits (salt, gypsum, borax). Salar de Uyuni (Bolivia) is the world's largest salt flat at 10,582 km².
Exam Tip — Water in Deserts: A common misconception is that water does not shape desert landscapes. In fact, most geographers argue water action (fluvial processes) is MORE important than wind in shaping desert landforms overall — wind shapes dunes and creates yardangs, but water shapes the wider landscape through wadis, alluvial fans, pediments, and playas. Make this point explicitly in high-mark answers.

Exam Tip — Atacama vs Sahara: If asked to compare two desert environments, use the structure: similarity → difference → evaluation. For example: "Both receive <250mm rainfall (similarity), however the Atacama is the world's driest desert receiving <1mm/year due to the triple effect of the Humboldt Current, Andean rain shadow, and subtropical high pressure, while the Sahara receives up to 25mm due to Hadley Cell alone (difference). This means the Atacama supports almost no life in its core while Sahara margins support sparse vegetation (evaluation/consequence)."

10. Tourism: Opportunity and Challenge

Tourism in hot deserts has grown rapidly since the 1990s, driven by rising global incomes, improved transport links to remote areas, and growing demand for "adventure" and "extreme" experiences. Desert tourism is now a significant source of income for countries including the UAE, Jordan, Morocco, Namibia, and Peru (Atacama).

Opportunities from Desert Tourism

  • Economic income: Tourism generates foreign exchange, taxes, and employment for local communities. In Jordan, Wadi Rum tourism generates millions of dollars annually; local Bedouin families run camps and guided tours.
  • Infrastructure development: Tourism investment often funds road construction, airport expansion, and utilities (electricity, communications) that also benefit local populations.
  • Cultural preservation: Tourist interest in traditional cultures (Bedouin, Tuareg, Berber) can incentivise communities to maintain traditional practices, crafts, language, and music that might otherwise be lost to modernisation.
  • Conservation funding: Ecotourism revenues can fund national park management, anti-poaching, and habitat restoration. Namibia's community conservancies, partly funded by safari tourism, have seen significant wildlife recovery (desert-adapted lions, elephants, rhinos).
  • UAE/Dubai example: Dubai's desert tourism (dune bashing, camel safaris, falconry, desert dining camps) forms a key part of its diversification away from oil dependency. The Dubai Desert Conservation Reserve (225 km²) protects desert biodiversity while enabling tourism.

Challenges from Desert Tourism

  • Environmental damage: Off-road vehicles (4x4s, quad bikes) compact desert soils, destroy fragile cryptobiotic soil crusts (which take decades to re-form), and crush vegetation. Tyre tracks in Peruvian deserts have damaged ancient Nazca Lines.
  • Water consumption: Tourist hotels and resorts in desert cities (Dubai, Las Vegas, Marrakech) consume enormous quantities of water for swimming pools, golf courses, and gardens — deepening water scarcity problems. Dubai uses ~550 litres/person/day (far above global average of 170 litres).
  • Litter and waste: Remote desert areas have no waste management infrastructure; tourist sites accumulate plastic waste, particularly along major desert trekking routes.
  • Cultural conflict: Mass tourism can undermine traditional cultures — traditional nomadic lifestyles commercialised into inauthentic "shows" for tourists; sacred sites can be damaged by visitor footfall (e.g. climbing Uluru was banned in 2019 due to Aboriginal spiritual significance and environmental damage).
  • Carbon footprint: Desert tourism typically requires long-haul flights, air-conditioned vehicles, and energy-intensive accommodation — a high carbon cost per tourist visit.
  • Carrying capacity: Fragile desert ecosystems have a low carrying capacity — even small numbers of visitors can cause irreversible damage to rare species, rock formations, and archaeological sites. The Atacama's famous "flowering desert" (desierto florido) events have attracted huge tourist numbers that trample the very blooms visitors come to see.
Exam Tip — Tourism as Opportunity and Challenge: Questions may ask you to "assess the extent to which tourism is an opportunity for desert areas." Structure your answer: explain the opportunities (with place-specific examples and data), then explain the challenges/costs, then make a justified conclusion. A strong conclusion might argue that ecotourism (small-scale, community-managed, environmentally sensitive) offers the best balance, while mass tourism does more harm than good.

Water Management in Deserts — Saudi Arabia in Depth

Saudi Arabia represents one of the most extreme examples globally of the tension between water demand and desert environment. It is almost entirely desert (the Rub' al Khali alone covers 650,000 km²) yet supports a population of ~35 million and aspirations for major economic development.

Fossil water (non-renewable aquifer): Groundwater stored in underground rock formations (aquifers) over thousands to millions of years during past wetter climatic periods. It is called "fossil" because it is not currently being recharged by rainfall — extracting it is effectively mining a finite resource that cannot be replaced on any human timescale.
  • The Saudi fossil water situation: Saudi Arabia relies on the Saq Aquifer — a vast sandstone aquifer system storing fossil water that accumulated 10,000–30,000 years ago during the last Ice Age when the Arabian Peninsula was much wetter. Extraction rates are approximately 17 times the natural recharge rate.
  • Scale of depletion: Between 1980 and 2012, Saudi Arabia extracted an estimated 300–400 km³ of fossil water — comparable to draining multiple Lake Eries. Water table levels have dropped by up to 100 metres in some areas.
  • Wheat production collapse: Saudi Arabia used fossil water to grow wheat in the desert from the 1970s to 2000s, briefly making it a net wheat exporter. By 2008, recognising aquifer depletion was threatening domestic water security, the government announced it would phase out wheat growing entirely by 2016. Production dropped from 4 million tonnes (peak) to near zero — a complete policy reversal.
  • Desalination dependency: Saudi Arabia now has 27 desalination plants producing approximately 70% of the country's drinking water — it is the world's largest producer of desalinated water. The Ras Al-Khair plant (2014) is the world's largest desalination facility, producing 1.025 million m³/day. However, desalination consumes enormous amounts of energy (powered mainly by fossil fuels), is very expensive (~$1/m³ vs $0.01 for pumped groundwater), and produces hypersaline brine that is discharged into the Gulf, damaging marine ecosystems.
  • NEOM and future challenges: Saudi Arabia's NEOM mega-city project (planned desert city for 9 million people in the Tabuk desert) will require extraordinary water supplies — planned to come entirely from solar-powered desalination. Critics question whether this is environmentally or economically sustainable.
Exam Tip — Water Management Evaluation: When evaluating water management strategies, consider: (1) Is the source sustainable or finite? Fossil water and desalination solve short-term problems but create long-term dependence. (2) What are the economic costs? Desalination is 10–100x more expensive than surface water. (3) What are the environmental costs? Brine discharge, energy consumption. (4) What happens when the source runs out or becomes unaffordable? Saudi Arabia's wheat abandonment shows that even wealthy countries must eventually confront environmental limits.

12. Desertification

Desertification: The process by which fertile land in dryland areas (arid, semi-arid, and dry sub-humid) gradually degrades and becomes desert-like. It involves loss of topsoil, vegetation, and land productivity. Defined by the UNCCD (United Nations Convention to Combat Desertification). Affects approximately 1.5 billion people globally and threatens ~40% of Earth's land surface.

Location: The Sahel Region

The Sahel is a semi-arid belt running across Africa from Mauritania and Senegal in the west to Eritrea and Ethiopia in the east — approximately 5,400 km long. It sits between the Sahara Desert to the north and the tropical savanna to the south. It is the world's most at-risk region for desertification.

OvergrazingToo many livestock (cattle, goats, camels) consume vegetation faster than it can regenerate; roots die; bare soil exposed to wind erosion; soil compacted by hoovesHuman (population growth, cattle as status symbol)
Deforestation (fuelwood collection)Trees and shrubs cut for firewood (80% of sub-Saharan Africa relies on wood for cooking fuel); roots no longer hold soil; wind erosion increasesHuman (poverty, lack of alternatives)
Over-cultivationLand farmed without rotation or fallow periods; soil nutrients exhausted; soil structure degrades; wind and rain erode bare soil between cropsHuman (food demand, poverty)
Population growthMore people = more demand for food and fuel from same fixed land area; fragile marginal land brought into cultivation; pressure increasesHuman
Climate change / droughtReduced and increasingly erratic rainfall across the Sahel (linked to warming Indian Ocean and shifts in ITCZ); longer drought periods mean vegetation cannot recover between dry seasonsPhysical (exacerbated by human climate change)

Effects of Desertification

  • Soil erosion: Topsoil blown away by wind or washed by flash floods, exposing infertile subsoil
  • Reduced crop yields: Degraded soil cannot support adequate harvests; food insecurity worsens
  • Famine and malnutrition: Crop failures lead to starvation — particularly affecting children under 5
  • Migration: People abandon degraded land and move to urban areas or other countries (climate/environmental refugees)
  • Conflict: Competition for remaining productive land and water resources between farmers and herders has intensified conflicts (e.g. Sahel violence in Burkina Faso, Mali, Niger)
  • Economic loss: UNCCD estimates desertification costs ~$42 billion per year in lost economic output

13. Case Study: Desertification in the Sahel

Case Study: Desertification in the Sahel

Location: A belt of semi-arid land approximately 350–500 km wide stretching ~5,400 km across Africa: Mauritania, Senegal, Mali, Burkina Faso, Niger, Chad, Sudan, South Sudan, Ethiopia, Eritrea.

Population: ~135 million people depend on this region for subsistence agriculture and pastoralism. Population is growing at ~3% per year — among the world's fastest rates — increasing pressure on land dramatically.

Historical famines:

  • 1968–1973: Prolonged drought; ~100,000 deaths; millions displaced
  • 1984–1985: Worst drought of the 20th century in Ethiopia/Sudan; ~400,000 deaths; Live Aid response
  • 2011–2012: Horn of Africa drought declared a famine; 260,000 deaths in Somalia alone
  • 2021–2023: Ongoing food crisis with 20+ million people facing acute food insecurity in the Sahel

The Great Green Wall project: A pan-African initiative launched in 2007 by the African Union — a planned 8,000 km × 15 km belt of native trees and vegetation stretching from Senegal to Djibouti, designed to combat desertification, improve food security, and sequester carbon (250 million tonnes by 2030).

  • Progress by 2023: Only ~15% of the planned wall has been planted/restored
  • Challenges: Funding gaps, political instability in the Sahel (multiple coups in Burkina Faso, Mali, Niger 2020–2023), drought, maintenance after planting, conflict making access impossible in some areas
  • Successes: In Senegal and Ethiopia, restored areas show improved soil health, food production, and biodiversity; ~12 million hectares restored

Water harvesting — Zai pits (Burkina Faso): Small pits (20–30 cm diameter, 20cm deep) dug in rows across degraded farmland. Fill with compost/manure. Rainwater collects in pits, allowing crops to be grown where soil is too hard for rain to penetrate. Developed by Yacouba Sawadogo — credited with restoring millions of hectares of Sahel farmland. A proven low-tech, low-cost solution.

Drought-resistant crops: NGOs and the CGIAR research network have developed drought-tolerant varieties of sorghum, millet, and cowpea. WEMA (Water Efficient Maize for Africa) project distributes drought-tolerant maize seeds to small farmers.

14. Case Study: Atacama Desert Mining (Chile)

Case Study: Resource Extraction in the Atacama Desert, Chile

Location: The Atacama Desert in northern Chile, primarily the Antofagasta Region. Elevation ranges from sea level to over 5,000m (altiplano). The driest non-polar desert on Earth.

Why it matters globally: The Atacama underpins the global green energy transition. Chile holds approximately 44% of the world's known lithium reserves, mostly in the Atacama Salt Flat (Salar de Atacama). Lithium is a critical component of lithium-ion batteries used in electric vehicles (EVs), smartphones, and grid-scale energy storage. Demand for lithium is projected to increase 40-fold by 2040 as the world transitions away from fossil fuels.

Copper: Chile also produces approximately 27% of the world's copper — much of it from Atacama mines. The Escondida mine (Antofagasta Region, partly owned by BHP) is the world's largest copper mine, producing ~5% of global supply. Copper is essential for electrical wiring, electric motors, and renewable energy infrastructure.

Economic benefits:

  • Mining accounts for ~55% of Chile's total export earnings and ~15% of GDP
  • Creates hundreds of thousands of direct and indirect jobs (though many are FIFO — fly-in-fly-out — workers from distant cities rather than local indigenous communities)
  • Antofagasta city has grown significantly as a mining service hub; high wages support local businesses
  • Government royalties from mining fund public services nationally (education, healthcare)
  • Chile's lithium wealth has led to intense international investment (US, China, EU competing for access)

Environmental challenges — water: This is the critical problem. Lithium is extracted from brine (salty water) pumped up from beneath salt flats. The process:

  • Companies pump brine from underground aquifers into large evaporation ponds; sun evaporates water, leaving lithium-rich residue over 12–18 months
  • This extraction significantly lowers the water table in the Salar de Atacama — a UNESCO Biosphere Reserve
  • Flamingo populations (Andean, Chilean, James's flamingos) that feed on algae in Atacama wetlands have declined; some wetland areas have shrunk by up to 65%
  • The Atacameno/Lickanantay indigenous people (who have lived around the salar for thousands of years) argue their water rights are being violated; traditional farming and llama herding made impossible in some areas
  • One litre of lithium production requires approximately 2,000 litres of water — in a desert that receives <1mm of rain per year

Environmental challenges — other:

  • Tailings (mining waste) contain toxic heavy metals (arsenic, lead, mercury) that can leach into groundwater
  • Dust from mine operations and tailings dams creates air pollution affecting nearby settlements
  • Road and infrastructure construction through pristine desert destroys unique biological soil crusts and archaeological sites (the Atacama contains some of the world's oldest human mummies — Chinchorro mummies — and pre-Inca ruins)

Evaluation — the lithium paradox: Lithium mining in the Atacama presents a genuine paradox: extracting this resource damages a unique desert ecosystem and indigenous communities, yet the lithium itself is needed for the electric vehicles and batteries that could reduce global carbon emissions and slow climate change. Chile's government is attempting to balance this by nationalising lithium production (2023 announcement) to ensure greater state control, environmental standards, and indigenous community benefit-sharing — but tensions remain unresolved.

Key statistics to remember: Chile: 44% of world lithium reserves; Escondida: 5% of world copper production; 2,000 litres water per litre of lithium; flamingo wetlands shrunk by up to 65%.

Stone Bunds — Evidence and Detail

Stone bunds are simple but highly effective contour barriers. The technique involves:

  1. Identifying the contour lines of a degraded slope (using a simple A-frame level made from two sticks and a string)
  2. Collecting rocks from the surrounding area and arranging them in lines following the contour — typically stones 10–20cm in diameter, placed in rows 20–30cm high
  3. Rainwater flowing down the slope is slowed and pooled behind the stone lines, allowing it to infiltrate the soil rather than running off
  4. Sediment carried by runoff is also trapped behind the bunds, gradually rebuilding topsoil

Documented outcomes in Burkina Faso:

  • Crop yields increased by 40–50% in the first season after bund construction
  • In Yatenga Province, stone bunds combined with zai pits have reclaimed an estimated 300,000 hectares of degraded land since the 1980s
  • Water table levels have risen by up to 3 metres in some areas where stone bunds have been used widely — improving well water supply for domestic use
  • Cost: approximately $50–100 per hectare using local labour and stone — extremely cost-effective compared to other interventions
  • Self-maintaining: communities maintain their own bunds as they directly benefit from improved harvests

FMNR — Farmer Managed Natural Regeneration

FMNR (Farmer Managed Natural Regeneration): A land restoration technique developed in Niger by Australian agronomist Tony Rinaudo (known as "The Man Who Stopped the Desert") in the 1980s. Rather than planting new trees (which are expensive and often fail to survive in harsh conditions), FMNR involves protecting and managing the natural regrowth of trees and shrubs from existing root systems that survived below-ground even when above-ground vegetation was destroyed by overgrazing, drought, or fuelwood cutting.

The FMNR method:

  • Farmers select the strongest shoots emerging from dormant underground tree stumps and root systems (which can remain alive for decades)
  • They prune competing weak shoots and protect the selected shoot from grazing animals
  • Trees can grow to 2–3 metres within 3–4 years — far faster than planting new seedlings
  • Farmers actively "manage" the trees — selectively harvesting branches for firewood and fodder while keeping the main trunk growing

Results in Niger (the "Re-greening of the Sahel"):

  • An estimated 5 million hectares of Niger's agricultural land has been restored using FMNR since the 1980s — the largest environmental transformation in African history
  • Approximately 200 million trees have been regenerated on farmer fields across Niger
  • Crop yields increased by 10–85% depending on location — trees provide shade (reducing crop water stress), fix nitrogen (improving soil fertility), and reduce wind erosion
  • Food security improved: surveys found that households in FMNR areas had on average 6 extra months of food security per year compared to control areas
  • Fuelwood supply improved dramatically — women (who collect fuelwood) reduced collection time from 2 hours to 30 minutes per day
  • Cost: near-zero, as no seeds or planting required; farmers do the work themselves
  • Technique has now been adopted across 24 countries in sub-Saharan Africa and beyond
Exam Tip — Management Strategy Comparison: FMNR vs Great Green Wall is an excellent comparison for evaluation questions. FMNR is: bottom-up (farmer-led), low-cost (near-zero), fast (results in 3–4 years), locally appropriate (uses species already adapted to local conditions), and proven (5 million hectares in Niger). The Great Green Wall is top-down (government/international), expensive (billions of dollars pledged), slow (15% complete after 16 years), and faces political barriers (coups, instability). This does NOT mean large-scale projects are useless — they can fund FMNR training and provide political will — but the lesson is that community-owned solutions tend to deliver better outcomes per dollar spent.
Exam Tip: For desertification questions, make sure you can distinguish between physical (climate) and human causes — many questions ask you to explain how BOTH contribute. The strongest answers also link causes together (e.g. population growth increases overgrazing, which combined with reduced rainfall creates a vicious cycle of degradation).
Key Comparison: In the exam you may be asked to compare management strategies. Low-tech, community-led approaches (zai pits, stone bunds, FMNR) are often more effective in practice than large-scale international projects (Great Green Wall) because they are owned by the community, use local knowledge, are cheap, and do not depend on political stability or international funding.
