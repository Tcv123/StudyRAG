4.2 Development and Inequality

Economic Growth vs Economic Development

Economic growth: An increase in real GDP over time; a quantitative measure of output.

Economic development: A broader concept encompassing improvements in living standards, health, education, political freedom, and well-being. Growth is necessary but not sufficient for development.

Growth without development can occur when: income gains go to a small elite; growth comes from resource extraction with little employment; environmental destruction accompanies growth; civil liberties are suppressed.

Inequality

Income inequality: The uneven distribution of income across a population.

Wealth inequality: The uneven distribution of assets (property, savings, shares) across a population. Wealth inequality is typically greater than income inequality.

Causes of Inequality

  • Wage differentials: Differences in skills, education, experience, productivity
  • Ownership of assets: Wealth generates income (dividends, rent, capital gains) concentrated among the rich
  • Tax system: Regressive taxes increase inequality; progressive taxes reduce it
  • Globalisation: Benefits skilled workers disproportionately; low-skilled jobs offshored
  • Technological change: Automation reduces demand for routine jobs, increases returns to high-skill work
  • Labour market imperfections: Discrimination, monopsony power, weak unions
  • Inherited wealth: Intergenerational transfer perpetuates inequality
  • Government policy: Welfare provision, minimum wage, education access

The Lorenz Curve

Lorenz curve: A graphical representation of the distribution of income or wealth. The further the curve is from the line of perfect equality (45-degree line), the greater the inequality.
Lorenz curves: Country B (red) has greater inequality than Country A (blue). The Gini coefficient measures the area between the Lorenz curve and the line of perfect equality.

The Gini Coefficient

Gini coefficient: A numerical measure of inequality derived from the Lorenz curve. Ranges from 0 (perfect equality) to 1 (perfect inequality). Calculated as the area between the Lorenz curve and the line of equality, divided by the total area below the line of equality.

Consequences of Inequality

  • Economic: Reduced social mobility; lower aggregate demand (rich have lower MPC); under-investment in human capital by the poor; potential demand-deficient unemployment
  • Social: Health inequalities; higher crime rates; reduced trust and social cohesion; political instability
  • Positive view: Some inequality provides incentives for enterprise, risk-taking, and innovation; rewards skill and effort

Poverty

Absolute poverty: Income insufficient to meet basic needs (food, shelter, clean water, healthcare). The World Bank defines extreme poverty as living on less than $2.15/day (2022 PPP).

Relative poverty: Income significantly below the average for a society (typically below 60% of median household income). Can exist even in wealthy countries.

Causes of Poverty in Developing Countries

  • Low productivity: Poor infrastructure, limited capital, low technology
  • Lack of education/skills: Low human capital investment
  • Poor governance: Corruption, weak institutions, political instability
  • Primary product dependency: Volatile commodity prices, declining terms of trade
  • Debt burden: Debt repayments divert funds from development spending
  • Population growth: Rapid growth outstrips economic growth per capita
  • Geography/climate: Landlocked countries, disease burden, natural disasters
  • Historical factors: Colonialism, conflict, institutional legacies

Other Approaches

  • Fair trade: Guarantees minimum prices for producers in developing countries. Benefits small producers but limited scale and may distort markets.
  • Tourism: Generates foreign exchange and employment. Risk of dependency, environmental damage, and leakage of revenue to foreign-owned hotels.
  • Industrialisation (Lewis model): Transfer surplus labour from agriculture to manufacturing; increases productivity and wages. Assumes manufacturing jobs are available.
Exam Tip: When evaluating development strategies, always consider: the specific context of the country; short-run vs long-run effects; who benefits and who loses; sustainability; the role of institutions and governance; and whether a combination of strategies is needed rather than one approach alone.

Sustainability and the Environment

Sustainable development: Development that meets the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs (Brundtland definition). Requires balancing economic growth, environmental protection, and social equity.

Conflicts Between Growth and Sustainability

  • GDP growth often involves increased resource use, pollution, and carbon emissions
  • Deforestation for agriculture; overfishing; water depletion
  • Developing countries argue they need growth before they can afford environmental protection
  • Environmental Kuznets Curve: suggests pollution rises with income initially but falls after a threshold (debated)

Policies for Sustainable Development

  • Carbon taxes and tradable permits: Internalise environmental externalities
  • Investment in renewable energy: Reduce dependency on fossil fuels
  • Regulation: Emission standards, protected areas, sustainable forestry
  • International agreements: Paris Climate Accord, SDGs (Sustainable Development Goals)
  • Green technology transfer: Developed countries share clean technology with developing countries
  • Circular economy: Reduce, reuse, recycle; minimise waste
The three pillars of sustainable development: economic, environmental, and social

Role of International Institutions

InstitutionRole in Development
World BankProvides loans and grants for development projects (infrastructure, education, health); offers technical expertise; focuses on poverty reduction

UN/UNDPSets global development agenda (SDGs); publishes HDI; coordinates humanitarian aid and development programmes

Criticisms of International Institutions

  • Structural adjustment programmes (IMF/World Bank) impose austerity and liberalisation that may harm the poor
  • Voting power reflects economic weight, not population (Western bias)
  • Conditions attached to loans may not suit local contexts
  • WTO rules may disadvantage developing countries (agricultural subsidies in developed nations)
  • Debt created by loans may worsen long-term dependency
Exam Tip: In questions about development, always distinguish between growth and development. Use specific country examples where possible. Evaluate whether strategies address root causes or symptoms. Consider the role of governance and institutions, as even good strategies fail without effective implementation.
