Theme 4.2 Development and Inequality — Edexcel B

1. Economic Growth vs Economic Development

Economic growth: An increase in the real GDP of an economy over time — a quantitative measure of increased output.

Economic development: A broader, qualitative concept encompassing improvements in living standards, well-being, freedom, education, health, and the reduction of poverty and inequality. Development includes growth but goes far beyond it.

A country can have economic growth without development — for example, if growth is concentrated among the wealthy, destroys the environment, or fails to improve health and education for the majority. Equally, some development improvements (e.g., better governance, women's rights) can occur without rapid GDP growth.

2.2 Composite Indicators

Human Development Index (HDI)

HDI: A composite index developed by the UN, combining three dimensions: (1) health — life expectancy at birth, (2) education — mean years of schooling and expected years of schooling, (3) income — GNI per capita (PPP, logged). Scored from 0 to 1, with 1 being the highest level of development.

Strengths and Weaknesses of HDI

  • Strengths: Broader than GDP alone; simple and comparable; highlights countries where income growth has not translated into development (e.g., oil-rich states with low education/health).
  • Weaknesses: Only three dimensions — ignores inequality, political freedom, environmental sustainability, gender equality, and security. Uses national averages — masks within-country inequality. Data quality varies. Does not capture informal economy or subjective well-being.

Other Composite Measures

  • Inequality-adjusted HDI (IHDI): Adjusts the HDI downward based on inequality in each dimension. Countries with high inequality see a larger drop from HDI to IHDI.
  • Gender Inequality Index (GII): Measures gender-based disadvantage in reproductive health, empowerment, and the labour market.
  • Multidimensional Poverty Index (MPI): Measures overlapping deprivations across health, education, and living standards at the household level.
Case Study: Saudi Arabia — Growth Without Full Development
Saudi Arabia has a GDP per capita (PPP) of over $55,000 — among the highest in the world. However, its HDI (0.875) ranks significantly lower than many countries with lower incomes (e.g., Japan, 0.920, with lower GDP per capita). Saudi Arabia's development is limited by: restrictions on women's rights (though recent reforms have improved this), dependence on oil (limited economic diversification), a large migrant worker population with limited rights, and constraints on political freedom and civil liberties. This illustrates how income alone does not equal development.

3. Factors Affecting Development

3.1 Economic Factors

  • Savings and investment: The Harrod-Domar model emphasises that growth requires investment, which requires savings. Many developing countries have low savings rates due to poverty, limiting investment and creating a poverty trap.
  • Primary product dependency: Countries reliant on exporting a narrow range of commodities (oil, copper, cocoa) are vulnerable to price volatility and long-term declining terms of trade (Prebisch-Singer hypothesis).
  • Debt: Many developing countries carry heavy external debt burdens — debt servicing diverts resources from health, education, and infrastructure.
  • Access to financial markets: Underdeveloped banking systems and lack of access to credit prevent entrepreneurs from investing.
  • Infrastructure: Poor roads, ports, electricity, and telecommunications raise costs and deter investment.

3.2 Non-Economic Factors

  • Governance and institutions: Corruption, weak rule of law, insecure property rights, and political instability deter investment and waste resources. The World Bank's governance indicators show a strong correlation between institutional quality and development.
  • Geography and climate: Landlocked countries, those in tropical disease zones, or those vulnerable to natural disasters face higher development costs.
  • Education and human capital: Low levels of education reduce productivity and innovation capacity.
  • Health: Disease burdens (malaria, HIV/AIDS, tuberculosis) reduce workforce productivity and divert government spending.
  • Conflict: War destroys infrastructure, displaces populations, and diverts spending from development to military.
  • Demographics: High population growth can outstrip economic growth, reducing GDP per capita. A high dependency ratio strains resources.
  • Gender inequality: Excluding women from education and the workforce wastes half the potential human capital.
  • Colonial legacy: Arbitrary borders, extractive institutions, and dependent trade patterns established during colonialism continue to affect development in many countries.
Case Study: Sub-Saharan Africa's Development Challenges
Sub-Saharan Africa is the world's least developed region — home to 27 of the 32 countries classified as "low human development." The challenges are interconnected: weak governance and corruption (Transparency International ranks many African countries in the bottom quartile); debt burdens (many spent more on debt servicing than healthcare before the HIPC initiative); disease (Sub-Saharan Africa has 11% of the world's population but 60% of HIV-positive people); conflict (over 20 armed conflicts active in 2023); poor infrastructure (only 25% of roads are paved); and primary product dependency (oil, minerals, and agricultural commodities dominate exports). However, some countries show that these challenges can be overcome — Rwanda has achieved rapid growth and development through strong governance and investment in education and technology.

4. Strategies for Promoting Development

4.1 Market-Oriented Strategies

Trade Liberalisation

Reducing tariffs and trade barriers to integrate into the global economy. Benefits include access to larger markets, technology transfer, competitive pressure driving efficiency, and specialisation based on comparative advantage.

Evaluation: Trade liberalisation has contributed to rapid growth in East Asia (South Korea, China, Vietnam). However, it can destroy infant industries, increase vulnerability to global shocks, and widen inequality if gains are concentrated among elites. The sequencing matters — liberalisation works best alongside strong institutions and gradual reform.

Attracting Foreign Direct Investment (FDI)

Encouraging multinational corporations to invest by offering tax incentives, special economic zones, infrastructure, and a stable business environment. FDI can bring capital, technology, management skills, and access to global supply chains.

Evaluation: FDI can create jobs and transfer technology, but profits may be repatriated rather than reinvested locally. MNCs may exploit cheap labour and weak environmental regulations. Transfer pricing can reduce tax revenue for the host country. Benefits depend heavily on the regulatory framework and bargaining power of the host government.

Microfinance

Providing small loans to individuals and small businesses who lack access to traditional banking. Pioneered by Grameen Bank in Bangladesh (founded by Muhammad Yunus, Nobel Peace Prize 2006).

  • Benefits: Enables entrepreneurship, empowers women (the majority of borrowers), helps people escape poverty traps.
  • Limitations: High interest rates compared to conventional banking; some borrowers take multiple loans and fall into debt traps; limited evidence of transformative impact on wider economic development; works best alongside education and infrastructure.

4.2 Interventionist Strategies

Aid

Aid: Financial or technical assistance given by governments (bilateral), international organisations (multilateral — e.g., World Bank, IMF), or NGOs to developing countries to promote development.
  • Types: Tied aid (must be spent on donor-country goods), untied aid, project aid (specific infrastructure), programme aid (budget support), humanitarian/emergency aid, technical assistance.
  • Arguments for: Fills the savings-investment gap; funds essential services (healthcare, education); provides humanitarian relief; can support institutional reform.
  • Arguments against: May create dependency; can be diverted by corrupt governments; tied aid serves donor interests; may crowd out domestic saving and enterprise; the "aid curse" mirrors the resource curse.
Case Study: Aid Effectiveness — Comparing Ethiopia and Somalia
Ethiopia has been one of Africa's largest aid recipients (receiving over $4 billion per year), and has achieved rapid economic growth (averaging over 10% per year from 2005-2015), significant poverty reduction, and improvements in education and healthcare. Aid was relatively well-targeted and supported government development plans. By contrast, Somalia — also a major aid recipient — has seen far less development impact due to decades of conflict, state collapse, and governance failures. Much aid has been diverted, wasted, or consumed by emergency humanitarian needs rather than long-term development. This comparison illustrates that aid effectiveness depends critically on governance, stability, and institutional quality in the recipient country.

Debt Relief

Cancelling or reducing the debt obligations of developing countries to free up resources for development spending. The Heavily Indebted Poor Countries (HIPC) Initiative (1996) and Multilateral Debt Relief Initiative (MDRI, 2005) cancelled over $100 billion of debt for the poorest countries.

Infrastructure Development

Government investment in roads, railways, ports, electricity, water supply, and telecommunications — essential for reducing costs, connecting markets, and attracting private investment.

Education and Healthcare Investment

Building human capital through investment in schools, universities, teacher training, hospitals, and public health programmes. This improves productivity, reduces infant mortality, increases life expectancy, and creates a skilled workforce.

4.3 Industrialisation Strategies

Import Substitution Industrialisation (ISI)

Replacing imports with domestically produced goods, using tariffs and quotas to protect domestic industries. Widely adopted in Latin America (1950s-80s) and India.

  • Advantages: Develops domestic industry, reduces import dependence, creates manufacturing jobs.
  • Disadvantages: Protected industries may remain inefficient; limited by domestic market size; retaliation from trading partners; misallocation of resources.

Export-Led Growth

Focusing on producing goods for export by exploiting comparative advantage, attracting FDI, and keeping the exchange rate competitive. The strategy adopted by the East Asian "tiger" economies (South Korea, Taiwan, Singapore, Hong Kong).

  • Advantages: Access to global markets; competitive pressure drives efficiency; technology transfer; foreign exchange earnings.
  • Disadvantages: Vulnerable to global demand fluctuations; may involve low wages and poor working conditions initially; dependent on open markets in destination countries.
Case Study: South Korea's Development Transformation
South Korea is perhaps the most remarkable development success story. In 1960, its GDP per capita was comparable to Ghana's. Today, it is a high-income OECD country with GDP per capita (PPP) over $50,000 and an HDI of 0.929. The strategy combined: heavy government investment in education (near-universal literacy by the 1970s, now one of the highest university attendance rates globally); export-led industrialisation supported by strategic government intervention (targeting shipbuilding, steel, electronics, and automobiles); land reform that reduced inequality; and high savings and investment rates. However, development also came with costs — long working hours, intense social pressure, high inequality compared to other developed nations, and environmental degradation.

5. Poverty and Inequality

5.1 Absolute vs Relative Poverty

Absolute poverty: A fixed standard — living below a minimum income level needed to meet basic needs (food, shelter, clothing). The World Bank defines extreme poverty as living on less than $2.15 per day (PPP, 2022 prices). About 700 million people still live in extreme poverty.

Relative poverty: Being poor relative to others in your society. In the UK, relative poverty is typically defined as living in a household with income below 60% of the median. A person can be in relative poverty in the UK but not in absolute poverty (they can afford basic needs but are significantly poorer than most of their society).

5.2 Causes of Poverty

  • Low wages and unemployment.
  • Lack of education and skills — limits earning potential.
  • Poor health — reduces ability to work and creates expenses.
  • Discrimination — gender, race, caste, disability.
  • Geography — rural areas often have fewer economic opportunities.
  • Conflict and political instability.
  • The poverty trap/cycle: Low income leads to low savings, low investment in human capital, low productivity, and continued low income — a self-reinforcing cycle.

5.3 Income and Wealth Inequality

Income inequality: Unequal distribution of income among individuals or households in an economy.

Wealth inequality: Unequal distribution of assets (property, shares, savings, pensions). Wealth inequality is typically much greater than income inequality — in the UK, the wealthiest 10% own approximately 44% of total wealth, while the bottom 50% own less than 9%.

5.4 Causes of Inequality

  • Wage differentials: Skills, education, experience, and occupation cause large income differences.
  • Globalisation: Benefits skilled workers and capital owners disproportionately; may reduce wages for low-skilled workers in developed countries.
  • Technological change: Skill-biased technological change increases demand for (and wages of) skilled workers while displacing low-skilled jobs.
  • Tax and benefit system: Regressive taxes increase inequality; progressive taxes and benefits reduce it.
  • Asset ownership: Those who own property and shares benefit from rising asset prices — exacerbating wealth inequality.
  • Inheritance: Wealth passed between generations perpetuates inequality.
  • Labour market changes: Decline of trade unions, rise of gig economy, zero-hours contracts.

6. Measuring Inequality — Lorenz Curve and Gini Coefficient

6.1 The Lorenz Curve

Lorenz curve: A graph showing the cumulative percentage of income (or wealth) received by the cumulative percentage of the population, ranked from poorest to richest. The further the Lorenz curve is from the line of perfect equality (45-degree line), the greater the inequality.
Figure 1: Lorenz curves. Country A has moderate inequality; Country B has high inequality (curve further from the line of equality). The area between the equality line and the Lorenz curve determines the Gini coefficient.

6.2 The Gini Coefficient

Gini coefficient: A numerical measure of inequality derived from the Lorenz curve, ranging from 0 (perfect equality — everyone has the same income) to 1 (perfect inequality — one person has all the income). Calculated as the area between the line of equality and the Lorenz curve, divided by the total area under the line of equality.
Gini = Area A ÷ (Area A + Area B)

6.3 Limitations of the Gini Coefficient

  • Two countries can have the same Gini but very different Lorenz curves (different patterns of inequality).
  • Does not show where in the distribution the inequality lies (top, middle, or bottom).
  • Measures income inequality only — does not capture wealth inequality, access to services, or quality of life.
  • Data quality varies between countries and over time.
  • Based on household surveys which may undercount very high and very low incomes.
Case Study: UK Inequality Trends
UK income inequality rose sharply during the 1980s (Gini rose from 0.25 in 1979 to 0.34 by 1990) due to tax cuts for high earners, deregulation, decline of trade unions, and rising unemployment. Since then, the Gini has remained relatively stable at around 0.34-0.36. However, wealth inequality has continued to widen — driven by rising house prices (homeowners benefit while renters do not), growth in financial assets, and inheritance. The COVID-19 pandemic initially compressed inequality (furlough protected low-income jobs), but the subsequent cost-of-living crisis hit lower-income households disproportionately (energy and food take a larger share of their spending). The IFS estimates that the poorest fifth of UK households spend proportionally twice as much on energy as the richest fifth.

7. Government Policies to Reduce Poverty and Inequality

7.1 Progressive Taxation

A tax system where the marginal tax rate increases with income — higher earners pay a larger proportion of their income in tax. The UK income tax system is progressive: 0% (personal allowance up to 12,570), 20% (basic rate), 40% (higher rate), and 45% (additional rate above 125,140).

7.2 Transfer Payments and Benefits

Government payments to individuals: Universal Credit, State Pension, Child Benefit, Housing Benefit, disability allowances. These directly increase the incomes of the poorest households.

7.3 Minimum Wage

The National Living Wage (11.44/hr in 2024 for over-21s) ensures a floor on wages. Directly benefits the lowest-paid workers, though very high levels could reduce employment.

7.4 Public Services

Free or subsidised education (state schools, universities), healthcare (NHS), and housing reduce the impact of income inequality on actual living standards. The value of these "benefits in kind" significantly reduces inequality measured by consumption rather than income.

7.5 Education and Training

Investment in human capital gives people the skills to earn higher incomes — the most effective long-term strategy for reducing inequality and poverty, though it takes years to have an impact.

Evaluation: Policies to Reduce Inequality
  • Progressive taxation: Effective at redistribution, but very high marginal rates may discourage work, enterprise, and investment — potentially reducing growth (Laffer curve argument). May also encourage tax avoidance and evasion.
  • Benefits: Directly reduce poverty, but may create dependency, reduce work incentives (poverty trap/unemployment trap), and are costly for the taxpayer.
  • Minimum wage: Benefits those in work but does not help the unemployed. May cause unemployment in competitive labour markets, though evidence suggests moderate increases have little negative employment effect.
  • Trade-off between equality and efficiency: Excessive redistribution may reduce incentives and economic growth, ultimately leaving less for everyone. However, extreme inequality can also harm growth (reduced social mobility, political instability, underinvestment in human capital).
  • Pre-distribution vs redistribution: Some argue it is better to tackle inequality at source (education, competition policy, labour market reform) rather than relying solely on taxes and benefits after the fact.
Case Study: Nordic Model — Sweden
Sweden combines a strong market economy with an extensive welfare state. It has one of the lowest Gini coefficients (0.29) and highest HDI scores (0.947) in the world. Key features include: highly progressive taxation (top marginal income tax rate around 55%), generous universal public services (free healthcare, education through university, subsidised childcare), strong trade unions negotiating wages, active labour market policies (retraining programmes for displaced workers), and a social safety net that provides security without excessive dependency. However, Sweden has seen inequality rising since the 1990s due to financial deregulation, tax cuts, and immigration integration challenges. The model also requires very high tax rates, which may not be politically feasible in other countries.
Exam Tip: Development questions often require you to evaluate strategies. A strong answer will: (1) explain how the strategy works using economic theory, (2) use real-world examples to support your argument, (3) consider the short-run vs long-run effects, (4) discuss who benefits and who loses, (5) consider whether the strategy is appropriate for the specific country's circumstances (one size does not fit all), and (6) consider whether complementary policies are needed alongside the main strategy.
