1.1 How Markets Work and Surplus

Demand

Demand: The quantity of a good or service that consumers are willing and able to purchase at a given price in a given time period. The law of demand states that, ceteris paribus, as price falls, quantity demanded rises.

The Demand Curve

The demand curve slopes downward from left to right, reflecting the inverse relationship between price and quantity demanded. This occurs due to:

  • Income effect: As price falls, real income rises so consumers can buy more
  • Substitution effect: As price falls relative to substitutes, consumers switch towards the cheaper good
  • Diminishing marginal utility: Additional units yield less satisfaction, so consumers only buy more at lower prices

Shifts in Demand

A movement along the demand curve is caused by a change in price. A shift of the entire demand curve is caused by non-price factors:

Supply

Supply: The quantity of a good or service that producers are willing and able to offer for sale at a given price in a given time period. The law of supply states that, ceteris paribus, as price rises, quantity supplied rises.

The Supply Curve

The supply curve slopes upward from left to right because higher prices increase the incentive for firms to produce (higher profit margins) and can cover higher marginal costs of production.

Market Equilibrium

Equilibrium: The point where quantity demanded equals quantity supplied. At this point there is no tendency for price to change. The equilibrium price is also called the market-clearing price.
Supply and demand diagram showing market equilibrium at point E

Disequilibrium

Excess supply (surplus): When price is above equilibrium, Qs > Qd. Unsold stock forces producers to lower prices until equilibrium is restored.

Excess demand (shortage): When price is below equilibrium, Qd > Qs. Competition among buyers drives prices up until equilibrium is restored.

Consumer and Producer Surplus

Consumer surplus: The difference between what consumers are willing to pay and what they actually pay. Graphically, it is the area below the demand curve and above the equilibrium price.

Producer surplus: The difference between the price producers receive and the minimum price they would be willing to accept. Graphically, it is the area above the supply curve and below the equilibrium price.
Consumer surplus (blue) and producer surplus (green) at market equilibrium

Total economic welfare = Consumer surplus + Producer surplus. In a free market at equilibrium, total welfare is maximised (allocative efficiency). Any deviation from equilibrium creates deadweight welfare loss.

Exam Tip: Always label your surplus diagrams clearly. Show CS as the triangle above price and below demand, PS as the triangle below price and above supply. When answering questions about changes in surplus, shade the areas that are gained/lost and explain direction of change.

Price Elasticity of Demand (PED)

PED: A measure of the responsiveness of quantity demanded to a change in price.
PED = % change in quantity demanded / % change in price

PED is always negative (due to the inverse relationship) but we usually refer to the absolute value.

ValueDescriptionExample
PED = 0Perfectly inelasticLife-saving medication
0 < |PED| < 1InelasticPetrol, cigarettes
|PED| = 1Unit elasticRevenue stays constant
|PED| > 1ElasticLuxury holidays, branded goods
PED = infinityPerfectly elasticPerfect competition (theory)

Determinants of PED

  • Availability of substitutes: More substitutes = more elastic
  • Necessity vs luxury: Necessities are inelastic; luxuries are elastic
  • Proportion of income: Higher proportion = more elastic
  • Time period: Demand is more elastic in the long run
  • Addiction/habit: Habit-forming goods are more inelastic
  • Brand loyalty: Strong loyalty = more inelastic
  • Definition of the market: Narrow definitions (e.g. Coca-Cola) are more elastic than broad (e.g. soft drinks)

Income Elasticity of Demand (YED)

YED: A measure of the responsiveness of quantity demanded to a change in income.
YED = % change in quantity demanded / % change in income
ValueType of GoodExamples
YED < 0Inferior goodSupermarket own-brand products, bus travel
0 < YED < 1Normal good (necessity)Bread, toothpaste, electricity
YED > 1Normal good (luxury)Designer clothes, sports cars, fine dining

Significance of YED

  • Firms producing luxury goods benefit disproportionately during economic growth
  • Firms producing inferior goods may benefit during recessions
  • Helps firms plan production and diversify product ranges
  • Governments use YED data to forecast tax revenues from specific goods

Cross Elasticity of Demand (XED)

XED: A measure of the responsiveness of quantity demanded of good A to a change in the price of good B.
XED = % change in Qd of Good A / % change in price of Good B
ValueRelationshipExamples
XED > 0 (positive)SubstitutesCoca-Cola and Pepsi, butter and margarine
XED < 0 (negative)ComplementsPrinters and ink, cars and petrol
XED = 0Unrelated goodsBread and televisions

The magnitude shows the strength of the relationship. A high positive XED indicates close substitutes; a large negative XED indicates strong complements.

Significance of XED

  • Firms use XED for pricing strategies (if competitor raises price, gauge how much demand shifts)
  • Helps firms identify competitive threats
  • Useful for competition authorities assessing market definition
  • Guides complementary product pricing (e.g. loss leaders)

Price Elasticity of Supply (PES)

PES: A measure of the responsiveness of quantity supplied to a change in price.
PES = % change in quantity supplied / % change in price

PES is always positive (due to the positive relationship between price and quantity supplied).

Determinants of PES

  • Spare capacity: More spare capacity = more elastic supply
  • Stock availability: More stocks = more elastic
  • Time period: Supply is more elastic in the long run
  • Factor mobility: Easily transferable factors = more elastic
  • Nature of the product: Perishable goods have inelastic supply
  • Barriers to entry: Low barriers = more elastic long-run supply
Time PeriodPESReason
Momentary (market period)Perfectly inelasticNo time to change output
Short runRelatively inelasticAt least one factor fixed

Applications of Elasticity

Taxation and Elasticity

The incidence of an indirect tax depends on PED and PES:

  • If demand is inelastic relative to supply, consumers bear a greater proportion of the tax burden
  • If supply is inelastic relative to demand, producers bear a greater proportion
  • Governments target inelastic goods (tobacco, alcohol, petrol) because tax revenue is higher and quantity reduction is smaller

Subsidies and Elasticity

The benefit of a subsidy also depends on relative elasticities:

  • If demand is inelastic, consumers benefit more (lower prices)
  • If supply is inelastic, producers benefit more (higher revenue)
Exam Tip: When discussing elasticity in essays, always provide a real-world example, state the formula, calculate if data is given, and link to business or government decision-making. For evaluation, discuss limitations such as ceteris paribus assumptions, data accuracy, and time lags in estimating elasticity values.

Price Mechanism and Functions

The price mechanism performs three key functions in a free market:

  1. Rationing function: Prices allocate scarce resources to those willing and able to pay
  2. Signalling function: Prices convey information about relative scarcity and changing market conditions
  3. Incentive function: Rising prices incentivise firms to increase supply and consumers to reduce demand
