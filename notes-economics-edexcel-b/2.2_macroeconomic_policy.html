2.2 Macroeconomic Policy

Aggregate Demand (AD)

Aggregate Demand: The total planned expenditure on goods and services produced in an economy at a given price level in a given time period.
AD = C + I + G + (X - M)

Components of AD

ComponentDescriptionDeterminants
C (Consumption)Household spending on goods/services (~60% of AD in UK)Income, interest rates, confidence, wealth, credit availability, taxes
I (Investment)Firm spending on capital goodsInterest rates, confidence, profit levels, technology, government incentives
G (Government spending)Public expenditure on goods/servicesFiscal policy decisions, political priorities
(X-M) Net exportsExports minus importsExchange rate, relative prices, income abroad, competitiveness

The AD Curve

AD slopes downward because at lower price levels:

  • Wealth effect: Real value of wealth increases, boosting consumption
  • Interest rate effect: Lower prices reduce demand for money, lowering interest rates, stimulating C and I
  • International competitiveness effect: Lower domestic prices make exports cheaper and imports dearer

Aggregate Supply (AS)

Aggregate Supply: The total output that all firms in an economy are willing and able to produce at a given price level in a given time period.

Short-Run Aggregate Supply (SRAS)

SRAS slopes upward because higher prices lead to higher output in the short run (some costs are fixed, e.g. wages set by contracts). Shifts of SRAS are caused by changes in costs of production: wages, raw material prices, exchange rate, indirect taxes.

Long-Run Aggregate Supply (LRAS)

LRAS is vertical at the full employment level of output (Yfe). In the long run, output is determined by supply-side factors (quantity and quality of factors of production), not the price level. Shifts of LRAS are caused by: changes in labour force size/quality, capital investment, technology, institutional factors.

Fiscal Policy

Fiscal policy: The use of government spending (G) and taxation (T) to influence aggregate demand and achieve macroeconomic objectives. Controlled by HM Treasury and announced in the Budget.

Expansionary Fiscal Policy

Increase in G and/or decrease in T to boost AD during a recession:

  • Increases disposable income, raising consumption
  • Increased government spending directly adds to AD
  • Subject to the multiplier effect

Contractionary Fiscal Policy

Decrease in G and/or increase in T to reduce AD during inflationary boom:

  • Reduces disposable income, dampening consumption
  • Reduced government spending directly subtracts from AD

Automatic Stabilisers

Fiscal mechanisms that automatically moderate economic fluctuations without active policy changes:

  • Progressive taxation: In a boom, incomes rise, pushing people into higher tax brackets, automatically reducing disposable income
  • Welfare payments: In a recession, more people claim benefits, automatically sustaining incomes and spending

Limitations of Fiscal Policy

  • Time lags: Recognition, decision, and implementation lags (12-18 months)
  • Crowding out: Higher government borrowing may raise interest rates, reducing private sector investment
  • National debt: Budget deficits add to the national debt; sustainability concerns
  • Political constraints: Tax rises and spending cuts are electorally unpopular
  • Multiplier uncertainty: Difficult to predict the size of the multiplier effect
  • Ricardian equivalence: Consumers may save more if they expect future tax rises to pay for current spending

Monetary Policy

Monetary policy: The use of interest rates, money supply, and other monetary tools to influence aggregate demand. In the UK, conducted by the Bank of England's Monetary Policy Committee (MPC), independent since 1997.

The Transmission Mechanism

How a change in Bank Rate affects the economy:

  1. MPC changes Bank Rate (base rate)
  2. Commercial banks adjust lending/savings rates
  3. Affects cost of borrowing and return on savings
  4. Influences consumption (C) and investment (I)
  5. Also affects exchange rate and asset prices
  6. Changes in AD affect output, employment, and inflation

Effects of Lower Interest Rates

  • Cheaper borrowing encourages consumption and investment
  • Lower mortgage payments increase disposable income
  • Reduced incentive to save; money spent instead
  • Exchange rate may depreciate (capital outflows), boosting net exports
  • Asset prices rise (houses, shares), creating wealth effect

Quantitative Easing (QE)

QE: An unconventional monetary policy tool where the central bank creates new electronic money to purchase government bonds and other financial assets from financial institutions, increasing the money supply and lowering long-term interest rates.

How it works: Bank of England buys bonds from banks, increasing their reserves. Banks should then lend more to businesses and consumers. Also pushes down bond yields and raises asset prices.

Limitations: Banks may not lend (liquidity trap); money may inflate asset prices rather than reaching real economy; risk of future inflation; increases inequality (asset holders benefit).

Limitations of Monetary Policy

  • Time lags: Takes 18-24 months for full effect on inflation
  • Liquidity trap: At very low rates, further cuts ineffective (consumers/firms won't borrow)
  • Consumer confidence: If confidence is low, cheaper credit won't stimulate spending
  • Exchange rate effects: May cause import-price inflation if currency depreciates
  • Asset bubbles: Low rates for extended periods may inflate housing/stock bubbles
  • Blunt instrument: Cannot target specific sectors or regions

The Multiplier Effect

The multiplier: The process by which an initial injection into the circular flow leads to a larger final increase in national income. The size of the multiplier depends on the marginal propensity to consume (MPC) and the marginal propensity to withdraw (MPW).
Multiplier (k) = 1 / (1 - MPC) = 1 / MPW
MPW = MPS + MPT + MPM

Where: MPS = marginal propensity to save; MPT = marginal propensity to tax; MPM = marginal propensity to import

The multiplier process: initial injection leads to successive rounds of spending, with withdrawals reducing each round

Factors Affecting the Size of the Multiplier

FactorLarger MultiplierSmaller Multiplier
Savings rateLow MPSHigh MPS
Tax ratesLow tax ratesHigh tax rates
Import propensityLow MPM (closed economy)High MPM (open economy)
Spare capacityLarge output gapEconomy near full capacity
Consumer confidenceHigh confidenceLow confidence (save more)
Exam Tip: The multiplier works in reverse too (negative multiplier). A withdrawal of spending (e.g. austerity cuts) leads to a larger final fall in GDP. Always discuss the multiplier when evaluating the effectiveness of fiscal policy.

Supply-Side Policies

Supply-side policies: Government policies designed to increase the productive potential of the economy by shifting LRAS to the right. They aim to improve efficiency, productivity, and competitiveness.

Market-Based (Interventionist vs Free-Market)

Free-Market Supply-SideInterventionist Supply-Side
Deregulation (reduce red tape)Education and training investment
PrivatisationInfrastructure spending (roads, broadband)
Trade union reformR&D subsidies and tax credits
Income tax cuts (incentive to work)Regional development grants
Corporation tax cuts (incentive to invest)Industrial strategy (supporting key sectors)
Flexible labour marketsHealthcare investment (productivity)
Reducing welfare (incentive to work)Public sector housing (labour mobility)

Benefits of Supply-Side Policies

  • Increase potential output without causing inflation
  • Reduce NAIRU (lower structural unemployment)
  • Improve international competitiveness
  • Can achieve growth and low inflation simultaneously (no trade-off)
  • Address root causes of economic problems

Limitations of Supply-Side Policies

  • Long time lags: Education/infrastructure take years to impact productivity
  • Uncertain outcomes: No guarantee of success
  • Opportunity cost: Government spending on supply-side has alternatives
  • Inequality: Free-market policies may increase income inequality
  • Cannot address demand-deficient unemployment: Supply-side alone insufficient in recession
  • Political difficulty: Some reforms (e.g. welfare cuts) are unpopular
Successful supply-side policies shift LRAS right, increasing potential output

Policy Conflicts and Combinations

The Policy Mix

In practice, governments use a combination of fiscal, monetary, and supply-side policies. The optimal mix depends on:

  • The nature of the economic problem (demand-side vs supply-side)
  • The current position in the economic cycle
  • The time horizon (short-run stabilisation vs long-run growth)
  • Political constraints and institutional arrangements
Exam Tip: In 25-mark essays, always discuss the policy mix rather than single policies in isolation. Explain how fiscal and monetary policy can work together (or conflict), and how supply-side policies provide long-run solutions. Use AD/AS diagrams to illustrate policy effects and always evaluate with reference to time lags, effectiveness, and side effects.

The Phillips Curve

The short-run Phillips curve suggests an inverse relationship between inflation and unemployment. Policymakers face a trade-off: reducing unemployment may require accepting higher inflation.

Long-run view (Monetarist): The long-run Phillips curve is vertical at the NAIRU. Attempts to reduce unemployment below NAIRU through demand management only cause accelerating inflation with no permanent reduction in unemployment.
