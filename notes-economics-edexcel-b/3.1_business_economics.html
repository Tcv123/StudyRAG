3.1 Business Economics

Revenue

Total Revenue (TR): The total income received by a firm from selling its output. TR = P x Q.

Average Revenue (AR): Revenue per unit sold. AR = TR / Q = Price (the demand curve is the AR curve).

Marginal Revenue (MR): The additional revenue gained from selling one more unit. MR = change in TR / change in Q.

Revenue Curves

For a price-taking firm (perfect competition), AR = MR = Price (horizontal demand curve).

For a price-making firm (monopoly/imperfect competition), the demand curve slopes downward, so MR lies below AR. MR falls twice as steeply as AR for a linear demand curve.

AR and MR curves for a price-making firm. MR has twice the gradient of AR.

Relationship Between Revenue and Elasticity

  • When |PED| > 1 (elastic): MR is positive, TR is rising
  • When |PED| = 1 (unit elastic): MR = 0, TR is at its maximum
  • When |PED| < 1 (inelastic): MR is negative, TR is falling

Costs of Production

Short-Run Costs

Fixed costs (FC): Costs that do not vary with output in the short run (e.g. rent, salaries, insurance).

Variable costs (VC): Costs that change directly with output (e.g. raw materials, energy, piece-rate wages).

Total cost (TC): FC + VC.
TC = FC + VC
AC (ATC) = TC / Q = AFC + AVC
MC = change in TC / change in Q

Short-Run Cost Curves

Short-run cost curves: MC cuts ATC and AVC at their minimum points

Key relationships:

  • MC intersects ATC at the minimum point of ATC (productive efficiency)
  • MC intersects AVC at the minimum point of AVC
  • When MC < ATC, ATC is falling; when MC > ATC, ATC is rising
  • The U-shape of cost curves is explained by the law of diminishing marginal returns

The Law of Diminishing Marginal Returns

Law of diminishing marginal returns: In the short run, as additional units of a variable factor (e.g. labour) are added to a fixed factor (e.g. capital), the marginal product of the variable factor will eventually decrease. This is a short-run concept (at least one factor is fixed).

Long-Run Costs and Economies of Scale

Long-Run Average Cost (LRAC)

In the long run, all factors are variable. The LRAC curve is an envelope of short-run average cost curves, showing the lowest average cost achievable at each output level.

Economies of scale: Reductions in long-run average cost as a firm increases its scale of production. The LRAC curve is downward-sloping in this region.

Diseconomies of scale: Increases in long-run average cost as a firm grows beyond its optimal size. The LRAC curve is upward-sloping in this region.

Minimum Efficient Scale (MES): The lowest level of output at which LRAC is minimised. Beyond this point, further expansion does not reduce average costs.

Types of Economies of Scale

Type (Internal)Explanation
TechnicalLarge firms use specialised, efficient machinery; indivisibilities in capital; container principle (doubling dimensions increases volume by 8x but surface area by 4x)
Purchasing (Bulk-buying)Buying inputs in large quantities gives bargaining power for discounts

Risk-bearingDiversification across products/markets reduces risk

External Economies of Scale

Cost reductions that benefit all firms in an industry as the industry grows:

  • Skilled labour pool develops in the region
  • Specialist suppliers and support services emerge
  • Knowledge spillovers and shared infrastructure
  • Examples: Silicon Valley (tech), City of London (finance)

Diseconomies of Scale

  • Communication problems: Larger organisations face slower, distorted communication
  • Coordination difficulties: Managing complex operations becomes harder
  • Motivation/morale: Workers feel alienated in large firms; principal-agent problem
  • Bureaucracy: Excessive layers of management slow decision-making

Profit

Normal profit: The minimum level of profit needed to keep a firm in the industry in the long run. It is an opportunity cost included in the cost curves. At normal profit, TR = TC (economic profit = 0).

Supernormal (abnormal/economic) profit: Profit above normal profit. TR > TC. This attracts new firms into the market (if barriers are low).

Subnormal (loss): Profit below normal profit. TR < TC. Firm will exit in the long run if this persists.
Profit = TR - TC
Profit per unit = AR - ATC
Supernormal profit shown as the shaded area where AR > ATC at the profit-maximising output

Business Objectives

Profit Maximisation

Profit maximisation: Occurs where MC = MR (and MC is rising). At this output, the gap between TR and TC is maximised.

This is the traditional assumption in economic theory but may not reflect real-world behaviour due to imperfect information, separation of ownership and control, and satisficing behaviour.

Revenue Maximisation

Occurs where MR = 0 (total revenue is at its maximum). Managers may pursue this to maximise sales commission or market share. Output is higher than profit-maximising level.

Sales Maximisation

Occurs where AR = ATC (normal profit constraint). The firm sells as much as possible while still covering all costs. Associated with William Baumol's theory.

Satisficing

Satisficing: Achieving a satisfactory level of profit rather than maximum profit. Reflects the principal-agent problem where managers pursue their own goals (prestige, salary, leisure) rather than shareholders' interest in profit maximisation.

Other Objectives

  • Survival: Priority for new firms or during recession
  • Market share growth: Pricing to gain dominance (even at short-term loss)
  • Social/ethical objectives: Corporate social responsibility, sustainability
  • Managerial utility maximisation: Managers maximise own benefits (Williamson)
Exam Tip: When evaluating business objectives, consider: the market structure (competitive vs monopoly), the time period (short-run survival vs long-run profit), whether ownership is separated from control (principal-agent problem), and external pressures (shareholders, stakeholders, regulators).

Price Discrimination

Price discrimination: Charging different prices to different consumers for the same product, where the price difference is not justified by cost differences. Requires: market power, ability to segment the market, and prevention of resale.

Degrees of Price Discrimination

DegreeDescriptionExample
First degree (perfect)Each consumer charged their maximum willingness to payAuctions, one-to-one negotiation
Second degreeDifferent prices for different quantities (bulk discounts)Buy-one-get-one-free, volume discounts
Third degreeDifferent prices for different market segmentsStudent/senior discounts, peak/off-peak pricing

Conditions for Price Discrimination

  • Firm must have price-setting power (not a price taker)
  • Must be able to identify and separate market segments with different PED
  • Must be able to prevent resale (arbitrage) between segments

Consequences

  • Increases producer surplus and total revenue
  • Reduces consumer surplus (transfers to producer)
  • May increase output (closer to allocative efficiency if output rises)
  • Can enable firms to cross-subsidise (e.g. off-peak rail travel)
  • May be seen as unfair to those paying higher prices
