Perfect Competition

Perfect competition assumptions: Many buyers and sellers; homogeneous products; perfect information; no barriers to entry or exit; firms are price takers; profit maximisation objective.

Short-Run Equilibrium

In the short run, a perfectly competitive firm can earn supernormal profit (if P > ATC), normal profit (P = ATC), or make a loss (P < ATC but P > AVC, so firm continues in short run).

The firm's short-run supply curve is its MC curve above the AVC curve (shutdown point).

Long-Run Equilibrium

Supernormal profits attract new firms; supply increases, price falls until only normal profit remains. Losses cause firms to exit; supply decreases, price rises until normal profit is restored.

Perfect competition long-run equilibrium: firm earns normal profit; P = MC = min ATC

Efficiency in Perfect Competition

  • Allocative efficient: P = MC (yes, in both short and long run)
  • Productive efficient: Producing at minimum ATC (yes, in long run only)
  • Dynamic efficient: No supernormal profit to fund R&D (no)

Monopoly

Pure monopoly: A single firm supplies the entire market with no close substitutes. In UK competition law, a firm has monopoly power with 25%+ market share. Monopolies exist due to high barriers to entry.

Monopoly Equilibrium

Monopoly equilibrium: MC = MR determines output (Qm), price read from AR curve. DWL = deadweight welfare loss.

Oligopoly

Oligopoly: A market dominated by a few large firms. Key features: high concentration ratio (top 3-5 firms have 60%+ market share), interdependence between firms, and high barriers to entry.

Key Characteristics

  • Interdependence: Each firm's decisions on price, output, and marketing are influenced by the expected reactions of rival firms
  • Non-price competition: Firms compete through advertising, branding, quality, loyalty schemes rather than price
  • Price rigidity: Prices tend to be stable (sticky) due to fear of price wars
  • High barriers to entry: Brand loyalty, economies of scale, high set-up costs

The Kinked Demand Curve

Explains price rigidity in oligopoly. If a firm raises its price, rivals will not follow (firm loses customers, elastic demand above current price). If a firm cuts its price, rivals will match the cut (small gain in sales, inelastic demand below current price). Result: firms have little incentive to change price.

The kinked demand curve model: price rigidity explained by asymmetric rival responses

Collusion

Collusion: When firms agree (formally or tacitly) to limit competition, typically by fixing prices, restricting output, or dividing markets.

Cartel: A formal (illegal) agreement between firms to coordinate behaviour. Example: OPEC (oil production quotas).

Tacit collusion: Firms follow the behaviour of a price leader without explicit agreement (legal grey area).

Conditions Favouring Collusion

  • Few firms in the market (easier to coordinate)
  • Similar cost structures
  • High barriers to entry
  • Homogeneous products
  • Stable demand conditions
  • Weak competition regulation

Game Theory and the Prisoner's Dilemma

Game theory analyses strategic decision-making when outcomes depend on others' actions. The prisoner's dilemma shows why firms may not collude even when cooperation would benefit both.

Firm B: High PriceFirm B: Low Price
Firm A: High PriceBoth earn 5m (collude)A earns 1m, B earns 8m
Firm A: Low PriceA earns 8m, B earns 1mBoth earn 3m (compete)

The dominant strategy for both firms is to set low prices (Nash equilibrium), even though both would be better off colluding at high prices. This is because each firm fears being undercut.

Contestable Markets

Contestable market: A market where there is free entry and exit, with zero or very low sunk costs. The threat of new entrants (hit-and-run competition) constrains the behaviour of existing firms, even if the market is concentrated.

Features of Contestability

  • Low sunk costs (can exit without significant losses)
  • No significant barriers to entry
  • Perfect knowledge of technology and market conditions
  • Incumbent firms cannot respond fast enough to deter entry

Implications

Even a monopoly in a perfectly contestable market will behave competitively: pricing near P = AC (normal profit), being productively efficient, and minimising X-inefficiency. The threat of entry acts as a competitive discipline.

Exam Tip: Contestability is a key evaluation tool. When assessing monopoly or oligopoly behaviour, always consider whether the market is contestable. If sunk costs are low and entry is easy, firms may behave competitively despite market concentration. Discuss how technology and deregulation have increased contestability in many markets (e.g. airlines, banking, ride-hailing).

Competition Policy

Competition policy: Government action to promote competitive markets and prevent the abuse of market power. In the UK, enforced by the Competition and Markets Authority (CMA).

Key Areas

  • Merger regulation: CMA investigates mergers that could reduce competition (if combined market share exceeds 25% or turnover exceeds threshold)
  • Anti-competitive practices: Prohibits cartels, price fixing, market sharing, predatory pricing
  • Market investigations: CMA can investigate markets where competition appears weak and impose remedies

Regulation of Natural Monopolies

Natural monopolies (where one firm can supply the market at lower cost than two or more) are regulated rather than broken up:

  • Price capping (RPI - X): Prices can only rise by inflation minus an efficiency factor
  • Rate of return regulation: Limits the profit rate the firm can earn
  • Quality standards: Regulators set minimum service levels
  • Ofgem, Ofwat, Ofcom: Sector-specific regulators in UK
