2.1 National Economic Performance

Macroeconomic Objectives

The UK government has four main macroeconomic objectives:

  1. Economic growth: Sustained increase in real GDP (target: around 2-3% per annum)
  2. Low and stable inflation: CPI target of 2% (+/- 1%)
  3. Low unemployment: Full employment (not zero unemployment)
  4. Balance of payments equilibrium: Sustainable current account position

Additional objectives include: equitable income distribution, balanced government budget, environmental sustainability.

Economic Growth and GDP

Gross Domestic Product (GDP): The total market value of all final goods and services produced within a country's borders in a given time period. It is the most widely used measure of national output and economic performance.

Measuring GDP

GDP can be measured using three equivalent approaches:

ApproachWhat it MeasuresMethod
Output (Production)Value added at each stage of productionSum of value added across all industries
IncomeTotal incomes earned in productionWages + Profits + Rent + Interest
ExpenditureTotal spending on final goods/servicesC + I + G + (X - M)
GDP (Expenditure) = C + I + G + (X - M)

Real vs Nominal GDP

  • Nominal GDP: GDP measured at current prices (not adjusted for inflation)
  • Real GDP: GDP measured at constant prices (adjusted for inflation using a base year)
  • GDP per capita: Real GDP divided by population; better for comparing living standards

The Economic (Business/Trade) Cycle

The economic cycle showing boom, slowdown, recession, and recovery phases relative to trend growth
PhaseCharacteristics
BoomHigh GDP growth, low unemployment, rising inflation, positive output gap, high consumer/business confidence
SlowdownGrowth rate declines, inflation may persist, confidence begins to fall
RecessionTwo consecutive quarters of negative GDP growth; rising unemployment, falling demand, negative output gap
RecoveryGDP growth returns positive; unemployment begins to fall, confidence improves
Output gap: The difference between actual GDP and potential GDP (trend output). A positive output gap means the economy is operating above capacity (inflationary pressure). A negative output gap means spare capacity exists (deflationary pressure/unemployment).

Limitations of GDP as a Measure of Living Standards

  • Does not account for income distribution (inequality)
  • Ignores the informal/shadow economy and unpaid work (e.g. housework, volunteering)
  • Does not measure quality of life, leisure time, or well-being
  • Environmental degradation may increase GDP (clean-up costs) while reducing welfare
  • Does not consider composition of output (military vs healthcare spending)
  • PPP adjustments needed for international comparisons
  • Population size differences (GDP per capita is better)

Inflation

Inflation: A sustained increase in the general price level over time, resulting in a fall in the purchasing power of money. Measured by CPI (Consumer Prices Index) in the UK with a target of 2%.

Measuring Inflation

  • CPI (Consumer Prices Index): Measures price changes of a basket of 700+ goods and services weighted by consumer spending patterns. Used for the Bank of England target.
  • CPIH: Includes owner-occupier housing costs (considered more comprehensive)
  • RPI (Retail Prices Index): Older measure; includes mortgage interest payments; tends to be higher than CPI

Causes of Inflation

TypeCauseExamples
Demand-pullAD increases faster than AS, pulling up pricesConsumer spending boom, excessive money supply growth, falling exchange rate boosting exports
Cost-pushRising costs of production push up pricesOil price rises, higher wages, raw material costs, depreciation increasing import prices

Consequences of Inflation

  • Redistribution of income: Harms savers and those on fixed incomes; benefits borrowers
  • Reduced international competitiveness: If inflation exceeds trading partners'
  • Menu costs: Costs of updating prices (labels, menus, catalogues)
  • Shoe-leather costs: Time and effort shopping around for best prices
  • Business uncertainty: Reduces investment if firms cannot predict future costs
  • Wage-price spiral: Workers demand higher wages to keep up with prices, raising costs further
  • Fiscal drag: Nominal incomes pushed into higher tax brackets

Deflation

Deflation: A sustained fall in the general price level (negative inflation). Can be caused by falling AD (harmful) or improving technology/productivity (benign).

Harmful deflation risks: Consumers delay purchases (expecting further falls); real debt burdens increase; profit margins squeezed; deflationary spiral with rising unemployment.

Unemployment

Unemployment: People of working age who are actively seeking work but unable to find a job. Measured by the ILO Labour Force Survey (internationally comparable) or the Claimant Count (those claiming unemployment benefits).

Types of Unemployment

TypeCauseDuration
FrictionalPeople between jobs; searching for best matchShort-term
StructuralMismatch of skills/location; occupational/geographical immobilityLong-term
Cyclical (demand-deficient)Insufficient AD during recessionMedium-term
SeasonalRegular fluctuations in demand (e.g. tourism, agriculture)Predictable
Real wage (classical)Wages held above equilibrium (e.g. minimum wage, unions)Persistent
TechnologicalAutomation replacing human labourCan be long-term

Consequences of Unemployment

  • Individual: Loss of income, lower living standards, stress/mental health issues, de-skilling
  • Government: Higher benefit spending, lower tax revenue, increased budget deficit
  • Economy: Lost output (negative output gap), hysteresis effects, reduced growth
  • Society: Increased crime, social exclusion, regional disparities, inequality
Natural rate of unemployment (NAIRU): The rate of unemployment when the labour market is in equilibrium; consists of frictional and structural unemployment. The economy is at full employment when cyclical unemployment is zero (actual unemployment = NAIRU).

Balance of Payments

Balance of payments: A record of all financial transactions between a country and the rest of the world over a given time period. It always balances overall (current account + capital account + financial account = 0).

Components of the Current Account

ComponentDescription
Trade in goodsExports and imports of physical products
Trade in servicesExports and imports of services (finance, tourism, consulting)
Primary incomeInvestment income (dividends, interest) and employee compensation
Secondary income (transfers)Government transfers (e.g. foreign aid, EU contributions), remittances

Current Account Deficit

When total debits (imports, outflows) exceed total credits (exports, inflows). The UK has run a persistent current account deficit.

Causes: Strong consumer demand for imports; deindustrialisation; high exchange rate reducing export competitiveness; relative inflation; low productivity.

Significance: May indicate uncompetitiveness; requires financing through capital inflows (foreign investment/borrowing); may cause currency depreciation; could indicate living beyond means.

Exam Tip: A current account deficit is not necessarily a problem. It may be financed by FDI (beneficial), reflect strong consumer confidence, or be a short-term position. Always evaluate the sustainability, size, and cause of the deficit.

Human Development Index (HDI)

HDI: A composite measure of development published by the UN, combining three dimensions: health (life expectancy at birth), education (mean/expected years of schooling), and standard of living (GNI per capita at PPP). Scored 0-1.
The three dimensions of the Human Development Index

Advantages of HDI over GDP

  • Measures broader aspects of development beyond income
  • Captures health and education outcomes
  • Allows comparison across countries on a standardised scale
  • Highlights cases where high GDP does not translate to development

Limitations of HDI

  • Does not capture inequality within countries
  • Ignores political freedom, environment, and security
  • Data may be unreliable for some developing nations
  • Only three indicators; misses other dimensions of well-being
  • National averages can hide significant regional variation

Conflicts Between Macroeconomic Objectives

ConflictExplanation
Growth vs InflationRapid growth can cause demand-pull inflation (Phillips curve short-run)
Growth vs Current AccountGrowth increases import spending (higher incomes), worsening the deficit
Growth vs EnvironmentHigher output increases pollution and resource depletion
Low Inflation vs Low UnemploymentShort-run Phillips curve trade-off; reducing unemployment may raise inflation
Growth vs EqualityGrowth may disproportionately benefit the wealthy initially
Exam Tip: When discussing conflicts, always evaluate whether the trade-off exists in both the short run and long run. Many conflicts (e.g. Phillips curve) may only hold in the short run; in the long run, supply-side improvements can reduce both inflation and unemployment simultaneously.
