4.1 International Economics and Globalisation

Globalisation

Globalisation: The increasing integration and interdependence of world economies through the growth of international trade, capital flows, migration, and the spread of technology and culture.

Causes of Globalisation

  • Trade liberalisation: Reduction of tariffs and trade barriers through WTO agreements and free trade agreements
  • Technological advancement: Internet, containerisation, cheaper transport (reduced costs of trade)
  • Financial deregulation: Removal of capital controls enabling free flow of investment
  • Growth of MNCs: Multinational corporations operating across borders
  • Political changes: Fall of communism, opening of China and India
  • Migration: Movement of labour across borders

Consequences of Globalisation

Comparative Advantage and the Basis for Trade

Absolute advantage: When a country can produce a good using fewer resources than another country.

Comparative advantage: When a country can produce a good at a lower opportunity cost than another country. Even if one country has absolute advantage in all goods, both countries benefit from specialising in the good where they have comparative advantage and trading.

Numerical Example

Wheat (units/worker)Cloth (units/worker)
Country A105
Country B44

Country A has absolute advantage in both goods. But opportunity cost of 1 wheat: A = 0.5 cloth, B = 1 cloth. Country A has comparative advantage in wheat (lower opportunity cost). Country B has comparative advantage in cloth (opportunity cost of 1 cloth = 1 wheat in B, vs 2 wheat in A). Both benefit if A specialises in wheat and B in cloth.

Assumptions and Limitations of Comparative Advantage

  • Assumes constant costs of production (no economies of scale)
  • Assumes no transport costs
  • Assumes perfect factor mobility within countries but immobility between
  • Assumes free trade with no barriers
  • Ignores quality differences and exchange rate effects
  • Static model: does not account for changing comparative advantage over time
  • Real world: countries may not specialise fully; strategic trade policy exists

Terms of Trade

Terms of trade: The ratio of export prices to import prices, expressed as an index. An improvement (rise) means a country can buy more imports per unit of exports.
Terms of Trade Index = (Index of Export Prices / Index of Import Prices) x 100

Free Trade vs Protectionism

Arguments for Free Trade

  • Exploits comparative advantage, increasing global output and efficiency
  • Greater consumer choice and lower prices through competition
  • Economies of scale from access to larger markets
  • Promotes innovation through competitive pressure
  • Encourages FDI and technology transfer

Types of Protectionism

MethodDescriptionEffects
TariffTax on imported goodsRaises import price, increases domestic production, reduces imports, raises government revenue
QuotaPhysical limit on quantity of importsRestricts supply, raises price, no government revenue (rents go to importers)
Subsidy (to domestic firms)Payment to domestic producersLowers domestic costs, increases domestic output, reduces imports, cost to taxpayer
Non-tariff barriersRegulations, standards, red tapeIncreases costs for foreign firms, can be disguised protectionism
EmbargoComplete ban on imports from a countryEliminates trade with target country, used for political reasons
Effect of a tariff: domestic production rises (Q1 to Q2), imports fall (Q2-Q3 vs Q1-Q4), two deadweight loss triangles created

Arguments for Protectionism

  • Infant industry: Protect new industries until they achieve economies of scale
  • Protect strategic industries: Defence, food security, key infrastructure
  • Anti-dumping: Prevent foreign firms selling below cost to destroy competitors
  • Protect employment: Prevent structural unemployment from import competition
  • Correct trade deficit: Reduce imports to improve current account
  • Prevent race to the bottom: Protect environmental and labour standards
  • Retaliation: Respond to unfair trade practices by partners

Arguments Against Protectionism

  • Reduces consumer welfare (higher prices, less choice)
  • Misallocation of resources (away from comparative advantage)
  • Reduces competitive pressure, leading to inefficiency
  • Infant industries may never grow up (perpetual protection)
  • Invites retaliation, risking trade wars
  • Deadweight welfare loss

Exchange Rates

Exchange rate: The price of one currency expressed in terms of another. It is determined in the foreign exchange (forex) market by the demand and supply of currencies.

Exchange Rate Systems

SystemDescriptionExamples
FloatingDetermined solely by market forces of supply and demandUK (GBP), US (USD), Euro
FixedGovernment/central bank pegs currency at set rate, intervenes to maintain itHong Kong dollar (pegged to USD)
Managed floatPrimarily market-determined but central bank intervenes occasionallyMost currencies in practice
Increase in demand for GBP causes appreciation (e1 to e2)

Factors Affecting Exchange Rates (Floating)

  • Interest rates: Higher rates attract hot money (capital inflows), increasing demand for currency (appreciation)
  • Inflation: Higher inflation reduces competitiveness, reducing demand for exports and currency (depreciation)
  • Trade balance: Trade surplus increases demand for currency (appreciation)
  • Speculation: Expectations of future movements cause self-fulfilling changes
  • FDI flows: Inward FDI increases demand for currency
  • Quantitative easing: Increases money supply, may weaken currency
  • Political stability: Uncertainty causes capital flight (depreciation)

Effects of Exchange Rate Changes

Appreciation (stronger currency)Depreciation (weaker currency)
ExportsMore expensive abroad (less competitive)Cheaper abroad (more competitive)
ImportsCheaper (lower cost of imported goods)More expensive (higher import costs)
InflationDownward pressure (cheaper imports)Upward pressure (import-price inflation)
Current accountTends to worsen (if Marshall-Lerner holds)Tends to improve (if Marshall-Lerner holds)
GrowthMay slow (reduced net exports)May boost (increased net exports)

The Marshall-Lerner Condition and J-Curve

Marshall-Lerner condition: A depreciation will improve the current account only if the sum of PED for exports and PED for imports is greater than 1 (|PEDx| + |PEDm| > 1).

J-curve effect: In the short run, a depreciation may worsen the current account because import prices rise before volumes adjust (contracts locked in). Over time, as demand adjusts, the current account improves, tracing a J-shaped path.

World Trade Organisation (WTO)

WTO: International organisation that promotes and regulates global free trade. Established 1995, succeeding GATT. Based on principles of non-discrimination (most-favoured nation), reciprocity, and transparency.

Functions

  • Negotiates trade liberalisation rounds
  • Settles trade disputes between member nations
  • Monitors national trade policies
  • Provides technical assistance to developing countries

Criticisms of WTO

  • Slow progress in negotiations (Doha Round stalled)
  • Powerful countries dominate; developing countries underrepresented
  • Cannot enforce rulings effectively against large economies
  • May undermine environmental and labour standards

Trading Blocs

Trading bloc: A group of countries that have agreed to reduce or eliminate trade barriers between members, while potentially maintaining common barriers against non-members.
TypeFeaturesExample
Free Trade AreaNo tariffs between members; each sets own external tariffsUSMCA, EFTA
Customs UnionFree trade between members + Common External Tariff (CET)EU Customs Union
Single/Common MarketCustoms union + free movement of labour, capital, servicesEU Single Market
Economic/Monetary UnionSingle market + common currency and harmonised economic policiesEurozone

Trade Creation and Trade Diversion

Trade creation: When joining a bloc leads to switching from a high-cost domestic producer to a lower-cost member producer (welfare-improving).

Trade diversion: When joining a bloc leads to switching from a low-cost non-member producer to a higher-cost member producer due to the CET (welfare-reducing).
Exam Tip: When evaluating trading blocs, always discuss both trade creation (benefits) and trade diversion (costs). The net welfare effect depends on which is larger. Also consider dynamic effects: increased competition, FDI attraction, and political integration.
