⚗ 1: Volumetric Analysis / Titration

Apparatus

  • 50 cm³ burette, clamp and stand
  • 25.0 cm³ pipette and filler
  • Conical flasks (250 cm³), white tile
  • Suitable indicator or self-indicating reagent
  • Volumetric flask (for preparing standard solutions)

Method

  1. Acid-base: Pipette 25.0 cm³ of one solution into a conical flask. Add indicator. Titrate from the burette until permanent colour change. Repeat for concordant results (within 0.10 cm³).
  2. Back titration: React the sample with known excess of reagent. Titrate the remaining unreacted reagent. Calculate original amount by difference. Useful when the analyte is insoluble (e.g., CaCO₃ in antacid tablets).
  3. Redox (KMnO₄): Pipette acidified Fe²⁺ into flask. Titrate with KMnO₄ until permanent faint pink (self-indicating). Use H₂SO₄ to acidify (not HCl or HNO₃).
  4. Calculate using n = cV and stoichiometric ratios from balanced equations.

Safety

  • Acids and alkalis — corrosive/irritant, goggles.
  • KMnO₄ — oxidising agent, stains; wear gloves.
  • Use pipette filler — never mouth-pipette.

Analysis

Calculate: Mean concordant titre. n(known) = c × V. Use stoichiometry for n(unknown). c = n/V.

Back titration: n(original) = n(added) - n(remaining after back-titration).

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Overshooting end pointSystematicAdd drop-wise near end point
Parallax reading buretteRandomEye level; bottom of meniscus (top for KMnO₄)
Not rinsing burette correctlySystematicRinse with solution to be used
Including rough titre in meanSystematicOnly average concordant results; exclude rough
Exam Tip: For back titrations, always set up the calculation as: moles reacted with analyte = total moles added − moles determined by titration. Common example: finding % CaCO₃ in limestone — add excess HCl, then titrate remaining HCl with NaOH.
Worked Example — Back Titration:

1.50 g of impure CaCO₃ was dissolved in 50.0 cm³ of 1.00 mol dm⁻³ HCl (excess). The remaining HCl required 24.8 cm³ of 0.500 mol dm⁻³ NaOH to neutralise it.

n(HCl total) = 1.00 × 0.0500 = 0.0500 mol
n(NaOH) = 0.500 × 0.0248 = 0.0124 mol = n(HCl remaining)
n(HCl reacted with CaCO₃) = 0.0500 − 0.0124 = 0.0376 mol
n(CaCO₃) = 0.0376 / 2 = 0.0188 mol (from CaCO₃ + 2HCl equation)
Mass CaCO₃ = 0.0188 × 100.1 = 1.88 g... but we only had 1.50 g. Check: the CaCO₃ is impure, so the actual pure CaCO₃ mass = 0.0188 × 100.1 = 1.88 g — this exceeds the sample mass, indicating an error. Recalculating with correct titre would give a realistic % purity.

⚗ 2: Enthalpy Determination by Calorimetry

Apparatus

  • Neutralisation: Polystyrene cup, lid, thermometer, measuring cylinder, stopwatch
  • Combustion: Spirit burner, copper calorimeter, thermometer, balance, draught shield, measuring cylinder

Method (Neutralisation)

  1. Measure 25 cm³ of acid into the polystyrene cup. Record temperature for 3 minutes.
  2. At minute 4, add 25 cm³ of alkali. Stir. Record temperature for 6+ minutes.
  3. Use graphical extrapolation to find ΔT. Calculate q = mcΔT. Then ΔH = -q/n.

Method (Combustion)

  1. Measure 100 cm³ of water into the calorimeter. Record initial temperature.
  2. Weigh the spirit burner. Light it and heat the water until temperature rises ~20 °C.
  3. Extinguish, reweigh. Calculate mass burned, q = mcΔT, n = mass/Mᵣ. ΔH = -q/n.

Safety

  • Acids/alkalis — corrosive, goggles.
  • Alcohols are flammable — keep away from other ignition sources.
  • Hot apparatus — use tongs.

Analysis

Plot: Temperature vs. time for extrapolation (neutralisation).

Compare: Experimental vs. literature ΔH values. Discuss reasons for discrepancy.

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Heat loss to surroundingsSystematicLid, insulation, draught shield, extrapolation
Incomplete combustionSystematicEnsure good air supply; clean wick
Evaporation of alcoholSystematicCap promptly; weigh quickly
Heat capacity assumptionsSystematicAcknowledged; dilute solution ≈ water
Exam Tip: The experimental ΔH(combustion) is always less negative (less exothermic) than the data book value. The main reason is heat loss to surroundings. Other factors include incomplete combustion and heat absorbed by the apparatus. Always state these clearly for full marks.

⚗ 3: Rates of Reaction

Apparatus

  • Continuous: Conical flask, gas syringe, delivery tube, stopwatch, measuring cylinder
  • Initial rates: Beakers/flasks, stopwatch, measuring cylinders, iodine clock reagents + starch

Method

  1. Continuous monitoring: Collect gas at timed intervals from a reaction (e.g., CaCO₃ + HCl). Plot volume vs. time. Initial rate = gradient at t = 0.
  2. Initial rates (iodine clock): Vary [one reactant], keep total volume constant. Time the colour change. Rate = 1/t. Plot rate vs. [varied species] to determine order.
  3. Repeat with temperature variation to investigate Arrhenius relationship.

Safety

  • HCl is irritant — goggles.
  • SO₂ from thiosulfate reactions — ventilation.

Analysis

Continuous: Tangent at t = 0 gives initial rate. Compare different conditions.

Initial rates: Rate vs. [X]: straight line through origin = 1st order; parabola = 2nd order.

Arrhenius: ln(rate) vs. 1/T gives straight line; gradient = -Eₐ/R.

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Gas lost before sealingSystematicSeal quickly
Subjective colour changeRandomSame observer; colorimeter
Temperature variationSystematicThermostatted water bath
Volume measurement errorRandomUse burettes for better precision
Exam Tip: To determine order from initial rate data: if doubling [X] doubles rate, the reaction is first order in X. If doubling [X] quadruples rate, it is second order. Zero order means the rate is unaffected by changing [X]. The overall order is the sum of individual orders.

⚗ 4: Qualitative Analysis

Apparatus

  • Test tubes, nichrome wire, Bunsen burner
  • NaOH(aq), NH₃(aq), AgNO₃(aq), BaCl₂(aq), dilute HNO₃, HCl
  • Limewater, litmus paper

Method

  1. Flame tests: Clean wire → dip in sample → observe colour in Bunsen flame.
  2. Cation tests: Add NaOH — observe precipitate colour and behaviour in excess.
  3. Halide tests: Acidify with HNO₃, add AgNO₃. Confirm with NH₃: AgCl dissolves in dilute NH₃, AgBr in concentrated NH₃, AgI does not dissolve.
  4. Sulfate: Acidify with HNO₃, add BaCl₂ → white precipitate.
  5. Carbonate: Add HCl → effervescence, gas turns limewater milky.
  6. Ammonium: Add NaOH, warm → NH₃ turns damp red litmus blue.

Safety

  • AgNO₃ stains — gloves. BaCl₂ toxic — wash hands.
  • Conc. HCl and NH₃ — fume cupboard.

Analysis

Record: Observations table for each test. Match results to identify unknown ions.

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
ContaminationSystematicClean all equipment; use separate droppers
Na⁺ masking flame coloursSystematicView through cobalt blue glass
Not acidifying before testsSystematicAlways acidify with HNO₃
Colour confusion (cream vs white)RandomCompare alongside known standards
Exam Tip: To distinguish between halide silver precipitates further: add dilute NH₃ — AgCl dissolves (soluble complex forms); add conc. NH₃ — AgBr dissolves. AgI does not dissolve in either. This is a common extension question.

⚗ 5: Organic Preparation

Apparatus

  • Round-bottomed flask, anti-bumping granules
  • Reflux condenser (vertical) and distillation condenser (angled)
  • Separating funnel, conical flask
  • Drying agent (anhydrous MgSO₄ or Na₂SO₄)
  • Buchner funnel + vacuum pump (for solids)
  • Melting point apparatus, thermometer

Method

  1. Reaction: Heat reaction mixture under reflux for the required time.
  2. Separation (liquids): Distil to collect crude product. Wash in separating funnel with Na₂CO₃ (removes acid) then water. Dry with anhydrous drying agent. Filter. Redistil at correct boiling point.
  3. Purification (solids): Dissolve crude solid in minimum hot solvent. Hot filter. Cool to crystallise. Buchner filter. Wash with cold solvent. Dry.
  4. Test purity: Melting point (solids — sharp mp = pure) or boiling point (liquids).
  5. Calculate percentage yield.

Safety

  • Organic chemicals are flammable — electric heating, no flames.
  • Work in fume cupboard.
  • Vent separating funnel to release pressure.

Analysis

Calculate: % yield = (actual/theoretical) × 100. % atom economy = (Mᵣ desired product / total Mᵣ all products) × 100.

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Product lost in transfersSystematicMinimise transfers; rinse with small amounts
Incomplete reactionSystematicReflux longer; use excess reagent
Product still wetSystematicDry thoroughly; use desiccator
Wrong fraction collectedSystematicMonitor thermometer; collect narrow range
Exam Tip: Always explain the purpose of each step: reflux = complete reaction; distillation = separate by boiling point; washing with Na₂CO₃ = removes acidic impurities; drying agent = removes water; recrystallisation = removes soluble impurities from solid. Each step reduces yield, so high yields require careful technique.

⚗ 6: Functional Group Identification Tests

Apparatus

  • Test tubes, water bath (60-70 °C)
  • Bromine water, Tollens' reagent, Fehling's A+B, 2,4-DNP solution
  • Acidified K₂Cr₂O₇, Na₂CO₃ solution, I₂/NaOH

Method

  1. C=C: Add Br₂(aq) — decolourises = alkene.
  2. CHO (aldehyde): Tollens' (silver mirror) or Fehling's (brick-red precipitate).
  3. C=O (carbonyl): 2,4-DNP — orange precipitate = aldehyde or ketone.
  4. OH (alcohol 1°/2°): Warm with acidified K₂Cr₂O₇ — orange to green.
  5. COOH: Add Na₂CO₃ — effervescence = carboxylic acid.
  6. CH₃CO- (methyl ketone): Iodoform test — yellow precipitate.

Safety

  • K₂Cr₂O₇ is carcinogenic — gloves, fume cupboard.
  • Tollens' — prepare fresh, dispose immediately (explosive on standing).
  • Bromine water is toxic and corrosive — fume cupboard.

Analysis

Record: Table of positive/negative results. Use the pattern to identify functional groups.

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Dirty glasswareSystematicClean thoroughly; Tollens' needs very clean tubes
Insufficient heatingSystematicUse water bath at correct temperature
Using old Tollens' reagentSystematicPrepare fresh immediately before use
False positives from contaminationSystematicRun blank tests; use fresh reagents
Exam Tip: To distinguish aldehyde from ketone: both give orange ppt with 2,4-DNP, but only aldehydes give a positive Tollens'/Fehling's. Tertiary alcohols do NOT react with K₂Cr₂O₇ (no colour change) because they cannot be oxidised without breaking C-C bonds.

⚗ 7: Electrochemical Cells and EMF Measurement

Apparatus

  • Beakers, metal electrodes (Zn, Cu, Fe, Mg, etc.)
  • 1.0 mol dm⁻³ metal salt solutions
  • Salt bridge (KNO₃ soaked filter paper or agar gel)
  • High-resistance voltmeter, wires, crocodile clips
  • Emery paper

Method

  1. Clean electrodes with emery paper to remove oxide.
  2. Place each electrode in its corresponding 1.0 mol dm⁻³ metal ion solution.
  3. Connect the two half-cells with a salt bridge.
  4. Connect electrodes to the high-resistance voltmeter.
  5. Record the EMF and note the polarity (which is +, which is -).
  6. Test multiple metal combinations and compare with E° values.

Safety

  • Metal salt solutions may be toxic — gloves and goggles.
  • Dispose of solutions correctly.

Analysis

Calculate: E°(cell) = E°(cathode) - E°(anode). Compare measured with theoretical.

Investigate: Effect of changing concentration on EMF (qualitative Nernst).

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Oxide layer on electrodesSystematicClean with emery paper before use
Non-standard concentrationsSystematicUse volumetric flask to prepare
Salt bridge drying outSystematicFresh salt bridge; good immersion
Voltmeter not high enough resistanceSystematicUse high-resistance voltmeter or potentiometer
Exam Tip: The more reactive metal is the anode (negative terminal, oxidation). The less reactive is the cathode (positive terminal, reduction). A high-resistance voltmeter is essential because if current flows, the cell does work and the measured EMF is less than the true EMF. Remember: AN OX (anode = oxidation), RED CAT (reduction = cathode).

⚗ 8: pH Curves and Buffer Solutions

Apparatus

  • Calibrated pH meter/probe
  • Burette with NaOH (or HCl)
  • Beaker with acid (or base)
  • Magnetic stirrer
  • Buffer solutions for calibration

Method

  1. Calibrate pH meter with standard buffers.
  2. Pipette 25.0 cm³ of acid into beaker. Record initial pH.
  3. Add base from burette in small increments. Record pH after each addition.
  4. Use 0.1-0.2 cm³ increments near the steep equivalence region.
  5. Continue well past equivalence until pH plateaus.
  6. Plot pH vs. volume to produce the titration curve.
  7. Buffer investigation: Prepare buffer (HA + NaA). Add small amounts of HCl and NaOH. Show pH barely changes compared to water.

Safety

  • NaOH — corrosive, goggles and gloves.
  • pH probes — fragile, handle carefully.

Analysis

Identify: Equivalence point, half-equivalence point (pH = pKₐ for WA/SB), buffer region.

Select indicator: Must have colour change within the steep region of the curve.

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Uncalibrated pH meterSystematicCalibrate with 2-3 buffers
Too-large increments near equivalenceSystematicUse 0.1 cm³ additions
pH not stabilisedRandomWait for stable reading; stir continuously
CO₂ absorptionSystematicFresh NaOH; keep stoppered
Exam Tip: For weak acid + strong base: initial pH > 1 (partially dissociated), buffer region before equivalence, equivalence pH > 7, half-equivalence pH = pKₐ. The buffer region exists because both HA and A⁻ are present in significant quantities. Use phenolphthalein for this combination.

⚗ 9: Colorimetry and Transition Metal Complexes

Apparatus

  • Colorimeter with selectable filters, cuvettes
  • Standard solutions of known concentration (e.g., CuSO₄)
  • Volumetric flasks for dilution
  • Unknown concentration solution

Method

  1. Select the appropriate colour filter (complementary to the solution colour).
  2. Zero the colorimeter using distilled water as a blank.
  3. Prepare at least 5 standard solutions of known concentration.
  4. Measure the absorbance of each standard and the unknown.
  5. Plot a calibration curve: absorbance (y) vs. concentration (x).
  6. Read the unknown concentration from the graph using its absorbance value.

Safety

  • Transition metal salts — wear gloves.
  • Handle cuvettes by the top edges only.

Analysis

Plot: Calibration curve (should be linear at low concentrations per Beer-Lambert Law).

Read: Unknown concentration by interpolation (never extrapolation).

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Fingerprints/scratches on cuvetteSystematicHandle by top; clean with lens tissue; same orientation
Wrong filterSystematicUse complementary colour; determine λmax experimentally
Bubbles in solutionRandomDegas solutions; tap to remove bubbles
Standards incorrectly preparedSystematicUse volumetric flasks and pipettes
Exam Tip: The calibration curve must pass through the origin (zero concentration = zero absorbance after blanking). Only use values within the range of the calibration standards (interpolation, not extrapolation). If the curve is not linear at high concentrations, the Beer-Lambert Law breaks down — use only the linear portion.

⚗ 10: Chromatography

Apparatus

  • TLC plates (silica or alumina) or chromatography paper
  • Developing chamber (beaker with watch glass/lid)
  • Capillary tubes, pencil, ruler
  • Suitable solvent(s)
  • UV lamp or ninhydrin/iodine for visualisation

Method

  1. Draw a pencil origin line 1 cm from the bottom of the plate/paper.
  2. Spot samples (and known references) onto the origin using capillary tubes.
  3. Pour solvent into the chamber to 0.5 cm depth (below origin). Allow to saturate.
  4. Place the plate/paper in the chamber. Cover. Let solvent rise.
  5. Remove when solvent front is near the top. Mark the solvent front immediately.
  6. Visualise spots (UV, iodine, or ninhydrin for amino acids).
  7. Measure distances and calculate Rf values.

Safety

  • Solvents are flammable — no flames, fume cupboard.
  • UV lamp — avoid direct exposure to eyes.
  • Ninhydrin — irritant, wear gloves.

Analysis

Calculate: Rf = distance by spot / distance by solvent. Compare with reference standards.

TLC: Adsorption chromatography — polar substances have lower Rf (interact more with polar stationary phase).

Paper: Partition chromatography — substances partition between water in paper and mobile solvent.

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Spots too large / overloadedSystematicSmall concentrated spots; dry between applications
Origin below solvent levelSystematicCheck depth carefully
Chamber not saturatedSystematicLine with filter paper; wait before inserting plate
Solvent front not marked in timeRandomMark immediately on removal
Exam Tip: TLC uses adsorption (interaction with solid stationary phase). Paper chromatography uses partition (between two liquids). In TLC, more polar compounds have lower Rf because they adsorb more strongly to the polar silica. Rf values are only comparable under identical conditions.

⚗ 11: Equilibrium Investigation (Le Chatelier's)

Apparatus

  • Test tubes, test tube rack
  • CoCl₂ solution (equilibrium mixture)
  • Concentrated HCl, distilled water
  • Water baths (ice and hot water)
  • Teat pipettes

Method

  1. Prepare cobalt chloride equilibrium mixture (purple at room temperature).
  2. Divide into several test tubes.
  3. Add HCl: Observe shift to blue (increased [Cl⁻] shifts equilibrium right).
  4. Add water: Observe shift to pink (dilutes Cl⁻, shifts equilibrium left).
  5. Cool in ice: Observe shift to pink (exothermic direction favoured).
  6. Heat in hot water: Observe shift to blue (endothermic direction favoured).
  7. Record all observations and explain using Le Chatelier's principle.

Safety

  • CoCl₂ is toxic and a suspected carcinogen — gloves.
  • Conc. HCl is corrosive — fume cupboard.
  • Hot water — care with burns.

Analysis

Record: Table of condition changed, direction of shift, colour observed, explanation.

Key: Forward reaction is endothermic. Heating shifts right (endothermic favoured). Adding Cl⁻ shifts right. Adding H₂O shifts left.

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Subjective colour judgementRandomUse colorimeter for quantitative data
Temperature not maintainedSystematicMonitor with thermometer; maintain bath
Equilibrium not re-establishedSystematicAllow sufficient time
Uncontrolled amounts addedSystematicMeasure volumes with pipette
Exam Tip: A catalyst does NOT shift the position of equilibrium and does NOT change Kc. It only increases the rate at which equilibrium is reached. In exam questions, always state that Le Chatelier's principle predicts the system opposes the change imposed. Only temperature changes Kc; concentration and pressure changes only shift the position.

⚗ 12: Moles and Gas Volume Determination

Apparatus

  • Conical flask with bung and delivery tube
  • Gas syringe (100 cm³)
  • Balance (to 0.001 g)
  • Measuring cylinder
  • Magnesium ribbon (weighed accurately)
  • Excess dilute HCl
  • Thermometer and barometer (to record conditions)

Method

  1. Weigh a known length of clean Mg ribbon accurately (e.g., ~0.03 g).
  2. Place excess dilute HCl (e.g., 20 cm³ of 1 mol dm⁻³) in the conical flask.
  3. Connect the delivery tube to the gas syringe. Check there are no leaks.
  4. Add the Mg ribbon to the flask, quickly insert the bung, and allow the reaction to proceed.
  5. Wait until the reaction is complete (Mg fully dissolved, no more gas produced).
  6. Record the final volume of H₂ gas in the syringe.
  7. Record room temperature and atmospheric pressure.
  8. Calculate: n(Mg) = mass/24.3. n(H₂) = n(Mg). Molar gas volume = V(gas)/n(H₂).
  9. Alternatively, use pV = nRT to calculate n from p, V, and T, then find Mᵣ.

Safety

  • H₂ gas is flammable — no naked flames.
  • Dilute HCl is irritant — goggles.
  • Gas syringe may pop out — clamp securely.

Analysis

Calculate: Molar gas volume at room conditions. Expected ~24 dm³ mol⁻¹ (24,000 cm³ mol⁻¹) at ~298 K, 101 kPa.

Alternative: Use pV = nRT to find n, then compare with calculated n from mass.

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
H₂ gas escaping before sealingSystematicSeal quickly; use an inverted flask method
Gas syringe frictionSystematicLubricate; tap gently
Mg oxide layer on ribbonSystematicClean with emery paper before weighing
Gas not at room temperatureSystematicAllow time for gas to cool before reading
H₂ slightly soluble in water/acidSystematicNegligible at normal conditions; acknowledged
Exam Tip: The molar gas volume at RTP (room temperature and pressure, ~298 K, 100 kPa) is approximately 24.0 dm³ mol⁻¹. At STP (273 K, 100 kPa) it is 22.7 dm³ mol⁻¹. Always state the conditions. Use pV = nRT when exact conditions matter: p in Pa, V in m³, T in K, R = 8.314.
Worked Example — Water of Crystallisation:

Mass of crucible = 22.50 g. Mass of crucible + hydrated salt = 27.48 g. Mass after heating to constant mass = 25.28 g.

Mass of water lost = 27.48 − 25.28 = 2.20 g → n(H₂O) = 2.20 / 18.0 = 0.122 mol
Mass of anhydrous salt = 25.28 − 22.50 = 2.78 g → n(MgSO₄) = 2.78 / 120.4 = 0.0231 mol
Ratio = 0.122 / 0.0231 = 5.28 ≈ 5, so formula is MgSO₄·5H₂O (but the accepted value is 7 — incomplete dehydration or experimental error may account for this discrepancy).
