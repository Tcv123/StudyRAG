Topic 9 — Thermodynamics

9.1 Internal Energy

Internal energy is the sum of the randomly distributed kinetic energies and potential energies of all the molecules in a system. It is not the same as temperature — internal energy depends on both temperature and the state of the substance.

The kinetic energy component is associated with the random translational, rotational, and vibrational motion of molecules. It depends on temperature — higher temperature means greater average kinetic energy.

The potential energy component is associated with the intermolecular forces (bonds) between molecules. It depends on the separation of molecules and therefore on the state of matter.

When you heat a substance:

  • If temperature rises: internal energy increases due to increased kinetic energy of molecules
  • If a change of state occurs (at constant temperature): internal energy increases due to increased potential energy as molecules move further apart (bonds breaking), but the kinetic energy remains constant
A common exam question asks why temperature stays constant during a change of state. The answer: all energy input goes into breaking intermolecular bonds (increasing potential energy) rather than increasing kinetic energy. Since temperature is related to the average kinetic energy, it does not change.

9.2 Specific Heat Capacity

Specific heat capacity (c) is the energy required to raise the temperature of 1 kg of a substance by 1 K (or 1°C), without a change of state.
Unit: J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹
Q = mcΔθ

where Q = energy transferred (J), m = mass (kg), c = specific heat capacity (J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹), Δθ = temperature change (K or °C).

A 2.0 kg aluminium block at 20°C is heated with a 50 W heater for 5 minutes. Calculate the final temperature, assuming no heat loss. (c for aluminium = 900 J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹)

Energy supplied: Q = Pt = 50 × (5 × 60) = 15 000 J
Q = mcΔθ, so Δθ = Q / (mc) = 15 000 / (2.0 × 900) = 8.3 K
Final temperature = 20 + 8.3 = 28.3°C

In practice, the actual temperature rise would be less because some heat is lost to the surroundings.

Experimental Determination

A typical experiment uses an electrical heater immersed in a block or liquid:

  • Measure mass of substance (m) using a balance
  • Record initial temperature (θ₁) using a thermometer
  • Heat with known power P for time t, so energy supplied = Pt = VIt
  • Record final temperature (θ₂)
  • Calculate: c = Pt / (m × Δθ)

Sources of error: heat losses to surroundings (gives a value of c that is too high), uneven heating, thermometer response time. Insulation, stirring, and a snug-fitting heater in a purpose-drilled hole help reduce errors.

In calculations, remember that energy supplied by the heater may not all go into raising the temperature — some is lost to the surroundings. The experimental value of c will typically be higher than the true value because you need to supply more energy than mcΔθ to achieve the observed temperature change.

9.3 Specific Latent Heat

Specific latent heat (L) is the energy required to change the state of 1 kg of a substance without a change in temperature.
Specific latent heat of fusion (Lf): solid → liquid (or liquid → solid)
Specific latent heat of vaporisation (Lv): liquid → gas (or gas → liquid)
Unit: J kg⁻¹
Q = mL

During a change of state, temperature remains constant because all energy input goes into breaking/forming intermolecular bonds (changing potential energy), not increasing kinetic energy.

Lv is always much larger than Lf for the same substance because vaporisation requires completely separating molecules against intermolecular forces, whereas fusion only requires disrupting the ordered lattice structure.

How much energy is needed to convert 0.50 kg of ice at −10°C to steam at 100°C?

Step 1 — Heat ice from −10°C to 0°C: Q₁ = mciceΔθ = 0.50 × 2100 × 10 = 10 500 J
Step 2 — Melt ice at 0°C: Q₂ = mLf = 0.50 × 334 000 = 167 000 J
Step 3 — Heat water from 0°C to 100°C: Q₃ = mcwaterΔθ = 0.50 × 4200 × 100 = 210 000 J
Step 4 — Vaporise water at 100°C: Q₄ = mLv = 0.50 × 2 260 000 = 1 130 000 J

Total = 10 500 + 167 000 + 210 000 + 1 130 000 = 1 517 500 J ≈ 1.5 MJ

Note that most of the energy (74%) goes into vaporisation alone.

Heating Curves

A heating curve plots temperature against time (or energy) for a substance being heated at constant power:

  • Sloped sections: Temperature is rising — substance is in a single phase. Gradient depends on specific heat capacity (steeper = lower c or lower mass).
  • Flat sections: Temperature constant — change of state occurring. Length depends on specific latent heat (longer = higher L or greater mass).

9.4 Gas Laws

Boyle's Law

Boyle's law: For a fixed mass of gas at constant temperature, the pressure is inversely proportional to the volume.
pV = constant   (at constant T)
p₁V₁ = p₂V₂

Microscopic explanation: At constant temperature, reducing the volume means the same number of molecules occupy a smaller space. They hit the walls more frequently (shorter distance between walls), so the force per unit area (pressure) increases.

Charles's Law

Charles's law: For a fixed mass of gas at constant pressure, the volume is directly proportional to the absolute temperature.
V / T = constant   (at constant p)
V₁ / T₁ = V₂ / T₂

Pressure Law (Gay-Lussac's Law)

Pressure law: For a fixed mass of gas at constant volume, the pressure is directly proportional to the absolute temperature.
p / T = constant   (at constant V)
p₁ / T₁ = p₂ / T₂

Microscopic explanation: At constant volume, increasing temperature means molecules move faster (higher average KE). They hit the walls harder (greater impulse per collision) and more frequently, so pressure increases.

All gas law calculations require temperature in kelvin. Convert from Celsius: T(K) = T(°C) + 273.15. A common error is forgetting to convert. A temperature of 0°C is NOT zero on the kelvin scale — it is 273 K.
A gas occupies 2.0 × 10⁻³ m³ at 20°C and 1.0 × 10⁵ Pa. It is heated to 100°C at constant pressure. What is its new volume?

Using Charles's law (constant pressure): V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂
T₁ = 20 + 273 = 293 K, T₂ = 100 + 273 = 373 K
V₂ = V₁ × T₂/T₁ = 2.0 × 10⁻³ × 373/293 = 2.5 × 10⁻³ m³

9.5 Ideal Gas Equation

Combining the three gas laws gives the ideal gas equation:

pV = nRT = NkT

where:

pPressurePa
VVolumem³
nNumber of molesmol

NNumber of moleculesdimensionless
kBoltzmann constant1.38 × 10⁻²³ J K⁻¹
TAbsolute temperatureK

Key relationships: R = NA × k, where NA = 6.02 × 10²³ mol⁻¹ (Avogadro's number). Also: N = n × NA.

An ideal gas is one that obeys the ideal gas equation (pV = nRT) at all pressures and temperatures. The assumptions are: (1) molecules have negligible volume, (2) no intermolecular forces except during collisions, (3) collisions are perfectly elastic. Real gases approximate ideal behaviour at low pressures and high temperatures (when molecules are far apart and intermolecular forces are negligible).
A sealed container of volume 0.050 m³ holds nitrogen gas at 27°C and 2.0 × 10⁵ Pa. Calculate: (a) the number of moles, (b) the number of molecules.

(a) pV = nRT, so n = pV/(RT) = (2.0 × 10⁵ × 0.050) / (8.314 × 300) = 10 000 / 2494 = 4.01 mol

(b) N = nNA = 4.01 × 6.02 × 10²³ = 2.41 × 10²⁴ molecules

9.6 Kinetic Theory of Gases

Assumptions of the Kinetic Theory

  1. The gas contains a very large number of molecules in rapid, random motion
  2. The volume of the molecules is negligible compared to the volume of the container
  3. Collisions between molecules and with the walls are perfectly elastic (no kinetic energy is lost)
  4. The time of a collision is negligible compared to the time between collisions
  5. There are no intermolecular forces between molecules (except during collisions)
  6. Molecules exert pressure on the container walls due to their collisions with the walls

The Kinetic Theory Equation

pV = ⅓ Nm<c²>

where N is the number of molecules, m is the mass of one molecule, and <c²> is the mean square speed.

The root mean square (rms) speed is: crms = √<c²>

Deriving the Pressure Equation

The derivation considers one molecule bouncing between two walls of a cubic container of side L:

  1. A molecule of mass m with x-component of velocity cx hits a wall
  2. Change in momentum per collision: Δp = 2mcx (elastic collision, velocity reverses)
  3. Time between successive hits on the same wall: Δt = 2L/cx
  4. Force from one molecule: F = Δp/Δt = 2mcx / (2L/cx) = mcx²/L
  5. For N molecules: total force = Nm<cx²>/L
  6. Pressure = Force/Area = Nm<cx²>/(L × L²) = Nm<cx²>/V
  7. Since <c²> = <cx²> + <cy²> + <cz²> = 3<cx²> (by symmetry): p = Nm<c²>/(3V)

Molecular Kinetic Energy and Temperature

Combining pV = nRT with pV = ⅓Nm<c²>:

½m<c²> = ¾kT

This is one of the most important results in thermodynamics: the average translational kinetic energy of a gas molecule is directly proportional to the absolute temperature. It depends only on temperature, not on the mass of the molecule or the type of gas.

Calculate the rms speed of nitrogen molecules (M = 28 g/mol = 0.028 kg/mol) at 300 K.

½m<c²> = ¾kT
<c²> = 3kT/m

Mass of one molecule: m = M/NA = 0.028 / 6.02 × 10²³ = 4.65 × 10⁻²⁶ kg
<c²> = 3 × 1.38 × 10⁻²³ × 300 / (4.65 × 10⁻²⁶) = 2.67 × 10⁵ m² s⁻²
crms = √(2.67 × 10⁵) = 517 m s⁻¹

Alternatively, using crms = √(3RT/M) = √(3 × 8.314 × 300 / 0.028) = 517 m s⁻¹
The rms speed is NOT the same as the mean speed. The rms speed is always slightly larger than the mean speed because squaring the speeds gives more weight to faster molecules. In calculations, always use crms = √(3kT/m) or √(3RT/M).

Maxwell-Boltzmann Distribution

Not all molecules in a gas have the same speed. The distribution of molecular speeds is described by the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution:

  • The distribution is not symmetrical — it has a tail extending to high speeds
  • The peak (most probable speed) shifts to higher speeds at higher temperatures
  • The distribution broadens and flattens at higher temperatures
  • The area under the curve is constant (total number of molecules does not change)
  • The mean speed, rms speed, and most probable speed are all different, with: most probable < mean < rms

9.7 Absolute Zero and the Kelvin Scale

Absolute zero (0 K = −273.15°C) is the temperature at which molecules have minimum kinetic energy (they do not completely stop due to quantum zero-point energy, but classically they would have zero KE). It is the lowest possible temperature and cannot actually be reached (Third Law of Thermodynamics).

The kelvin scale is defined so that a temperature change of 1 K is equal to a temperature change of 1°C. The only difference is the zero point: 0 K = −273.15°C.

In the ideal gas equation and kinetic theory, you MUST use absolute temperature (kelvin). If you use Celsius, you will get the wrong answer. A negative Celsius temperature does not mean negative kinetic energy — it just means below the freezing point of water.
