Checking Homogeneity of Equations

To verify an equation is dimensionally consistent, substitute the SI base units for every term and check both sides match.

Worked Example: Verify that E = mc2 is homogeneous.

Solution:
LHS: E has units of J = kg m2 s-2
RHS: mc2 = kg × (m s-1)2 = kg × m2 s-2 = kg m2 s-2
Both sides have units of kg m2 s-2, so the equation is homogeneous.
Worked Example: Show that the equation v = u + at is homogeneous.

Solution:
LHS: v has units of m s-1
RHS: u has units of m s-1; at has units of m s-2 × s = m s-1
u + at = m s-1 + m s-1 = m s-1. Both sides match.
To check if an equation is homogeneous, substitute the SI base units into both sides. If they match, the equation is dimensionally consistent. However, a homogeneous equation may still have incorrect numerical constants or be missing dimensionless factors — homogeneity is necessary but not sufficient for correctness.

Converting Units

To convert between prefixed units, multiply or divide by the appropriate power of 10:

  • 3.5 km = 3.5 × 103 m = 3500 m
  • 250 nm = 250 × 10-9 m = 2.50 × 10-7 m
  • 4.7 MΩ = 4.7 × 106 Ω
  • 15 μF = 15 × 10-6 F = 1.5 × 10-5 F
A very common error is forgetting to convert prefixed units before substituting into equations. For example, if a diameter is given as 0.50 mm, you must convert to 5.0 × 10-4 m before using it in A = πr2. Pay particular attention to squared and cubed units: 1 cm2 = 10-4 m2 (not 10-2), and 1 cm3 = 10-6 m3 (not 10-3).

1.3 Significant Figures and Decimal Places

Significant figures (s.f.) are the number of meaningful digits in a value. They indicate the precision of a measurement.

Rules for Counting Significant Figures

  • All non-zero digits are significant: 3456 has 4 s.f.
  • Zeros between non-zero digits are significant: 3006 has 4 s.f.
  • Leading zeros are NOT significant: 0.0045 has 2 s.f.
  • Trailing zeros after a decimal point ARE significant: 2.50 has 3 s.f.
  • Trailing zeros without a decimal point are ambiguous: 1500 could be 2, 3, or 4 s.f. (use scientific notation to be clear: 1.50 × 103 = 3 s.f.)

Using Significant Figures in Calculations

  • Multiplication / Division: The answer should have the same number of s.f. as the value with the fewest s.f.
  • Addition / Subtraction: The answer should have the same number of decimal places as the value with the fewest d.p.
  • Final answers are typically given to 2 or 3 s.f. in A-Level Physics.
  • Do not round intermediate calculations — only round the final answer.
If the question gives data to 3 s.f., give your answer to 2 or 3 s.f. Never give more significant figures in your answer than in the data you were given. A common mark scheme note is "accept 2 or 3 s.f." for the final answer.

Worked Example: Estimate the number of atoms in a human body.

Solution:
Mass of a person ≈ 70 kg. The human body is mostly water, so treat as water (molar mass = 18 g/mol = 0.018 kg/mol).
Number of moles ≈ 70 / 0.018 ≈ 3900 mol
Number of molecules ≈ 3900 × 6 × 1023 ≈ 2.3 × 1027 molecules
Each water molecule has 3 atoms, so number of atoms ≈ 3 × 2.3 × 1027 ≈ 7 × 1027 atoms
Order of magnitude: 1028
For estimation questions, show your reasoning clearly. State your assumptions (e.g. "assume the body is mostly water"). An answer within an order of magnitude of the "correct" answer typically earns full marks. The process is more important than the exact number.

1.5 Planning Experiments and Variables

Independent variable (IV): The variable you deliberately change during the experiment.
Dependent variable (DV): The variable you measure as a result of changing the IV.
Control variables: All other variables that must be kept constant to ensure a fair test.

Planning an Experiment

A complete experimental plan should include:

  • Clearly stated IV, DV, and all significant control variables with how each is maintained
  • List of apparatus with specifications (e.g. "micrometer screw gauge, resolution 0.01 mm")
  • A step-by-step method that could be replicated by another physicist
  • The range and intervals of the IV (at least 5–6 values, spread across a suitable range)
  • Number of repeat readings (at least 3) and calculation of mean
  • Safety precautions and risk assessment
  • What to plot and how to analyse the graph (e.g. "plot R vs L; gradient = ρ/A")

Types of Error

TypeDescriptionEffect on ResultsHow to Reduce
Random errorUnpredictable variation between measurements, causing scatter around the true valueResults scattered both above and below the true valueTake repeat readings and calculate the mean; use more precise instruments
Systematic errorA consistent error in the same direction every time (e.g. a zero error, miscalibrated instrument)All results shifted in the same direction from the true valueRecalibrate instruments; check for zero errors; use a different method
Zero errorA specific type of systematic error where an instrument does not read zero when it shouldAll readings are offset by a constant amountCheck and record the zero error; subtract it from all readings

Accuracy vs Precision

Accuracy: How close a measurement is to the true value. Affected by systematic errors.
Precision: How close repeat measurements are to each other. Affected by random errors.
Repeatability: How close results are when the same person repeats the experiment with the same equipment.
Reproducibility: How close results are when different people repeat the experiment, or when using different equipment or methods.

1.6 Uncertainties

Uncertainty: A measure of the range within which the true value is likely to lie. It quantifies the confidence in a measurement.

Absolute Uncertainty

The absolute uncertainty is expressed in the same units as the measurement.

  • For a single reading: Uncertainty = ± resolution of the instrument (or ± half the smallest division for analogue instruments).
  • For repeated readings: Uncertainty = (max value − min value) / 2. Use whichever is larger (resolution or half-range).

Percentage Uncertainty

Percentage uncertainty = (absolute uncertainty / measured value) × 100%

Combining Uncertainties

OperationRuleExample
Adding or subtracting: y = a ± bAdd the absolute uncertainties: Δy = Δa + ΔbIf a = 5.0 ± 0.1 and b = 3.0 ± 0.2, then a − b = 2.0 ± 0.3
Multiplying or dividing: y = a × b or y = a/bAdd the percentage uncertainties: %Δy = %Δa + %ΔbIf %Δa = 2% and %Δb = 3%, then %Δy = 5%
Raising to a power: y = anMultiply the percentage uncertainty by n: %Δy = n × %ΔaIf a has 2% uncertainty, then a2 has 4% uncertainty and √a has 1%
Worked Example: A wire has diameter d = 0.52 ± 0.01 mm. Calculate the percentage uncertainty in the cross-sectional area A = πd2/4.

Solution:
% uncertainty in d = (0.01/0.52) × 100% = 1.92%
Since A depends on d2, the % uncertainty in A = 2 × 1.92% = 3.85% (approximately 4%)
This is why diameter is often the largest source of uncertainty in resistivity experiments.
When a quantity is squared (e.g. v2 or d2), the percentage uncertainty is doubled. When a quantity is square-rooted, the percentage uncertainty is halved. This is one of the most commonly tested uncertainty rules.

1.7 Graphs

Drawing Graphs

  • Use at least half the available graph paper for your plotted points (use appropriate scales).
  • Label both axes with the quantity and its unit, separated by a slash or in brackets: e.g. "velocity / m s-1" or "velocity (m s-1)".
  • Choose sensible scales — use easy divisions (1, 2, 5, or 10 per large square). Avoid awkward scales like 3 or 7 per square.
  • Plot points accurately with small, precise crosses (not dots or circles).
  • Draw a line of best fit — this can be a straight line or a smooth curve. It does not need to pass through every point, but should have roughly equal numbers of points on each side.
  • Do not force the line through the origin unless the theory predicts it should pass through (0, 0).

Gradient

gradient = Δy / Δx = (y2 − y1) / (x2 − x1)

Use a large triangle (at least half the line length) for accuracy. Choose two points on the line of best fit, not raw data points. The gradient often represents a physical quantity (e.g. gradient of v-t graph = acceleration; gradient of R-L graph = ρ/A).

Y-Intercept

The y-intercept is the value of y when x = 0. Read it directly from the graph if the y-axis starts at zero. If the axis does not start at zero, calculate it using y = mx + c with a point on the line and the gradient m. The y-intercept often represents a physical constant (e.g. in V = ε − Ir, the y-intercept of V vs I is ε).

Error Bars and Lines of Best/Worst Fit

  • Error bars extend above and below each point by the size of the absolute uncertainty.
  • Draw a best fit line that passes as close to as many points as possible.
  • Draw worst fit lines — the steepest and shallowest lines that still pass through all error bars.
  • Uncertainty in gradient = (steepest gradient − shallowest gradient) / 2.
  • Uncertainty in y-intercept = (steepest intercept − shallowest intercept) / 2.

Worked Example: An experiment gives data that is expected to follow I = I0e−λt. What should be plotted to produce a straight line, and how are I0 and λ determined?

Solution:
Take natural log of both sides: ln(I) = ln(I0) − λt
This has the form y = mx + c, so plot ln(I) vs t.
Gradient = −λ (so λ = magnitude of gradient).
Y-intercept = ln(I0), so I0 = eintercept.
Always use data points that lie ON your line of best fit when calculating the gradient, not raw data points from the table. The two points should be far apart for maximum accuracy. State the coordinates of both points and show your working.
