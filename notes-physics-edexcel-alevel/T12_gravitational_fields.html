Topic 12 — Gravitational Fields

12.1 Newton's Law of Gravitation

Newton's law of universal gravitation: Every particle in the universe attracts every other particle with a force that is directly proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between their centres.
F = GMm / r²

where:

  • F = gravitational force (N) — always attractive
  • G = universal gravitational constant = 6.674 × 10⁻¹¹ N m² kg⁻²
  • M, m = masses of the two objects (kg)
  • r = distance between the centres of the masses (m)

The negative sign is sometimes included (F = −GMm/r²) to indicate the force is attractive (directed towards the other mass). Gravity is always attractive — there is no gravitational repulsion.

The distance r is measured between the centres of the objects, not their surfaces. If given the radius of the Earth and the altitude of a satellite, you must add them: r = RE + h. This is one of the most common errors in gravitational field calculations.
Calculate the gravitational force between the Earth (M = 5.97 × 10²⁴ kg) and a 70 kg person standing on its surface (RE = 6.37 × 10⁶ m).

F = GMm/r² = (6.674 × 10⁻¹¹ × 5.97 × 10²⁴ × 70) / (6.37 × 10⁶)²
F = (2.789 × 10¹⁶) / (4.058 × 10¹³) = 687 N

This equals the person's weight: W = mg = 70 × 9.81 = 687 N ✓

12.2 Gravitational Field Strength

Gravitational field strength (g): The gravitational force per unit mass experienced by a small test mass placed at that point in the field. It is a vector quantity pointing towards the mass creating the field.
g = F / m

For a point mass or uniform sphere (outside the surface):

g = GM / r²

Units: N kg⁻¹ (equivalent to m s⁻²). At the Earth's surface: g ≈ 9.81 N kg⁻¹.

Key features of g:

  • g decreases with distance from the mass according to an inverse-square law
  • g at the surface of a planet depends on both the planet's mass and radius: g = GM/R²
  • Inside a uniform sphere, g decreases linearly with distance from the centre (g = 0 at the centre)
  • g is independent of the test mass — all objects experience the same gravitational field strength
Calculate the gravitational field strength on the surface of Mars (M = 6.42 × 10²³ kg, R = 3.39 × 10⁶ m).

g = GM/R² = (6.674 × 10⁻¹¹ × 6.42 × 10²³) / (3.39 × 10⁶)²
g = (4.285 × 10¹³) / (1.150 × 10¹³) = 3.73 N kg⁻¹

This is about 38% of Earth's surface gravity. A 70 kg astronaut would weigh 70 × 3.73 = 261 N on Mars, compared to 687 N on Earth.

12.3 Gravitational Field Patterns

Radial Fields

  • Field lines point radially inward towards the centre of the mass
  • Field strength decreases with distance (inverse square law)
  • Produced by a point mass or a spherical mass (outside the sphere)
  • Field lines are not parallel — they converge at the centre

Uniform Fields

  • Field lines are parallel and equally spaced
  • Field strength is constant at all points
  • A good approximation for the gravitational field near the Earth's surface over small regions (where the curvature is negligible)

Equipotentials

  • Equipotential lines (or surfaces) join points of equal gravitational potential
  • Equipotentials are always perpendicular to field lines
  • No work is done moving a mass along an equipotential surface
  • For a radial field, equipotentials are concentric circles (spheres in 3D) centred on the mass — they are closer together near the mass where the field is stronger
  • For a uniform field, equipotentials are equally spaced horizontal lines

12.4 Gravitational Potential

Gravitational potential (V): The work done per unit mass in bringing a small test mass from infinity to that point in the field. It is a scalar quantity and is always negative (since gravity is attractive and we define V = 0 at infinity).
V = −GM / r

Units: J kg⁻¹

Key points:

  • V is always negative (or zero at infinity)
  • V becomes more negative as you approach the mass (you go deeper into the "gravitational well")
  • The potential at infinity is defined as zero
  • V follows a 1/r relationship (not 1/r² like field strength)

Relationship Between g and V

g = −dV/dr

The gravitational field strength equals the negative gradient of the potential. On a V–r graph, g is the magnitude of the gradient at any point. The negative sign means g points in the direction of decreasing potential (towards the mass).

On a graph of V against r, the gradient gives −g. A steeper gradient means a stronger field. At the surface of a planet, the gradient is steepest. Compare with the electric field: E = −dV/dr. The mathematics is identical.

12.5 Gravitational Potential Energy

Gravitational potential energy (Ep): The work done in bringing a mass m from infinity to that point in the gravitational field of mass M.
Ep = −GMm / r

Note: Ep = mV. The GPE is negative because work must be done against gravity to move an object from that point to infinity (i.e., the object is bound to the gravitational field).

The change in GPE when moving between two points:

ΔEp = GMm(1/r₁ − 1/r₂)

where r₂ > r₁ (moving away from the mass).

Do not confuse Ep = mgh (valid only for uniform fields near the surface, where h << R) with Ep = −GMm/r (the general expression valid at any distance). Use the general form for orbital and escape velocity problems.
Calculate the energy needed to move a 500 kg satellite from an orbit of radius 7000 km to an orbit of radius 10 000 km around the Earth (M = 5.97 × 10²⁴ kg).

ΔEp = GMm(1/r₁ − 1/r₂)
= 6.674 × 10⁻¹¹ × 5.97 × 10²⁴ × 500 × (1/7.0×10⁶ − 1/1.0×10⁷)
= 1.991 × 10¹⁶ × (1.429×10⁻⁷ − 1.000×10⁻⁷)
= 1.991 × 10¹⁶ × 4.29 × 10⁻⁸
= 8.54 × 10⁸ J ≈ 854 MJ

Note: This is just the change in PE. The total energy change for the orbital transfer also involves a change in kinetic energy.

12.6 Escape Velocity

Escape velocity: The minimum speed an object must have at the surface to escape to infinity without any further energy input (i.e., to reach infinity with zero kinetic energy and zero potential energy).

Setting total energy at surface = total energy at infinity (= 0):

½mv² + (−GMm/r) = 0
vescape = √(2GM / r)

Key points:

  • Escape velocity does not depend on the mass of the escaping object — only on M and r of the body being escaped from
  • Escape velocity = √2 × orbital velocity at that radius
  • For Earth: vescape ≈ 11.2 km s⁻¹
  • If an object has speed less than vescape, it will return (or go into orbit if launched tangentially)
Calculate the escape velocity from the Moon's surface (M = 7.35 × 10²² kg, R = 1.74 × 10⁶ m).

vescape = √(2GM/R) = √(2 × 6.674×10⁻¹¹ × 7.35×10²² / 1.74×10⁶)
= √(5.63×10⁶) = 2370 m s⁻¹ ≈ 2.4 km s⁻¹

This is much less than Earth's 11.2 km s⁻¹, which is why the Moon has essentially no atmosphere — gas molecules easily reach escape velocity due to thermal motion.

12.7 Orbital Motion

For a satellite in a circular orbit, gravitational force provides the centripetal force:

GMm / r² = mv² / r

Orbital Speed

v = √(GM / r)

Orbital speed depends only on the mass of the central body and the orbital radius — not on the satellite's mass. A higher orbit means a slower orbital speed.

Orbital Period

Since v = 2πr / T:

T = 2πr / v = 2π √(r³ / GM)

Squaring:

T² = (4π² / GM) r³

This is Kepler's third law: T² is proportional to r³. A graph of T² vs r³ is a straight line through the origin with gradient 4π²/(GM).

Calculate the orbital radius and speed of a geostationary satellite (T = 24 hours = 86 400 s, MEarth = 5.97 × 10²⁴ kg).

From T² = (4π²/GM)r³:
r³ = GMT²/(4π²) = (6.674×10⁻¹¹ × 5.97×10²⁴ × (86400)²) / (4π²)
r³ = (2.975×10²⁴) / 39.48 = 7.534 × 10²²
r = (7.534 × 10²²)¹/³ = 4.22 × 10⁷ m ≈ 42 200 km from Earth's centre

Altitude above surface = 42 200 − 6370 = 35 800 km

Speed: v = 2πr/T = 2π × 4.22×10⁷ / 86400 = 3070 m s⁻¹ ≈ 3.1 km s⁻¹

12.8 Kepler's Laws

LawStatement
First LawAll planets orbit the Sun in ellipses with the Sun at one focus.
Second LawA line joining a planet to the Sun sweeps out equal areas in equal time intervals. (The planet moves faster when closer to the Sun.)
Third LawThe square of the orbital period is proportional to the cube of the semi-major axis (mean orbital radius): T² ∝ r³.
Kepler's third law can be verified experimentally by plotting T² against r³ — the result is a straight line through the origin with gradient 4π²/(GM). This allows the mass M of the central body to be determined. This technique is used to measure the mass of planets (from their moons' orbits), stars (from orbiting planets), and even black holes.

12.9 Types of Orbit

Geostationary Orbit

  • Orbital period = 24 hours (same as Earth's rotation)
  • Orbits directly above the equator (inclination = 0°)
  • Same direction as Earth's rotation (west to east)
  • Appears stationary relative to a point on the Earth's surface
  • Altitude ≈ 35 800 km above the surface (r ≈ 42 200 km from centre)
  • Uses: Communications satellites, TV broadcasting, weather monitoring

Low Earth Orbit (LEO)

  • Altitude: typically 200–2000 km above the surface
  • Orbital period: ~90 minutes to 2 hours
  • Can be at any inclination (including polar orbits at ~90°)
  • Satellites move relative to the Earth's surface
  • Uses: Earth observation, scientific research, International Space Station, imaging/reconnaissance, Starlink internet constellation

A higher orbit means a lower orbital speed but a longer orbital period. Do not confuse the two — students often incorrectly think faster orbit means shorter period at all radii. Use v = √(GM/r) to confirm that v decreases as r increases, and T = 2π√(r³/GM) to confirm that T increases as r increases.

Total Energy of a Satellite

For a satellite in a circular orbit:

  • Kinetic energy: Ek = ½mv² = GMm/(2r)
  • Potential energy: Ep = −GMm/r
  • Total energy: Etotal = Ek + Ep = −GMm/(2r)

The total energy is negative (the satellite is bound). Moving to a higher orbit increases Etotal (makes it less negative), which requires energy input. The kinetic energy decreases (slower speed) but the potential energy increases by twice as much.
