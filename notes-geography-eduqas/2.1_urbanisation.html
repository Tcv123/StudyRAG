2.1 Urbanisation and Urban Change

1. Global Urbanisation Trends

Urbanisation: The process by which an increasing proportion of a country's population comes to live in urban areas (towns and cities). It is measured as the percentage of total population living in urban areas — NOT the total number of people in cities.

2. Causes of Urbanisation

Urbanisation results from two processes: rural-urban migration (people moving from countryside to city) and natural increase (birth rate exceeding death rate within cities).

Natural Increase in Cities

In LICs and NEEs, cities also grow through natural increase — a higher birth rate than death rate within the urban population. Migrants are often young adults (reproductive age), which raises the urban birth rate. Improved healthcare in cities reduces child mortality compared to rural areas.

3. Urban Land Use Models

Urban Land Use Model: A simplified diagram showing how land use (residential, commercial, industrial) is typically arranged within a city. Models are generalisations based on observations of many cities.

Burgess Concentric Zone Model

Developed by E.W. Burgess in 1925 based on Chicago. Proposes that cities grow outward in concentric rings from a central CBD. Land values decrease with distance from the centre; older housing is near the centre, newer suburbs on the edge.

ZoneLand UseCharacteristics
1 — CBD (Central Business District)Shops, offices, entertainment, transport hubsHighest land values; tallest buildings; maximum accessibility; little residential
2 — Zone of Transition (Inner City)Industry, low-quality housing, warehousingOld terraced housing, immigrants, overcrowding; transitioning to new uses; declining industry
3 — Low-class ResidentialWorkers' housingSlightly better housing than Zone 2; older, smaller homes; working-class families
4 — Suburbs (Middle-class)Residential — semi-detached housing, gardensLower density; better housing quality; car ownership; schools; quiet neighbourhoods
5 — Rural-Urban FringeCommuter housing, business parks, retail parksNewest development; green belt pressure; out-of-town shopping; open space

Hoyt Sector Model (1939)

Homer Hoyt modified Burgess by showing that cities grow in sectors (wedges) along transport routes rather than perfect rings. Industrial areas develop along rivers and railways; wealthy residential areas develop away from industry on the opposite side. More realistic for many cities than the Burgess model.

Limitations of Models

  • Based on one city (Chicago in 1920s) — not universally applicable
  • Cities in LICs/NEEs often have the poorest housing on the edge (not the centre) as squatter settlements develop on cheap peripheral land
  • Don't account for planning restrictions, motorways, historic town centres, edge cities
  • Cities are becoming more fragmented and polycentric (multiple nuclei model)
Exam Tip: You may be asked to apply land use models to real cities. Remember to state the model's name and explain why reality might differ from the model — this shows geographical understanding and gains higher marks.

4. Urbanisation in LICs/NEEs — Squatter Settlements

Squatter settlement / Informal settlement / Slum: A densely populated area of a city where housing has been illegally built on land not owned by the residents, using temporary materials, without official permission. Known by various local names: favela (Brazil), barrio (Latin America), shanty town (general), bidonville (French Africa), bustee (India).

Why Do Squatter Settlements Develop?

  • Rural-urban migrants arrive in cities faster than formal housing can be built
  • Migrants cannot afford formal sector rents or house prices
  • Land on the city periphery, flood plains, steep hillsides, or near railways/factories is undesirable — squatters occupy it with less risk of eviction
  • Governments in LICs lack resources to provide social housing at scale
  • Informal settlements develop incrementally — start with temporary shelters, gradually improve over years

Characteristics of Squatter Settlements

FeatureDetail
HousingBuilt from salvaged materials — corrugated iron, plastic sheets, cardboard, breeze blocks. Often 1–2 rooms for extended families.

5. Case Study: Mumbai, India (NEE)

Case Study: Mumbai (NEE) — Urbanisation, Slums, and Improvement

Location and status: Mumbai (formerly Bombay) is India's financial capital, located on the west coast. It is India's largest city with ~21 million people in the city proper and ~24 million in the Greater Mumbai Metropolitan Region. It is one of the world's most densely populated cities.

Why it grew so rapidly: Mumbai was the centre of British colonial trade (textiles, finance); post-independence it became India's financial and entertainment (Bollywood) hub. Millions migrated from rural Maharashtra, Uttar Pradesh, and Bihar seeking work and better wages.

Dharavi: Located in central Mumbai, Dharavi covers approximately 2.1 km² and is home to an estimated 600,000–1 million people — making it one of Asia's most densely populated areas and one of its largest informal settlements (though not the largest, a common misconception). It has existed since the late 1800s.

  • Challenges: 1 toilet per ~1,440 residents; most homes are 10–15m²; average annual income ~$500; disease burden from poor sanitation; no legal land rights
  • BUT — the informal economy: Dharavi has an estimated annual economic turnover of $600–700 million USD. It contains ~15,000 single-room factories and 5,000 businesses in industries including recycling (processes ~80% of Mumbai's plastic waste), leather goods, pottery, garment manufacturing, and food production. A vibrant, functional community — not simply a place of despair.
  • Regeneration controversy: The Dharavi Redevelopment Project (proposed 2004, approved 2022) plans to demolish and rebuild Dharavi — providing free flats to existing residents but also building ~76,000 market-rate apartments for sale. Critics worry that community networks will be destroyed, informal businesses displaced, and many residents will not qualify for free housing.

Self-Help Schemes (SHS): Rather than top-down clearance, SHS involve the government providing materials, land tenure, loans, and technical advice while residents build and improve their own homes. Mumbai's Slum Rehabilitation Authority (SRA) has rehoused 600,000+ slum dwellers in permanent buildings. Benefits: cheaper per family than government-built housing; community ownership; preserves social networks. Limitations: quality of building can be poor; corruption in scheme allocation; some families remain ineligible.

Site and Service Schemes: The government provides a serviced plot of land (road access, basic water supply, sewerage connections) and residents build their own home on it. Used in Mumbai and across African cities. More affordable than completed housing; builds community investment.

7. Urbanisation in HICs — Counter-trends

In HICs, urbanisation has largely completed. Instead, three counter-trends are observed:

ProcessDefinitionCausesEffects
SuburbanisationMovement of people and businesses from the city centre to the outskirtsMass car ownership (20th century); desire for space, gardens, better schools; white flight (racial); council estate building on city fringesUrban sprawl; loss of agricultural land; decline of inner city; out-of-town retail; longer commutes
Counter-urbanisationMovement of people away from cities to smaller towns, villages, and rural areasRemote working (accelerated by COVID-19); desire for quality of life; internet connectivity in rural areas; retirement; lower house pricesRural house price inflation; pricing out of locals; loss of rural character; commuter villages; rural gentrification
Re-urbanisationReturn of people (often young professionals) to city centresRegeneration of inner-city areas (Docklands, Salford Quays); gentrification; desire for walkable, cultural urban living; delayed car ownership among millennialsGentrification displaces existing communities; housing prices rise; cultural homogenisation; loss of affordable housing
Gentrification: The process by which an area of lower-income, working-class housing is gradually taken over by higher-income, middle-class residents and businesses, raising property values and displacing existing residents who can no longer afford to live there. Example: Brixton (London), Hackney (London), Northern Quarter (Manchester).
Urban Sprawl: The unplanned, uncontrolled spread of urban areas into surrounding rural land. Driven by suburbanisation and out-of-town development. The UK Green Belt policy (1955) was designed to prevent urban sprawl around major cities.

8. Urban Functions and Hierarchy

Settlements can be arranged in a hierarchy based on population size and the range of services they provide. Higher-order settlements have more varied and specialised functions, a larger sphere of influence, and are further apart.

Settlement TypePopulationFunctions / ServicesSphere of Influence
Hamlet<100None / very basic (pub, farm)Very local (<1 km)
Village100–2,000Post office, primary school, church, local shopSurrounding farms (~5 km)
Town2,000–100,000Secondary school, supermarket, cinema, hospitalSeveral surrounding villages (20–50 km)
City100,000–1 millionUniversity, regional hospital, major shopping, theatresRegional (50–200 km)
Conurbation1–10 millionMultiple CBDs, international transport hub, financial servicesNational / international
Megacity>10 millionAll major global functions; global financial markets; multiple universitiesGlobal
Central Place Theory (Walter Christaller, 1933): Proposes that settlements are distributed across space in a regular, hierarchical pattern. Threshold = minimum population needed to support a service. Range = maximum distance people will travel for a service. Higher-order settlements (cities) have higher thresholds and ranges and are spaced further apart.
Exam Tip — Urbanisation Questions: Always be specific with data. Don't just say "Mumbai has a large population" — say "Mumbai has ~21 million people in the city proper, with an estimated 600,000–1 million living in Dharavi alone." Similarly, don't just say "urbanisation is fast in LICs" — give a figure: "The global urban population is expected to reach 68% by 2050, with most growth in Africa and Asia."
Key Distinction: Urbanisation = the PROCESS (increasing % urban). Urban growth = the increase in absolute NUMBER of people in cities. A city can grow in population without urbanisation occurring if the rural population grows equally fast. This distinction matters in exam answers.
