3.2 Climate Change

1. Defining Climate Change

Weather — the short-term atmospheric conditions at a specific time and place (temperature, rainfall, wind speed, cloud cover). Can change hour by hour.
Climate — the long-term average pattern of weather conditions for a region, calculated over at least 30 years. Changes slowly over decades or millennia.
Climate Change — a long-term shift in global average temperatures and precipitation patterns, beyond natural variability. The current concern is the unprecedented rate of warming since industrialisation (~1750) driven largely by human activities.

The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) — the UN scientific body that reviews global climate research — concluded in its 2021 report (AR6): "It is unequivocal that human influence has warmed the atmosphere, ocean and land."

3. Natural Causes of Climate Change

Milankovitch Cycles

Cyclical changes in Earth's orbit and orientation relative to the Sun drive natural climate cycles over tens of thousands of years:

  • Eccentricity (~100,000 years) — shape of Earth's orbit changes from nearly circular to more elliptical. When more elliptical, seasonal variation is greater and Earth receives less total solar energy annually.
  • Axial tilt (~41,000 years) — angle of Earth's axis varies from 22.1° to 24.5°. Greater tilt = more extreme seasons (hotter summers, colder winters). Current tilt is 23.5° and slowly decreasing.
  • Precession (~26,000 years) — Earth's axis wobbles like a spinning top. Changes which hemisphere experiences summer closest to the Sun, altering which seasons are more intense.

Together, these cycles explain the glacial and interglacial periods (ice ages) of the past 2.6 million years. However, they cannot explain the rapid warming since 1750 — Milankovitch cycles operate over far longer timescales and current orbital forcing actually points toward slight cooling over the next few thousand years.

Solar Variation

The Sun's energy output is not constant. It varies by approximately 0.1% over an 11-year sunspot cycle. Periods of high sunspot activity correspond to slightly higher solar output. Scientists have identified longer periods of very low solar activity — the Maunder Minimum (1645–1715) — which coincides with the coldest phase of the Little Ice Age. However, solar output has been flat or slightly declining since the 1980s, while global temperatures have continued to rise sharply. This rules out solar variation as a primary driver of current warming. Studies by the IPCC estimate solar forcing has contributed less than 0.1°C of the observed warming since 1950, compared to ~0.9°C from greenhouse gas forcing.

Volcanic Eruptions

Large explosive eruptions inject sulphur dioxide (SO₂) into the stratosphere. This forms sulphate aerosols — tiny droplets that reflect incoming solar radiation back to space, causing short-term surface cooling. Key examples:

  • Mt Pinatubo, Philippines (1991) — second-largest eruption of the 20th century; injected 20 million tonnes of SO₂ into the stratosphere. Global average temperature fell ~0.5°C for 1–2 years before aerosols settled. Crops failed in parts of North America (the "year without a summer" effect was moderate).
  • Laki, Iceland (1783) — fissure eruption releasing enormous SO₂ clouds; caused harvest failures across Europe and may have contributed to famine in Egypt by disrupting the Nile flood.
  • Tambora, Indonesia (1815) — the largest eruption in recorded history; caused 1816 to be known as the "Year Without a Summer" across the Northern Hemisphere; crop failures caused an estimated 71,000–200,000 deaths.

Volcanic eruptions produce cooling, not warming, and effects are temporary (1–3 years). They cannot account for the sustained warming trend observed since 1750. Some volcanoes also emit CO₂, but human emissions are now estimated to be 40–100 times greater than all volcanic CO₂ output combined.

Exam Tip: A common exam question asks you to distinguish between natural and human causes of climate change, then explain why scientists believe human causes are dominant. Key arguments: (1) the rate of warming is far faster than any natural cycle — 1°C in 150 years vs. 5–6°C over 5,000 years at end of last ice age; (2) ice core data shows CO₂ levels are unprecedented in 800,000 years; (3) solar output has not increased but temperature has; (4) isotopic "fingerprinting" of CO₂ in the atmosphere confirms it comes from fossil fuels (different carbon isotope ratio to volcanic CO₂).

4. Human Causes — The Enhanced Greenhouse Effect

Natural Greenhouse Effect — essential for life on Earth. Without it, average global temperature would be −18°C (instead of current +15°C). GHGs (CO₂, water vapour, methane) in the atmosphere trap some of the longwave infrared radiation emitted by Earth's surface after it absorbs incoming solar energy.

Enhanced Greenhouse Effect — human activities increasing the concentration of GHGs in the atmosphere. More GHG molecules means more infrared radiation is absorbed and re-emitted back toward Earth's surface before it can escape to space, causing global warming. The mechanism: GHG molecules vibrate when they absorb infrared energy, then release that energy in all directions — including back downward.

Current CO₂ levels: ~422 ppm (parts per million) in 2024 — the highest in at least 800,000 years (Vostok ice core data). Pre-industrial level was ~280 ppm. Rate of increase is ~2.5 ppm per year — faster than any natural rate seen in the ice core record.

Role of Deforestation

Forests act as carbon sinks — absorbing CO₂ from the atmosphere through photosynthesis. Deforestation contributes ~10% of global GHG emissions in two ways:

  1. Burning or decomposing trees releases stored carbon as CO₂.
  2. Fewer trees means less CO₂ is absorbed — reducing the natural sink capacity.

Amazon deforestation is a major concern — the Amazon has been deforested by ~20% since 1970. Some scientists warn it may reach a "tipping point" where it switches from a carbon sink to a carbon source. Parts of the eastern Amazon already emit more CO₂ than they absorb.

5. Impacts of Climate Change

The impacts of climate change are wide-ranging, affecting economies, societies, and natural environments. They are experienced unequally — Low Income Countries (LICs), small island states, and communities in the Global South face the greatest risks despite contributing the least to emissions.

Economic Impacts

  • Agriculture losses — changing rainfall patterns, more frequent droughts and floods, and extreme heat reduce crop yields. Sub-Saharan Africa crop yields predicted to fall 8–22% by 2050 (IPCC). Coffee production in Ethiopia — one of the world's leading exporters — faces a 40–59% reduction in suitable growing area by 2100.
  • Extreme weather damage costs — insurance industry losses from extreme weather have grown dramatically. Munich Re estimated $280 billion in economic losses from natural disasters in 2023, with extreme weather events accounting for the majority. The Caribbean economy loses an estimated 1–2% of GDP annually to hurricane damage — a figure set to rise.
  • Infrastructure damage — flooding, storm surges, and permafrost thaw damage roads, buildings, and pipelines. In Alaska, permafrost thaw is causing roads and buildings to subside; estimated repair costs exceed $5 billion. UK flooding in 2019–2020 cost £333 million in direct damages.
  • Tourism losses — the Great Barrier Reef contributes AUD$6.4 billion to the Australian economy annually; coral bleaching threatens this. Alpine ski resorts losing viability as snowfall decreases — Switzerland's ski industry faces collapse by 2100 under high-emission scenarios.
  • Fisheries decline — ocean warming and acidification disrupt fish stocks. The North Sea cod fishery has declined by 80% since 1970, partly due to warming waters reducing food availability for cod larvae.
  • Energy costs — increased cooling demand (air conditioning) and reduced efficiency of fossil fuel power plants in extreme heat raise energy costs. By 2050, US electricity demand for cooling is projected to rise 25%.

Social Impacts

  • Food insecurity — changing monsoons threaten rice and wheat harvests for 2 billion people in South Asia. World Food Programme estimates climate change could push an additional 183 million people into hunger by 2050.
  • Water stress — glacial retreat in the Himalayas threatens freshwater supply for over 1.9 billion people who depend on rivers fed by glacial meltwater (Ganges, Indus, Yangtze). Short-term increase in meltwater is followed by long-term reduction as glaciers disappear.
  • Health impacts — malaria mosquitoes (Anopheles) moving to higher altitudes in Africa and Asia as temperatures rise; now found in Ethiopian Highlands where the population has no immunity. Heatwave mortality: European heatwave (2003) killed ~70,000 people; UK heatwave (July 2022, 40.3°C — the first time 40°C was recorded) caused an estimated 3,000 excess deaths. Flood-driven cholera outbreaks are increasing (e.g. Cyclone Idai, Mozambique, 2019).
  • Climate refugees and displacement — World Bank estimates 216 million internal climate migrants by 2050. Bangladesh: 17 million people could be displaced by a 1m sea level rise. Kiribati and Tuvalu face complete inundation. Syria's 2006–2010 drought (linked to climate change) contributed to internal displacement that preceded the Syrian conflict — an example of climate change acting as a "threat multiplier."
  • Mental health — "eco-anxiety," trauma after extreme weather events, and loss of cultural identity for communities displaced from ancestral lands are growing concerns, particularly among young people.

Environmental Impacts

  • Arctic sea ice loss — Arctic sea ice extent has declined by 13% per decade since 1979. The Arctic is warming 4x faster than the global average due to the albedo feedback loop (see Section 4 diagram).
  • Ocean acidification — oceans absorb ~26% of human CO₂ emissions; CO₂ dissolves to form carbonic acid. Ocean pH has fallen from 8.2 to 8.1 since pre-industrial times — a 30% increase in acidity. This weakens the shells of molluscs and corals. Great Barrier Reef has lost 50% of its coral cover since 1995; 2016 and 2017 bleaching events affected 91% of reefs surveyed.
  • Permafrost thaw — Arctic permafrost stores twice as much carbon as is currently in the atmosphere. Thawing releases methane (a powerful GHG), creating a dangerous positive feedback loop. Siberia's permafrost is thawing at an accelerating rate; sinkholes called "thermokarst" are appearing across Siberia.
  • Ecosystem disruption and biodiversity loss — IPCC: 18–35% of species face extinction risk with 3°C warming. Polar bears losing hunting habitat as sea ice retreats earlier each spring; body condition scores declining (thinner bears, fewer cubs). Coral triangle (SE Asia) supports 75% of world's coral species and 120 million people — severely threatened. Phenological mismatch — flowers blooming and insects emerging out of sync disrupts pollination.
  • Sea level rise — thermal expansion (40%) + land ice melt (60%). IPCC projects 0.28–1.01m of sea level rise by 2100 depending on emissions. Beyond 2100, even if warming stabilises, seas will continue to rise for centuries. Greenland alone holds enough ice to raise global sea levels by 7m if completely melted.
Exam Tip: Climate change impacts are geographically unequal — LICs are most vulnerable despite contributing least to the problem. This "climate justice" argument is key for evaluation questions. HIC carbon emissions cause disproportionate harm to LIC populations who lack resources to adapt. Always link economic, social, and environmental impacts together — they are interconnected (e.g. coral reef loss = environmental impact + economic impact on tourism and fisheries + social impact on food security for coastal communities).

6. Case Studies

Case Study: Arctic Sea Ice Loss — Impacts on Wildlife and Indigenous Communities

Location & Scale: The Arctic Ocean and surrounding tundra, including northern Canada, Alaska, Russia, Greenland, and Scandinavia. The Arctic is warming at 3–4 times the global average rate, making it the fastest-warming region on Earth.

Scale of Ice Loss:

  • Arctic sea ice extent has declined by approximately 13% per decade since satellite records began in 1979 (NASA).
  • Summer sea ice volume has declined by over 75% since the 1980s.
  • September 2012 saw the lowest Arctic sea ice extent ever recorded — 3.41 million km² (compared to ~7 million km² in the 1980s).
  • Scientists predict the Arctic Ocean could be essentially ice-free in summer by the 2050s under current emission trends.

Impacts on Wildlife:

  • Polar bears (Ursus maritimus) — depend on sea ice as a hunting platform to catch ringed seals. With ice forming later in autumn and breaking up earlier in spring, polar bears have a shorter hunting season. Western Hudson Bay polar bear population has declined by 30% since the 1980s. Bears are coming ashore thinner and in poorer body condition; cub survival rates are falling. The IUCN lists polar bears as Vulnerable; some models predict a 30% population decline by 2050.
  • Walruses — use sea ice as a resting platform between foraging dives. Without ice, tens of thousands are forced ashore at once (a "haul-out"), causing deadly stampedes. In 2019, a haul-out of 35,000 walruses in Alaska led to hundreds of deaths from trampling.
  • Narwhals, beluga whales, and ringed seals — habitat and food sources disrupted. Increased killer whale presence in newly ice-free Arctic waters is decimating narwhal populations previously protected by ice barriers.
  • Arctic cod — a key food source in the Arctic food web; its habitat is under pressure from warming and acidifying waters. Decline cascades through the food chain to seals, bears, and seabirds.

Impacts on Indigenous Communities:

  • Inuit peoples (Canada, Greenland, Alaska) have lived in the Arctic for thousands of years, developing deep cultural and practical knowledge of sea ice. An estimated 180,000 Inuit live in the Arctic.
  • Traditional hunting — Inuit hunt ringed seals, polar bears, walruses, and narwhals from sea ice. Unpredictable ice conditions make hunting more dangerous; hunters are falling through thinner ice more frequently. In 2019, eight Inuit hunters from Igloolik, Nunavut drowned when their snowmobiles broke through ice that had been reliable for generations.
  • Food security — country food (traditional food obtained through hunting, fishing, foraging) provides 50–75% of Inuit caloric intake in remote communities and is central to cultural identity. Declining wildlife availability is driving food insecurity; studies show increasing rates of food insecurity in Inuit communities.
  • Permafrost thaw — Inuit communities built on permafrost are seeing homes, roads, and water infrastructure sink and crack. Shishmaref, Alaska (600 Inuit residents) has lost 38 metres of coastline since the 1970s and faces relocation at a cost of $180 million.
  • Cultural and spiritual loss — Inuit language has over 50 words for sea ice; entire vocabulary and knowledge systems tied to ice conditions are becoming obsolete. Elders describe a world that has become unrecognisable. The Inuit Circumpolar Council has submitted formal petitions to the UN Inter-American Commission on Human Rights, arguing that climate change violates their fundamental rights.
  • Positive economic impacts (contested) — the opening of the Northwest Passage as a shipping route and access to Arctic resources brings economic opportunity but also threatens the region with pollution and industrial development, compounding environmental pressures.

Conclusion: Arctic sea ice loss is a powerful example of how climate change disproportionately affects communities — particularly indigenous peoples — who have contributed least to GHG emissions. It also illustrates the concept of positive feedback loops: less ice → lower albedo → more absorption → more warming → less ice.

Case Study: Maldives and Pacific Islands — Sea Level Rise Threat

Location: The Maldives, an island nation in the Indian Ocean comprising 1,200 islands and 26 atolls; total population ~540,000. Also relevant: Kiribati, Tuvalu, Marshall Islands, and Funafuti (Tuvalu's capital, the most densely populated area anywhere in the Pacific).

The Physical Threat:

  • The Maldives has an average elevation of just 1.5 metres above sea level — 80% of land is less than 1 metre above sea level. It is one of the lowest-lying nations on Earth.
  • Sea level in the Indian Ocean has risen approximately 8mm per year since 1993 — faster than the global average of 3.3mm per year due to regional ocean dynamics.
  • IPCC projections: under a medium-emissions scenario, sea level could rise 0.4–0.6m by 2100 — enough to render most of the Maldives uninhabitable through storm surge, saltwater intrusion of freshwater, and coastal erosion even without permanent inundation.
  • High-tide flooding events that previously occurred once every 50 years now occur annually on some atolls.
  • Tuvalu (area: 26 km²; 11,000 people) — storm surge events now regularly flood homes and contaminate the freshwater lens (thin layer of fresh groundwater). The country's highest natural point is 4.6m. In 2022, Tuvalu's foreign minister delivered a speech to COP27 standing knee-deep in seawater to illustrate the threat.

Social and Economic Impacts:

  • Tourism industry under threat — tourism accounts for 28% of Maldives' GDP. Coral reef bleaching (driven by ocean warming and acidification) is destroying the marine ecosystem that attracts visitors. Nearly all coral in the Maldives bleached in 1998 and again in 2016.
  • Freshwater crisis — saltwater intrusion into groundwater supplies contaminates the freshwater lens on which atolls depend. The Maldives now relies heavily on desalination — expensive and energy-intensive.
  • Forced relocation planning — the Maldives government is purchasing land in Sri Lanka, India, and Australia as a potential homeland of last resort. The government has announced plans to raise artificial islands (like Hulhumalé) and reclaim land to create higher ground.
  • Kiribati has purchased 6,000 acres of land in Fiji (the "Kiribati Purchase") as a potential relocation site for its 120,000 citizens. This represents the first planned relocation of an entire nation due to climate change.
  • Climate justice — the Maldives emits approximately 0.0003% of global CO₂. The country's president has described the situation as a "death sentence" for his people, and the Maldives has been a vocal advocate at COP summits for the 1.5°C target and for Loss and Damage funding.

Responses:

  • The Maldives government has invested heavily in sea walls, storm barriers, and elevated infrastructure, but these are short-term measures that cannot address long-term inundation.
  • The country aims to become carbon-neutral by 2030 — a symbolic gesture given its minimal emissions — to demonstrate international leadership.
  • The Alliance of Small Island States (AOSIS) — representing 44 countries — has been a powerful voice in international climate negotiations, advocating for the 1.5°C target enshrined in the Paris Agreement.

Exam point: The Maldives and Tuvalu are powerful examples for answering questions about climate justice, the differential impact of climate change, and the gap between those responsible for emissions and those who suffer the consequences.

7. Physical Impacts — Positive Feedback Loops

Positive feedback loops amplify warming and are one of the most alarming aspects of climate science. They mean that warming triggers further warming.

Other key positive feedback loops include:

  • Permafrost-methane feedback — as Arctic permafrost thaws, it releases methane and CO₂ stored for millennia (the permafrost carbon pool is estimated at 1,500 billion tonnes of carbon — nearly double the current atmospheric carbon stock). This additional CO₂ and methane causes further warming, thawing more permafrost.
  • Water vapour feedback — as temperatures rise, more water evaporates. Water vapour is itself a powerful greenhouse gas, amplifying warming. This approximately doubles the warming effect of CO₂ alone.
  • Forest dieback — warmer temperatures and changing rainfall patterns cause forest die-off and more wildfires. Dead/burning forest releases stored carbon, reducing carbon sink capacity and accelerating warming.

10. International Responses and Agreements

Kyoto Protocol (1997)

Adopted at COP3 in Kyoto, Japan. Entered into force in 2005 after Russia's ratification provided the required threshold.

  • Key provisions: The first legally binding international climate treaty. Set specific emission reduction targets for developed (Annex I) countries: an average of 5.2% below 1990 levels by the commitment period 2008–2012. Created three "flexible mechanisms" — Emissions Trading, the Clean Development Mechanism (CDM), and Joint Implementation — to allow countries to meet targets cost-effectively.
  • Strengths: First legally binding treaty; created an international framework; carbon markets (including the EU ETS) developed out of Kyoto mechanisms; some countries (EU) did meet targets.
  • Weaknesses: USA signed but never ratified (Senate refused). Did not include developing nations (China, India, Brazil) — who argued their historical emissions were low and they had a right to develop. Canada withdrew in 2011. By the second commitment period (2012–2020), only 37 countries participated. Global emissions continued to rise throughout the Kyoto period. The Protocol is now widely viewed as having failed to address the scale of the problem.

Copenhagen Accord (2009)

COP15, Copenhagen, Denmark. Widely seen as a diplomatic failure. World leaders were expected to agree a binding successor to Kyoto, but negotiations collapsed. The resulting non-binding "accord" was "noted" (not formally adopted). Countries submitted voluntary pledges but the accord included no binding emission targets. However, it was the first agreement to include the principle of limiting warming to 2°C and established the concept of $100 billion per year in climate finance from rich to poor nations by 2020 — a target that was not fully met until 2023.

Paris Agreement (2015)

COP21, Paris, France. Adopted by 196 parties — the most universally agreed international climate deal. Entered into force 4 November 2016.

  • Temperature target: Limit global warming to well below 2°C above pre-industrial levels, and pursue efforts to limit it to 1.5°C. The 1.5°C target was a major diplomatic victory for small island states.
  • Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs): Each country sets its own emissions reduction pledges. NDCs must be submitted every five years and are expected to become progressively more ambitious ("ratchet mechanism"). NDCs are not legally binding — only the obligation to submit them is binding.
  • Climate finance: Developed nations reaffirmed the commitment to mobilise $100 billion per year by 2020 to help LICs adapt and reduce emissions. A new, higher target is to be set for post-2025.
  • Loss and Damage: Recognised the need to address climate impacts that exceed adaptation capacity, but the mechanism was limited.
  • Strengths: Near-universal participation (195 signatories); regular review mechanism; 1.5°C ambition is meaningful; includes LICs in the framework; transparent reporting requirements.
  • Weaknesses: NDCs are not legally binding; current collective pledges put the world on track for approximately 2.5–3°C of warming, far exceeding the 1.5°C target (UNEP Emissions Gap Report 2023); climate finance target of $100bn often fell short; USA withdrew under President Trump (2017–2021) then rejoined under Biden; developing nations argue the burden is unfair given historical emissions.

COP26 — Glasgow (2021)

  • Glasgow Climate Pact: Called for phasing down (not out) "unabated" coal power and phasing out "inefficient" fossil fuel subsidies — historic first mention of fossil fuels in a COP decision but language was weakened by India/China at the last moment.
  • Deforestation pledge: Over 130 countries, covering 90% of world's forests, pledged to halt and reverse deforestation by 2030, backed by $19 billion in public and private funding.
  • Global Methane Pledge: Over 100 countries pledged to reduce methane emissions 30% below 2020 levels by 2030 — significant given methane's high short-term warming potential.
  • Finance: $130 trillion in private finance pledged via the Glasgow Financial Alliance for Net Zero (GFANZ) — though the quality and accountability of these pledges was widely questioned.
  • NDC ratchet: Countries with insufficient NDCs asked to submit stronger plans by end of 2022 — compliance was mixed.
  • Weaknesses: "Unabated coal" loophole allows continued use with CCS. Deforestation pledge not legally binding (a similar pledge in 2014 from New York had little effect). $100bn climate finance target was again not met. Loss and Damage fund not established (only agreed at COP28).

COP28 — Dubai (2023)

  • First agreement to explicitly mention "transitioning away from fossil fuels" — a historic first, though "phase-out" language was rejected by oil-producing states.
  • Loss and Damage Fund formally established and operationalised — $700 million pledged in initial contributions (compared to estimated need of $400 billion per year).
  • Tripling of renewable energy capacity globally by 2030 agreed.
  • Hosted by the UAE (a major oil producer), with Sultan Al Jaber — the CEO of Abu Dhabi National Oil Company — as COP28 president, attracting significant controversy over conflict of interest.

Key Criticisms of International Climate Agreements

  • Non-binding nature — without legal enforcement, countries can fall short without penalty. The difference between Kyoto (binding targets) and Paris (voluntary NDCs) reflects the political reality that binding agreements are hard to achieve globally.
  • Historical responsibility vs current emissions — the USA and EU have emitted the majority of cumulative GHGs since the Industrial Revolution. China and India argue they have a right to develop economically as HICs did. China is now the world's largest annual emitter (~27%) but its per-capita emissions (~8 tonnes CO₂/year) remain below the USA (~14 tonnes CO₂/year).
  • Insufficient ambition — UNEP Emissions Gap Report (2023): current policies put world on track for 2.5–3°C warming. A 45% emissions reduction by 2030 is needed to stay on the 1.5°C pathway. Only a handful of countries have NDCs consistent with 1.5°C.
  • Climate finance shortfall — the $100bn/year commitment to LICs was not met until 2023, three years late; much of the "finance" is loans rather than grants, increasing debt burdens on vulnerable nations. Estimates of LIC adaptation needs range from $160bn–$340bn per year by 2030.

11. UK-Specific Impacts of Climate Change

The UK is warming at a rate consistent with global trends. The UK Climate Change Risk Assessment (CCRA3, 2022) identifies urgent risks for the UK across multiple sectors.

Flooding

  • The UK's wettest year on record was 2000, closely followed by 2019 and 2020. Atmospheric rivers and more intense low-pressure systems are linked to climate change.
  • Winter 2019–20 flooding — Storm Dennis and Storm Ciara brought record rainfall to Yorkshire, Derbyshire, and Wales; the River Calder at Sowerby Bridge (West Yorkshire) reached its highest ever recorded level. Over 4,600 homes flooded; estimated costs exceeded £500 million.
  • By 2050, the number of properties at significant flood risk in England could increase from 520,000 to over 1.5 million (Climate Change Committee).
  • Surface water flooding (sewers overwhelmed by intense rainfall) is a growing problem in urban areas — Storm Babet (2023) caused major surface water flooding across Scotland.

Heatwaves

  • July 2022 heatwave — temperatures reached 40.3°C at Coningsby, Lincolnshire — the first time 40°C was ever recorded in the UK. The previous record was 38.7°C (2019). The Met Office had never before issued a Red Heat Warning. Estimated 3,000 excess deaths attributed to the heat.
  • UK infrastructure was ill-prepared: road surfaces buckled, railway lines warped (requiring speed restrictions), Luton Airport runway damaged. Schools were not equipped with adequate ventilation or shading.
  • Climate attribution studies suggest the July 2022 temperatures were made at least 10 times more likely by human-caused climate change.
  • By 2050, summers as hot as 2022 could become the average in southern England under medium-emissions scenarios.

Agricultural Change

  • Potential benefits (short term): Warmer temperatures extend the growing season by approximately 4–6 weeks for some crops. New crops becoming viable in southern England — maize, sunflowers, and certain wine grape varieties now grown commercially. The UK wine industry has expanded dramatically: over 900 vineyards, producing award-winning sparkling wines in Kent and Sussex.
  • Risks: Increased summer drought threatens rain-fed agriculture, particularly in eastern England (already the driest region, receiving less rainfall than Jerusalem). Soil moisture deficits have increased since the 1970s. New pests and diseases moving northward (e.g. bluetongue virus, spread by midges — reached UK in 2023 for first time). Apple orchards in Kent threatened by late spring frosts (warming winters mean trees lose frost-hardiness; late cold snaps then kill blossom).
  • Oilseed rape (canola) yields have declined as cabbage stem flea beetle infestations have worsened — partly linked to warmer winters that allow beetle populations to survive at higher densities.

Coastal Change

  • UK sea levels have risen by approximately 10–20cm since 1900. Thames Estuary rising at 3.5mm/year (including land subsidence).
  • East Yorkshire's Holderness coast loses an average of 2 metres per year to erosion — the fastest-eroding coastline in Europe. Villages including Skipsea and Atwick are at severe risk within decades. The village of Mappleton spent £2 million on rock groynes in 1991 to protect itself, but accelerated erosion of neighbouring unprotected coast resulted.
  • Managed retreat is increasingly recognised as necessary for low-value agricultural land along vulnerable coasts.

Biodiversity in the UK

  • Over 60% of UK species have shifted their ranges northward since the 1970s, tracking warming temperatures.
  • New species arriving: little egrets, ring-necked parakeets, and bee-eaters now breeding in the UK. Edible dormice spreading.
  • Species declining: mountain hares, capercaillie, dotterel, and other cold-adapted upland species losing habitat as snowfall decreases in Scottish Highlands. Puffin colonies threatened by sand eel decline (sand eels moving northward as waters warm, reducing food availability for puffins).

12. Individual Actions

While global policy is essential, individual behaviour contributes to the problem and can be part of the solution. However, the relative impact of individual vs. systemic change is debated.

Carbon footprint — the total amount of greenhouse gases generated by an individual's activities, expressed as CO₂ equivalent (CO₂e). UK average: ~10 tonnes CO₂e/year. Global sustainable level: ~2 tonnes CO₂e/year. USA average: ~14 tonnes CO₂e/year. Bangladesh average: ~0.5 tonnes CO₂e/year.

Some critics argue that focusing on individual carbon footprints (a concept popularised by BP's 2004 advertising campaign) shifts responsibility from corporations and governments to individuals. Systemic change (policy, infrastructure, corporate regulation) is needed alongside individual action to achieve emissions reductions at the scale and speed required.

13. Exam Tips and Technique

Using Specific Data: Every factual point you make in an exam should include specific data where possible. Weak answer: "The Arctic is melting and this is affecting polar bears." Strong answer: "Arctic sea ice extent has declined by 13% per decade since 1979, reducing polar bears' hunting platform; the Western Hudson Bay population has declined by 30% since the 1980s as a result."
Cause vs Effect vs Response: Many climate change questions ask you to explain causes, describe effects, or evaluate responses. Keep these clearly separate. Causes = GHGs from human activities (+ natural factors). Effects = physical and human impacts. Responses = mitigation and adaptation strategies. Do not confuse them in an answer.
Natural vs Human Causes (a common question): Always acknowledge both exist, then explain why human causes are dominant. Use three arguments: (1) rate of warming too fast to be natural — 1°C in 150 years; (2) CO₂ at 422 ppm — highest in 800,000 years; (3) isotopic fingerprinting confirms fossil fuel CO₂. Conclude that IPCC (2021) states it is "unequivocal" that human influence has caused warming.
Mitigation vs Adaptation Evaluation Question: A common 8-mark question asks which is more important or more effective. Structure: define both → give examples of each → evaluate (mitigation = tackles root cause but slow and requires global cooperation; adaptation = essential now but has limits and does not stop long-term problem) → conclude with a clear, justified judgement. Avoid sitting on the fence — examiners want a clear conclusion.
Climate Justice and Inequalities: Always use the concept of climate justice when evaluating impacts or responses. Key points: LICs contribute least to emissions but face greatest impacts; HICs have historical responsibility for cumulative emissions; climate finance from HICs to LICs is a matter of justice not charity. This perspective is excellent for evaluation marks on 8-mark questions.
Structure for 8-Mark Evaluation Questions: Use this framework — (1) State the argument/strategy clearly; (2) Explain how it works; (3) Give a specific named example with statistics; (4) Evaluate — what works well and what are the limitations; (5) Consider the opposing view or an alternative; (6) Conclude with an overall justified judgement using "overall" or "on balance." Use connectives: "however," "nevertheless," "on the other hand," "despite this," "in contrast," "this suggests that."
Positive Feedback Loops — a Frequent Examiner Favourite: Know the ice-albedo feedback loop in detail — it appears regularly. Step by step: warming → ice melts → dark ocean exposed → albedo falls from 85% to 6% → more solar radiation absorbed → more warming → more ice melts. Also know the permafrost-methane loop. Use the phrase "positive feedback loop" explicitly in your answer for full marks — this shows precise geographical literacy.
International Agreements Evaluation: For Paris Agreement questions, know: the 1.5°C target, NDCs (voluntary, reviewed every 5 years), $100bn climate finance, Loss and Damage. The key weakness is NDCs are not legally binding — current pledges = 2.5–3°C. Compare to Kyoto (legally binding but excluded major emitters and USA never ratified). Conclude: Paris has broader participation but weaker enforcement — the question is whether voluntary action is enough. Evidence suggests it is not without further strengthening.
UK Case Study Reminders: For UK-specific questions, use: July 2022 heatwave (40.3°C, 3,000 excess deaths); Winter 2019–20 flooding (Yorkshire, £500m+); Thames Barrier (closed 200+ times); Holderness coast erosion (2m/year); Kent/Sussex wine industry (growing season lengthening). These are high-quality UK-specific examples that demonstrate geographical knowledge at the GCSE level expected by Eduqas.
