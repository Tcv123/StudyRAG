3.2 Climate Change

1. Defining Climate Change

Weather — the short-term atmospheric conditions at a specific time and place (temperature, rainfall, wind speed, cloud cover). Can change hour by hour.
Climate — the long-term average pattern of weather conditions for a region, calculated over at least 30 years. Changes slowly over decades or millennia.
Climate Change — a long-term shift in global average temperatures and precipitation patterns, beyond natural variability. The current concern is the unprecedented rate of warming since industrialisation (~1750) driven largely by human activities.

The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) — the UN scientific body that reviews global climate research — concluded in its 2021 report (AR6): "It is unequivocal that human influence has warmed the atmosphere, ocean and land."

3. Natural Causes of Climate Change

Milankovitch Cycles

Cyclical changes in Earth's orbit and orientation relative to the Sun drive natural climate cycles over tens of thousands of years:

  • Eccentricity (~100,000 years) — shape of Earth's orbit changes from nearly circular to more elliptical. When more elliptical, seasonal variation is greater.
  • Axial tilt (~41,000 years) — angle of Earth's axis varies from 22.1° to 24.5°. Greater tilt = more extreme seasons (hotter summers, colder winters).
  • Precession (~26,000 years) — Earth's axis wobbles like a spinning top. Changes which hemisphere experiences summer closest to the Sun.

Together, these cycles explain the glacial and interglacial periods (ice ages) of the past 2.6 million years. However, they cannot explain the rapid warming since 1750 — Milankovitch cycles operate over far longer timescales.

Other Natural Factors

  • Volcanic eruptions — large eruptions inject sulphur dioxide (SO₂) into the stratosphere, forming sulphate aerosols that reflect incoming solar radiation. Causes short-term cooling. Mt Pinatubo (Philippines, 1991): global average temperature fell ~0.5°C for 1–2 years. Laki (Iceland, 1783): caused famines across Europe. Note: short-term cooling effect, not long-term warming.
  • Solar output (sunspot activity) — Sun's energy output varies by ~0.1% over an 11-year sunspot cycle. Some scientists link reduced sunspot activity (Maunder Minimum, 1645–1715) to the Little Ice Age. Effect is minor and cannot explain the current warming trend.
Exam Tip: A common exam question asks you to distinguish between natural and human causes of climate change, then explain why scientists believe human causes are dominant. Key arguments: the rate of warming is far faster than any natural Milankovitch cycle; ice core data shows CO₂ levels are unprecedented in 800,000 years; the warming correlates precisely with industrialisation, not with natural cycles.

4. Human Causes — The Enhanced Greenhouse Effect

Natural Greenhouse Effect — essential for life on Earth. Without it, average global temperature would be −18°C (instead of current +15°C). GHGs (CO₂, water vapour, methane) in the atmosphere trap some of the longwave infrared radiation emitted by Earth's surface.

Enhanced Greenhouse Effect — human activities increasing the concentration of GHGs in the atmosphere, trapping more heat and causing global warming.

Current CO₂ levels: ~422 ppm (parts per million) in 2024 — the highest in at least 800,000 years (Vostok ice core data). Pre-industrial level was ~280 ppm. Rate of increase is ~2.5 ppm per year.

Role of Deforestation

Forests act as carbon sinks — absorbing CO₂ from the atmosphere through photosynthesis. Deforestation contributes ~10% of global GHG emissions in two ways:

  1. Burning or decomposing trees releases stored carbon as CO₂.
  2. Fewer trees means less CO₂ is absorbed — reducing the natural sink capacity.

Amazon deforestation is a major concern — the Amazon has been deforested by ~20% since 1970. Some scientists warn it may reach a "tipping point" where it switches from a carbon sink to a carbon source.

5. Impacts of Climate Change — Physical

  • Rising sea levels — caused by: (1) thermal expansion of warming ocean water (responsible for ~40% of rise); (2) melting land ice (glaciers, Greenland and Antarctic ice sheets). Threatens low-lying areas: Bangladesh (~30% could be flooded with 1m rise), Maldives (average elevation 1.5m above sea level), Tuvalu, Thames Estuary, Florida, Netherlands.
  • More frequent and intense extreme weather events — warmer oceans provide more energy for hurricanes/typhoons; more evaporation leads to more intense rainfall and flooding; droughts more severe as evaporation increases. Australian Black Summer bushfires (2019–20): record temperatures (+4°C above average), 18 million hectares burned, 3 billion animals killed. California drought (2012–2017): worst in 1,200 years — reservoir levels fell to single digits %.
  • Arctic melting and the albedo feedback loop (positive feedback):
    1. Arctic sea ice melts
    2. Dark ocean water exposed (low albedo — absorbs 94% of solar radiation vs. ice which reflects 80–90%)
    3. Ocean absorbs more heat → more warming → more ice melts
    The Arctic is warming 4x faster than the global average.
  • Ocean acidification — oceans absorb ~26% of human CO₂ emissions. CO₂ dissolves in seawater to form carbonic acid (H₂CO₃), lowering ocean pH. Since pre-industrial times, ocean pH has fallen from 8.2 to 8.1 (30% more acidic). Impacts: coral bleaching (Great Barrier Reef lost 50% of coral since 1995), shellfish shells weakened, marine food webs disrupted.
  • Permafrost thaw (positive feedback) — frozen ground in Arctic regions contains vast stores of methane and CO₂ (twice as much carbon as currently in the atmosphere). As permafrost thaws, these gases are released, accelerating warming further. Siberian permafrost thaw releasing measurable methane increases already detected.
  • Ecosystem disruption — coral reef bleaching, polar bear habitat loss (Arctic sea ice loss reducing hunting season), disruption of migration patterns, species extinction risk (IPCC: 18–35% of species at risk with 3°C warming).

Exam Tip: Climate change impacts are geographically unequal — LICs are most vulnerable despite contributing least to the problem. This "climate justice" argument is key for evaluation questions. HIC carbon emissions cause disproportionate harm to LIC populations who lack resources to adapt.

Exam Tip: Mitigation vs adaptation is a common comparison question. Key distinction: mitigation tackles the cause (reducing GHG emissions); adaptation tackles the effects (adjusting to changes). Both are necessary. Some argue adaptation is increasingly essential because current pledges are insufficient to prevent 2°C of warming.

Key Criticisms of International Climate Agreements

  • Non-binding nature — without legal enforcement, countries can fall short without penalty.
  • Historical responsibility vs current emissions — HICs are responsible for the majority of cumulative GHG emissions (USA + Europe have emitted more than China since 1850), yet LICs bear the worst impacts. China and India argue they have a right to develop like HICs did.
  • Insufficient ambition — UNEP Emissions Gap Report (2023): current policies put world on track for 2.5–3°C warming, far exceeding the 1.5°C Paris target. 45% emissions reduction needed by 2030 to stay on track.

10. Individual Actions

While global policy is essential, individual behaviour contributes to the problem and can be part of the solution. However, the relative impact of individual vs. systemic change is debated.

Carbon footprint — the total amount of greenhouse gases generated by an individual's activities, expressed as CO₂ equivalent (CO₂e). UK average: ~10 tonnes CO₂e/year. Global sustainable level: ~2 tonnes CO₂e/year.

Some critics argue that focusing on individual carbon footprints (a concept popularised by BP's 2004 advertising campaign) shifts responsibility from corporations and governments to individuals. Systemic change (policy, infrastructure, corporate regulation) is needed alongside individual action to achieve emissions at the scale required.

Exam Tip: For 8-mark evaluation questions on climate change responses, structure your answer: state the strategy → explain how it works → give a specific example → evaluate its effectiveness (advantages AND disadvantages/limitations) → conclude with a justified overall judgement. Use connectives like "however," "nevertheless," and "overall" to signal evaluation.
