3.1 Tectonic Hazards

1. Structure of the Earth

The Earth is made up of four concentric layers, each with different properties:

Convection currents in the mantle are the driving force behind plate movement. Hot magma rises, spreads laterally, cools, then sinks again — dragging tectonic plates with it. This process is called seafloor spreading at mid-ocean ridges.

2. Plate Tectonics

Theory of Continental Drift (Wegener, 1912)

Alfred Wegener proposed that today's continents were once joined as a supercontinent called Pangaea. Evidence:

  • Jigsaw fit — coastlines of S. America and Africa fit together remarkably closely.
  • Fossil evidence — identical fossils of Glossopteris (a fern) and Mesosaurus (a freshwater reptile) found on opposite sides of the Atlantic — impossible if they had always been separated by ocean.
  • Rock matching — identical rock sequences found in Brazil and West Africa.
  • Mid-ocean ridges — new oceanic crust forms at mid-ocean ridges as plates pull apart.
  • Paleomagnetism — alternating bands of magnetic polarity on the seafloor either side of mid-ocean ridges prove seafloor is spreading symmetrically.
Tectonic plates — rigid segments of Earth's crust and upper mantle (lithosphere) that float on the semi-molten asthenosphere. Major plates: Eurasian, North American, South American, African, Indo-Australian, Pacific (largest), Antarctic. Several smaller plates: Juan de Fuca, Caribbean, Philippine, etc.

3. Constructive (Divergent) Plate Boundaries

  • Plates move apart; magma rises through the gap and solidifies, forming new oceanic crust.
  • Process creates: mid-ocean ridges (underwater mountain chains), rift valleys, and shield volcanoes.
  • Mid-Atlantic Ridge — Iceland sits on this ridge. Iceland experiences frequent volcanic eruptions (Eyjafjallajokull 2010; Fagradalsfjall eruptions 2021–2023) due to its position on the constructive boundary.
  • East African Rift Valley — continental divergence creating a rift valley (Lakes Tanganyika, Malawi, Turkana).
  • Volcanoes: Shield volcanoes — wide, gently sloping, basaltic (low-viscosity) lava, less explosive. Example: Mauna Loa, Hawaii; Etna (partly).
  • Earthquakes: Generally shallow focus and low magnitude — less destructive.

4. Destructive (Convergent) Plate Boundaries

Ocean-Continent (Subduction)

  • Denser oceanic crust subducts beneath lighter continental crust into the mantle — forms a subduction zone and deep-sea trench.
  • Oceanic crust melts in the Benioff zone; magma is forced upward through the continental crust, forming composite (stratovolcanoes): steep, explosive, andesitic lava (high viscosity). Examples: Andes (Chile, Peru), Cascade Range (Mt St Helens, USA — erupted 1980), Mt Pinatubo (Philippines — erupted 1991).
  • Earthquakes: deep focus (Benioff zone), high magnitude — very destructive.

Ocean-Ocean Convergence

  • One oceanic plate subducts under the other. Forms: deep-sea trenches (Mariana Trench — deepest point on Earth, 11,034m) and island arcs — chains of volcanic islands (Japan, Caribbean islands).

Continent-Continent Convergence

  • Neither plate subducts (both are too buoyant). Instead, crust crumples and folds upward to form fold mountain ranges. No volcanoes. Powerful earthquakes.
  • Example: Himalayas — Indo-Australian plate colliding with Eurasian plate. Still rising ~5mm/year. Nepal Earthquake 2015 a result of this collision zone.
  • Alps — African plate colliding with Eurasian.

5. Conservative (Transform) Plate Boundaries

  • Plates slide past each other horizontally — no crust created or destroyed.
  • No volcanic activity (no subduction, no magma generation).
  • Friction builds between plates — when released, produces very powerful earthquakes (shallow focus, extremely destructive near populated areas).
  • San Andreas Fault, California — North American plate vs Pacific plate (Pacific moving NW at ~5cm/year). San Francisco earthquake 1906 (estimated 7.9 magnitude). Los Angeles earthquake 1994 — Northridge, 6.7 magnitude, 57 deaths, $20bn damage.
  • The Dead Sea Transform fault (Jordan/Israel/Palestine) is another example.

Volcanic Hazards

  • Lava flows — slow-moving (most people can escape), but destroy property and farmland.
  • Pyroclastic flows — fast-moving (up to 700km/h), superheated (400–700°C) mixtures of gas, ash, and rock. Most deadly volcanic hazard. Killed ~29,000 in Mt Pelée, Martinique (1902).
  • Ash fall — suffocates crops, collapses buildings, disrupts air travel (Eyjafjallajokull 2010 — 100,000 flights cancelled).
  • Lahars — volcanic mudflows. Melted snow/ice mixes with ash. Armero, Colombia (1985): lahar from Nevado del Ruiz killed 23,000.
  • Volcanic gases — SO₂, CO₂ toxic; can cause acid rain.
  • Tsunamis — underwater eruptions can trigger tsunamis.

Benefits of Volcanoes

  • Fertile soils — volcanic ash weathered to nutrient-rich soils. Java (Indonesia) is one of the world's most densely populated areas due to highly fertile volcanic soils.
  • Geothermal energy — Iceland generates 25% of electricity from geothermal energy — heats 90% of homes.
  • Tourism — Mt Etna, Kilauea, Yellowstone attract millions of visitors.
  • Minerals — valuable minerals (copper, gold, sulphur) deposited around volcanoes.

7. Earthquakes

Focus — the point inside the Earth where an earthquake originates (where rock fractures/slips). Also called the hypocenter.
Epicentre — the point on the Earth's surface directly above the focus. Most severe shaking occurs here.
Seismic waves — energy waves that travel outward from the focus.

Types of Seismic Waves

  • P-waves (Primary) — compression waves; travel through solid and liquid; fastest; arrive first on seismograph.
  • S-waves (Secondary) — shear waves; only travel through solid; slower; cannot travel through Earth's liquid outer core.
  • Surface waves (Love and Rayleigh) — travel along Earth's surface; slowest but most destructive — cause most of the ground shaking and building damage.

Measuring Earthquakes

  • Richter Scale — measures magnitude (energy released). Logarithmic: each point = 10x more energy. 0–2 (not felt) → 5–6 (moderate damage) → 7+ (severe) → 9+ (extreme, rare).
  • Moment Magnitude Scale (Mw) — now preferred by scientists; similar logarithmic scale but more accurate for large earthquakes.
  • Mercalli Scale — measures intensity (damage and shaking felt at a location). Rated I–XII. Subjective but useful where seismometers are absent.

8. Tsunamis

A tsunami is a series of powerful ocean waves caused by a large underwater disturbance (earthquake, landslide, volcanic eruption). Most tsunamis are caused by submarine earthquakes — where the seafloor suddenly shifts vertically, displacing a vast column of water.

  • Deep water: wave speed ~800 km/h, wavelength 100–200 km, amplitude just 1–2m (often unnoticed at sea).
  • Shallow water (approaching coast): speed slows dramatically but wave height increases enormously ("shoaling") — can reach 30–40m.
  • Warning signs: sea withdraws rapidly from shore (draw-back) before the wave arrives.
2004 Indian Ocean Tsunami (Boxing Day Tsunami)
  • Cause: 9.1 magnitude submarine earthquake (destructive boundary — Indo-Australian plate subducting under Burma plate) 250km off Sumatra, Indonesia.
  • Scale: 230,000+ deaths across 14 countries (Indonesia, Sri Lanka, India, Thailand — worst). 1.7 million displaced.
  • Wave height: Up to 30m in Sumatra; 6–9m in Sri Lanka/India.
  • Speed: ~800 km/h across Indian Ocean — reached Sri Lanka in ~2 hours, Africa in ~7 hours.
  • Response: Led to creation of Indian Ocean Tsunami Warning System (2006). Pacific Tsunami Warning Centre (PTWC) already existed since 1949.

9. Case Study 1: Nepal Earthquake, April 2015 (LIC)

Nepal Earthquake — 25 April 2015 | 7.8 Magnitude

Location: 80km NW of Kathmandu, shallow focus (15km depth), continent-continent destructive boundary (Indo-Australian plate colliding with Eurasian plate — Himalayas).

Primary Effects

  • 9,000+ deaths; 23,000+ injured.
  • 600,000+ homes destroyed (mostly mud-brick construction).
  • 8,000+ schools and 1,000 health facilities damaged.
  • Historic Durbar Square, Kathmandu (UNESCO World Heritage Site) — temples collapsed.
  • Dharahara Tower (19th century) collapsed, killing 180 people sheltering nearby.

Secondary Effects

  • Massive landslides — Langtang village buried (350 people), trekking routes blocked.
  • Avalanches on Everest — 19 climbers killed at Base Camp; worst Everest disaster at the time.
  • 3 million people left homeless — displacement raised risk of disease (cholera, diarrhoea) as sanitation systems destroyed.
  • Crops destroyed ahead of harvest — food insecurity.
  • Tourism collapsed — 30% of GDP affected in the short term.

Why Was Impact So Severe? (LIC factors)

  • Poor building quality — most rural housing built from unmortared stone or mud brick. No earthquake-resistant design. Urban buildings often poorly constructed despite regulations.
  • Remote mountainous terrain — roads blocked by landslides; helicopter access only; aid reached many areas weeks late.
  • Population density — Kathmandu Valley densely populated (1.5 million+), high exposure.
  • Poverty — Nepal is one of Asia's poorest nations; GNI per capita ~$1,200. Limited government capacity to prepare or respond.

Responses

  • Immediate: International aid poured in — India first responder (NDRF teams). USA, UK, China, Pakistan, EU — $4.4bn pledged internationally. UN OCHA coordinated. Nepali Army deployed search and rescue.
  • Short-term issues: Aid bottleneck at Kathmandu airport (only one runway); looting of relief supplies; corruption allegations; political disputes delayed relief distribution.
  • Long-term: "Build Back Better" reconstruction programme — earthquake-resistant design. By 2020 (5 years later), only ~65% of damaged homes rebuilt. Slow bureaucracy, land disputes, funding mismanagement criticised heavily by survivors and NGOs.

10. Case Study 2: Japan Earthquake and Tsunami, March 2011 (HIC)

Tohoku Earthquake and Tsunami — 11 March 2011 | 9.0 Magnitude

Location: 70km off Sendai coast, NE Japan. Destructive boundary — Pacific plate subducting under North American/Eurasian plate (Japan Trench). Strongest earthquake ever recorded in Japan; 4th strongest globally since 1900.

Primary Effects

  • 15,900+ deaths (mostly by tsunami, not earthquake — buildings withstood shaking well).
  • 130,000+ buildings destroyed; 1 million+ damaged.
  • Fukushima Daiichi nuclear power plant — tsunami overwhelmed sea defences (14m wave vs 10m sea wall), causing cooling systems to fail — nuclear meltdown in 3 reactors. Level 7 nuclear emergency (same as Chernobyl).
  • Tsunami: heights up to 40m at some coastal points; travelled up to 10km inland; swept away entire coastal towns (Ishinomaki, Rikuzentakata).

Secondary Effects

  • Nuclear disaster: 154,000 people evacuated from 20km exclusion zone around Fukushima. Long-term radiation contamination of soil and sea. As of 2023, exclusion zones still partially in place.
  • Global nuclear energy debate reignited — Germany accelerated nuclear phase-out; Japan shut all 50 reactors for safety checks.
  • Supply chain disruption — Japan produces 60% of world's silicon wafers for semiconductors; global electronics industry affected for months.
  • Economic cost: ~$235bn — most expensive natural disaster in history.

Why Japan Coped Better (HIC factors)

  • Earthquake-resistant buildings — strict building codes enforced since 1981 (and revised after 1995 Kobe quake): cross-bracing, shock absorbers, base isolators, automatic gas shut-off.
  • Early warning system — Japan Meteorological Agency issued earthquake warning 90 seconds before strong shaking arrived; automated systems shut down trains and industrial machinery.
  • Tsunami warning — coastal warning issued within 3 minutes; evacuation sirens activated.
  • Regular drills — Disaster Prevention Day (1 September) — nationwide earthquake drills every year since 1960.
  • Organised emergency response — Self-Defence Forces (100,000 deployed), pre-positioned emergency supplies, efficient hospital system.

BUT — Limitations Even for an HIC

  • Tsunami overwhelmed sea walls designed for smaller events — underestimation of risk.
  • Nuclear disaster was unexpected and unprecedented — containment plans inadequate.
  • Evacuation traffic jams reduced escape rates in some coastal areas.
  • Elderly population (Japan has world's oldest population) — struggled to evacuate quickly.

11. Managing Tectonic Hazards

Management falls into four broad categories: Prediction, Protection, Planning, and Aid.

Prediction

  • Volcanoes — ground swelling (tiltmeters), increased seismic activity (seismometers), gas emissions (SO₂ and CO₂ levels rise before eruptions), thermal cameras detecting heat changes. Mt Pinatubo (1991) — successful 2-week warning: 60,000 people evacuated, saved thousands of lives.
  • Earthquakes — cannot be predicted with sufficient accuracy for evacuation. Seismometers detect P-waves seconds before damaging S/surface waves arrive (seconds to ~1 minute warning). Long-term probabilistic hazard mapping identifies high-risk areas.

Protection (Engineering)

  • Earthquake-resistant buildings: cross-bracing (steel frames), base isolators (rubber pads between building and foundations absorb shaking), counterweights (dampers on tall buildings — Taipei 101 has an 800-tonne pendulum), automatic gas shut-off, fire-resistant materials.
  • Tsunami sea walls: Japan's Kamaishi sea wall (4km long, 63m deep foundations, 8m above sea level) — largest in the world at construction. The 2011 tsunami overtopped it but it reduced wave height and gave more evacuation time.
  • Breakwaters and coastal barriers — Japan invested $12bn in tsunami sea walls along 400km of coastline after 2011.

Planning

  • Land-use zoning — restricting development in high-risk zones near faults or low-lying coasts.
  • Evacuation routes — clearly signed, regularly rehearsed.
  • Regular drills — Japan (annual), California (Great ShakeOut — 10 million+ participants).
  • Community education — knowing what to do in the first 72 hours (the critical "golden hour" window for survivors).

Aid

International response critical for LICs (Nepal, Haiti 2010). UNOCHA coordinates. Issues: logistics, corruption, dependency. Long-term reconstruction aid must focus on building back safer, not just rebuilding the same vulnerable structures.

Exam Tip: For "evaluate management strategies" questions, use the framework: Prediction is difficult for earthquakes (but useful for volcanoes); Protection is effective but expensive (only HICs can fully implement it); Planning is most cost-effective and works in both HICs and LICs; Aid is vital in the short term but must avoid dependency. Always refer to a named example for each strategy.
