3.1 Periodicity — Period 3

Periodicity: The repeating pattern of physical and chemical properties of elements across periods in the periodic table. This periodicity arises because electron configurations repeat in a systematic pattern — each new period begins filling a new principal energy level.

Trends Across Period 3 (Na to Ar)

PropertyTrend Na → ArExplanation
Atomic radiusDecreasesIncreasing nuclear charge pulls electrons closer; same number of shielding shells (all in period 3)
First ionisation energyGeneral increase with dips at Al and SIncreasing nuclear charge. Dip at Al: 3p electron is higher in energy and easier to remove than 3s (in Mg). Dip at S: the first paired 3p electron experiences repulsion, making it easier to remove than an unpaired 3p electron (in P).
ElectronegativityIncreases (Na to Cl)Increasing nuclear charge and decreasing atomic radius means bonding electrons are more strongly attracted. Noble gases are excluded (they do not normally form bonds).
Melting pointIncreases Na→Si, drops sharply at P, low P→ArNa, Mg, Al: metallic bonding strengthens (more delocalised electrons, smaller ionic radius). Si: giant covalent (very high mp). P, S, Cl, Ar: simple molecular structures with weak London forces (low mp).
Electrical conductivityHigh for Na, Mg, Al; semiconductor Si; none for P→ArMetals have delocalised electrons that carry charge. Si is a semiconductor (small band gap). Non-metals have no free electrons or ions.

Period 3 Oxides

There is a clear trend in the acid-base character of Period 3 oxides: metal oxides (Na2O, MgO) are basic, Al2O3 is amphoteric, and non-metal oxides (SiO2, P4O10, SO3) are acidic.

Period 3 Chlorides

Ionic chlorides (NaCl, MgCl2) dissolve in water — the ions separate and become hydrated. Covalent chlorides (AlCl3, SiCl4, PCl5) undergo hydrolysis — the covalent bonds are broken by water, producing HCl (white misty fumes in moist air) and an acidic solution.

SiCl4(l) + 2H2O(l) → SiO2(s) + 4HCl(g)
PCl5(s) + 4H2O(l) → H3PO4(aq) + 5HCl(g)
Exam Tip: The key distinction: ionic chlorides dissolve (ions separate but no new substances form), while covalent chlorides hydrolyse (bonds break, new substances form including HCl). The white misty fumes of HCl in moist air are a key observation to mention in exam answers about covalent chloride hydrolysis.

3.2 Group 1 — Alkali Metals

Group 1 metals (Li, Na, K, Rb, Cs) have one electron in their outer s sub-shell. They are the most reactive metals, readily losing this single electron to form M+ ions with a noble gas configuration.

Trends Down Group 1

PropertyTrendExplanation
Atomic radiusIncreasesAdditional electron shells
First ionisation energyDecreasesOuter electron further from nucleus, more shielded
ReactivityIncreasesEasier to lose the outer electron (lower IE)
Melting pointDecreasesMetallic bonding weakens as ionic radius increases and charge density decreases
DensityIncreases (general trend)Mass increases faster than volume

Reactions of Group 1 Metals

With water:

2M(s) + 2H2O(l) → 2MOH(aq) + H2(g)

Vigour increases down the group. Lithium fizzes gently on the surface. Sodium melts into a shiny ball, fizzes vigorously, and moves around on the surface. Potassium reacts even more vigorously and ignites the hydrogen gas with a lilac flame. Rubidium and caesium react explosively.

With oxygen: Lithium forms the normal oxide Li2O. Sodium forms the peroxide Na2O2. Potassium forms the superoxide KO2. All burn with characteristic flame colours.

With chlorine: 2M(s) + Cl2(g) → 2MCl(s). All form white ionic chlorides.

Flame colours: Li = crimson red, Na = yellow/orange, K = lilac.

3.3 Group 2 — Alkaline Earth Metals

Group 2 metals (Be, Mg, Ca, Sr, Ba) have two electrons in their outer s sub-shell. They form M2+ ions by losing both outer electrons.

Trends Down Group 2

PropertyTrendExplanation
Atomic radiusIncreasesMore electron shells
First ionisation energyDecreasesGreater distance and more shielding
ReactivityIncreasesEasier to remove two outer electrons
Melting pointGeneral decrease (irregular)Weaker metallic bonding as ionic radius increases

Reactions of Group 2

With water:

M(s) + 2H2O(l) → M(OH)2(aq) + H2(g)

Magnesium reacts very slowly with cold water (forms a thin Mg(OH)2 layer) but reacts vigorously with steam: Mg + H2O → MgO + H2. Calcium fizzes steadily in cold water. Strontium and barium react increasingly vigorously.

With oxygen: 2M(s) + O2(g) → 2MO(s). All burn with characteristic flame colours: Ca = orange-red, Sr = crimson red, Ba = pale green.

Thermal Stability of Group 2 Compounds

Carbonates: MCO3(s) → MO(s) + CO2(g). Thermal stability increases down the group (BaCO3 requires the highest temperature). This is because larger cations (e.g. Ba2+) have lower charge density and are less able to polarise and distort the carbonate ion. Smaller, more highly charged cations (e.g. Mg2+) have greater polarising power and can more easily destabilise the carbonate ion, making it decompose at lower temperatures.

Nitrates: 2M(NO3)2(s) → 2MO(s) + 4NO2(g) + O2(g). Same trend — stability increases down the group for the same reason.

Hydroxides: M(OH)2(s) → MO(s) + H2O(g). Same trend applies.

Solubility Trends in Group 2

Compound TypeSolubility Trend Down Group 2Key Examples

Exam Tip: The test for sulfate ions uses BaCl2(aq) with dilute HCl first (to remove carbonates/sulfites that would give false positives). A white precipitate of BaSO4 confirms sulfate is present. Do NOT use H2SO4 as the acid (it adds sulfate ions). The precipitate is insoluble in excess acid, confirming it is BaSO4 and not BaCO3.

3.4 Group 7 — Halogens

The halogens (F, Cl, Br, I) are non-metals that exist as diatomic molecules (X2). They have 7 outer electrons and gain one electron to form X- halide ions, or share one electron in covalent bonds.

Halogen Displacement Reactions

A more reactive (higher) halogen can oxidise (displace) a less reactive halide ion from solution. This is because the more reactive halogen is a stronger oxidising agent — it gains electrons more readily.

Halogen addedKCl(aq)KBr(aq)KI(aq)
Cl2(aq)No reactionOrange/brown solution (Br2 formed)Brown solution (I2 formed)
Br2(aq)No reactionNo reactionBrown/yellow solution (I2 formed)
I2(aq)No reactionNo reactionNo reaction

Example equation: Cl2(aq) + 2KBr(aq) → 2KCl(aq) + Br2(aq). Ionic equation: Cl2(aq) + 2Br-(aq) → 2Cl-(aq) + Br2(aq). Chlorine oxidises bromide ions; chlorine is reduced from 0 to -1, bromine is oxidised from -1 to 0.

Reactions of Halide Ions with Concentrated H2SO4

This reaction reveals the increasing reducing power of halide ions down the group:

  • NaCl + H2SO4: Steamy white fumes of HCl. Cl- is not a strong enough reducing agent to reduce the sulfuric acid. This is a straightforward acid-base reaction.
  • NaBr + H2SO4: Initially produces HBr (steamy fumes), but Br- can reduce H2SO4 to SO2 (choking gas). Orange/brown vapour of Br2 is also seen.
  • NaI + H2SO4: Produces HI initially, but I- is such a strong reducing agent that it reduces H2SO4 further to SO2, then to S (yellow solid), and even to H2S (rotten egg smell). Purple vapour of I2 is also produced.
Exam Tip: The reactions of halide ions with conc. H2SO4 are commonly examined. Know all the observations for each halide: Cl- gives only white fumes (HCl); Br- gives white fumes + orange vapour + choking SO2; I- gives all of these plus yellow solid (S), black solid/purple vapour (I2), and rotten egg gas (H2S). State the oxidation state changes.

Disproportionation of Chlorine

Cold dilute NaOH:

Cl2(aq) + 2NaOH(aq) → NaCl(aq) + NaClO(aq) + H2O(l)

Chlorine is simultaneously oxidised (0 → +1 in NaClO) and reduced (0 → -1 in NaCl). This is disproportionation.

Hot concentrated NaOH:

3Cl2(aq) + 6NaOH(aq) → 5NaCl(aq) + NaClO3(aq) + 3H2O(l)

Chlorine is reduced to -1 (in NaCl) and oxidised to +5 (in NaClO3).

Disproportionation: A reaction in which the same element in one species is simultaneously oxidised and reduced, forming two products with different oxidation states of that element.

Tests for Halide Ions

Add dilute HNO3 (to remove interfering carbonate and sulfite ions), then AgNO3(aq):

Halide IonPrecipitateColourSolubility in NH3
Cl-AgClWhiteSoluble in dilute NH3
Br-AgBrCreamSoluble in concentrated NH3 only
I-AgIYellowInsoluble in NH3 (even concentrated)

The ammonia test distinguishes them further: AgCl dissolves easily in dilute ammonia because the Ag+ forms a soluble diamminesilver(I) complex: AgCl + 2NH3 → [Ag(NH3)2]+ + Cl-. AgBr requires concentrated NH3. AgI is too insoluble even for concentrated NH3 to dissolve it.

Exam Tip: Always add dilute nitric acid before silver nitrate. If you use HCl, the Cl- from HCl gives a false positive. Never use HCl or H2SO4 for this test. If asked how to distinguish between the three precipitates, use the ammonia solubility test.

3.5 Transition Metals

Transition metal: A d-block element that forms at least one stable ion with an incomplete (partially filled) d sub-shell. This excludes scandium (Sc3+: [Ar] 3d0) and zinc (Zn2+: [Ar] 3d10) because their ions have empty or full d sub-shells.

Electron Configurations

Transition metals fill the 3d sub-shell after the 4s. When forming ions, electrons are removed from 4s before 3d. Anomalous configurations: Cr = [Ar] 3d5 4s1 (half-filled d is stable) and Cu = [Ar] 3d10 4s1 (full d is stable).

Characteristic Properties of Transition Metals

1. Variable Oxidation States

The 3d and 4s sub-shells are close in energy, so different numbers of electrons can be removed without a dramatic increase in ionisation energy. This allows multiple stable oxidation states.

MetalCommon oxidation statesKey species
V+2, +3, +4, +5V2+ (violet), V3+ (green), VO2+ (blue), VO2+ (yellow)
Cr+2, +3, +6Cr3+ (green), Cr2O72- (orange), CrO42- (yellow)
Mn+2, +4, +7Mn2+ (pale pink), MnO2 (black solid), MnO4- (purple)
Fe+2, +3Fe2+ (pale green), Fe3+ (yellow/brown)
Cu+1, +2Cu+ (white/colourless), Cu2+ (blue)

2. Coloured Ions (d-d Transitions)

When ligands surround a transition metal ion, the five d orbitals split into two energy levels (crystal field splitting). Electrons absorb visible light of energy equal to the splitting energy (Δ) to transition from the lower to the upper level. The colour observed is the complementary colour of the light absorbed.

Factors affecting colour (and Δ): the identity of the metal ion, its oxidation state, the type of ligands, and the coordination number/geometry.

Why Sc3+ and Zn2+ are colourless: Sc3+ has 3d0 (no d electrons to promote), and Zn2+ has 3d10 (no empty d orbitals to promote into).

3. Catalytic Activity

Transition metals are effective catalysts because their variable oxidation states allow them to accept and donate electrons, providing alternative reaction pathways with lower activation energy.

4. Complex Ion Formation

Complex ion: A central metal ion surrounded by ligands bonded through dative (coordinate) bonds.
Ligand: A species with a lone pair that donates it to the central metal ion.
Coordination number: The total number of dative bonds to the central metal ion.
Ligand TypeExamplesBonds per ligand
MonodentateH2O, NH3, Cl-, CN-, OH-1
BidentateEthane-1,2-diamine (en), ethanedioate (C2O42-)2
HexadentateEDTA4-6

Common shapes:

  • Coordination number 6 → Octahedral (90°): [Cu(H2O)6]2+, [Fe(CN)6]3-
  • Coordination number 4 → Tetrahedral (109.5°): [CuCl4]2-, [CoCl4]2-
  • Coordination number 4 → Square planar (90°): cisplatin [PtCl2(NH3)2]
  • Coordination number 2 → Linear (180°): [Ag(NH3)2]+

Ligand Substitution Reactions

Copper(II) complexes:

[Cu(H2O)6]2+ + 4Cl- ⇌ [CuCl4]2- + 6H2O

Pale blue → yellow-green. Octahedral (6) → tetrahedral (4). Cl- is larger than H2O, so only 4 can fit.

[Cu(H2O)6]2+ + 4NH3 → [Cu(NH3)4(H2O)2]2+ + 4H2O

Pale blue → deep blue. Coordination number stays at 6 (octahedral). Only 4 of 6 waters replaced (partial substitution) because NH3 and H2O are similar in size.

The Chelate Effect

Substitution by polydentate ligands (bidentate, hexadentate) is thermodynamically favourable because it increases entropy. Replacing 6 monodentate H2O ligands with 3 bidentate en ligands increases the number of free species in solution (from 4 species on the left to 7 on the right), giving a positive ΔS and hence a more negative ΔG.

Exam Tip: When describing ligand substitution: state (1) the colour change, (2) any change in coordination number, (3) any change in shape, (4) whether substitution is partial or complete, (5) write the full balanced equation with square brackets and charges. For chelate effect questions, explain entropy increase in terms of the number of particles.

Procedure: Clean a nichrome wire loop by dipping it in concentrated HCl and holding it in a blue Bunsen flame until no colour is produced. Then dip the wire into the sample (or into HCl and then the solid sample) and hold in the flame. Record the flame colour.

Exam Tip: Flame test colours are caused by electrons in metal ions being excited to higher energy levels by the flame energy, then falling back down and emitting light of specific wavelengths. Each metal ion has a unique set of energy levels, producing a characteristic colour. Sodium contamination (yellow flame) is very common — view potassium's lilac flame through blue glass to filter out the yellow.
