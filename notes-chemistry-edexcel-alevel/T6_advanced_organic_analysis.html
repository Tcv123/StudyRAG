6.1 Aromatic Chemistry — Benzene

Structure of Benzene

Benzene (C6H6) is a planar, cyclic molecule. The Kekule model of alternating single and double bonds is incorrect because:

  • All six C-C bonds are the same length (0.140 nm), intermediate between single (0.154 nm) and double (0.134 nm) bonds.
  • The enthalpy of hydrogenation is 152 kJ mol-1 less exothermic than expected for three isolated C=C bonds, demonstrating extra thermodynamic stability (delocalisation energy).
  • Benzene undergoes electrophilic substitution rather than addition, preserving the aromatic ring stability.
Delocalised model: Each carbon is sp2 hybridised with one unhybridised p orbital perpendicular to the ring. The six p orbitals overlap sideways to form a continuous ring of delocalised π electron density above and below the plane, stabilising the molecule.
hyd should be -360 kJ/mol vs Delocalised (correct) All bonds equal, substitution not addition ΔHhyd = -208 kJ/mol (152 kJ more stable) 6 p-orbitals overlap → delocalised π system → extra thermodynamic stability

Electrophilic Substitution of Benzene

Benzene reacts with electrophiles via substitution (not addition), preserving aromatic stability. The mechanism has three steps: (1) generate the electrophile, (2) π electrons attack electrophile forming a Wheland intermediate (non-aromatic, positively charged), (3) H+ is lost to restore aromaticity.

Nitration

C6H6 + HNO3 → C6H5NO2 + H2O

Conditions: Conc. HNO3 + conc. H2SO4, 50°C. Electrophile: NO2+ (nitronium ion) from HNO3 + H2SO4 → NO2+ + HSO4- + H2O. H2SO4 is regenerated (catalyst).

Friedel-Crafts Acylation

C6H6 + RCOCl → C6H5COR + HCl

Conditions: RCOCl + AlCl3 catalyst, reflux, anhydrous. Electrophile: RCO+ from RCOCl + AlCl3 → RCO+ + AlCl4-.

Friedel-Crafts Alkylation

C6H6 + RCl → C6H5R + HCl

Conditions: RCl + AlCl3. Electrophile: R+. Alkylation can lead to polysubstitution (alkyl groups activate the ring); acylation is preferred for monosubstitution.

Phenol

Phenol (C6H5OH) has an -OH directly on the ring. The oxygen lone pair delocalises into the ring, increasing electron density. Consequences:

  • More reactive towards electrophilic substitution than benzene (reacts with Br2(aq) at room temperature without a catalyst, giving a white precipitate of 2,4,6-tribromophenol)
  • More acidic than aliphatic alcohols (pKa = 10). The phenoxide ion is stabilised by delocalisation. But weaker than carboxylic acids — does NOT react with Na2CO3.

Reduction of Nitrobenzene to Phenylamine

C6H5NO2 + 6[H] → C6H5NH2 + 2H2O

Conditions: Sn + conc. HCl, reflux. Then add excess NaOH to liberate the free amine.

Exam Tip: In electrophilic substitution mechanisms: show the electrophile generation, a curly arrow from the ring π system to the electrophile, the Wheland intermediate with a + charge, and loss of H+ to restore aromaticity. For Friedel-Crafts, also show regeneration of AlCl3.

6.2 Aldehydes and Ketones

Aldehyde (RCHO): Carbonyl at end of chain (bonded to at least one H).
Ketone (RCOR'): Carbonyl within chain (bonded to two C atoms).
Both contain the polar C=O (δ+C=Oδ-), making the carbon electrophilic and susceptible to nucleophilic addition.

Nucleophilic Addition with HCN

RCHO + HCN → RCH(OH)CN   (hydroxynitrile)

Conditions: NaCN/KCN in dilute H2SO4 (generates HCN in situ). CN- is the nucleophile.

Mechanism: (1) CN- lone pair attacks δ+ C of C=O, π bond breaks, electrons move to O, forming alkoxide RCH(CN)O-. (2) O- protonated by HCN → -OH formed, CN- regenerated.

Key points: Extends carbon chain by 1. Creates a chiral centre → racemic mixture. The -CN can be hydrolysed to -COOH or reduced to -CH2NH2.

Reduction with NaBH4

NaBH4 provides H- (hydride) as the nucleophile. Aldehydes → primary alcohols. Ketones → secondary alcohols. Conditions: NaBH4 in water/methanol, room temperature.

6.3 Carboxylic Acids and Esters

Carboxylic acids (-COOH) are weak acids: RCOOH ⇌ RCOO- + H+. The carboxylate ion is stabilised by delocalisation over both oxygens.

Key reactions: With metals (H2 evolved), carbonates (CO2 evolved — effervescence test), bases (neutralisation), alcohols (esterification with H2SO4 catalyst).

Esters

Named: alkyl from alcohol + -oate from acid. Hydrolysis: Acid hydrolysis (reversible) → acid + alcohol. Base hydrolysis (irreversible, saponification) → sodium salt + alcohol.

6.4 Acyl Chlorides and Acid Anhydrides

Acyl chlorides (RCOCl) react vigorously at room temperature via nucleophilic addition-elimination:

NucleophileProducts
H2ORCOOH + HCl (white fumes)
R'OHRCOOR' + HCl (ester formed)
NH3 (excess)RCONH2 + NH4Cl (amide)
R'NH2RCONHR' + R'NH3+Cl- (N-substituted amide)

Advantages over carboxylic acids: faster, irreversible, higher yield, no catalyst needed.

Acid anhydrides undergo the same reactions more gently. By-product is RCOOH (not HCl) — safer, cheaper, preferred industrially.

6.5 Amines

Amine: Organic compound with a nitrogen lone pair. Primary (RNH2), secondary (R2NH), tertiary (R3N). The lone pair makes amines basic (accept H+) and nucleophilic (donate lone pair to δ+ carbon).

Preparation: (1) RBr + excess NH3 (sealed tube, heat). (2) RCN + 4[H] (LiAlH4 or H2/Ni). (3) ArNO2 + 6[H] (Sn/conc. HCl, then NaOH).

Basicity order: Aliphatic amines > NH3 > phenylamine. Alkyl groups donate electron density to N (+I effect). In phenylamine, the lone pair delocalises into the ring, making it less available.

6.6 Amino Acids and Proteins

General formula: H2N-CHR-COOH. At the isoelectric point, they exist as zwitterions: +H3N-CHR-COO-. In acid: cation. In base: anion. All (except glycine) have a chiral centre.

Peptide bonds (-CONH-): Formed by condensation between -COOH of one amino acid and -NH2 of another, with loss of H2O. Proteins are polypeptides. Hydrolysis: 6M HCl reflux 24h, or enzymes.

6.7 Polymers

Addition Polymers

From alkene monomers (C=C opens). No by-product. 100% atom economy. Non-biodegradable. E.g. poly(ethene), PVC, PTFE.

Condensation Polymers

Polyesters: Diacid + diol → ester linkages (-COO-) + H2O. E.g. PET.

Polyamides: Diacid + diamine → amide linkages (-CONH-) + H2O. E.g. Nylon 6,6. Proteins are natural polyamides.

Condensation polymers are more biodegradable than addition polymers (ester/amide linkages can be hydrolysed by enzymes, acid, or base).

Exam Tip: To find monomers from a polymer: locate the linkage (-COO- or -CONH-), break it, add -OH and -H where water was removed. Practise in both directions.

6.11 NMR Spectroscopy

13C NMR

Number of peaks = number of different carbon environments. Peak height does NOT indicate number of carbons. Chemical shifts: alkyl C (5-40 ppm), C-O (50-90), C=C/aromatic (110-160), C=O acid/ester (160-185), C=O aldehyde/ketone (190-220).

1H NMR — Three Key Pieces of Information

1. Chemical shift (δ)

Position relative to TMS (0 ppm). Key values: R-CH3 (0.7-1.6), R-CH2-X (3.0-4.2), R-OH (0.5-6.0, variable), R-CHO (9.0-10.0), Ar-H (6.5-8.0), R-COOH (10.0-12.0).

2. Integration

Peak area ∝ number of H atoms in that environment. Given as a ratio.

3. Splitting (n+1 rule)

A proton with n equivalent neighbours on adjacent carbons splits into (n+1) peaks: 0 → singlet, 1 → doublet, 2 → triplet, 3 → quartet.

D2O Shake

Adding D2O makes O-H and N-H peaks disappear (proton exchange with deuterium). Identifies exchangeable protons.

Exam Tip: O-H and N-H appear as broad singlets (no splitting due to rapid exchange). In combined-technique questions: MS for Mr, IR for functional groups, 13C NMR for carbon environments, 1H NMR for H environments and connectivity. Degree of unsaturation (DBE) = (2C + 2 + N - H - X)/2; DBE of 4 suggests a benzene ring.

6.12 Chromatography

TLC (Thin Layer Chromatography)

Stationary: silica/alumina plate. Mobile: organic solvent. Rf = distance by substance / distance by solvent front. Quick qualitative identification and purity check.

GC (Gas Chromatography)

Stationary: high-bp liquid in column. Mobile: inert carrier gas. Separates volatile compounds. Retention time identifies components. Peak area gives quantitative composition.

GC-MS

GC separates the mixture; MS identifies each component by molecular mass and fragmentation pattern. Extremely powerful for forensics, environmental monitoring, drug testing, and quality control.

HPLC

Stationary: fine particles in column. Mobile: liquid solvent at high pressure. For non-volatile and thermally unstable compounds (pharmaceuticals, proteins, biological samples).
