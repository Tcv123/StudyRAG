4.1 Nomenclature and Functional Groups

Organic chemistry is the study of carbon-containing compounds (excluding CO, CO2, carbonates, and cyanides). Carbon atoms form four covalent bonds and can bond to each other in chains, rings, and branches, giving rise to millions of organic compounds.

IUPAC Naming Rules

  1. Find the longest continuous carbon chain containing the principal functional group — this gives the root name: meth- (1C), eth- (2C), prop- (3C), but- (4C), pent- (5C), hex- (6C), hept- (7C), oct- (8C).
  2. Number carbons from the end nearest to the principal functional group (or nearest branch if no functional group suffix).
  3. Name and number all substituents (methyl, ethyl, chloro, bromo) as prefixes. List alphabetically (ignoring multiplying prefixes).
  4. The functional group determines the suffix: -ane (alkane), -ene (alkene), -ol (alcohol), -al (aldehyde), -one (ketone), -oic acid (carboxylic acid), -amine, -amide, -nitrile.
  5. Use di-, tri-, tetra- for multiple identical substituents. Use commas between numbers, hyphens between numbers and letters.

Homologous series: A family of organic compounds with the same functional group, the same general formula, similar chemical properties, and a gradual trend in physical properties. Each successive member differs by CH2.

Types of Formulae

  • Molecular formula: Actual number of each atom (e.g. C4H10O).
  • Empirical formula: Simplest whole-number ratio (e.g. C2H5O for the above).
  • Structural formula: Minimal detail showing arrangement (e.g. CH3CH2CH2CH2OH).
  • Displayed formula: Shows every bond between every atom.
  • Skeletal formula: Carbon backbone shown as lines; H atoms on C atoms omitted; heteroatoms (O, N, Cl) and their H atoms shown explicitly.
Exam Tip: Practise converting between all five types of formula. Common errors include miscounting hydrogen atoms in skeletal formulae (remember each carbon makes 4 bonds total) and confusing structural and displayed formulae.

4.2 Isomerism

Isomers: Molecules with the same molecular formula but different structural or spatial arrangements of atoms.

Structural Isomerism

Same molecular formula, different structural formulae. Three types:

  • Chain isomerism: Different carbon skeleton arrangements (e.g. butane vs methylpropane, both C4H10). Branched isomers have lower boiling points (reduced surface area for London forces).
  • Position isomerism: Same chain and functional group but at different positions (e.g. butan-1-ol vs butan-2-ol, both C4H9OH).
  • Functional group isomerism: Same molecular formula but different functional groups (e.g. propanal CH3CH2CHO and propanone CH3COCH3, both C3H6O; or ethanol C2H5OH and methoxymethane CH3OCH3, both C2H6O).

E/Z (Geometric) Isomerism

E/Z isomerism: A type of stereoisomerism that arises when there is: (1) restricted rotation about a C=C double bond, AND (2) two different groups attached to each carbon of the double bond.
Z (zusammen): Higher priority groups on the same side.
E (entgegen): Higher priority groups on opposite sides.
Priority is assigned by atomic number using Cahn-Ingold-Prelog rules (higher atomic number = higher priority).

Optical Isomerism

Chiral centre: A carbon atom bonded to four different groups. This produces two non-superimposable mirror image forms called enantiomers.
Racemic mixture (racemate): A 50:50 mixture of both enantiomers showing no net rotation of plane-polarised light (the rotations cancel).

Enantiomers have identical physical and chemical properties except: (1) they rotate plane-polarised light in opposite directions, (2) they react differently with other chiral molecules (crucial in biochemistry — enzymes are chiral). When a new chiral centre is created from a planar precursor (e.g. nucleophilic addition to an aldehyde), a racemic mixture is formed because the reagent attacks equally from both sides.

4.3 Alkanes

Alkanes are saturated hydrocarbons (CnH2n+2) containing only C-C and C-H single bonds. They are relatively unreactive because: (1) C-C (346 kJ mol-1) and C-H (413 kJ mol-1) bonds are strong, (2) the C-H bond is almost non-polar, providing no site for nucleophilic or electrophilic attack.

Boiling Point Trends

  • Increase with chain length (more electrons → stronger London forces)
  • Decrease with branching (less surface area for intermolecular contact)

Combustion

Complete: CnH2n+2 + (3n+1)/2 O2 → nCO2 + (n+1)H2O (excess oxygen; produces CO2, a greenhouse gas)

Incomplete: Limited oxygen produces CO (toxic — binds irreversibly to haemoglobin) and/or C (soot/particulates causing respiratory problems) and H2O.

Other pollutants from engines: NOx (from N2 + O2 at high temperatures; causes acid rain and smog), SO2 (from sulfur impurities; causes acid rain), unburned hydrocarbons. Catalytic converters reduce these: 2CO + 2NO → 2CO2 + N2.

Free Radical Substitution

Alkanes react with halogens (Cl2, Br2) in UV light via a free radical chain mechanism with three stages:

Free radical: A species with an unpaired electron, formed by homolytic fission of a covalent bond (each atom takes one electron). Shown with a dot notation (e.g. Cl•).

1. Initiation (UV breaks the Cl-Cl bond):

Cl2 → 2Cl•

2. Propagation (chain reaction — each step produces a new radical):

CH4 + Cl• → •CH3 + HCl
•CH3 + Cl2 → CH3Cl + Cl•

3. Termination (two radicals combine, ending the chain):

Cl• + Cl• → Cl2     •CH3 + Cl• → CH3Cl     •CH3 + •CH3 → C2H6

Limitations: Further substitution gives a mixture of products (CH2Cl2, CHCl3, CCl4). For longer alkanes, different H atoms can be substituted, giving position isomers. This makes free radical substitution a poor preparative method for specific halogenoalkanes.

Exam Tip: Use single-headed curly arrows (fishhooks) for radical mechanisms to show movement of single electrons. Use the dot notation for unpaired electrons. Label all three stages clearly. The ethane (C2H6) by-product in termination is evidence for the radical mechanism.

4.4 Alkenes

Alkenes are unsaturated hydrocarbons containing a C=C double bond (general formula CnH2n). The C=C consists of a σ bond (head-on overlap) and a π bond (sideways overlap of p orbitals). The π bond is a region of high electron density, making alkenes reactive towards electrophiles.

Electrophilic Addition

Electrophile: An electron-pair acceptor attracted to regions of high electron density. It accepts a pair of electrons from the π bond to form a new bond.

The mechanism involves two steps: (1) the π electrons attack the electrophilic part of the reagent, forming a carbocation intermediate, (2) the nucleophilic counter-ion attacks the carbocation.

Markownikoff's rule: When HX adds across an unsymmetrical alkene, H adds to the carbon with more H atoms already. The major product has X on the more substituted carbon, because the more substituted carbocation intermediate is more stable (tertiary > secondary > primary, stabilised by the inductive effect of alkyl groups).

Test for C=C: Add bromine water (orange). If it decolourises, an alkene is present.

Addition Polymerisation

nCH2=CH2 → -(CH2-CH2)n-   (poly(ethene))

The C=C opens, each carbon forming a bond to the adjacent monomer. No by-products (100% atom economy). Addition polymers are non-biodegradable (strong, non-polar C-C backbone). Disposal: landfill, incineration (energy recovery), recycling, or feedstock recycling (cracking back to monomers).

4.5 Halogenoalkanes

Classification

  • Primary: Halogen on a carbon bonded to one other carbon (e.g. CH3CH2Br)
  • Secondary: Halogen on a carbon bonded to two other carbons (e.g. (CH3)2CHBr)
  • Tertiary: Halogen on a carbon bonded to three other carbons (e.g. (CH3)3CBr)

The C-X bond is polar (δ+C-Xδ-) because halogens are more electronegative than carbon. The δ+ carbon is susceptible to nucleophilic attack.

Nucleophilic Substitution

Nucleophile: An electron-pair donor with a lone pair that attacks an electron-deficient (δ+) carbon. Examples: OH-, CN-, NH3, H2O.

SN2 Mechanism (primary halogenoalkanes)

One-step concerted mechanism. The nucleophile attacks the δ+ carbon from the opposite side to the halogen (backside attack). A transition state [Nu---C---X]‡ forms, then the halide leaves. Rate = k[RX][Nu-] (second order, bimolecular). Produces inversion of configuration. Favoured by primary substrates (less steric hindrance).

SN1 Mechanism (tertiary halogenoalkanes)

Two-step mechanism. Step 1 (slow, RDS): C-X bond breaks heterolytically to form a planar carbocation + X-. Step 2 (fast): nucleophile attacks the carbocation from either side. Rate = k[RX] (first order, unimolecular). Produces a racemic mixture if a chiral centre forms. Favoured by tertiary substrates (most stable carbocation).

Elimination

With hot ethanolic KOH, halogenoalkanes undergo elimination (not substitution). OH- acts as a base, abstracting H+ from an adjacent carbon. The C-X bond breaks, forming a C=C double bond and producing an alkene + KX + H2O.

Reactivity of Halogenoalkanes

Rate of hydrolysis depends on C-X bond strength: C-I (238 kJ/mol, fastest) > C-Br (276) > C-Cl (338) > C-F (484, slowest). Despite C-F being the most polar, it is unreactive because the bond is too strong to break.

4.6 Alcohols

Classification and Properties

  • Primary (1°): C-OH bonded to one other C (e.g. butan-1-ol)
  • Secondary (2°): C-OH bonded to two other Cs (e.g. butan-2-ol)
  • Tertiary (3°): C-OH bonded to three other Cs (e.g. 2-methylpropan-2-ol)

Alcohols have higher boiling points than alkanes of similar Mr because -OH groups form hydrogen bonds with other alcohol molecules and with water. Short-chain alcohols are miscible with water.

Oxidation of Alcohols

Oxidising agent: acidified K2Cr2O7 / H2SO4. Colour change: orange Cr2O72- → green Cr3+ when oxidation occurs.

Alcohol TypeWith distillationUnder reflux
PrimaryAldehyde (removed before further oxidation)Carboxylic acid

Equations using [O] notation:

Primary: RCH2OH + [O] → RCHO + H2O   (distil)
Then: RCHO + [O] → RCOOH   (reflux)
Secondary: R2CHOH + [O] → R2CO + H2O
Exam Tip: Distillation removes the volatile aldehyde as it forms, preventing further oxidation. Reflux keeps the mixture at boiling without losing products, allowing further oxidation to the carboxylic acid. Always state which apparatus is used.

Dehydration (Elimination)

CH3CH2OH → CH2=CH2 + H2O

Conditions: concentrated H2SO4 at 170°C, or pass vapour over heated Al2O3.

Esterification

RCOOH + R'OH ⇌ RCOOR' + H2O

Conditions: concentrated H2SO4 catalyst, heat under reflux. Reversible equilibrium — yield improved by using excess of one reactant or removing water. Esters have characteristic fruity smells; used as flavourings, solvents, perfumes, plasticisers, and in biodiesel.

Substitution to Halogenoalkanes

  • ROH + HBr → RBr + H2O (using NaBr + conc. H2SO4 to generate HBr)
  • ROH + PCl5 → RCl + POCl3 + HCl (white fumes of HCl confirm -OH present)
  • ROH + SOCl2 → RCl + SO2 + HCl (preferred — gaseous by-products leave)

Combustion

C2H5OH + 3O2 → 2CO2 + 3H2O. Ethanol as a biofuel: closer to carbon-neutral than fossil fuels (CO2 released = CO2 absorbed during crop growth), renewable, but land-use competition with food production is a concern.

4.7 Organic Mechanisms — Curly Arrows

Curly arrow: Represents the movement of an electron pair in a mechanism.
Full curly arrow: Shows movement of a pair of electrons (heterolytic bond making/breaking). Used in ionic mechanisms (electrophilic addition, nucleophilic substitution, etc.).
Half curly arrow (fishhook): Shows movement of a single electron (homolytic fission). Used in radical mechanisms only.

Rules for drawing curly arrows:

  • Must start from a bond or a lone pair (never from a positive charge or empty space)
  • Must point to an atom (forming a lone pair) or to the space between two atoms (forming a new bond)
  • Show δ+ and δ- charges on polar bonds
  • Show all intermediate species, charges, and lone pairs where relevant
  • Label the type of mechanism and the type of bond fission

Mass Spectrometry

The molecular ion peak (M+) gives the relative molecular mass. Fragmentation patterns help deduce structure. Common losses: 15 (CH3), 17 (OH), 28 (CO), 29 (CHO or C2H5), 45 (OC2H5).

Exam Tip: Systematic identification approach: (1) use 2,4-DNPH to confirm C=O, (2) Tollens' to distinguish aldehyde from ketone, (3) Na2CO3 to test for carboxylic acid, (4) bromine water for C=C, (5) K2Cr2O7 to classify alcohol type. Combine test-tube results with IR and mass spec data for positive identification.
