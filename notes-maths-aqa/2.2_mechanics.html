Mechanics

Kinematics (Motion in a Straight Line)

Key quantities:
  • \(s\) = displacement (m)
  • \(u\) = initial velocity (m/s)
  • \(v\) = final velocity (m/s)
  • \(a\) = acceleration (m/s\(^2\))
  • \(t\) = time (s)

SUVAT Equations (Constant Acceleration)

EquationMissing Variable
\(v = u + at\)\(s\)
\(s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2\)\(v\)
\(s = vt - \frac{1}{2}at^2\)\(u\)
\(v^2 = u^2 + 2as\)\(t\)
\(s = \frac{1}{2}(u + v)t\)\(a\)

Worked Example

A particle starts from rest and accelerates at \(10\) m/s\(^2\) for \(4\) s. Find the distance travelled.

\(u = 0\), \(a = 10\), \(t = 4\), find \(s\):

$$s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2 = 0 + \frac{1}{2}(10)(16) = 80 \text{ m}$$

Velocity-Time Graphs

  • Gradient = acceleration
  • Area under the graph = displacement
Exam Tip: SUVAT only works for constant acceleration. If acceleration varies, use calculus: \(v = \frac{ds}{dt}\), \(a = \frac{dv}{dt}\), \(s = \int v\, dt\).

Variable Acceleration

When acceleration is not constant, use calculus: $$v = \frac{ds}{dt} \qquad a = \frac{dv}{dt} = \frac{d^2s}{dt^2}$$ $$s = \int v\, dt \qquad v = \int a\, dt$$

Worked Example

A particle has velocity \(\mathbf{v} = (3t^2)\mathbf{i} + (2t)\mathbf{j}\). Find the acceleration at \(t = 2\).

$$\mathbf{a} = \frac{d\mathbf{v}}{dt} = (6t)\mathbf{i} + 2\mathbf{j}$$
$$\text{At } t = 2: \quad \mathbf{a} = 12\mathbf{i} + 2\mathbf{j}$$

Forces and Newton's Laws

Newton's Three Laws

  1. An object remains at rest or in uniform motion unless acted on by a resultant force
  2. \(\mathbf{F} = m\mathbf{a}\) — the resultant force equals mass times acceleration
  3. Every action has an equal and opposite reaction

Common Forces

ForceSymbolDirection
Weight\(W = mg\)Vertically downward
Normal reaction\(R\) or \(N\)Perpendicular to surface
Friction\(F = \mu R\)Opposite to motion/tendency
Tension\(T\)Along the string, away from object

Worked Example

A 5 kg block on a rough surface (\(\mu = 0.4\)) is pulled with 30 N horizontally. Find the acceleration.

Normal reaction: \(R = mg = 5 \times 9.8 = 49\) N

Friction: \(F = \mu R = 0.4 \times 49 = 19.6\) N

$$a = \frac{30 - 19.6}{5} = \frac{10.4}{5} = 2.08 \text{ m/s}^2$$

Connected Particles

Pulleys

For two particles of mass \(m_1\) and \(m_2\) connected by a string over a smooth pulley (with \(m_1 > m_2\)):

$$a = \frac{(m_1 - m_2)g}{m_1 + m_2} \qquad T = \frac{2m_1 m_2 g}{m_1 + m_2}$$

Worked Example

Masses of 3 kg and 2 kg are connected over a smooth pulley. Find the acceleration (\(g = 10\)).

$$a = \frac{(3-2) \times 10}{3+2} = \frac{10}{5} = 2 \text{ m/s}^2$$
Exam Tip: For connected particle problems, draw a separate force diagram for each particle and apply \(F = ma\) to each. The string tension is the same throughout (for a light, inextensible string).

Motion on Inclined Planes

Resolve forces parallel and perpendicular to the slope:

  • Along the plane: \(mg\sin\alpha\) (down the slope)
  • Perpendicular: Normal reaction \(R = mg\cos\alpha\)
  • Friction: \(F = \mu R = \mu mg\cos\alpha\)

Worked Example

A 3 kg object slides down a smooth incline at \(30°\). Find its acceleration.

$$a = g\sin 30° = 9.8 \times 0.5 = 4.9 \text{ m/s}^2$$

Projectiles

A projectile launched at speed \(u\) at angle \(\theta\) to the horizontal:
  • Horizontal: constant velocity \(u\cos\theta\) (no horizontal acceleration)
  • Vertical: initial velocity \(u\sin\theta\), acceleration \(-g\)

Key Formulae

$$\text{Time of flight} = \frac{2u\sin\theta}{g}$$
$$\text{Maximum height} = \frac{u^2\sin^2\theta}{2g}$$
$$\text{Range} = \frac{u^2\sin 2\theta}{g}$$

Worked Example

A ball is projected at \(30°\) with speed \(20\) m/s. Find the horizontal and vertical components of velocity.

$$v_x = 20\cos 30° = 10\sqrt{3} \approx 17.3 \text{ m/s}$$
$$v_y = 20\sin 30° = 10 \text{ m/s}$$
Exam Tip: Treat horizontal and vertical motion separately. The horizontal velocity is constant (no air resistance). Use SUVAT vertically with \(a = -g\) (taking upward as positive).

Moments

Moment of a force about a point = force \(\times\) perpendicular distance from the point to the line of action. $$M = F \times d$$

For equilibrium:

  • Resultant force = 0
  • Sum of clockwise moments = sum of anticlockwise moments (about any point)

Worked Example

A uniform beam of length 6 m and weight 120 N rests on supports at its ends A and B. A 60 N weight is placed 2 m from A. Find the reaction at A.

Taking moments about B:

$$R_A \times 6 = 120 \times 3 + 60 \times 4 = 360 + 240 = 600$$
$$R_A = 100 \text{ N}$$

Check: \(R_B = 120 + 60 - 100 = 80\) N.

Momentum and Impulse

Momentum: \(p = mv\) (vector quantity, units: kg m/s or N s)
Impulse: \(I = Ft = mv - mu\) (change in momentum)
Conservation of momentum: In a closed system, total momentum before = total momentum after.
$$m_1 u_1 + m_2 u_2 = m_1 v_1 + m_2 v_2$$

Coefficient of Restitution

$$e = \frac{\text{speed of separation}}{\text{speed of approach}} \qquad 0 \leq e \leq 1$$

\(e = 1\): perfectly elastic. \(e = 0\): perfectly inelastic (objects coalesce).

Worked Example

A 0.5 kg ball moving at 4 m/s strikes a wall and rebounds at 3 m/s. Find the impulse.

Taking rebound direction as positive:

$$I = mv - mu = 0.5(3) - 0.5(-4) = 1.5 + 2 = 3.5 \text{ N s}$$

Work, Energy, and Power

Work done: \(W = Fs\cos\theta\) (force \(\times\) displacement \(\times\) cosine of angle between them)
Kinetic energy: \(KE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2\)
Gravitational PE: \(GPE = mgh\)
Power: \(P = \frac{W}{t} = Fv\)

Work-Energy Principle

Net work done on a body = change in kinetic energy:

$$W_{\text{net}} = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 - \frac{1}{2}mu^2$$

Conservation of Energy

Total energy is conserved (KE + PE + work done against friction):

$$\frac{1}{2}mu^2 + mgh_1 = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 + mgh_2 + F_{\text{friction}} \times d$$

Worked Example

A 2 kg block slides 5 m down a rough incline (\(30°\), \(\mu = 0.3\)). Find its speed at the bottom (starting from rest).

Height lost: \(h = 5\sin 30° = 2.5\) m

Friction: \(F = \mu mg\cos 30° = 0.3 \times 2 \times 9.8 \times \cos 30° \approx 5.09\) N

$$\frac{1}{2}(2)v^2 = 2(9.8)(2.5) - 5.09(5)$$
$$v^2 = 49 - 25.45 = 23.55 \implies v \approx 4.85 \text{ m/s}$$
Exam Tip: In energy problems, always draw a clear diagram and define your reference level for GPE. The work-energy approach often avoids the need to find acceleration — useful when the force varies or the path is complex.

Resolving Forces

Any force can be resolved into two perpendicular components:

$$F_x = F\cos\theta \qquad F_y = F\sin\theta$$

The resultant of two perpendicular forces \(F_x\) and \(F_y\):

$$R = \sqrt{F_x^2 + F_y^2} \qquad \tan\alpha = \frac{F_y}{F_x}$$

Worked Example

Two forces of 5 N and 12 N act perpendicular to each other. Find the resultant.

$$R = \sqrt{5^2 + 12^2} = \sqrt{25 + 144} = \sqrt{169} = 13 \text{ N}$$
