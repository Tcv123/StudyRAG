Kinematics (Constant Acceleration)

SUVAT Equations

Variables: \(s\) = displacement, \(u\) = initial velocity, \(v\) = final velocity, \(a\) = acceleration, \(t\) = time.
EquationMissing Variable
\(v = u + at\)\(s\)
\(s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2\)\(v\)
\(s = vt - \frac{1}{2}at^2\)\(u\)
\(v^2 = u^2 + 2as\)\(t\)
\(s = \frac{1}{2}(u + v)t\)\(a\)

Worked Example: SUVAT

A car accelerates from rest at \(3 \text{ m s}^{-2}\) for 8 seconds. Find the distance travelled.

Solution: \(u = 0\), \(a = 3\), \(t = 8\), find \(s\).

$$s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2 = 0 + \frac{1}{2}(3)(64) = 96 \text{ m}$$

Velocity-Time Graphs

Key facts about v-t graphs:

  • Gradient = acceleration
  • Area under graph = displacement
  • Horizontal line = constant velocity (zero acceleration)

Variable Acceleration

When acceleration is not constant, use calculus:

  • \(v = \dfrac{ds}{dt}\) (velocity is the derivative of displacement)
  • \(a = \dfrac{dv}{dt} = \dfrac{d^2s}{dt^2}\) (acceleration is the derivative of velocity)
  • \(s = \int v\,dt\) and \(v = \int a\,dt\)

Worked Example: Variable Acceleration

A particle moves such that \(v = 3t^2 - 6t + 2\) m/s. Find the acceleration and displacement at \(t = 3\).

Solution:

$$a = \frac{dv}{dt} = 6t - 6$$

At \(t = 3\): \(a = 18 - 6 = 12 \text{ m s}^{-2}\).

$$s = \int_0^3 (3t^2 - 6t + 2)\,dt = \left[t^3 - 3t^2 + 2t\right]_0^3 = 27 - 27 + 6 = 6 \text{ m}$$

Forces

Common Forces:
  • Weight: \(W = mg\), where \(g = 9.8 \text{ m s}^{-2}\), acts downward
  • Normal reaction (R or N): perpendicular to the surface of contact
  • Friction (F): opposes motion, \(F \leq \mu R\). At the point of sliding, \(F = \mu R\)
  • Tension (T): in strings and ropes, acts along the string
  • Thrust/Compression: in rods, can push as well as pull

Newton's Laws

Newton's Laws:
  1. A body remains at rest or in uniform motion unless acted upon by a resultant force.
  2. \(F = ma\) — the resultant force equals mass times acceleration.
  3. For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.

On an inclined plane at angle \(\theta\) to the horizontal:

  • Component of weight parallel to slope: \(mg\sin\theta\)
  • Component of weight perpendicular to slope: \(mg\cos\theta\)
  • Normal reaction: \(R = mg\cos\theta\) (when no other perpendicular forces)

Worked Example: Inclined Plane

A 5 kg block slides down a rough plane inclined at 30 degrees. The coefficient of friction is \(\mu = 0.3\). Find the acceleration.

Solution: Resolve perpendicular to the slope:

$$R = mg\cos 30° = 5 \times 9.8 \times \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} = 42.43 \text{ N}$$

Friction: \(F = \mu R = 0.3 \times 42.43 = 12.73\) N (acts up the slope).

Resolve parallel to the slope (Newton's second law):

$$ma = mg\sin 30° - F$$
$$5a = 5 \times 9.8 \times 0.5 - 12.73 = 24.5 - 12.73 = 11.77$$
$$a = 2.35 \text{ m s}^{-2}$$

Connected Particles and Pulleys

Assumptions for pulley problems:
  • The string is light (massless) and inextensible
  • The pulley is smooth (frictionless) and light
  • Tension is the same throughout the string
  • Both particles have the same magnitude of acceleration

Worked Example: Atwood's Machine

Two particles of mass 3 kg and 5 kg are connected by a light inextensible string over a smooth pulley. Find the acceleration and tension.

Solution: The heavier mass (5 kg) accelerates downward.

For the 5 kg mass (downward positive): \(5g - T = 5a\)

For the 3 kg mass (upward positive): \(T - 3g = 3a\)

Adding the equations:

$$5g - 3g = 8a \implies 2g = 8a \implies a = \frac{g}{4} = 2.45 \text{ m s}^{-2}$$

Substituting back: \(T = 3g + 3a = 3(9.8) + 3(2.45) = 29.4 + 7.35 = 36.75\) N.

Projectiles

Projectile Motion: An object launched with initial speed \(u\) at angle \(\theta\) to the horizontal (neglecting air resistance):
  • Horizontal: \(x = ut\cos\theta\) (constant velocity)
  • Vertical: \(y = ut\sin\theta - \frac{1}{2}gt^2\) (acceleration \(-g\))

Key projectile formulae (launched from ground level):

  • Time of flight: \(T = \dfrac{2u\sin\theta}{g}\)
  • Maximum height: \(H = \dfrac{u^2\sin^2\theta}{2g}\)
  • Range: \(R = \dfrac{u^2\sin 2\theta}{g}\)

Worked Example: Projectile

A ball is kicked from ground level at \(20 \text{ m s}^{-1}\) at \(30°\) above the horizontal. Find: (a) time of flight, (b) maximum height, (c) range.

Solution:

(a) \(T = \dfrac{2 \times 20 \times \sin 30°}{9.8} = \dfrac{20}{9.8} = 2.04\) s

(b) \(H = \dfrac{20^2 \times \sin^2 30°}{2 \times 9.8} = \dfrac{400 \times 0.25}{19.6} = 5.10\) m

(c) \(R = \dfrac{20^2 \times \sin 60°}{9.8} = \dfrac{400 \times 0.866}{9.8} = 35.3\) m

Moments

Moment of a Force: The moment of a force about a point is: $$\text{Moment} = F \times d$$ where \(d\) is the perpendicular distance from the point to the line of action of the force. Measured in N m.

For equilibrium:

  • The resultant force is zero (translational equilibrium)
  • The sum of clockwise moments equals the sum of anticlockwise moments about any point (rotational equilibrium)

Worked Example: Moments

A uniform beam of length 6 m and weight 200 N is supported at points A and B, where A is at one end and B is 4 m from A. A load of 300 N hangs from the other end. Find the reactions at A and B.

Solution: Take moments about A (eliminates \(R_A\)):

$$R_B \times 4 = 200 \times 3 + 300 \times 6$$
$$4R_B = 600 + 1800 = 2400 \implies R_B = 600 \text{ N}$$

Resolve vertically: \(R_A + R_B = 200 + 300 = 500\), so \(R_A = 500 - 600 = -100\) N.

The negative sign means \(R_A\) acts downward (the beam would lift at A without a support holding it down).

Exam Tip: When solving moments problems, take moments about a point where an unknown force acts to eliminate it from the equation. For a uniform beam, the weight acts at the midpoint. Always draw a clear diagram labelling all forces and distances.
Exam Tip: In mechanics problems, always state the positive direction clearly and use consistent signs throughout. Draw a force diagram before writing equations -- this helps avoid sign errors and missing forces.
