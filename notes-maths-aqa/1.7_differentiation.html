Differentiation

Differentiation from First Principles

Definition: The derivative of \(f(x)\) from first principles is: $$f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}$$ This gives the gradient of the tangent to the curve at any point.

Worked Example: First Principles for \(f(x) = x^3\)

Solution:

$$f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{(x+h)^3 - x^3}{h}$$

Expand \((x+h)^3 = x^3 + 3x^2h + 3xh^2 + h^3\):

$$= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{3x^2h + 3xh^2 + h^3}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} (3x^2 + 3xh + h^2) = 3x^2$$

Differentiation Rules

Chain Rule

Chain Rule: If \(y = f(g(x))\), then: $$\frac{dy}{dx} = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)$$ Or equivalently: if \(y = f(u)\) and \(u = g(x)\), then \(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{dy}{du} \cdot \dfrac{du}{dx}\).

Worked Example: Chain Rule

Differentiate \(y = (3x^2 + 1)^5\).

Solution: Let \(u = 3x^2 + 1\), so \(y = u^5\).

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = 5u^4 \cdot 6x = 30x(3x^2 + 1)^4$$

Product Rule

Product Rule: If \(y = uv\), then: $$\frac{dy}{dx} = u\frac{dv}{dx} + v\frac{du}{dx}$$

Worked Example: Product Rule

Differentiate \(y = x^2 e^{3x}\).

Solution: Let \(u = x^2\) and \(v = e^{3x}\).

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = x^2 \cdot 3e^{3x} + e^{3x} \cdot 2x = e^{3x}(3x^2 + 2x) = xe^{3x}(3x + 2)$$

Quotient Rule

Quotient Rule: If \(y = \dfrac{u}{v}\), then: $$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{v\dfrac{du}{dx} - u\dfrac{dv}{dx}}{v^2}$$

Worked Example: Quotient Rule

Differentiate \(y = \dfrac{\sin x}{x^2}\).

Solution: Let \(u = \sin x\), \(v = x^2\):

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{x^2 \cos x - \sin x \cdot 2x}{x^4} = \frac{x\cos x - 2\sin x}{x^3}$$
Exam Tip: Remember the quotient rule with the mnemonic "low d-high minus high d-low, over low squared". Always simplify your final answer by factorising where possible.

Tangents and Normals

At the point \((a, f(a))\):

  • Tangent line: \(y - f(a) = f'(a)(x - a)\)
  • Normal line: \(y - f(a) = -\dfrac{1}{f'(a)}(x - a)\) (perpendicular to tangent)

Implicit Differentiation

Implicit Differentiation: When \(y\) is defined implicitly by an equation (not written as \(y = ...\)), differentiate each term with respect to \(x\), applying the chain rule to terms involving \(y\): $$\frac{d}{dx}[f(y)] = f'(y) \cdot \frac{dy}{dx}$$

Worked Example: Implicit Differentiation

Find \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\) given \(x^2 + y^2 = 25\).

Solution: Differentiate both sides with respect to \(x\):

$$2x + 2y\frac{dy}{dx} = 0$$
$$\frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{x}{y}$$

Worked Example: Harder Implicit Differentiation

Find \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\) given \(x^2y + xy^3 = 6\).

Solution: Use the product rule on each term:

$$\left(2xy + x^2\frac{dy}{dx}\right) + \left(y^3 + 3xy^2\frac{dy}{dx}\right) = 0$$
$$\frac{dy}{dx}(x^2 + 3xy^2) = -(2xy + y^3)$$
$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{-(2xy + y^3)}{x^2 + 3xy^2}$$

Stationary Points

Definition: A stationary point occurs where \(f'(x) = 0\). The nature of the stationary point is determined by the second derivative or by examining the sign of the first derivative either side of the point.
Second Derivative TestNature
\(f''(a) > 0\)Local minimum at \(x = a\)
\(f''(a) < 0\)Local maximum at \(x = a\)
\(f''(a) = 0\)Inconclusive — check sign of \(f'(x)\) either side

Worked Example: Finding Stationary Points

Find the stationary points of \(f(x) = 2x^3 - 9x^2 + 12x - 4\) and determine their nature.

Solution:

$$f'(x) = 6x^2 - 18x + 12 = 6(x^2 - 3x + 2) = 6(x-1)(x-2)$$

Setting \(f'(x) = 0\): \(x = 1\) or \(x = 2\).

$$f''(x) = 12x - 18$$

At \(x = 1\): \(f''(1) = -6 < 0\) — local maximum. \(f(1) = 1\), so max at \((1, 1)\).

At \(x = 2\): \(f''(2) = 6 > 0\) — local minimum. \(f(2) = 0\), so min at \((2, 0)\).

Connected Rates of Change

Connected Rates of Change: Use the chain rule to relate rates: $$\frac{dA}{dt} = \frac{dA}{dr} \cdot \frac{dr}{dt}$$ This connects the rate of change of one variable to another when both depend on time.

Worked Example: Connected Rates

A spherical balloon is being inflated so that its volume increases at \(20 \text{ cm}^3/\text{s}\). Find the rate of increase of the radius when \(r = 5\) cm.

Solution: \(V = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3\), so \(\frac{dV}{dr} = 4\pi r^2\).

$$\frac{dr}{dt} = \frac{dV}{dt} \cdot \frac{dr}{dV} = 20 \cdot \frac{1}{4\pi r^2}$$

When \(r = 5\): \(\dfrac{dr}{dt} = \dfrac{20}{4\pi(25)} = \dfrac{1}{5\pi} \approx 0.0637\) cm/s.

Parametric Differentiation

Parametric Differentiation: If \(x = f(t)\) and \(y = g(t)\), then: $$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{\dfrac{dy}{dt}}{\dfrac{dx}{dt}}$$

Worked Example: Parametric Curve

A curve is defined by \(x = t^2 + 1\), \(y = t^3 - t\). Find \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\) and the equation of the tangent when \(t = 2\).

Solution:

$$\frac{dx}{dt} = 2t, \quad \frac{dy}{dt} = 3t^2 - 1$$
$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{3t^2 - 1}{2t}$$

When \(t = 2\): \(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{11}{4}\), and the point is \((5, 6)\).

$$y - 6 = \frac{11}{4}(x - 5) \implies y = \frac{11}{4}x - \frac{31}{4}$$
Exam Tip: For second parametric derivatives, use: $$\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = \frac{\dfrac{d}{dt}\!\left(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\right)}{\dfrac{dx}{dt}}$$ Do NOT simply differentiate \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\) with respect to \(t\) and forget to divide by \(\dfrac{dx}{dt}\).
