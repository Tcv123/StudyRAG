S1 — Lifestyle, Health and Risk

1.1 The Cardiovascular System

Structure of the Heart

The human heart is a muscular double pump consisting of four chambers: two thin-walled atria that receive blood and two thick-walled ventricles that pump blood out. The left ventricle wall is substantially thicker than the right because it must generate enough pressure to push blood around the entire systemic circulation, while the right ventricle only needs to push blood to the nearby lungs.

Cardiac cycle: The sequence of events in one complete heartbeat, including atrial systole, ventricular systole, and diastole. One complete cycle lasts approximately 0.8 seconds at a resting heart rate of 75 beats per minute.

Key structures include the atrioventricular valves (bicuspid on the left, tricuspid on the right) which prevent backflow from ventricles to atria, and the semilunar valves in the aorta and pulmonary artery which prevent backflow into the ventricles during diastole. The valves open and close in response to pressure differences between the chambers and vessels.

Arteries have thick walls with abundant elastic tissue that stretches and recoils to maintain blood pressure between heartbeats. This creates a smooth, continuous flow in arteries despite the pulsatile output of the heart. Veins have thinner walls because blood pressure is much lower; they rely on skeletal muscle contraction, valves, and the breathing mechanism to return blood to the heart.

Exam Tip: When comparing arteries and veins in exam questions, always mention the relative thickness of the wall, the size of the lumen, and the presence or absence of valves. Link structure to function for full marks.

The Cardiac Cycle in Detail

During atrial systole, the atria contract and blood is pushed through the open atrioventricular valves into the ventricles. During ventricular systole, the ventricles contract from the apex upward, the atrioventricular valves snap shut (producing the first heart sound), and blood is forced through the semilunar valves into the aorta and pulmonary artery. During diastole, the heart muscle relaxes, semilunar valves close (producing the second heart sound), and blood flows passively from the veins into the atria.

Pressure changes drive the cardiac cycle. When ventricular pressure exceeds atrial pressure, the AV valves close. When ventricular pressure exceeds arterial pressure, the semilunar valves open. Understanding these pressure relationships is essential for interpreting cardiac cycle graphs.

1.2 Water and Transport in the Blood

Properties of Water

Hydrogen bond: A weak electrostatic attraction between a slightly positive hydrogen atom (bonded to an electronegative atom) and a lone pair of electrons on a nearby electronegative atom (oxygen or nitrogen). In water, each molecule can form up to four hydrogen bonds with neighbouring molecules.

Water is essential for life due to several properties arising from hydrogen bonding:

  • High specific heat capacity — a large amount of energy is needed to raise the temperature of water, so aquatic environments and body fluids remain relatively stable in temperature.
  • High latent heat of evaporation — evaporation of sweat cools the body effectively because breaking hydrogen bonds requires significant energy.
  • Cohesion — hydrogen bonds between water molecules create surface tension and allow water to be pulled up xylem vessels in plants (transpiration stream).
  • Solvent properties — the polar nature of water allows it to dissolve ionic and polar substances, making it an excellent transport medium for blood plasma.
  • Maximum density at 4 °C — ice floats, insulating water beneath and allowing aquatic organisms to survive in winter.

Blood as a Transport Medium

Blood plasma is the liquid component of blood, consisting of about 90% water. It transports dissolved substances including glucose, amino acids, urea, hormones, antibodies, carbon dioxide (as hydrogencarbonate ions), and plasma proteins such as fibrinogen and albumin. Red blood cells (erythrocytes) transport oxygen bound to haemoglobin; white blood cells (leucocytes) are part of the immune response; platelets are cell fragments involved in blood clotting.

1.3 Lipids and Diet

Structure of Lipids

Lipids are biological molecules that are insoluble in water but soluble in organic solvents. The main types relevant to health are triglycerides and cholesterol.

Triglyceride: A lipid formed by the condensation of one glycerol molecule with three fatty acid molecules, producing three ester bonds and releasing three molecules of water. Triglycerides are the main form of energy storage in animals.

Fatty acids can be saturated (no C=C double bonds, maximum hydrogen atoms, straight chains that pack closely) or unsaturated (one or more C=C double bonds, fewer hydrogen atoms, kinked chains that do not pack as closely). Saturated fats tend to be solid at room temperature and are associated with increased cardiovascular risk. Polyunsaturated and monounsaturated fats are liquid at room temperature and are considered healthier.

Cholesterol and Lipoproteins

Cholesterol is a steroid lipid with a four-ring structure. It is essential for cell membrane structure (regulating fluidity), steroid hormone synthesis, bile salt production, and vitamin D synthesis. However, elevated blood cholesterol levels are a major risk factor for atherosclerosis and cardiovascular disease.

Cholesterol is transported in the blood by lipoproteins:

A high LDL:HDL ratio is a significant risk factor for coronary heart disease. Diet, exercise, genetics, and medication (statins) can all influence this ratio.

Energy and Diet

Energy balance determines whether body mass increases, decreases, or remains stable. Energy intake from food is measured in kilojoules (kJ) or kilocalories (kcal). Lipids provide more than twice the energy per gram (37 kJ/g) compared to carbohydrates (16 kJ/g) or proteins (17 kJ/g) because lipids have a higher proportion of carbon-hydrogen bonds that can be oxidised.

BMI = mass (kg) / height² (m²)

A BMI of 18.5–24.9 is considered normal; 25–29.9 is overweight; 30+ is obese. However, BMI has limitations as it does not distinguish between fat and muscle mass, nor does it indicate fat distribution (visceral fat is more dangerous than subcutaneous fat).

1.4 Cardiovascular Disease

Atherosclerosis

Atherosclerosis: The progressive build-up of fatty deposits (atheromas or plaques) within the walls of arteries, particularly at points of branching or turbulent flow. This narrows the lumen, restricts blood flow, and reduces oxygen delivery to tissues.

The process begins when the endothelial lining of an artery is damaged (by high blood pressure, toxins from smoking, or high blood glucose). White blood cells and cholesterol (from LDL) accumulate beneath the endothelium, forming a fatty streak. Over time this develops into a fibrous plaque with a lipid core capped by smooth muscle and collagen. The plaque narrows the arterial lumen and can eventually rupture, triggering a blood clot (thrombus).

Coronary Heart Disease and Stroke

If atherosclerosis occurs in the coronary arteries, reduced blood flow to the cardiac muscle leads to angina (chest pain during exertion) or, if a thrombus completely blocks the artery, a myocardial infarction (heart attack). The cardiac muscle downstream of the blockage is deprived of oxygen and dies.

A stroke (cerebrovascular accident) occurs when blood supply to part of the brain is interrupted, either by a thrombus blocking a cerebral artery (ischaemic stroke, ~85% of cases) or by a burst blood vessel causing bleeding into brain tissue (haemorrhagic stroke). Brain cells deprived of oxygen die within minutes, potentially causing permanent disability or death.

Blood Clotting (Thrombosis)

When a blood vessel is damaged or a plaque ruptures, the clotting cascade is initiated. Platelets adhere to exposed collagen, become activated, and release clotting factors. The cascade converts the inactive plasma protein prothrombin into the active enzyme thrombin, which then converts soluble fibrinogen into insoluble fibrin threads. These threads form a mesh that traps red blood cells, forming a clot that seals the wound.

Exam Tip: The clotting cascade involves a series of enzyme-catalysed reactions (a cascade amplification). Remember: damaged tissue → platelet activation → prothrombin → thrombin → fibrinogen → fibrin. Calcium ions and vitamin K are essential cofactors.

1.5 Risk Factors for Cardiovascular Disease

Modifiable Risk Factors

  • Smoking: Carbon monoxide reduces the oxygen-carrying capacity of haemoglobin; nicotine increases heart rate and blood pressure; chemicals damage the endothelium, promoting atherosclerosis.
  • High blood pressure (hypertension): Increases the force on artery walls, damaging the endothelium and promoting plaque formation. Defined as systolic >140 mmHg or diastolic >90 mmHg.
  • High blood cholesterol: Particularly high LDL and low HDL. Excess LDL deposits cholesterol in artery walls.
  • Obesity: Especially central (visceral) obesity. Increases blood pressure, blood lipids, and risk of type 2 diabetes.
  • Physical inactivity: Exercise increases HDL, reduces blood pressure, improves insulin sensitivity, and helps maintain healthy weight.
  • Diet: High saturated fat, high salt (increases blood pressure), low fibre, low fruit/vegetable intake all increase risk.
  • Excessive alcohol: Raises blood pressure and can cause cardiomyopathy.

Non-Modifiable Risk Factors

  • Age: Risk increases with age as arteries lose elasticity and plaques accumulate over time.
  • Sex: Males have higher risk before age 50; after menopause, female risk rises as oestrogen levels fall (oestrogen is cardioprotective).
  • Genetics/family history: Some individuals inherit genes that predispose them to high cholesterol (familial hypercholesterolaemia), high blood pressure, or diabetes.
  • Ethnicity: Some ethnic groups have statistically higher rates of certain cardiovascular conditions.

Correlation and Causation

Correlation: A statistical relationship between two variables — as one changes, the other tends to change. Correlation does not prove causation; there may be confounding variables or the relationship may be coincidental.

Epidemiological studies (e.g., cohort studies, case-control studies) provide evidence for risk factors. A causal link is supported when: the correlation is strong and consistent across studies; there is a dose-response relationship; a plausible biological mechanism exists; and the relationship is temporally correct (the risk factor precedes the disease).

Exam Tip: Always distinguish between correlation and causation in exam answers. Use phrases like "associated with increased risk" rather than "causes" unless a causal mechanism has been established. Discuss confounding variables and limitations of studies for evaluation marks.

1.6 Studying Risk and Interpreting Data

Types of Study

Cohort study: A large group of people is followed over time, and their exposure to risk factors and subsequent health outcomes are recorded. This allows researchers to identify associations between lifestyle factors and disease. The Framingham Heart Study is a famous example.

Case-control study: People with a disease (cases) are compared with similar people without the disease (controls), and researchers look back at their past exposure to risk factors.

Intervention study (clinical trial): Participants are randomly assigned to treatment or control groups to test whether an intervention (drug, lifestyle change) reduces disease risk. Randomised controlled trials provide the strongest evidence for causation.

Interpreting Data

When evaluating evidence for risk factors, consider sample size (larger is more reliable), whether the study controlled for confounding variables, whether the results have been replicated, and whether conclusions are supported by the data presented. Relative risk and absolute risk are important measures:

Relative risk = (incidence in exposed group) / (incidence in unexposed group)

A relative risk of 1.0 means no difference in risk; greater than 1.0 means increased risk; less than 1.0 means reduced risk (protective factor).

Absolute risk is the actual probability of developing a condition over a specified time period, while relative risk compares probabilities between groups. A large relative risk may correspond to a small absolute risk if the condition is rare.

Evaluating Health Claims

Media reports about health risks often oversimplify scientific findings. Scientists must evaluate whether studies are peer-reviewed, whether sample sizes are adequate, whether confounding variables were controlled, whether results are statistically significant, and whether the conclusions are supported by the methodology. Personal anecdotes and testimonials are not scientific evidence.

Exam Tip: In data interpretation questions, always refer to specific figures from the data, calculate percentage changes where appropriate, identify trends, and suggest explanations. Remember to discuss reliability (repeatability, reproducibility) and validity (whether the study actually measures what it claims to measure).
