Converting Units

To convert between prefixed units, multiply or divide by the appropriate power of 10:

  • 3.5 km = 3.5 × 103 m = 3500 m
  • 250 nm = 250 × 10-9 m = 2.50 × 10-7 m
  • 4.7 MΩ = 4.7 × 106 Ω

Always convert to SI base units before substituting into equations.

1.3 Significant Figures and Decimal Places

Significant figures (s.f.) are the number of meaningful digits in a value. Leading zeros are not significant; trailing zeros after a decimal point are significant.

Rules for Significant Figures

  • All non-zero digits are significant: 3456 has 4 s.f.
  • Zeros between non-zero digits are significant: 3006 has 4 s.f.
  • Leading zeros are NOT significant: 0.0045 has 2 s.f.
  • Trailing zeros after a decimal point ARE significant: 2.50 has 3 s.f.

Using Significant Figures in Calculations

  • Your final answer should have the same number of significant figures as the least precise value used in the calculation.
  • Keep extra significant figures during intermediate steps to avoid rounding errors.
In exam calculations, give your answer to 2 or 3 significant figures unless told otherwise. If data is given to 3 s.f., give your answer to 3 s.f.

1.5 Planning Experiments and Variables

Independent variable — the variable that you deliberately change during the experiment.

Dependent variable — the variable that you measure as a result of changing the independent variable.

Control variables — variables that must be kept constant to ensure a fair test.

Planning an Experiment

  • Identify the independent, dependent, and control variables.
  • Choose appropriate measuring instruments with suitable resolution.
  • Plan to take repeat readings and calculate a mean to reduce random error.
  • Consider the range of values and the number of data points (at least 5-6).
  • Identify potential sources of systematic and random error.
  • Write a clear method that another person could follow to replicate the experiment.

Types of Error

TypeDescriptionHow to Reduce
Random errorUnpredictable variation between measurements, causing scatter around the true valueTake repeat readings and calculate the mean
Systematic errorA consistent error in the same direction every time (e.g., a zero error)Recalibrate instruments; check for zero errors before use

1.6 Uncertainties

Uncertainty is a measure of the range within which the true value is likely to lie. It quantifies the confidence in a measurement.

Absolute Uncertainty

The absolute uncertainty is expressed in the same units as the measurement. For a single reading, it is typically half the smallest division of the measuring instrument (the resolution).

For repeated readings:

Absolute uncertainty = (max value - min value) / 2

Percentage Uncertainty

Percentage uncertainty = (absolute uncertainty / measured value) × 100%

Combining Uncertainties

OperationRule
Adding or subtracting quantitiesAdd the absolute uncertainties
Multiplying or dividing quantitiesAdd the percentage uncertainties
Raising to a power (xn)Multiply the percentage uncertainty by n
When a quantity is squared (e.g., v2), the percentage uncertainty is doubled. When a quantity is square-rooted, the percentage uncertainty is halved.

1.7 Graphs

Drawing Graphs

  • Use at least half the graph paper for your plotted points.
  • Label both axes with the quantity and its unit (e.g., "velocity / m s-1").
  • Choose sensible scales — use easy divisions (1, 2, 5, 10 per square).
  • Plot points accurately with small crosses.
  • Draw a line of best fit — this can be a straight line or a smooth curve.

Gradient

The gradient of a straight-line graph is calculated using:

gradient = Δy / Δx = (y2 - y1) / (x2 - x1)

Use a large triangle (at least half the line length) for accuracy. The gradient often represents a physical quantity (e.g., gradient of a distance-time graph = velocity).

Y-Intercept

The y-intercept is the value of y when x = 0. It may represent a physical constant in the experiment (e.g., the y-intercept of a V against I graph gives EMF if the equation is rearranged as V = ε - Ir).

Error Bars

  • Error bars are drawn on each point to represent the uncertainty in the measurement.
  • They extend above and below (or left and right) of the plotted point by the size of the absolute uncertainty.
  • Draw a best fit line that passes as close to as many points as possible.
  • Draw worst fit lines — the steepest and shallowest lines that still pass through all error bars.

Uncertainty in Gradient

Uncertainty in gradient = (gradient of steepest line - gradient of shallowest line) / 2

Uncertainty in Y-Intercept

Uncertainty in intercept = (intercept of steepest - intercept of shallowest) / 2
Always use data points that lie ON your line of best fit when calculating the gradient, not raw data points from the table. The two points should be far apart for maximum accuracy.
