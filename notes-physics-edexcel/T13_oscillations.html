Topic 13 — Oscillations

13.1 Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM)

Simple harmonic motion: An oscillation in which the acceleration of the object is directly proportional to its displacement from the equilibrium position and is always directed towards that equilibrium position.

The defining equation of SHM:

a = −ω²x

where:

  • a = acceleration (m s⁻²)
  • ω = angular frequency (rad s⁻¹)
  • x = displacement from equilibrium (m)

The negative sign indicates the acceleration is always in the opposite direction to the displacement — it always acts as a restoring force back towards equilibrium.

13.2 Equations of SHM

Angular Frequency

ω = 2π / T = 2πf

where T is the period (s) and f is the frequency (Hz).

Displacement

x = A cos(ωt)

(assuming maximum displacement at t = 0; use x = A sin(ωt) if starting from equilibrium)

Velocity

v = −Aω sin(ωt)

The velocity can also be expressed in terms of displacement:

v = ±ω√(A² − x²)

Maximum Velocity

vmax = Aω

This occurs at the equilibrium position (x = 0).

Acceleration

a = −Aω² cos(ωt) = −ω²x

Maximum Acceleration

amax = Aω²

This occurs at the maximum displacement (x = ±A).

The choice of cos or sin depends on the initial conditions. If the object starts at maximum displacement (x = A at t = 0), use x = A cos(ωt). If it starts at equilibrium moving in the positive direction, use x = A sin(ωt). Always state your assumption clearly.

13.3 Phase Relationships

Quantity Equation (starting at x = A) Phase relationship
Displacement (x) A cos(ωt) Reference
Velocity (v) −Aω sin(ωt) Leads displacement by π/2 (90°)
Acceleration (a) −Aω² cos(ωt) Leads displacement by π (180°) — i.e., in antiphase

Key points:

  • Velocity is maximum when displacement is zero, and zero when displacement is maximum.
  • Acceleration is maximum (in magnitude) when displacement is maximum, and zero when displacement is zero.
  • Acceleration and displacement are always in antiphase (180° out of phase).
  • Velocity leads displacement by a quarter cycle (π/2 radians).

13.4 SHM Systems

Simple Pendulum

For small angular displacements (less than ~10°):

T = 2π √(L / g)

where:

  • L = length of the pendulum (from pivot to centre of mass of bob) (m)
  • g = gravitational field strength (m s⁻²)

Note: The period does not depend on the mass of the bob or the amplitude (for small oscillations).

Mass–Spring System

T = 2π √(m / k)

where:

  • m = mass of the oscillating object (kg)
  • k = spring constant (N m⁻¹)

Note: The period does not depend on the amplitude or on g. This formula applies for both horizontal and vertical mass–spring systems.

For a pendulum: T depends on L and g (not mass). For a mass–spring: T depends on m and k (not g or amplitude). These are very common exam questions — make sure you know which factors affect the period and which do not.

13.5 Energy in SHM

In simple harmonic motion, energy is continuously exchanged between kinetic energy and potential energy. The total mechanical energy remains constant (in the absence of damping).

Kinetic Energy

Ek = ½mv² = ½mω²(A² − x²)

Potential Energy

Ep = ½mω²x²

Total Energy

Etotal = ½mω²A² = constant

Energy Variation

Position Displacement KE PE
Equilibrium x = 0 Maximum (½mω²A²) Zero
Maximum displacement x = ±A Zero Maximum (½mω²A²)

On a graph of energy against displacement:

  • KE is an inverted parabola (maximum at x = 0, zero at x = ±A).
  • PE is an upward parabola (zero at x = 0, maximum at x = ±A).
  • Total energy is a horizontal line (constant).

On a graph of energy against time:

  • Both KE and PE oscillate sinusoidally at twice the frequency of the oscillation (2f).
  • KE and PE are in antiphase with each other.
  • Total energy remains constant.
The total energy of the oscillation is proportional to the square of the amplitude: E ∝ A². Doubling the amplitude quadruples the total energy.

13.6 Free and Forced Oscillations

Free oscillation: An oscillation where no external driving force acts on the system after the initial displacement. The system oscillates at its natural frequency (f₀).
Natural frequency (f₀): The frequency at which a system oscillates freely when displaced from equilibrium and released. Determined by the physical properties of the system.
Forced oscillation: An oscillation maintained by a periodic external driving force. The system oscillates at the frequency of the driving force (the driving frequency, fd).

13.7 Damping

Damping: The reduction in amplitude (and energy) of an oscillation caused by dissipative forces (such as friction or air resistance) acting on the system.

Types of Damping

Type Description Amplitude behaviour
Light (under) damping Small resistive forces. System oscillates with gradually decreasing amplitude over many cycles. Exponential decrease; many oscillations visible
Heavy (over) damping Large resistive forces. System returns very slowly to equilibrium without oscillating. No oscillations; very slow return
Critical damping The system returns to equilibrium in the shortest possible time without oscillating. No oscillations; fastest return to zero
Critical damping is used in car shock absorbers and door closing mechanisms — the system must return to equilibrium quickly without bouncing. In exams, you should be able to sketch all three types of damping on the same displacement–time graph.

13.8 Resonance

Resonance: The phenomenon that occurs when the driving frequency equals the natural frequency of the system (fd = f₀), resulting in a maximum transfer of energy to the system and hence maximum amplitude of oscillation.

The Resonance Curve

A graph of amplitude against driving frequency shows:

  • A peak at the natural frequency f₀.
  • The amplitude is maximum at resonance.
  • At driving frequencies much lower or much higher than f₀, the amplitude is small.

Effect of Damping on Resonance

  • Increasing damping reduces the peak amplitude at resonance.
  • Increasing damping broadens the resonance curve (the peak becomes flatter and wider).
  • Increasing damping causes the resonant frequency to shift slightly below f₀ (though for light damping this shift is negligible).
  • With very heavy damping, the resonance peak virtually disappears.
Damping level Peak amplitude Curve width Resonant frequency
No damping Infinite (theoretically) Infinitely narrow Exactly f₀
Light damping Large Narrow (sharp peak) Very close to f₀
Heavy damping Small Wide (broad peak) Slightly below f₀

Applications and Examples of Resonance

Application Explanation
Shock absorbers (car suspension) Critical damping prevents resonance of the car body on springs when driving over bumps. Without damping, the car would bounce violently at certain speeds.
Tuning circuits (radio) An LC circuit resonates at a specific frequency determined by L and C. By adjusting these, the circuit selects (amplifies) the desired radio frequency from all the signals received by the antenna.
Musical instruments The body of a guitar or violin resonates with the vibrating strings, amplifying the sound.
MRI scanners Hydrogen nuclei resonate at specific radio frequencies in a magnetic field, allowing imaging of body tissues.
Bridges and buildings If wind or pedestrians drive a bridge at its natural frequency, dangerous resonance can occur (e.g., Tacoma Narrows Bridge, 1940). Dampers are installed to prevent this.
When describing resonance, always state three things: (1) the driving frequency equals the natural frequency, (2) maximum energy transfer occurs, and (3) the amplitude reaches a maximum. For full marks on "explain resonance" questions, include all three points.
