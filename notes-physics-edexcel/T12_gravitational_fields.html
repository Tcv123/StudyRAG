Topic 12 — Gravitational Fields

12.1 Newton's Law of Gravitation

Newton's law of universal gravitation: Every particle in the universe attracts every other particle with a force that is directly proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between their centres.
F = −GMm / r²

where:

  • F = gravitational force (N) — always attractive
  • G = universal gravitational constant = 6.674 × 10⁻¹¹ N m² kg⁻²
  • M, m = masses of the two objects (kg)
  • r = distance between the centres of the masses (m)

The negative sign indicates the force is attractive (directed towards the other mass).

The distance r is measured between the centres of the objects, not their surfaces. If given the radius of the Earth and the altitude of a satellite, you must add them: r = R_E + h.

12.2 Gravitational Field Strength

Gravitational field strength (g): The gravitational force per unit mass experienced by a small test mass placed at that point in the field. It is a vector quantity pointing towards the mass creating the field.
g = F / m

For a point mass or uniform sphere (outside the surface):

g = GM / r²

Units: N kg⁻¹ (equivalent to m s⁻²).

At the Earth's surface: g ≈ 9.81 N kg⁻¹.

12.3 Gravitational Field Patterns

Radial Fields

  • Field lines point radially inward towards the centre of the mass.
  • Field strength decreases with distance (inverse square law).
  • Produced by a point mass or a spherical mass (outside the sphere).
  • Field lines are not parallel — they converge at the centre.

Uniform Fields

  • Field lines are parallel and equally spaced.
  • Field strength is constant at all points.
  • A good approximation for the gravitational field near the Earth's surface over small regions (where the curvature is negligible).

Equipotentials

  • Equipotential lines (or surfaces) join points of equal gravitational potential.
  • Equipotentials are always perpendicular to field lines.
  • No work is done moving a mass along an equipotential surface.
  • For a radial field, equipotentials are concentric circles (spheres in 3D) centred on the mass.
  • For a uniform field, equipotentials are equally spaced horizontal lines.

12.4 Gravitational Potential

Gravitational potential (V): The work done per unit mass in bringing a small test mass from infinity to that point in the field. It is a scalar quantity and is always negative (since gravity is attractive and we define V = 0 at infinity).
V = −GM / r

Units: J kg⁻¹

Key points:

  • V is always negative (or zero at infinity).
  • V becomes more negative as you approach the mass.
  • The potential at infinity is defined as zero.

Relationship Between g and V

g = −dV/dr

The gravitational field strength equals the negative gradient of the potential. On a V–r graph, g is the magnitude of the gradient at any point. The negative sign means g points in the direction of decreasing potential (towards the mass).

On a graph of V against r, the gradient gives g. A steeper gradient means a stronger field. At the surface of a planet, the gradient is steepest.

12.5 Gravitational Potential Energy

Gravitational potential energy (E_p): The work done in bringing a mass from infinity to that point in the field.
E_p = −GMm / r

Note: E_p = mV. The GPE is negative because work must be done against gravity to move an object from that point to infinity (i.e., the object is bound to the gravitational field).

The change in GPE when moving between two points:

ΔE_p = −GMm(1/r₁ − 1/r₂)
Do not confuse E_p = mgh (valid only for uniform fields near the surface) with E_p = −GMm/r (the general expression for any distance). Use the general form for orbital and escape velocity problems.

12.6 Escape Velocity

Escape velocity: The minimum speed an object must have at the surface to escape to infinity without any further energy input (i.e., to reach infinity with zero kinetic energy).

Setting KE + PE = 0 at infinity:

½mv² + (−GMm/r) = 0
vescape = √(2GM / r)

Note: Escape velocity does not depend on the mass of the escaping object — only on M and r of the body being escaped from.

12.7 Orbital Motion

For a satellite in a circular orbit, gravitational force provides the centripetal force:

GMm / r² = mv² / r

Orbital Speed

v = √(GM / r)

Orbital speed depends only on the mass of the central body and the orbital radius — not on the satellite's mass.

Orbital Period

Since v = 2πr / T:

T = 2πr / v = 2π √(r³ / GM)

Squaring:

T² = (4π² / GM) r³

This is Kepler's third law: T² is proportional to r³.

12.8 Kepler's Laws

Law Statement
First Law All planets orbit the Sun in ellipses with the Sun at one focus.
Second Law A line joining a planet to the Sun sweeps out equal areas in equal time intervals. (The planet moves faster when closer to the Sun.)
Third Law The square of the orbital period is proportional to the cube of the semi-major axis (mean orbital radius): T² ∝ r³.
Kepler's third law can be verified experimentally by plotting T² against r³ — the result is a straight line through the origin with gradient 4π²/(GM).

12.9 Types of Orbit

Geostationary Orbit

  • Orbital period = 24 hours (same as Earth's rotation).
  • Orbits directly above the equator.
  • Same direction as Earth's rotation (west to east).
  • Appears stationary relative to a point on the Earth's surface.
  • Altitude ≈ 35,800 km above the surface (r ≈ 42,200 km from centre).
  • Uses: Communications satellites, TV broadcasting, weather monitoring.

Low Earth Orbit (LEO)

  • Altitude: typically 200–2000 km above the surface.
  • Orbital period: ~90 minutes to 2 hours.
  • Can be at any inclination (including polar orbits).
  • Satellites move relative to the Earth's surface.
  • Uses: Earth observation, scientific research, GPS constellation, International Space Station, imaging/reconnaissance.
Property Geostationary Low Earth Orbit
Altitude ~35,800 km 200–2000 km
Period 24 hours ~90 min – 2 hours
Orbital speed ~3.1 km/s ~7.5–8 km/s
Inclination 0° (equatorial) Any (including polar)
Position relative to surface Stationary Moves across the sky
A higher orbit means a lower orbital speed but a longer orbital period. Do not confuse the two — students often incorrectly think faster orbit means shorter period at all radii. Use v = √(GM/r) and T = 2π√(r³/GM) to check.
