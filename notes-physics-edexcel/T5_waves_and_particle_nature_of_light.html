Topic 5 — Waves and Particle Nature of Light

5.1 Types of Waves

Transverse wave: Oscillations are perpendicular to the direction of energy transfer. Examples: light, all EM waves, water surface waves, waves on a string, S-waves (seismic).

Longitudinal wave: Oscillations are parallel to the direction of energy transfer. Examples: sound, ultrasound, P-waves (seismic), pressure waves in a slinky.

Longitudinal waves consist of compressions (high pressure/density regions) and rarefactions (low pressure/density regions). Only transverse waves can be polarised.

Polarisation

Polarisation: Restricting the oscillations of a transverse wave to a single plane. Only transverse waves can be polarised (this is evidence that light is a transverse wave). Unpolarised light oscillates in all planes perpendicular to the direction of travel.

A polarising filter (Polaroid) only transmits waves oscillating in one plane. When two polarising filters are aligned, light passes through. When they are crossed (at 90 degrees), no light passes. This is called Malus's Law: I = I0cos2θ.

Real-World Application: Polaroid sunglasses reduce glare from reflections (light reflected off water or roads is partially polarised horizontally; vertical polarising lenses block this). LCD screens use polarising filters. 3D cinema glasses use differently polarised light for each eye.

The intensity of a wave is proportional to the square of the amplitude: I ∝ A2.

Phase and Phase Difference

Phase difference: How much one wave is ahead of or behind another, measured in degrees (0-360) or radians (0-2π).
  • In phase: Phase difference = 0 (or 2π, 4π, ...) = nλ path difference. Waves reinforce.
  • Antiphase: Phase difference = π (or 3π, 5π, ...) = (n+½)λ path difference. Waves cancel.
Phase difference = (Δx / λ) × 2π rad = (Δx / λ) × 360°

5.3 Superposition and Interference

Principle of superposition: When two or more waves overlap at a point, the resultant displacement equals the vector sum of the individual displacements at that point and at that instant.

Interference

  • Constructive: Waves in phase (path difference = nλ). Amplitudes add. Bright fringe / loud sound.
  • Destructive: Waves in antiphase (path difference = (n+½)λ). Amplitudes cancel. Dark fringe / silence.

Conditions for Observable Interference

  • Sources must be coherent — same frequency and constant phase relationship.
  • Waves must have similar amplitudes for complete cancellation.
  • For transverse waves, they must be polarised in the same plane (or unpolarised).

Young's Double Slit Experiment

Coherent light passes through two narrow slits and produces an interference pattern of equally spaced bright and dark fringes on a screen.

w = λD / d

where w = fringe spacing, λ = wavelength, D = slit-to-screen distance, d = slit separation.

Worked Example: In a Young's double slit experiment, a laser of wavelength 635 nm is used. The slit separation is 0.50 mm and the screen is 2.0 m away. Calculate the fringe spacing.

Solution:
w = λD/d = (635 × 10-9 × 2.0) / (0.50 × 10-3) = 1.27 × 10-6 / 5.0 × 10-4 = 2.54 × 10-3 m = 2.5 mm
Young's double slit experiment provides evidence for the wave nature of light. The experiment requires coherent sources (achieved by using a laser or by passing light through a single slit first to create a point source, then through two slits). With white light, the central fringe is white, but subsequent fringes show coloured edges because different wavelengths have different fringe spacings.

A stationary wave is formed by the superposition of two progressive waves of equal frequency and amplitude travelling in opposite directions. Energy is stored but not transferred along the wave.

5.5 Diffraction

Diffraction: The spreading of a wave as it passes through a gap or around an obstacle. Maximum diffraction occurs when the gap width is approximately equal to the wavelength.

Single Slit Diffraction

A single slit produces a central maximum that is twice the width of subsidiary maxima. Most of the intensity is in the central maximum. Wider slit = narrower central maximum (less diffraction).

Diffraction Grating

A grating with many equally spaced slits produces sharp, bright maxima at specific angles:

d sin θ = nλ

where d = slit spacing = 1/N (N = number of slits per metre), θ = angle of nth order maximum.

Worked Example: A diffraction grating has 500 lines per mm. Light of wavelength 590 nm is used. Calculate (a) the slit spacing, (b) the angle of the 2nd order maximum, (c) the maximum observable order.

Solution:
(a) d = 1/(500 × 103) = 1/(5 × 105) = 2.0 × 10-6 m
(b) d sinθ = nλ. sinθ = nλ/d = 2 × 590 × 10-9 / 2.0 × 10-6 = 0.590
θ = sin-1(0.590) = 36.2 degrees
(c) Max order: nmax = d/λ = 2.0 × 10-6 / 590 × 10-9 = 3.39, so nmax = 3 (rounded down)
The maximum order observable is found by setting sinθ = 1 (i.e. θ = 90 degrees): nmax = d/λ, rounded down to the nearest integer. For each order, there is a maximum on both sides of the central maximum, so the total number of maxima = 2nmax + 1 (including the zero order).

5.6 Refraction and Total Internal Reflection

Refraction: The change in direction of a wave as it passes between media with different wave speeds, caused by a change in velocity. The frequency stays constant; the wavelength changes.

Snell's Law

n1 sin θ1 = n2 sin θ2
n = c / v

where n = refractive index (dimensionless), c = speed of light in vacuum, v = speed of light in the medium. When light enters a denser medium (higher n), it slows down and bends towards the normal.

Total Internal Reflection (TIR)

Occurs when light travels from a denser to a less dense medium and the angle of incidence exceeds the critical angle:

sin θc = n2 / n1

Conditions: (1) light goes from higher n to lower n; (2) angle of incidence > critical angle.

Worked Example: Calculate the critical angle for glass (n = 1.50) to air (n = 1.00).

Solution:
sin θc = n2/n1 = 1.00/1.50 = 0.667
θc = sin-1(0.667) = 41.8 degrees
Real-World Application: Optical fibres use TIR to transmit light signals over long distances with minimal loss. Light enters the fibre at a shallow angle and reflects off the cladding (which has a lower refractive index than the core) repeatedly along the fibre. Used in telecommunications, medical endoscopes, and high-speed internet.

5.7 Lenses

Converging (Convex) Lens

Converges parallel rays to the principal focus (F). Focal length f is positive. Can form real or virtual images.

Diverging (Concave) Lens

Diverges rays so they appear to come from a virtual focus. Focal length f is negative. Always produces virtual, upright, diminished images.

Lens Equation

1/f = 1/v + 1/u

Sign convention: real is positive, virtual is negative for image distance.

Magnification and Power

Magnification = v/u = image height / object height
Power P = 1/f (dioptres, D; f in metres)

Converging lenses have positive power; diverging lenses have negative power.

5.9 Photoelectric Effect

The photoelectric effect is the emission of electrons from a metal surface when EM radiation of sufficiently high frequency is incident. It provides key evidence for the particle (photon) nature of light.

Key Observations (that wave theory cannot explain)

  • Emission is instantaneous (no time delay, even at low intensity).
  • Below a threshold frequency f0, no electrons are emitted regardless of intensity.
  • Increasing intensity increases the number of photoelectrons, not their maximum KE.
  • Increasing frequency increases the maximum KE of the emitted electrons.

Einstein's Photoelectric Equation

hf = φ + Ek(max)
  • h = Planck's constant = 6.63 × 10-34 J s
  • f = frequency of incident photon
  • φ = work function = minimum energy to release an electron = hf0
  • Ek(max) = maximum kinetic energy of emitted photoelectron = ½mv2max
k(max)
Worked Example: The work function of sodium is 2.28 eV. UV light of wavelength 250 nm is incident on a sodium surface. Calculate (a) the energy of each photon, (b) the maximum KE of the emitted electrons, (c) the maximum speed of the electrons.

Solution:
(a) E = hc/λ = (6.63 × 10-34 × 3.0 × 108) / (250 × 10-9) = 7.96 × 10-19 J
= 7.96 × 10-19 / 1.60 × 10-19 = 4.97 eV
(b) Ek(max) = hf − φ = 4.97 − 2.28 = 2.69 eV = 4.30 × 10-19 J
(c) Ek = ½mv2. v = √(2Ek/m) = √(2 × 4.30 × 10-19 / 9.11 × 10-31) = 9.71 × 105 m s-1
The photoelectric effect cannot be explained by the wave model of light. Waves would predict: (1) any frequency could cause emission given enough time/intensity, (2) there would be a time delay as energy accumulates, (3) higher intensity would give higher KE. All three predictions are wrong. The photon model explains everything: one photon transfers all its energy to one electron in a single interaction.

5.10 Wave-Particle Duality

Wave-particle duality: All matter and radiation exhibit both wave and particle properties. Light behaves as waves (diffraction, interference) and as particles (photoelectric effect). Electrons behave as particles (deflection by fields) and as waves (electron diffraction).

de Broglie Wavelength

λ = h / mv = h / p

Every moving particle has an associated wavelength. The faster or more massive the particle, the shorter its wavelength. Wave behaviour is observable when λ is comparable to the size of obstacles (e.g. electrons diffracted by atomic spacing ~10-10 m).

Worked Example: Calculate the de Broglie wavelength of an electron accelerated through a PD of 200 V.

Solution:
KE = eV = 1.60 × 10-19 × 200 = 3.20 × 10-17 J
½mv2 = 3.20 × 10-17, so v = √(2 × 3.20 × 10-17 / 9.11 × 10-31) = 8.38 × 106 m s-1
λ = h/mv = 6.63 × 10-34 / (9.11 × 10-31 × 8.38 × 106) = 8.67 × 10-11 m (0.087 nm)
This is comparable to atomic spacing, so electron diffraction is observable.

5.11 Energy Levels and Photon Emission

Electrons in atoms exist in discrete energy levels. The ground state (n = 1) is the lowest. When an electron transitions between levels, it emits or absorbs a photon whose energy exactly equals the energy gap.
ΔE = E2 − E1 = hf = hc/λ
  • Emission: Electron drops to a lower level, emitting a photon with E = ΔE between levels.
  • Absorption: Electron absorbs a photon and jumps to a higher level. Photon energy must exactly match the gap.
  • Line spectra: Discrete energy levels produce characteristic line spectra. Emission spectra show bright lines; absorption spectra show dark lines against a continuous background.

The Electronvolt

1 eV = 1.60 × 10-19 J

The energy gained by one electron accelerated through 1 V. Convenient for atomic-scale energies.

Energy levels are negative (bound states). The ground state is the most negative. Convert eV to joules (multiply by 1.60 × 10-19) before using E = hf to find frequency or wavelength. The ionisation energy from the ground state equals the magnitude of the ground state energy (13.6 eV for hydrogen).

⚗ Core Practical 2: Stationary waves on a string

Aim

To investigate how frequency, length, and tension affect stationary waves on a vibrating string. Verify f = (1/2L)√(T/μ).

Apparatus

Signal generator, vibration generator, string (known μ), pulley, slotted masses, metre ruler.

Method

  1. Attach string to vibrator. Pass over pulley with hanging mass (tension T = mg).
  2. Slowly increase frequency until the fundamental (1 antinode) forms. Record f and L.
  3. Find higher harmonics. For each: λ = 2L/n.
  4. Vary tension (change mass) and repeat. Plot f vs 1/L or f2 vs T.

Safety

Safety screen in case string snaps. Cushion under masses. Clamp vibrator firmly.

Sources of Error

ErrorTypeImprovement
Judging exact resonanceRandomUse paper rider (falls off at antinode at resonance)
String vibrates in wrong planeRandomEnsure vibrator aligned; string horizontal
Frequency dial inaccurateSystematicUse digital frequency meter
At the fundamental, λ = 2L. For the nth harmonic: λ = 2L/n, fn = nf1. The number of antinodes equals the harmonic number.

⚗ Core Practical 6: Sonometer investigation (length and tension effects on resonant frequency)

Aim

To investigate how the resonant frequency of a stretched wire depends on length and tension using a sonometer.

Apparatus

Sonometer (hollow wooden box with wire), two movable bridges, tuning fork(s) of known frequency (or microphone + CRO), mass hanger and masses, metre ruler, paper rider.

Method

  1. Set tension T = mg using hanging mass. Place paper rider on wire at midpoint.
  2. Strike tuning fork, press base on sonometer box.
  3. Adjust bridge position until resonance (paper rider flies off). Measure vibrating length L.
  4. Effect of length: Keep T constant, use different tuning forks, find resonant L each time. Plot f vs 1/L.
  5. Effect of tension: Keep L constant, vary hanging mass, find resonance. Plot f2 vs T.

Analysis

f = (1/2L)√(T/μ). Plot f vs 1/L: gradient = (1/2)√(T/μ). Plot f2 vs T: gradient = 1/(4L2μ).

Safety

Goggles (wire under tension). Cushion under masses. Do not over-tighten wire.

Sources of Error

ErrorTypeImprovement
Judging exact resonance pointRandomPaper rider method; try microphone + CRO for precise match
Measuring L to wrong bridge edgeRandomMeasure from inner edge of each bridge; repeat
Friction at pulley reduces effective tensionSystematicUse low-friction pulley
The paper rider confirms resonance: maximum energy transfer from tuning fork to wire causes it to vibrate violently and throw off the rider. If the wire is not at resonance, the rider stays still.
