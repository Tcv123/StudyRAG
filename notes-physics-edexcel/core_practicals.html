⚗ CP1: Acceleration of a Freely Falling Object

Apparatus

  • Electromagnet with release switch
  • Steel ball bearing
  • Trapdoor switch and electronic timer (or light gates)
  • Metre ruler / 2 m ruler
  • Clamp stand and boss

Method

  1. Clamp the electromagnet at height s above the trapdoor.
  2. When the switch is released, the timer starts; the ball falls and hits the trapdoor, stopping the timer.
  3. Record time t. Repeat 3 times at each height and take the mean.
  4. Vary s (at least 6 values, 0.2–1.0 m).
  5. Plot s vs t². Gradient = g/2.

Safety

  • Soft pad beneath trapdoor to catch ball
  • Stable clamp stand; keep feet clear

Analysis

s = ½gt² ⇒ gradient of s vs t² = g/2

Expected: g ≈ 9.81 m s−2.

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Residual magnetism delays ball releaseSystematicUse a non-magnetic release mechanism or demagnetiser
Air resistance on ballSystematicUse a small, dense ball
Height measurement uncertaintyRandomUse a 2 m ruler; measure from bottom of ball to trapdoor
Timer precisionRandomUse electronic timer; repeat and average
Exam Tip: A non-zero y-intercept on the s vs t² graph suggests systematic error (e.g. timing delay from residual magnetism). Light gates can remove this source of error entirely.
Worked Example: A student records the following data: s = 0.80 m, mean t = 0.404 s. Calculate g.

s = ½gt², so g = 2s/t² = 2 × 0.80 / (0.404)² = 1.60 / 0.163 = 9.8 m s−2

The percentage uncertainty in t is typically ~1-2% (from reaction time when using electronic timer), giving ~2-4% uncertainty in g (since t is squared).

⚗ CP2: Frequency of Stationary Waves on a String

Apparatus

  • Signal generator and vibrator
  • String (nylon thread), pulley, bench clamp
  • Slotted masses and hanger (for tension)
  • Metre ruler

Method

  1. Attach the string to the vibrator, pass it over a pulley, and hang masses to provide tension T = mg.
  2. Adjust the frequency until the fundamental mode (1 loop) is observed.
  3. Record the frequency and measure the vibrating length L.
  4. Increase frequency to find 2nd, 3rd, etc. harmonics.
  5. Vary tension by changing masses and repeat.

Safety

  • Safety screen in case string snaps
  • Soft pad beneath hanging masses

Analysis

f = (1/2L)√(T/μ)

Plot f vs 1/L (constant T): straight line through origin, gradient = (1/2)√(T/μ).

Plot f vs √T (constant L): straight line through origin.

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Judging resonance by eyeRandomUse a paper rider; resonance flicks it off
String mass not negligibleSystematicInclude string mass when calculating μ
Measuring vibrating lengthRandomUse a mm ruler; measure node to node
Mass hanger mass omittedSystematicInclude hanger mass in tension
Exam Tip: The vibrator is not quite at a node, so the measured L is slightly longer than the true resonant length. Measure between nodes rather than from the vibrator end for better accuracy.

⚗ CP3: Young Modulus of a Wire

Apparatus

  • Long thin wire (copper, ~2 m)
  • Micrometer screw gauge
  • Travelling microscope or vernier scale (for extension)
  • Slotted masses and hanger
  • Tape measure, G-clamp, safety goggles

Method

  1. Measure the wire diameter at several points using a micrometer. Calculate mean d and A = πd²/4.
  2. Measure original length L with tape measure.
  3. Add masses in steps (e.g. 100 g). After each addition, measure extension with travelling microscope.
  4. Record F = mg and ΔL at each step.
  5. Unload to check elastic behaviour (wire returns to original length).
  6. Plot stress vs strain. Gradient = Young modulus E.

Safety

  • Wear safety goggles — wire may snap
  • Cushion or sand tray beneath masses
  • Do not exceed the elastic limit

Analysis

E = stress / strain = (F/A) / (ΔL/L) = FL / (AΔL)

Plot stress (σ) vs strain (ε). Gradient of linear region = E.

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Wire diameter variesRandomMultiple readings at different positions; use mean
Kinks in wireSystematicApply small initial load to straighten wire
Thermal expansionSystematicUse a reference wire for compensation
Small extensions hard to measureRandomUse travelling microscope; use long, thin wire
Exam Tip: The percentage uncertainty in diameter is the dominant source because d appears squared in the area formula. Always use a micrometer and take at least 6 readings at different positions and orientations.
Worked Example: A copper wire has: L = 2.50 m, d = 0.28 mm, extension = 1.8 mm under a load of 30 N. Calculate the Young modulus.

A = πd²/4 = π(0.28 × 10−3)²/4 = 6.16 × 10−8 m²
Stress = F/A = 30 / 6.16 × 10−8 = 4.87 × 108 Pa
Strain = ΔL/L = 1.8 × 10−3 / 2.50 = 7.2 × 10−4
E = stress/strain = 4.87 × 108 / 7.2 × 10−4 = 6.8 × 1011 Pa
(Accepted value for copper: ~1.1 × 1011 Pa — the discrepancy suggests the diameter was measured inaccurately, since it has the largest percentage uncertainty.)

⚗ CP4: Resistivity of a Wire

Apparatus

  • Constantan wire (~1 m, taped to ruler)
  • Ammeter and voltmeter
  • Cell/power supply and switch
  • Micrometer screw gauge
  • Metre ruler, crocodile clips

Method

  1. Measure wire diameter at several points using micrometer. Calculate A = πd²/4.
  2. Set up circuit: cell, switch, ammeter in series with wire; voltmeter across wire.
  3. Use clips to select length L. Record V and I. Calculate R = V/I.
  4. Repeat for 6+ lengths (0.1–1.0 m).
  5. Plot R vs L. Gradient = ρ/A, so ρ = gradient × A.

Safety

  • Switch off between readings to prevent heating
  • Low voltage (< 6 V); do not touch energised wire

Analysis

R = ρL/A ⇒ plot R vs L, gradient = ρ/A

Expected: ρ (constantan) ≈ 4.9 × 10−7 Ω m.

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Wire heating changes RSystematicSwitch off between readings; use low current
Contact resistance at clipsSystematicClean contacts; ensure firm grip
Diameter variationRandomMultiple readings; take mean
Length measurement at clipsRandomTape wire to ruler; align clips carefully
Exam Tip: Constantan is chosen because its resistivity barely changes with temperature, unlike copper. The graph of R vs L should pass through the origin; a non-zero intercept suggests contact resistance.

⚗ CP5: EMF and Internal Resistance

Apparatus

  • Cell (1.5 V), variable resistor, ammeter, voltmeter
  • Switch, connecting wires

Method

  1. Connect cell, ammeter, variable resistor in series. Voltmeter across cell terminals.
  2. For each resistance setting, record V and I.
  3. Repeat for 6+ settings. Switch off between readings.
  4. Plot V vs I.

Safety

  • Do not short-circuit the cell
  • Switch off between readings to preserve cell

Analysis

V = ε − Ir. Plot V vs I: y-intercept = ε, gradient = −r.

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Cell runs downSystematicSwitch off between readings; work quickly
Ammeter resistanceSystematicUse low-resistance digital ammeter
Voltmeter draws currentSystematicUse high-impedance digital voltmeter
Fluctuating readingsRandomWait for stable readings; take repeats
Exam Tip: The x-intercept gives the short-circuit current ε/r. The gradient magnitude gives internal resistance. Typical r for a cell: 0.5–2 Ω.

⚗ CP6: Sonometer Investigation

Apparatus

  • Sonometer (wooden board with wire and movable bridges)
  • Set of tuning forks (known frequencies)
  • Slotted masses and hanger (tension)
  • Metre ruler, paper rider

Method

  1. Set a known tension T by hanging masses from one end of the wire.
  2. Strike a tuning fork and hold it near the wire. Move one bridge until the wire resonates (paper rider jumps off).
  3. Measure the resonant length L between the bridges.
  4. Repeat with different tuning forks (different frequencies) or different tensions.
  5. Plot f vs 1/L (constant T) or f vs √T (constant L).

Safety

  • Safety glasses in case wire snaps
  • Do not over-tension the wire
  • Secure masses so they cannot fall

Analysis

f = (1/2L)√(T/μ)

Plot f vs 1/L: straight line through origin. Gradient = (1/2)√(T/μ).

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Difficulty judging exact resonanceRandomUse a paper rider to detect resonance clearly
Bridge position measurementRandomUse mm ruler; align carefully
Wire may not vibrate in fundamentalSystematicPluck gently at centre; listen for pure tone
Tuning fork frequency uncertaintySystematicUse calibrated, high-quality forks
Exam Tip: A sonometer differs from the vibrator method (CP2) in that resonance is matched to external tuning forks rather than driven by a signal generator. Both investigate the same equation f = (1/2L)√(T/μ).

⚗ CP7/CP15: Capacitor Charge and Discharge

Apparatus

  • Capacitor (e.g. 470–2200 μF electrolytic)
  • Resistor (chosen so τ = RC is 10–30 s)
  • DC power supply, two-way switch
  • Voltmeter or datalogger, stopwatch

Method

  1. Connect C and R in series with supply and two-way switch. Datalogger across C.
  2. Charge: flip switch to supply side. Record V vs t as capacitor charges.
  3. Discharge: flip switch to disconnect supply. Record V vs t during discharge.
  4. Plot ln(V) vs t for discharge. Gradient = −1/RC.

Safety

  • Correct polarity for electrolytic capacitors
  • Discharge capacitor before handling
  • Low voltage (< 12 V)

Analysis

V = V0e−t/RC ⇒ ln V = ln V0 − t/(RC)

Plot ln(V) vs t: straight line, gradient = −1/RC, y-intercept = ln V0.

Time constant τ = RC is time for V to fall to 37% of V0.

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Voltmeter draws currentSystematicUse high-impedance voltmeter or datalogger
Manual timing errorsRandomUse datalogger for continuous recording
Capacitor leakageSystematicUse high-quality capacitor
Temperature changesSystematicConduct in stable temperature environment
Exam Tip: Choose R and C so the time constant τ = RC is convenient (10–30 s). After 5τ, the capacitor is effectively fully charged or discharged. The charge on the capacitor is Q = CV, so you can also plot ln(Q) vs t.

⚗ CP8: Unknown Mass from Spring Oscillation Period

Apparatus

  • Helical spring, clamp stand
  • Set of known slotted masses
  • Unknown mass
  • Stopwatch
  • Ruler (optional)

Method

  1. Hang a known mass from the spring. Pull down slightly and release.
  2. Time 10 oscillations. Calculate T. Calculate T².
  3. Repeat for 6+ known masses.
  4. Plot T² vs m (calibration graph). Should be a straight line through the origin.
  5. Hang the unknown mass, measure T, calculate T².
  6. Read off the unknown mass from the calibration graph.

Safety

  • Secure clamp stand with G-clamp
  • Do not overload the spring beyond its elastic limit
  • Keep clear of oscillating masses

Analysis

T = 2π√(m/k) ⇒ T² = (4π²/k)m

Gradient of T² vs m = 4π²/k. Read unknown mass from the graph using its T² value.

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Miscounting oscillationsRandomUse fiducial marker; count from equilibrium
Non-vertical oscillationRandomRelease carefully from rest; use guide tube
Spring mass not negligibleSystematicAdd meff = mspring/3 to the oscillating mass
Reaction timeRandomTime 10+ oscillations
Exam Tip: If the graph does not pass through the origin, the spring has an effective mass of its own (approximately 1/3 of the spring mass). The x-intercept gives this effective mass as a negative offset.

⚗ CP9: Absorption of Gamma Radiation by Lead

Apparatus

  • Gamma source (e.g. cobalt-60, sealed)
  • GM tube and counter/ratemeter
  • Set of lead sheets (varying thickness)
  • Ruler or vernier callipers (to measure lead thickness)
  • Source holder and clamp

Method

  1. Measure background count rate (no source, count for 60+ seconds).
  2. Place source at fixed distance from GM tube. Record count rate with no absorber.
  3. Insert lead sheets of known thickness between source and detector.
  4. Record corrected count rate (subtract background) for each thickness.
  5. Repeat for 6+ thicknesses.
  6. Plot ln(corrected count rate) vs thickness x.

Safety

  • Handle source with tongs; never bare hands
  • Keep maximum distance; minimise exposure time
  • Store source in lead-lined container
  • Follow ALARA principle

Analysis

Intensity follows: I = I0 e−μx

Plot ln(C) vs x: straight line with gradient = −μ (linear attenuation coefficient).

Half-value thickness: x½ = ln 2 / μ.

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Random nature of decayRandomCount for long periods; take repeats
Background radiation fluctuatesRandomMultiple background readings; long count time
Lead sheets not uniform thicknessSystematicMeasure thickness at multiple points with callipers
Scattered radiation reaching detectorSystematicCollimate the beam; use narrow source-detector geometry
Exam Tip: The half-value thickness is the absorber thickness needed to halve the intensity. For gamma radiation in lead, this is typically a few mm to a few cm depending on the energy of the gamma photons.

⚗ CP10: Specific Heat Capacity

Apparatus

  • Metal block (aluminium or copper) with holes for heater and thermometer
  • Immersion heater
  • Thermometer (or temperature probe)
  • Ammeter, voltmeter
  • Power supply, stopwatch
  • Balance (to measure block mass)
  • Insulation (lagging) for the block

Method

  1. Measure the mass of the metal block using a balance.
  2. Insert the heater and thermometer into the block. Add a drop of oil to ensure good thermal contact.
  3. Record the initial temperature θ1.
  4. Switch on the heater. Record V, I, and time t.
  5. Heat for a set time (e.g. 10 minutes). Record final temperature θ2.
  6. Calculate: c = VIt / (mΔθ), where Δθ = θ2 − θ1.

Safety

  • Metal block gets very hot — use tongs or heat-resistant gloves
  • Do not touch the heater element
  • Ensure heater is fully inserted to avoid overheating exposed parts
  • Place block on a heat-resistant mat

Analysis

Energy supplied: E = VIt. Energy gained: E = mcΔθ.

c = VIt / (mΔθ)

Alternatively, plot Δθ vs t. Gradient = VI/(mc), so c = VI/(m × gradient).

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Heat loss to surroundingsSystematic (c appears too high)Insulate the block with lagging
Thermometer not at thermal equilibrium with blockSystematicUse oil in thermometer hole; wait for steady reading
Energy lost heating heater itselfSystematicEnsure heater is small relative to block
Voltage/current fluctuationsRandomMonitor V and I throughout; use regulated supply
Exam Tip: Heat losses mean the measured c is always slightly higher than the true value, because some electrical energy is lost to the surroundings rather than heating the block. Insulation and a cooling correction can reduce this.

⚗ CP11: Viscosity by Falling Ball (Stokes' Law)

Apparatus

  • Tall measuring cylinder filled with glycerol (or viscous oil)
  • Small steel ball bearings (various sizes)
  • Micrometer (to measure ball diameter)
  • Stopwatch
  • Ruler (for distance between timing marks)
  • Thermometer
  • Elastic bands (for timing marks on the cylinder)

Method

  1. Measure the diameter of the ball bearing using a micrometer. Calculate radius r.
  2. Mark two reference lines on the cylinder (well below the surface so the ball reaches terminal velocity).
  3. Gently drop the ball into the centre of the liquid surface.
  4. Time how long the ball takes to travel between the two marks. Calculate terminal velocity v = d/t.
  5. Repeat 3 times and take the mean.
  6. Repeat with different ball sizes to investigate how η depends on r.
  7. Record the temperature of the liquid (viscosity is temperature-dependent).

Safety

  • Clean up spilled glycerol immediately (slipping hazard)
  • Retrieve balls with a magnet to avoid tipping the cylinder
  • Ensure the cylinder is stable and cannot topple

Analysis

At terminal velocity, drag force = weight − upthrust:

6πηrv = (4/3)πr³(ρ − σ)g

η = 2r²(ρ − σ)g / (9v)

where ρ = density of ball, σ = density of fluid.

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Ball not at terminal velocity between marksSystematicPlace top mark well below the surface
Ball not falling centrally (wall effects)SystematicUse a wide cylinder; drop ball in the centre
Temperature changes affect viscositySystematicMonitor temperature; work quickly
Timing reaction timeRandomUse a longer distance between marks; video analysis
Exam Tip: Stokes' law only applies at low Reynolds numbers (laminar flow). Use small, slow-moving balls in viscous fluid. The ball must reach terminal velocity before timing begins, so place the top marker well below the liquid surface.

⚗ CP12: Boyle's Law

Apparatus

  • Sealed gas syringe or Boyle's law apparatus
  • Bourdon pressure gauge
  • Thermometer (to verify constant temperature)

Method

  1. Record initial volume V and pressure P.
  2. Slowly change the volume by pushing/pulling the piston. Wait for temperature to equilibrate.
  3. Record new V and P. Repeat for 6+ data points.
  4. Plot P vs 1/V. Expect a straight line through the origin.

Safety

  • Do not over-pressurise
  • Use safety screen with pressurised apparatus

Analysis

PV = nRT = constant (at constant T)

P vs 1/V: straight line through origin. Gradient = nRT.

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Gas temperature changes on compressionSystematicCompress slowly; wait for equilibrium
Friction in pistonSystematicLubricate; tap gently
Gas leaksSystematicCheck seals with soap solution
Parallax on pressure gaugeRandomRead at eye level; use digital gauge
Exam Tip: Plotting P vs 1/V gives a straight line that is easier to verify than plotting PV vs P (which should be a horizontal line). Ensure temperature stays constant; even small temperature changes will affect results.

⚗ CP13: Thermistor Calibration in Potential Divider

s R1 NTC V Water bath Vₒ⁵⁴ varies with temperature Plot Vₒ⁵⁴ vs θ for calibration

Apparatus

  • NTC thermistor
  • Fixed resistor R1
  • DC power supply
  • Voltmeter (across thermistor)
  • Water bath, thermometer
  • Beaker, stirrer, heat source

Method

  1. Set up the potential divider: supply → R1 → thermistor → back to supply. Voltmeter across the thermistor.
  2. Place the thermistor in a water bath at a known temperature.
  3. Record the temperature θ and voltage V across the thermistor.
  4. Heat the water bath slowly. Record V at regular temperature intervals (e.g. every 5 °C).
  5. Continue from room temperature to ~80 °C.
  6. Plot V vs θ to create a calibration curve.

Safety

  • Handle hot water carefully; do not overheat
  • Electrical components must be kept dry
  • Use a heat-resistant mat

Analysis

For an NTC thermistor, resistance decreases as temperature rises. In the potential divider:

Vout = Vs × RT / (R1 + RT)

As temperature rises, RT falls, so Vout decreases. The calibration curve allows unknown temperatures to be determined from voltage readings.

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Temperature not uniform in water bathSystematicStir the water continuously
Thermistor response time lagSystematicHeat slowly; allow time for equilibrium
Self-heating of thermistor by currentSystematicUse a small current (high R1)
Thermometer reading uncertaintyRandomUse a digital thermometer with 0.1 °C resolution
Exam Tip: The sensitivity of the thermistor circuit depends on the choice of R1. Maximum sensitivity occurs when R1 is similar in value to the thermistor resistance at the temperature of interest. NTC means "negative temperature coefficient."

⚗ CP14: Pressure-Volume at Fixed Temperature

Apparatus

  • Gas syringe or Boyle's law apparatus immersed in water bath
  • Pressure gauge
  • Thermometer
  • Constant temperature water bath (thermostatically controlled if possible)

Method

  1. Immerse the gas apparatus in a constant-temperature water bath.
  2. Allow sufficient time for thermal equilibrium.
  3. Record V and P at this temperature.
  4. Change V slowly. Wait for temperature to re-equilibrate. Record new P.
  5. Repeat for 6+ volumes at the same temperature.
  6. Optionally repeat at a different temperature for comparison.
  7. Plot P vs 1/V.

Safety

  • Do not over-pressurise apparatus
  • Handle hot water carefully if using elevated temperatures
  • Safety screen near pressurised equipment

Analysis

PV = nRT = constant at fixed T

P vs 1/V: straight line through origin. Gradient = nRT. Compare gradients at different temperatures.

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Temperature not truly constantSystematicUse thermostatically controlled bath; allow equilibrium
Friction in pistonSystematicLubricate; tap gently to free
Gas leaksSystematicCheck seals; use Vaseline
Reading V and P simultaneouslyRandomWait for stable readings before recording
Exam Tip: This practical is an extension of CP12 (Boyle's Law) with explicit temperature control. The gradient of P vs 1/V increases at higher temperatures because nRT is larger. This is consistent with the ideal gas equation.

⚗ CP16: Specific Latent Heat

Apparatus

  • Immersion heater
  • Beaker (insulated) with crushed ice or boiling water
  • Thermometer
  • Top-pan balance
  • Ammeter, voltmeter
  • Power supply, stopwatch
  • Measuring cylinder (to collect meltwater or condensate)

Method (Latent Heat of Fusion)

  1. Place crushed ice in a funnel over a beaker on a balance. Allow meltwater to drip.
  2. First, measure the mass of ice that melts in a set time without the heater (control).
  3. Repeat with the heater on. Record V, I, and time t.
  4. The extra mass of ice melted due to the heater is Δm = mheater − mcontrol.
  5. Calculate Lf = VIt / Δm.

Method (Latent Heat of Vaporisation)

  1. Boil water in an insulated beaker on a balance. Record initial mass.
  2. Record V, I, and heat for a set time t.
  3. Record final mass. Mass of water evaporated: Δm = minitial − mfinal.
  4. Calculate Lv = VIt / Δm.

Safety

  • Boiling water and steam can cause severe burns — handle with care
  • Use insulated beaker; keep heater submerged
  • Ensure electrical connections are dry
  • Use a heat-resistant mat

Analysis

E = VIt = Δm × L

L = VIt / Δm

Expected: Lf (ice) ≈ 334 kJ kg−1; Lv (water) ≈ 2260 kJ kg−1.

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Heat loss to surroundingsSystematicInsulate the beaker; use a control (no heater) for fusion
Splashing during boilingSystematicUse a gentle heat; cover beaker loosely
Heater not fully submergedSystematicEnsure heater stays immersed throughout
Mass measurement while steam escapesRandomRead balance quickly; use a lid with a small hole
Exam Tip: The control experiment (melting without heater) is essential for latent heat of fusion. It accounts for heat absorbed from the surroundings. For vaporisation, energy is lost to heating the surroundings, so measured Lv is typically slightly higher than the accepted value.
