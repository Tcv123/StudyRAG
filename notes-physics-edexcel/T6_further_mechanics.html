Topic 6 — Further Mechanics

6.1 Impulse and Momentum

Momentum (p) is the product of an object's mass and velocity. It is a vector quantity.
p = mv   (unit: kg m s⁻¹ or N s)

Impulse

Impulse is defined as the change in momentum of an object. It equals the product of the resultant force and the time for which it acts.

Impulse = FΔt = Δp = mv − mu

The unit of impulse is N s (equivalent to kg m s⁻¹).

Force-Time Graphs

On a force-time graph, the area under the curve equals the impulse (change in momentum). This applies whether the force is constant or varying.

For a variable force, you estimate the area using counting squares or the trapezium rule.

If a question gives you a force-time graph, calculate the area under it to find the impulse. Remember to include units (N s).

6.2 Conservation of Momentum

The principle of conservation of linear momentum: In a closed system (no external resultant force), the total momentum before an interaction equals the total momentum after the interaction.

Conservation of Momentum in 2D

In two dimensions, momentum is conserved independently in each perpendicular direction. You resolve momentum into x and y components:

  • Total x-momentum before = Total x-momentum after
  • Total y-momentum before = Total y-momentum after

Vector diagrams can be drawn to show that the total momentum vectors form a closed triangle (or polygon) if momentum is conserved.

Elastic vs Inelastic Collisions

Property Elastic Collision Inelastic Collision
Momentum Conserved Conserved
Kinetic Energy Conserved Not conserved (some KE converted to other forms)
Total Energy Conserved Conserved
Examples Collisions between gas molecules, hard spheres Most real-world collisions, car crashes

A perfectly inelastic (totally inelastic) collision is one where the objects stick together after impact. Maximum kinetic energy is lost (consistent with momentum conservation).

To check if a collision is elastic, calculate the total KE before and after. If they are equal, it is elastic. Always show your working clearly.

6.3 Circular Motion

Circular motion occurs when an object moves in a circular path at constant speed. Although speed is constant, the velocity is continuously changing direction, so the object is accelerating.

Angular Velocity

Angular velocity (ω) is the rate of change of angular displacement. It is measured in radians per second (rad s⁻¹).

ω = 2π / T = 2πf

where T is the period (time for one complete revolution) and f is the frequency.

The relationship between linear speed v and angular velocity:

v = ωr

Centripetal Acceleration

An object in uniform circular motion has an acceleration directed towards the centre of the circle. This is the centripetal acceleration.

a = v² / r = ω²r

Centripetal Force

The centripetal force is the resultant force directed towards the centre of the circle that causes circular motion. It is not a new type of force — it is provided by existing forces (tension, gravity, friction, normal reaction, etc.).

F = mv² / r = mω²r
Never label a force as "centripetal force" on a free-body diagram. Instead, identify the real force providing the centripetal force (e.g., tension, weight, friction).

Examples of Circular Motion

Banked Tracks

On a banked track (no friction), the horizontal component of the normal reaction provides the centripetal force:

  • Vertical: N cos θ = mg
  • Horizontal: N sin θ = mv²/r
  • Dividing: tan θ = v²/(rg)

The banking angle determines the speed at which no friction is needed to maintain circular motion.

Vertical Circles

For an object on a string in a vertical circle:

  • At the top: T + mg = mv²/r (both tension and weight act towards centre)
  • At the bottom: T − mg = mv²/r (tension acts towards centre, weight acts away)

The minimum speed at the top occurs when tension T = 0:

v_min = √(rg)   (at the top)

Conical Pendulum

A conical pendulum consists of a mass on a string that traces a horizontal circle. The string makes angle θ with the vertical.

  • Vertical equilibrium: T cos θ = mg
  • Centripetal force: T sin θ = mω²r
  • Since r = L sin θ, dividing gives: tan θ = ω²r / g
