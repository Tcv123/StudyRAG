Topic 4 — Materials

4.1 Density

Density (ρ) is the mass per unit volume of a material. It is a scalar quantity measured in kg m-3.
ρ = m / V

where m = mass (kg) and V = volume (m³).

Measuring Density

  • Regular solids: Measure mass with a balance; calculate volume from dimensions (e.g., V = length × width × height for a cuboid, V = πr²h for a cylinder).
  • Irregular solids: Use a displacement method — submerge in water and measure the volume of water displaced.
  • Liquids: Measure mass of a known volume using a measuring cylinder and balance.

4.2 Hooke's Law

Hooke's Law: The extension of a spring is directly proportional to the applied force, provided the limit of proportionality is not exceeded.
F = kx

where F = force applied (N), k = spring constant (N m-1), x = extension (m).

The spring constant (k) is the force per unit extension. A stiffer spring has a larger value of k. The spring constant is equal to the gradient of the linear section of a force-extension graph.

Limit of Proportionality and Elastic Limit

  • Limit of proportionality: The point beyond which force is no longer proportional to extension (Hooke's law no longer applies). The graph is no longer linear.
  • Elastic limit: The point beyond which the material will not return to its original length when the force is removed. Permanent (plastic) deformation occurs beyond this point.

Springs in Series and Parallel

ArrangementCombined Spring Constant
Springs in series (end to end)1/ktotal = 1/k1 + 1/k2
Springs in parallel (side by side)ktotal = k1 + k2
Springs in series are like resistors in parallel — the total spring constant is LESS than either individual constant. Springs in parallel are like resistors in series — the total is the sum. Think of it as: in parallel, the load is shared so it is harder to extend.

4.3 Elastic Potential Energy

Elastic potential energy (Ee) is the energy stored in a stretched or compressed spring. It equals the work done in stretching the spring (the area under the force-extension graph in the linear region).
Ee = ½Fx = ½kx²

This equation only applies when Hooke's law is obeyed (i.e., within the limit of proportionality).

For a non-linear force-extension graph, the energy stored is still the area under the graph, but must be estimated (e.g., by counting squares).

4.4 Stress, Strain, and Young's Modulus

Tensile stress (σ) is the force per unit cross-sectional area. Measured in pascals (Pa) or N m-2.

Tensile strain (ε) is the fractional change in length. It is dimensionless (no units).

Young's modulus (E) is the ratio of stress to strain in the linear (elastic) region. It is a measure of the stiffness of a material. Measured in Pa.

Stress: σ = F / A
Strain: ε = ΔL / L
Young's modulus: E = σ / ε = (FL) / (AΔL)

Measuring Young's Modulus

A common experiment uses a long, thin wire:

  • Measure original length (L) with a metre ruler.
  • Measure diameter with a micrometer (take several readings at different points and calculate mean). Calculate area: A = π(d/2)².
  • Gradually add known masses and measure extension using a marker and ruler (or a travelling microscope for greater precision).
  • Plot a stress-strain graph; the gradient of the linear section = Young's modulus.

4.5 Stress-Strain Graphs

Key Points on a Stress-Strain Graph

  • Limit of proportionality (P): End of the linear region; beyond this, stress is no longer proportional to strain.
  • Elastic limit (E): Beyond this, the material undergoes permanent plastic deformation.
  • Yield point (Y): The point at which a large increase in strain occurs with little or no increase in stress. The material begins to "flow."
  • Ultimate tensile stress (UTS): The maximum stress the material can withstand before necking begins.
  • Fracture point (F): The stress at which the material breaks.

HardResistant to scratching or indentationDiamond

Comparing Stress-Strain Curves

  • Ductile material (e.g., copper): Long curve with significant plastic region before fracture. Shows clear yield point and necking.
  • Brittle material (e.g., glass): Straight line (linear elastic) up to fracture point. Very little strain at fracture. No plastic region.
  • Rubber: Large strain for relatively small stress. The loading and unloading curves differ (hysteresis). Returns to original length but does not obey Hooke's law.
  • Polythene: Low initial stiffness, then "gives" easily with large extension. Does not return to original length — permanent deformation.

4.7 Elastic and Plastic Deformation

Elastic deformation: The material returns to its original shape and size when the force is removed. No permanent change. Energy is fully recoverable.

Plastic deformation: The material is permanently deformed when the force is removed. It does not return to its original dimensions. Energy is dissipated (not recoverable).

Hysteresis

When rubber is loaded and then unloaded, the unloading curve lies below the loading curve. The area between the two curves represents the energy dissipated as thermal energy during one loading-unloading cycle. This is called hysteresis.

4.8 Energy Stored

The energy stored in a deformed material equals the area under the force-extension graph.

  • For a material obeying Hooke's law (linear graph): Energy = ½Fx = ½kx²
  • For a non-linear graph: estimate the area by counting squares or using the trapezium rule.
  • If the loading and unloading curves differ (hysteresis), the energy returned on unloading is the area under the unloading curve. The difference (area enclosed) is energy lost as heat.
The area under a force-extension graph = energy stored (in joules). The area under a stress-strain graph = energy stored per unit volume (in J m-3). Do not confuse the two.

⚗️ Core Practical 3: Determine the Young modulus of a material

Apparatus

  • Long thin wire (copper or steel, ~2–3 m), clamped at the top
  • Reference wire of the same material, clamped alongside
  • Micrometer screw gauge (resolution ±0.01 mm)
  • Metre ruler or tape measure (to measure original length L)
  • Vernier scale or travelling microscope (to measure small extensions)
  • Mass hanger and slotted masses (e.g. 100 g increments)
  • Safety goggles
  • G-clamp and wooden blocks for securing to beam

Method

  1. Clamp both wires to a rigid beam at the ceiling. Attach a small fixed mass to the reference wire to keep it taut.
  2. Measure the original length L of the test wire from the clamp to the marker (use a tape measure).
  3. Use the micrometer to measure the wire diameter d at least 3 positions along the wire and at different orientations. Calculate the mean diameter and cross-sectional area A = π(d/2)².
  4. Attach the mass hanger to the test wire. Zero the vernier scale against the reference wire marker.
  5. Add masses in steps (e.g. 100 g = 0.98 N each). After each addition, wait for the wire to stop oscillating, then read the extension ΔL from the vernier scale.
  6. Record at least 6 different loads. Do not exceed the elastic limit — check by removing the final mass to see if the wire returns to its original length.
  7. Calculate stress = F/A and strain = ΔL/L for each reading.
  8. Plot stress (y-axis) against strain (x-axis).

Safety

  • Wear safety goggles — the wire is under tension and could snap.
  • Place a cushion or box beneath the masses in case they fall.
  • Do not stand directly beneath the loaded wire.
  • Do not exceed the breaking stress of the wire.

Analysis

Young modulus is defined as:

E = stress / strain = (F/A) / (ΔL/L) = FL / (A ΔL)

The gradient of the stress-strain graph in the linear (elastic) region gives the Young modulus E.

Alternatively, plot F (y-axis) against ΔL (x-axis). The gradient = EA/L, so E = (gradient × L) / A.
