Topic 8 — Nuclear and Particle Physics

8.1 Atomic Structure

The atom consists of a small, dense, positively charged nucleus (containing protons and neutrons) surrounded by orbiting electrons.
Particle Relative Charge Relative Mass Location
Proton +1 1 Nucleus
Neutron 0 1 Nucleus
Electron −1 1/1836 ≈ 0.0005 Orbits / electron cloud

Nuclear Notation

A nuclide is represented as: AZX, where A = mass number (protons + neutrons), Z = atomic number (protons), and X is the chemical symbol.

Isotopes

Isotopes are atoms of the same element (same number of protons, same Z) but with different numbers of neutrons (different A). They have the same chemical properties but different physical properties (e.g., different mass, some may be radioactive).

8.2 Fundamental Particles

Quarks

Quarks are fundamental particles that make up hadrons. There are six flavours arranged in three generations:

Generation Quark Symbol Charge Baryon Number Strangeness
1st Up u +2/3 +1/3 0
1st Down d −1/3 +1/3 0
2nd Charm c +2/3 +1/3 0
2nd Strange s −1/3 +1/3 −1
3rd Top t +2/3 +1/3 0
3rd Bottom b −1/3 +1/3 0

Each quark has a corresponding antiquark with opposite charge, baryon number, and strangeness.

Leptons

Leptons are fundamental particles that do not experience the strong nuclear force.

Generation Lepton Symbol Charge Lepton Number
1st Electron e⁻ −1 +1
1st Electron neutrino νₑ 0 +1
2nd Muon μ⁻ −1 +1
2nd Muon neutrino ν_μ 0 +1
3rd Tau τ⁻ −1 +1
3rd Tau neutrino ν_τ 0 +1

Each lepton has a corresponding antilepton with opposite charge and lepton number.

Exchange Particles (Gauge Bosons)

Exchange particles (gauge bosons) are virtual particles that mediate the fundamental forces between other particles.
Force Exchange Particle Acts On Range
Electromagnetic Photon (γ) All charged particles Infinite
Strong nuclear Gluon (g) Quarks and gluons ~10⁻¹⁵ m
Weak nuclear W⁺, W⁻, Z⁰ All fermions ~10⁻¹⁸ m
Gravity Graviton (hypothetical) All particles with mass Infinite

8.3 Hadrons

Hadrons are composite particles made of quarks, held together by the strong force. They are divided into two groups:

Baryons (3 quarks)

  • Proton (p): uud — charge = +2/3 +2/3 −1/3 = +1
  • Neutron (n): udd — charge = +2/3 −1/3 −1/3 = 0
  • Baryon number = +1 for baryons, −1 for antibaryons

Mesons (quark-antiquark pair)

  • Pion (π⁺): ud̄ — charge = +2/3 +1/3 = +1
  • Pion (π⁻): ūd — charge = −2/3 −1/3 = −1
  • Pion (π⁰): uū or dd̄ — charge = 0
  • Kaon (K⁺): us̄ — contains an anti-strange quark, strangeness = +1
  • Baryon number = 0 for all mesons

Antiparticles

Every particle has a corresponding antiparticle with the same mass but opposite charge, baryon number, lepton number, and strangeness. For example: the antiproton (p̄) has quark composition ūūd̄ and charge −1.

8.4 Particle Interactions

Conservation Laws

In all particle interactions, the following quantities are conserved:

Quantity Conserved In
Charge (Q) All interactions
Baryon number (B) All interactions
Lepton number (L) All interactions
Strangeness (S) Strong and electromagnetic only (can change by 0 or ±1 in weak)
Energy and momentum All interactions
Strangeness is conserved in strong interactions but can change by ±1 in weak interactions. This is how strange particles are produced (in pairs via the strong force) but decay individually via the weak force.

Beta Decay at Quark Level

Beta-minus decay (β⁻): A neutron decays into a proton, emitting an electron and an electron antineutrino.

n → p + e⁻ + ν̄ₑ

At quark level: d → u + e⁻ + ν̄ₑ (mediated by W⁻ boson)

Beta-plus decay (β⁺): A proton decays into a neutron, emitting a positron and an electron neutrino.

p → n + e⁺ + νₑ

At quark level: u → d + e⁺ + νₑ (mediated by W⁺ boson)

8.5 Feynman Diagrams

Feynman diagrams are spacetime diagrams that represent particle interactions. Convention:

  • Time axis runs upward (or left to right — check the diagram)
  • Straight lines represent fermions (particles/antiparticles)
  • Wavy lines represent photons or W/Z bosons
  • Curly lines represent gluons
  • Antiparticles have arrows pointing backward in time
  • At each vertex, charge, baryon number, and lepton number are conserved

Electromagnetic interaction example: Electron-electron repulsion — two electrons exchange a virtual photon.

Weak interaction example: Beta-minus decay — a down quark emits a W⁻ boson and becomes an up quark. The W⁻ then decays into an electron and an electron antineutrino.

When drawing Feynman diagrams, always check conservation of charge at every vertex. The W⁺ carries charge +1 and the W⁻ carries charge −1. The Z⁰ and photon are neutral.

8.6 The Standard Model

The Standard Model of particle physics classifies all known fundamental particles and describes three of the four fundamental forces (excluding gravity).

  • Fermions (matter particles, spin ½): 6 quarks + 6 leptons (plus their antiparticles)
  • Bosons (force carriers, integer spin): photon, W±, Z⁰, gluons, Higgs boson

The Higgs boson was confirmed in 2012 at CERN. It is associated with the Higgs field, which gives particles their mass through interaction with the field.

8.7 Pair Production and Annihilation

Pair production: A photon with sufficient energy creates a particle-antiparticle pair. The minimum photon energy required equals the combined rest mass energy of the pair: E = 2mc².
γ → e⁻ + e⁺   (minimum photon energy = 2 × 0.511 MeV = 1.022 MeV)

Pair production must occur near a nucleus (to conserve momentum).

Annihilation: When a particle meets its antiparticle, they annihilate, converting their combined mass-energy into photon energy. Two photons are produced (to conserve momentum).
e⁻ + e⁺ → 2γ

Each photon has minimum energy E = mc² = 0.511 MeV (for electron-positron annihilation at rest).

In annihilation, two photons are produced travelling in opposite directions to conserve momentum. In pair production, a nearby nucleus is needed to absorb recoil momentum.
