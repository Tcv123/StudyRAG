Topic 9 — Thermodynamics

9.1 Internal Energy

Internal energy is the sum of the randomly distributed kinetic energies and potential energies of all the molecules in a system.

The kinetic energy component is associated with the random translational, rotational, and vibrational motion of molecules. It depends on temperature.

The potential energy component is associated with the intermolecular forces between molecules. It depends on the separation of molecules (i.e., the state of matter).

When you heat a substance:

  • If temperature rises: internal energy increases due to increased kinetic energy of molecules
  • If a change of state occurs (at constant temperature): internal energy increases due to increased potential energy as molecules move further apart (bonds breaking)

9.2 Specific Heat Capacity

Specific heat capacity (c) is the energy required to raise the temperature of 1 kg of a substance by 1 K (or 1°C), without a change of state.
Unit: J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹
Q = mcΔθ

where Q = energy transferred (J), m = mass (kg), c = specific heat capacity (J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹), Δθ = temperature change (K or °C).

Experimental Determination

A typical experiment uses an electrical heater immersed in a block or liquid:

  • Measure mass of substance (m)
  • Record initial temperature (θ₁)
  • Heat with known power P for time t, so energy supplied = Pt = VIt
  • Record final temperature (θ₂)
  • Calculate: c = Pt / (m × Δθ)

Sources of error: heat losses to surroundings, uneven heating, thermometer response time. Insulation and stirring help reduce errors.

In calculations, remember that energy supplied by the heater may not all go into raising the temperature — some is lost. The experimental value of c will typically be higher than the true value because of heat losses.

9.3 Specific Latent Heat

Specific latent heat (L) is the energy required to change the state of 1 kg of a substance without a change in temperature.
Specific latent heat of fusion (L_f): solid → liquid
Specific latent heat of vaporisation (L_v): liquid → gas
Unit: J kg⁻¹
Q = mL

During a change of state, temperature remains constant because all energy input goes into breaking/forming intermolecular bonds (changing potential energy), not increasing kinetic energy.

Heating Curves

A heating curve plots temperature against time (or energy) for a substance being heated at constant power:

  • Sloped sections: Temperature is rising — substance is in a single phase. Gradient depends on specific heat capacity (steeper = lower c or lower mass).
  • Flat sections: Temperature constant — change of state occurring. Length depends on specific latent heat (longer = higher L or greater mass).

9.4 Gas Laws

Boyle's Law

Boyle's law: For a fixed mass of gas at constant temperature, the pressure is inversely proportional to the volume.
pV = constant   (at constant T)
p₁V₁ = p₂V₂

Charles's Law

Charles's law: For a fixed mass of gas at constant pressure, the volume is directly proportional to the absolute temperature.
V / T = constant   (at constant p)
V₁ / T₁ = V₂ / T₂

Pressure Law (Gay-Lussac's Law)

Pressure law: For a fixed mass of gas at constant volume, the pressure is directly proportional to the absolute temperature.
p / T = constant   (at constant V)
p₁ / T₁ = p₂ / T₂
All gas law calculations require temperature in kelvin. Convert from Celsius: T(K) = T(°C) + 273.15. A common error is forgetting to convert.

9.5 Ideal Gas Equation

Combining the three gas laws gives the ideal gas equation:

pV = nRT = NkT

where:

Symbol Quantity Value / Unit
p Pressure Pa
V Volume m³
n Number of moles mol
R Molar gas constant 8.314 J mol⁻¹ K⁻¹
N Number of molecules dimensionless
k Boltzmann constant 1.38 × 10⁻²³ J K⁻¹
T Absolute temperature K

The relationship between the constants: R = N_A × k, where N_A = 6.02 × 10²³ mol⁻¹ (Avogadro's number).

Also: N = n × N_A (total number of molecules = moles × Avogadro's number).

An ideal gas is one that obeys the ideal gas equation (pV = nRT) at all pressures and temperatures. Real gases approximate ideal behaviour at low pressures and high temperatures (when molecules are far apart and intermolecular forces are negligible).

9.6 Kinetic Theory of Gases

Assumptions of the Kinetic Theory

  1. The gas contains a very large number of molecules in rapid, random motion
  2. The volume of the molecules is negligible compared to the volume of the container
  3. Collisions between molecules and with the walls are perfectly elastic
  4. The time of a collision is negligible compared to the time between collisions
  5. There are no intermolecular forces between molecules (except during collisions)
  6. Molecules exert pressure on the container walls due to their collisions with the walls

Derivation of the Pressure Equation

By considering the momentum change when a molecule bounces off a wall and using Newton's laws, the kinetic theory gives:

p = ⅓ρc̄²

or equivalently:

pV = ⅓Nmc̄²

where ρ is the gas density, m is the mass of one molecule, N is the total number of molecules, and c̄² is the mean square speed.

Key steps in the derivation:

  1. Consider one molecule of mass m moving with velocity component c_x towards a wall
  2. Change in momentum per collision = 2mc_x (elastic collision, reverses x-component)
  3. Time between collisions with same wall = 2L/c_x (L = length of container)
  4. Force from one molecule = rate of change of momentum = mc_x²/L
  5. Sum over all N molecules: F = Nm c̄_x² / L
  6. Pressure = F/A = Nm c̄_x² / V
  7. Since motion is random: c̄² = c̄_x² + c̄_y² + c̄_z² = 3c̄_x², so c̄_x² = c̄²/3
  8. Therefore: p = Nmc̄² / (3V), or pV = ⅓Nmc̄²

Mean Kinetic Energy

Combining pV = ⅓Nmc̄² with pV = NkT gives:

½mc̄² = (3/2)kT

This shows that the mean translational kinetic energy of a gas molecule is directly proportional to absolute temperature and is the same for all gases at the same temperature, regardless of molecular mass.

For one mole of gas:

½Mc̄² = (3/2)RT

where M is the molar mass.

Root Mean Square Speed

Root mean square (rms) speed is the square root of the mean square speed of molecules in a gas:
c_rms = √(c̄²) = √(3kT/m) = √(3RT/M)

Note: The rms speed is not the same as the mean speed. For a Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution, c_rms > c_mean > c_most probable.

When calculating rms speed, make sure mass is in kg (for single molecule, use m = M/N_A where M is molar mass in kg mol⁻¹). A common error is using molar mass in g mol⁻¹ without converting.
Quantity Formula Unit
Internal energy Sum of KE + PE of all molecules J
Heat capacity equation Q = mcΔθ J
Latent heat equation Q = mL J
Ideal gas equation pV = nRT = NkT —
Pressure equation p = ⅓ρc̄² Pa
Mean KE per molecule ½mc̄² = 3/2 kT J
rms speed c_rms = √(3kT/m) m s⁻¹
The derivation of p = ⅓ρc̄² is a common 6-mark question. Learn the key steps and be clear about where the factor of ⅓ comes from (random motion equally distributed in 3 dimensions).

⚗️ Core Practical 10: Determine the specific heat capacity of a material

Apparatus

  • Metal block (aluminium or copper) with holes for heater and thermometer
  • Immersion heater (12 V) that fits into the block
  • Thermometer or temperature sensor (resolution ±0.5 °C)
  • Ammeter and voltmeter (or joulemeter)
  • DC power supply
  • Stopwatch
  • Balance (resolution ±0.1 g)
  • Insulation (lagging/cotton wool) to wrap the block
  • A little oil in the thermometer hole for good thermal contact

Method

  1. Measure the mass m of the metal block on a balance.
  2. Insert the heater and thermometer into the block. Add a drop of oil to the thermometer hole for good thermal contact.
  3. Wrap the block in insulation to reduce heat losses.
  4. Record the initial temperature θ1.
  5. Switch on the heater. Record the ammeter reading I and voltmeter reading V (or use a joulemeter to read energy directly).
  6. Heat for a measured time t (e.g. 5 minutes). Monitor I and V throughout (they should be approximately constant).
  7. Switch off the heater. Stir the thermometer gently and record the maximum temperature θ2 (the temperature may continue to rise briefly as heat spreads through the block).
  8. Calculate: E = IVt (energy supplied), Δθ = θ2 − θ1.

Safety

  • The metal block will become hot — use tongs or insulated gloves to handle it.
  • Do not touch the heater element when in use.
  • Ensure the power supply is switched off before assembling or disassembling the circuit.
  • Keep water away from the electrical connections.

Analysis

E = mcΔθ   ⇒   c = E / (mΔθ) = IVt / (mΔθ)

Where c = specific heat capacity (J kg-1 K-1), E = energy supplied, m = mass, Δθ = temperature change.

Compare the result with accepted values (e.g. aluminium: 897 J kg-1 K-1, copper: 385 J kg-1 K-1).

For a more thorough analysis: heat for different times, plot Δθ against t (expect a straight line), and use the gradient with E = IVt to find c.

Sources of Error & Improvements

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Heat loss to surroundingsSystematic (gives c too large)Insulate the block well; use a cooling correction
Thermometer not in good thermal contact with blockSystematicAdd oil to the thermometer hole; wait for thermal equilibrium
Heater not transferring all energy to blockSystematicEnsure heater fits snugly; insulate the block
V and I not constant during heatingSystematicMonitor and record V and I throughout; use averages or a joulemeter
Temperature continues to rise after heater is switched offSystematicRecord the maximum temperature reached; allow time for equilibrium
Exam Tip: Heat losses always make the experimental value of c larger than the true value (because not all energy goes into raising the temperature of the block). This is a common evaluation point. To improve, use better insulation, a larger block, or apply a cooling correction.

⚗️ Core Practical 12: Investigate Boyle's law (pressure-volume relationship at constant temperature)

Apparatus

  • Sealed syringe (gas syringe) or Boyle's law apparatus (sealed tube with oil column)
  • Bourdon gauge or digital pressure sensor
  • Volume scale (on syringe barrel, or ruler alongside gas column)
  • Pump or piston to vary volume
  • Thermometer (to check temperature remains constant)

Method

  1. Set up the apparatus with a fixed amount of trapped gas connected to the pressure gauge.
  2. Record the initial volume V and pressure P.
  3. Slowly change the volume (compress or expand the gas) and wait for the pressure to stabilise before reading.
  4. Record at least 6 pairs of P and V readings across a wide range.
  5. After each change, wait at least 30 seconds for the gas to return to room temperature (compression heats the gas temporarily).
  6. Record the room temperature to confirm it stays approximately constant.
  7. Plot P (y-axis) against 1/V (x-axis).

Safety

  • Do not over-pressurise the syringe or apparatus — follow manufacturer guidelines.
  • If using a glass tube, handle carefully to avoid breakage.
  • Ensure all connections are secure to prevent gas leaks.

Analysis

Boyle's law states that at constant temperature:

PV = constant   ⇒   P ∝ 1/V

A graph of P vs 1/V should be a straight line through the origin. The gradient equals the constant (= nRT for an ideal gas).

Alternatively, plot PV vs P — this should give a horizontal straight line if Boyle's law holds.

Sources of Error & Improvements

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Gas temperature changes when compressed quicklySystematicChange volume slowly; wait for thermal equilibrium before reading
Gas leaks from the syringe or connectionsSystematicUse well-sealed apparatus; check for leaks before starting
Friction in the piston makes volume readings unreliableRandomLubricate the syringe; tap gently before reading
Parallax error when reading scalesRandomRead at eye level; use digital sensors where possible
Dead volume in connecting tubes not accounted forSystematicMinimise connecting tube length; add the dead volume to all readings
Exam Tip: If the graph of P vs 1/V does not pass through the origin, this indicates a systematic error (e.g. an uncorrected dead volume). If the line is straight but offset, the relationship P ∝ 1/V still holds but V needs correcting for the dead space.

⚗️ Core Practical 14: Investigate the relationship between pressure and volume at fixed temperature

Apparatus

  • Gas syringe or sealed gas column with oil plug
  • Bourdon gauge or digital pressure sensor
  • Constant-temperature water bath (or large beaker of water with heater and stirrer)
  • Thermometer
  • Ruler or volume scale
  • Foot pump or means to vary pressure
  • Stirrer

Method

  1. Place the sealed gas column (or syringe) inside the water bath. Allow time for the gas to reach the bath temperature.
  2. Record the temperature of the water bath using the thermometer. Keep this constant throughout.
  3. Record the initial pressure P and gas volume V.
  4. Use the pump or valve to change the pressure. After each change, stir the water and wait for the gas to return to the bath temperature before reading.
  5. Record at least 8 pairs of (P, V) readings.
  6. Stir the water bath continuously to maintain a uniform temperature. If using a heater, monitor the thermometer closely.
  7. Plot P vs 1/V (expect straight line through origin) and/or PV vs P (expect horizontal line).

Safety

  • Do not over-pressurise the apparatus.
  • Handle hot water carefully if using a heated water bath.
  • If using glass apparatus, handle gently to avoid breakage.
  • Keep electrical equipment away from the water bath.

Analysis

This is the same relationship as CP12 (Boyle's law: PV = constant at constant T), but the emphasis here is on precisely controlling temperature using a water bath.

PV = nRT = constant (at fixed T)

Plot P vs 1/V: straight line through origin confirms Boyle's law. The gradient = nRT.

Plot PV vs P: a horizontal line confirms PV is constant.

Sources of Error & Improvements

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Temperature of gas not equal to water bath temperatureSystematicWait longer between readings; stir the bath; use a thin-walled container for the gas
Water bath temperature drifts during experimentSystematicUse a thermostatically controlled water bath; monitor with a thermometer
Gas leaksSystematicCheck all seals before starting; use grease on joints
Friction in syringe pistonRandomTap the syringe gently; lubricate the piston
Adiabatic heating during rapid compressionSystematicChange volume slowly; wait for isothermal equilibrium
Exam Tip: CP12 and CP14 both investigate Boyle's law. The key difference is that CP14 explicitly uses a water bath to ensure constant temperature, making it a more controlled isothermal experiment. In CP12, you rely on slow volume changes and waiting for the gas to equilibrate with room temperature.

⚗️ Core Practical 16: Determine the specific latent heat of a phase change

Apparatus

  • For latent heat of fusion (ice): Crushed ice, funnel, beaker, immersion heater, joulemeter (or ammeter + voltmeter + stopwatch), balance, thermometer.
  • For latent heat of vaporisation (water): Beaker of boiling water, immersion heater, condenser or collection vessel, balance, joulemeter (or ammeter + voltmeter + stopwatch), thermometer.

Method (Latent Heat of Fusion)

  1. Place crushed ice in a funnel above a pre-weighed beaker on a balance.
  2. Allow the ice to drip for a few minutes without the heater. Measure the mass of water collected in a known time (this is the control — accounts for natural melting).
  3. Empty the beaker and re-weigh. Insert the heater into the ice and switch it on.
  4. Record the joulemeter reading (or ammeter, voltmeter, and time) to find the energy E supplied.
  5. Collect the meltwater for the same time period. Weigh the beaker + water and find the mass m of ice melted.
  6. Subtract the control mass (natural melting) to get the corrected mass Δm melted by the heater.
  7. Calculate: Lf = E / Δm.

Method (Latent Heat of Vaporisation)

  1. Bring water to a steady boil using the immersion heater. Record the initial mass on the balance.
  2. Allow boiling for a measured time t, keeping the power constant. Record E = IVt.
  3. Measure the final mass. The mass lost Δm = mass of steam produced.
  4. Calculate: Lv = E / Δm.

Safety

  • Boiling water and steam cause severe burns — handle with extreme care.
  • Use a heatproof mat and keep the beaker stable.
  • Do not let the heater run dry (always submerged in water or surrounded by ice).
  • Tie back hair; wear safety goggles if boiling vigorously.

Analysis

L = E / Δm = IVt / Δm

Expected values: Lf (ice) ≈ 334 kJ kg-1; Lv (water) ≈ 2260 kJ kg-1.

For a graphical method: vary the heating time, plot Δm against E. The gradient = 1/L, so L = 1/gradient.

Sources of Error & Improvements

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Heat losses to surroundingsSystematic (gives L too large)Insulate the apparatus; use a control experiment (no heater) to account for background melting/evaporation
Not all heater energy goes into phase change (some heats water)SystematicEnsure the water is already at 100 °C (for vaporisation) or ice is at 0 °C (for fusion) before timing
Steam escaping before being collectedSystematicUse a condenser or well-fitting lid with collection tube; or measure mass loss on balance
Natural melting of ice (without heater)SystematicRun a control with no heater for the same time; subtract the control mass
Water splashing during boilingRandomBoil gently; use a lid to contain splashes
Exam Tip: During a phase change, temperature remains constant despite continuous energy input. This is because the energy goes into breaking intermolecular bonds (fusion) or overcoming intermolecular forces to escape the liquid (vaporisation), not into increasing kinetic energy. The control experiment (no heater) is essential for an accurate value of Lf.
