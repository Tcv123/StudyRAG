Topic 10 — Space

10.1 Stellar Evolution

Life Cycle of Stars

All stars begin in the same way but their ultimate fate depends on their mass.

Low-mass star (M < 8M☉):
Nebula → Protostar → Main Sequence → Red Giant → Planetary Nebula → White Dwarf
High-mass star (M > 8M☉):
Nebula → Protostar → Main Sequence → Red Supergiant → Supernova → Neutron Star or Black Hole
Main sequence: The longest stable phase of a star's life, during which the inward gravitational force is balanced by the outward radiation pressure from hydrogen fusion in the core.

Stages Explained

  • Nebula: A large cloud of gas (mainly hydrogen) and dust. Gravity causes regions to collapse.
  • Protostar: A collapsing region that heats up due to gravitational potential energy being converted to kinetic (thermal) energy. Not yet hot enough for fusion.
  • Main Sequence: Core temperature reaches ~10⁷ K, hydrogen fusion begins. Hydrostatic equilibrium is established.
  • Red Giant / Supergiant: Hydrogen fuel in the core is exhausted. The core contracts and heats up, outer layers expand and cool (hence red colour). Helium (and heavier elements in massive stars) fuse in the core.
  • White Dwarf: The remnant core of a low-mass star. No fusion occurs; supported by electron degeneracy pressure. Gradually cools over billions of years.
  • Neutron Star: The ultra-dense remnant after a supernova of a high-mass star (core mass 1.4–3 M☉). Supported by neutron degeneracy pressure.
  • Black Hole: Forms if the remnant core mass exceeds ~3 M☉ (Tolman–Oppenheimer–Volkoff limit). Gravity is so strong that not even light can escape.
Remember that a star spends about 90% of its life on the main sequence. The more massive the star, the shorter its main-sequence lifetime because it burns fuel much faster.

10.2 The Hertzsprung–Russell Diagram

Hertzsprung–Russell (HR) diagram: A plot of stellar luminosity (or absolute magnitude) against surface temperature (or spectral class). Temperature increases from right to left on the x-axis.

Key Regions

Region Position on HR Diagram Characteristics
Main Sequence Diagonal band from top-left to bottom-right Hot, luminous blue stars at top; cool, dim red stars at bottom. Hydrogen fusion in core.
Red Giants Upper right Cool (3000–5000 K) but very luminous due to large radius.
Red Supergiants Top right Even larger and more luminous than red giants.
White Dwarfs Bottom left Hot (10,000–30,000 K) but very low luminosity due to tiny radius.
The x-axis of the HR diagram has temperature decreasing from left to right (opposite to what you might expect). Always check the axis labels in exam questions.

10.3 Luminosity and Brightness

Luminosity (L): The total power output of a star — the total energy radiated per second, measured in watts (W).
Apparent brightness (b): The intensity of light received at Earth from a star — the power per unit area at the observer, measured in W m⁻².

Apparent brightness depends on both the luminosity and the distance to the star:

b = L / (4πd²)

where d is the distance from the star to the observer.

10.4 Wien's Displacement Law

Wien's displacement law: The peak wavelength of the radiation emitted by a black body is inversely proportional to its absolute (surface) temperature.
λmax T = 2.898 × 10⁻³ m K

This means hotter stars emit radiation with a shorter peak wavelength (appearing bluer), while cooler stars have a longer peak wavelength (appearing redder).

10.5 Stefan–Boltzmann Law

Stefan–Boltzmann law: The total luminosity of a star (modelled as a black body) is proportional to the fourth power of its surface temperature and to its surface area.
L = 4πr²σT⁴

where:

  • L = luminosity (W)
  • r = radius of the star (m)
  • σ = Stefan–Boltzmann constant = 5.67 × 10⁻⁸ W m⁻² K⁻⁴
  • T = surface temperature (K)
You can combine Wien's law and Stefan–Boltzmann law to determine both the temperature and radius of a star from its spectrum and luminosity.

10.6 Standard Candles

Standard candle: An astronomical object whose absolute luminosity is known (or can be determined independently), allowing its distance to be calculated from its observed apparent brightness.

Cepheid Variable Stars

Cepheid variables are pulsating stars whose luminosity varies periodically. There is a well-established relationship between their period of pulsation and their absolute luminosity. By measuring the period, we determine the luminosity, and then using the apparent brightness, we can calculate the distance.

Type Ia Supernovae

Type Ia supernovae occur in binary star systems when a white dwarf accretes matter from a companion until it reaches the Chandrasekhar limit (~1.4 M☉) and explodes. Since they all explode at approximately the same mass, they have very similar peak luminosities, making them excellent standard candles for measuring very large (cosmological) distances.

10.7 The Doppler Effect

Doppler effect: The change in observed frequency (or wavelength) of a wave due to relative motion between the source and the observer.

For electromagnetic radiation (when v << c):

Δλ / λ = Δf / f ≈ v / c

where:

  • Δλ = change in wavelength (observed − emitted)
  • λ = emitted wavelength
  • v = speed of the source relative to the observer
  • c = speed of light

Redshift and Blueshift

  • Redshift (z > 0): Source is moving away — observed wavelength is longer (shifted towards the red end).
  • Blueshift (z < 0): Source is moving towards observer — observed wavelength is shorter (shifted towards the blue end).
z = Δλ / λ = v / c

where z is the redshift parameter.

10.8 Hubble's Law

Hubble's law: The recessional velocity of a distant galaxy is directly proportional to its distance from us.
v = H₀ d

where:

  • v = recessional velocity (km s⁻¹ or m s⁻¹)
  • H₀ = Hubble constant (approximately 67–73 km s⁻¹ Mpc⁻¹)
  • d = distance to the galaxy

Hubble's law implies that the universe is expanding — all distant galaxies are moving away from us, and the further away they are, the faster they recede.

Age of the Universe

If the expansion rate has been approximately constant, the age of the universe can be estimated as:

t ≈ 1 / H₀

Using H₀ ≈ 70 km s⁻¹ Mpc⁻¹, this gives an age of approximately 14 billion years.

Be careful with units when calculating the age. Convert H₀ to s⁻¹ by converting Mpc to metres (1 Mpc = 3.09 × 10²² m) and km to m.

10.9 Evidence for the Big Bang

The Big Bang theory states that the universe began from an extremely hot, dense singularity approximately 13.8 billion years ago and has been expanding ever since.

Key Evidence

Evidence Explanation
Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB) Radiation Uniform microwave radiation (peak wavelength ~1 mm, T ≈ 2.7 K) detected in all directions. This is the cooled remnant of the radiation from ~380,000 years after the Big Bang when the universe became transparent.
Expansion of the Universe Hubble's observations show all distant galaxies are receding, implying the universe is expanding — consistent with expansion from an initial singularity.
Hydrogen/Helium Abundance The observed ratio (~75% hydrogen, ~25% helium by mass) matches predictions of Big Bang nucleosynthesis — the formation of light elements in the first few minutes after the Big Bang.
The CMB was predicted by Gamow in 1948 and discovered accidentally by Penzias and Wilson in 1965. It is the strongest single piece of evidence for the Big Bang. Its near-perfect black-body spectrum at 2.7 K is exactly what is predicted by the model.
