Topic 2 — Mechanics

2.1 Scalars and Vectors

Scalar — a quantity that has magnitude only (e.g., mass, speed, energy, temperature, distance).

Vector — a quantity that has both magnitude and direction (e.g., force, velocity, acceleration, displacement, momentum).

Adding Vectors

Vectors are added using the triangle of vectors or parallelogram rule. For two vectors at right angles, use Pythagoras' theorem to find the resultant magnitude and trigonometry to find the angle:

Resultant = √(A² + B²)
θ = tan-1(opposite / adjacent)

Resolving Vectors into Components

Any vector can be split into two perpendicular components (typically horizontal and vertical):

Horizontal component: Fx = F cos θ
Vertical component: Fy = F sin θ

where θ is the angle between the vector and the horizontal.

When resolving forces on an inclined plane, resolve parallel and perpendicular to the slope — not horizontally and vertically. The component of weight along the slope is mg sin θ and perpendicular to the slope is mg cos θ.

2.2 Motion Graphs

Displacement-Time Graphs

  • Gradient = velocity
  • Horizontal line = stationary (zero velocity)
  • Straight line with positive gradient = constant velocity forwards
  • Curve with increasing gradient = acceleration
  • The instantaneous velocity at a point on a curve is found from the gradient of the tangent at that point.

Velocity-Time Graphs

  • Gradient = acceleration
  • Area under the graph = displacement
  • Horizontal line = constant velocity (zero acceleration)
  • Straight line with positive gradient = constant acceleration
  • Negative velocity = moving in the opposite direction

2.3 SUVAT Equations

The SUVAT equations apply to objects moving with constant (uniform) acceleration in a straight line. The five variables are: s (displacement), u (initial velocity), v (final velocity), a (acceleration), t (time).
EquationMissing Variable
v = u + ats
s = ut + ½at²v
s = vt - ½at²u
v² = u² + 2ast
s = ½(u + v)ta

Derivations

v = u + at — from the definition of acceleration: a = (v - u)/t, rearranged.

s = ut + ½at² — area under a v-t graph: s = ut + ½(v - u)t, then substitute v - u = at.

v² = u² + 2as — eliminate t: from v = u + at, t = (v-u)/a; substitute into s = ½(u+v)t to get s = (v² - u²)/2a, then rearrange.

Always list the known values of s, u, v, a, t before choosing which equation to use. Pick the equation that contains the three known quantities and the one unknown you need.

2.4 Projectile Motion

Projectile motion is the motion of an object under the influence of gravity alone (ignoring air resistance). The horizontal and vertical components of motion are independent of each other.

Key Principles

  • Horizontally: No acceleration (ignoring air resistance), so horizontal velocity is constant: x = vx t
  • Vertically: Acceleration = g = 9.81 m s-2 downwards. Use SUVAT equations with a = g.
  • At the highest point, the vertical component of velocity = 0 (but horizontal velocity remains unchanged).
  • Time of flight is determined entirely by the vertical motion.

Projectile Launched at Angle θ

Horizontal component: ux = u cos θ
Vertical component: uy = u sin θ

2.5 Newton's Laws of Motion

Newton's First Law: An object remains at rest or moves with constant velocity unless acted upon by a resultant force.

Newton's Second Law: The resultant force on an object is proportional to its rate of change of momentum. For constant mass: F = ma.

Newton's Third Law: If object A exerts a force on object B, then object B exerts an equal and opposite force on object A. These forces act on different objects, are the same type, and act along the same line.

Free Body Diagrams

A free body diagram shows all the forces acting on a single object. Each force is drawn as an arrow starting from the object, with length proportional to magnitude.

  • Weight (mg) acts downwards from the centre of mass
  • Normal contact force acts perpendicular to the surface
  • Friction acts parallel to the surface, opposing motion
  • Tension acts along a rope or string, away from the object
  • Air resistance acts opposite to the direction of motion
Newton's Third Law pairs must be: the same type of force, equal in magnitude, opposite in direction, and acting on two DIFFERENT objects. Weight and normal reaction are NOT a Newton's Third Law pair (they act on the same object and are different types of force).

2.6 Momentum

Momentum (p) is the product of an object's mass and velocity. It is a vector quantity measured in kg m s-1.
p = mv

Conservation of Momentum

In a closed system (no external forces), the total momentum before an event equals the total momentum after:

m1u1 + m2u2 = m1v1 + m2v2

2.7 Impulse

Impulse is the change in momentum of an object. It equals the force multiplied by the time for which it acts.
Impulse = FΔt = Δp = mv - mu

The area under a force-time graph equals the impulse (change in momentum).

Applications: crumple zones, airbags, and helmets increase the time of impact, reducing the average force on the person for the same change in momentum.

2.8 Work, Energy, and Power

Work Done

Work done is the energy transferred when a force moves an object through a distance in the direction of the force.
W = Fd cos θ

where θ is the angle between the force and the direction of motion. Work done is measured in joules (J). If θ = 90°, no work is done (e.g., centripetal force).

Kinetic Energy

Ek = ½mv²

Gravitational Potential Energy

Ep = mgh

where h is the vertical height change above a reference point.

Conservation of Energy

Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transferred from one form to another. In the absence of dissipative forces:

½mv² = mgh

Power

Power is the rate of doing work (or the rate of energy transfer). Measured in watts (W), where 1 W = 1 J s-1.
P = W / t = ΔE / t
P = Fv

This second equation is useful for finding the driving force at a given speed, or the power needed to maintain a constant velocity against resistive forces.

Efficiency

Efficiency = (useful energy output / total energy input) × 100%
Efficiency = (useful power output / total power input) × 100%
At constant velocity, driving force = resistive force (no net force, so no acceleration). Power at constant velocity is P = Fv where F is the driving force needed to overcome friction and drag.
