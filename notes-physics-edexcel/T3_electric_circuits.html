Topic 3 — Electric Circuits

3.1 Charge, Current, and Potential Difference

Electric current (I) is the rate of flow of charge. Measured in amperes (A).

Charge (Q) is measured in coulombs (C). One coulomb is the charge that flows when a current of 1 A passes for 1 second.

Potential difference (V) is the energy transferred per unit charge between two points. Measured in volts (V), where 1 V = 1 J C-1.

Q = It
V = W / Q

Conventional current flows from positive to negative (opposite to electron flow). In metals, charge is carried by free electrons (delocalised electrons).

3.2 Resistance and Ohm's Law

Resistance (R) is a measure of the opposition to current flow. It is defined as the ratio of potential difference to current. Measured in ohms (Ω).
V = IR    (or equivalently R = V / I)
Ohm's Law: For a conductor at constant temperature, the current through it is directly proportional to the potential difference across it. A component that obeys Ohm's law is called an ohmic conductor.

3.3 Resistivity

Resistivity (ρ) is a property of the material itself, independent of the dimensions of the sample. It is defined as the resistance of a 1 m length with a 1 m² cross-sectional area. Measured in Ω m.
ρ = RA / L

where R = resistance (Ω), A = cross-sectional area (m²), L = length (m).

Factors Affecting Resistance

  • Resistance is proportional to length (R ∝ L)
  • Resistance is inversely proportional to cross-sectional area (R ∝ 1/A)
  • Resistance depends on the material (resistivity ρ)
  • Resistance depends on temperature (for metals, R increases with T)
Remember to convert diameter to radius, then use A = πr² to find cross-sectional area. Also ensure length is in metres and area in m² before substituting into ρ = RA/L.

3.4 EMF and Internal Resistance

Electromotive force (EMF, ε) is the total energy transferred per unit charge by the power supply. It is the p.d. across the terminals when no current flows.

Internal resistance (r) is the resistance within the power supply itself, which causes energy to be dissipated inside the battery.

ε = I(R + r)
ε = V + Ir    (where V = IR is the terminal p.d.)

Rearranging: V = ε - Ir

This shows that the terminal p.d. (V) decreases as current increases. Plotting V against I gives a straight line with gradient = -r and y-intercept = ε.

Lost Volts

The lost volts (v = Ir) is the potential difference dropped across the internal resistance. It represents the energy per coulomb dissipated inside the battery.

3.5 Kirchhoff's Laws

Kirchhoff's First Law (Junction Rule): The sum of currents entering a junction equals the sum of currents leaving the junction. This is based on conservation of charge.

Kirchhoff's Second Law (Loop Rule): The sum of EMFs around any closed loop equals the sum of potential differences around that loop. This is based on conservation of energy.

ΣIin = ΣIout    (First Law)
Σε = ΣIR    (Second Law)

3.6 Series and Parallel Circuits

Series Circuits

  • Current is the same through all components: Itotal = I1 = I2 = I3
  • Total p.d. is shared between components: Vtotal = V1 + V2 + V3
  • Total resistance: Rtotal = R1 + R2 + R3

Parallel Circuits

  • P.d. is the same across each branch: Vtotal = V1 = V2 = V3
  • Total current is shared between branches: Itotal = I1 + I2 + I3
  • Combined resistance:
1/Rtotal = 1/R1 + 1/R2 + 1/R3

For two resistors in parallel:

Rtotal = (R1 × R2) / (R1 + R2)
The combined resistance of parallel resistors is always LESS than the smallest individual resistor. Adding more resistors in parallel always decreases total resistance.

3.7 Potential Dividers

A potential divider is a circuit with two (or more) resistors in series, used to produce an output voltage that is a fraction of the input voltage.
Vout = Vin × R2 / (R1 + R2)

where Vout is measured across R2.

Potential Dividers with Sensors

  • Thermistor in a potential divider: As temperature rises, thermistor resistance falls, so the share of voltage across it decreases (and the share across the other resistor increases).
  • LDR in a potential divider: As light intensity increases, LDR resistance falls, so voltage across it decreases.

This allows the output voltage to vary automatically with environmental conditions, which can be used to trigger switches or circuits.

3.8 Energy and Power in Circuits

Electrical power is the rate at which electrical energy is transferred by a component. Measured in watts (W).
P = IV

Substituting V = IR and I = V/R gives two alternative forms:

P = I²R
P = V² / R

Energy Transferred

E = IVt = I²Rt = V²t / R

where t is the time in seconds. Energy is measured in joules (J).

In a resistor, electrical energy is transferred to thermal energy (heating). In a motor, it is transferred to kinetic energy. In a lamp, it is transferred to light and thermal energy.

Use P = I²R when you know current and resistance. Use P = V²/R when you know voltage and resistance. Remember that the power dissipated in the internal resistance is "wasted" — it heats the battery.

⚗️ Core Practical 4: Determine the resistivity of a wire

Apparatus

  • Constantan wire (SWG 28 or 32, approx 1 m long)
  • Metre ruler (resolution ±1 mm)
  • Micrometer screw gauge (resolution ±0.01 mm)
  • Two crocodile clips
  • Ammeter (or milliammeter)
  • Voltmeter
  • Battery pack / power supply (low voltage, e.g. 1.5 V)
  • Switch
  • Connecting wires

Method

  1. Use the micrometer to measure the diameter of the wire at at least 3 different positions along its length, rotating 90° between readings. Calculate the mean diameter d.
  2. Calculate the cross-sectional area: A = π(d/2)².
  3. Tape the wire along the metre ruler. Attach one crocodile clip at the 0 cm mark (fixed).
  4. Attach the second crocodile clip at a measured length L (e.g. 20 cm). Connect the ammeter in series and voltmeter in parallel across the wire section.
  5. Close the switch briefly, read V and I, then open the switch to prevent heating.
  6. Calculate R = V / I for this length.
  7. Move the sliding clip to vary L (e.g. 20, 30, 40, 50, 60, 70, 80, 90, 100 cm). Record R for each L.
  8. Plot a graph of R (y-axis) against L (x-axis).

Safety

  • Use a low voltage (≤ 1.5 V) to prevent the wire from overheating.
  • Switch off between readings to avoid the wire heating up (which would change its resistance).
  • Do not touch the wire while current flows — it may become hot.
  • Ensure the wire is securely taped down so it cannot spring off.

Analysis

Since R = ρL / A, a graph of R vs L gives a straight line through the origin with:

gradient = ρ / A

Therefore: ρ = gradient × A

The accepted resistivity of constantan is approximately 4.9 × 10-7 Ω m.

Sources of Error & Improvements

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Wire heating up changes resistanceSystematicUse low current; switch off between readings; allow cooling time
Diameter of wire not uniform along its lengthRandomMeasure diameter at multiple points and orientations; take mean
Zero error on micrometerSystematicCheck and record the zero reading; subtract it from all measurements
Contact resistance at crocodile clipsSystematicEnsure clips make firm contact; use clean wire and clean clips
Measuring wire length to clip contact pointRandomMeasure to the inner jaw of the crocodile clip; use a set square
Exam Tip: The biggest source of uncertainty is usually the diameter measurement because it is small and squared when calculating area. A 5% error in d becomes a ~10% error in A and hence ρ. Always state this in evaluations.

⚗️ Core Practical 5: Determine the EMF and internal resistance of an electrical cell

Apparatus

  • Cell (e.g. 1.5 V AA cell — not a rechargeable cell)
  • Variable resistor (rheostat, e.g. 0–10 Ω)
  • Ammeter (0–1 A)
  • Voltmeter (0–2 V, high resistance)
  • Switch
  • Connecting wires

Method

  1. Connect the cell, switch, ammeter (in series), and variable resistor in a series loop.
  2. Connect the voltmeter in parallel across the cell terminals (not just the EMF source — include internal resistance).
  3. Close the switch and adjust the variable resistor to its maximum value. Record V and I.
  4. Decrease the resistance of the variable resistor in steps, recording V and I each time (at least 6 pairs of readings).
  5. Open the switch between readings to prevent the cell from running down and heating.
  6. Plot a graph of V (y-axis) against I (x-axis).

Safety

  • Do not short-circuit the cell (never set variable resistor to zero).
  • Switch off between readings to prevent heating and cell drainage.
  • Handle cells carefully; do not attempt to recharge non-rechargeable cells.

Analysis

The terminal voltage equation is:

V = ε − Ir

This has the form y = mx + c where:

  • y-intercept (at I = 0) = ε (the EMF of the cell)
  • Gradient = −r (the internal resistance)

Draw a line of best fit. Read off the y-intercept for ε and calculate the gradient magnitude for r.

Sources of Error & Improvements

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Cell runs down during experiment, changing εSystematicSwitch off between readings; take readings quickly; use a fresh cell
Cell heating up changes internal resistanceSystematicAllow cooling between readings; use low currents
Ammeter has non-zero resistanceSystematicUse a low-resistance ammeter; note its resistance and account for it
Voltmeter draws a small currentSystematicUse a high-resistance digital voltmeter to minimise current drawn

Exam Tip: If asked to determine EMF without drawing a graph, measure the voltmeter reading when no current flows (open circuit). In practice, a high-resistance voltmeter reading with the switch open gives a good approximation of ε. The graph method is more reliable because it uses multiple readings.

⚗️ Core Practical 13: Calibrate a thermistor in a potential divider circuit as a thermostat

Apparatus

  • NTC thermistor
  • Fixed resistor (value chosen to be similar to thermistor resistance at mid-range temperature)
  • DC power supply (e.g. 5 V)
  • Voltmeter (across the thermistor)
  • Thermometer (resolution ±0.5 °C, range 0–100 °C)
  • Beaker of water, Bunsen burner or electric heater, stirrer
  • Connecting wires

Method

  1. Set up the potential divider: power supply in series with the fixed resistor R1 and the NTC thermistor. Connect the voltmeter in parallel across the thermistor.
  2. Place the thermistor (waterproofed or in a sealed tube) into the water bath along with the thermometer.
  3. Record the temperature θ and the voltmeter reading Vout at room temperature.
  4. Heat the water slowly, stirring continuously. Record Vout at regular temperature intervals (e.g. every 5 °C from 20 °C to 80 °C).
  5. Allow the thermistor time to reach thermal equilibrium at each temperature before reading.
  6. Plot a calibration curve: Vout (y-axis) against temperature θ (x-axis).

Safety

  • Take care with hot water — do not heat above 80 °C to avoid scalding risks.
  • Ensure electrical connections are kept away from water.
  • If using a Bunsen burner, tie back hair and loose clothing.
  • Use a heatproof mat under the beaker.

Analysis

In a potential divider, the output voltage across the thermistor is:

Vout = Vs × Rthermistor / (R1 + Rthermistor)

As temperature increases, the NTC thermistor resistance decreases, so Vout decreases. The calibration curve allows any measured Vout to be converted to a temperature reading, making this a thermostat or electronic thermometer.

For a thermostat application, a comparator or switching circuit can be set to trigger at a specific Vout corresponding to the desired temperature.

Sources of Error & Improvements

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Temperature not uniform in water bathRandomStir the water continuously; wait for equilibrium
Thermistor not at same temperature as thermometerSystematicPlace both sensors close together; allow time for thermal equilibrium
Thermometer calibration errorSystematicUse a calibrated digital thermometer
Self-heating of thermistor by the currentSystematicUse a low supply voltage to minimise current through the thermistor
Evaporation of water changes bath levelSystematicTop up the water; use a lid on the beaker
Exam Tip: In a potential divider, when the thermistor resistance decreases (temperature rises), Vout across it decreases. If the thermistor and fixed resistor swap positions, the relationship reverses. Be clear about which component Vout is measured across.
