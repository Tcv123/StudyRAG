Topic 11 — Nuclear Radiation

11.1 Types of Nuclear Radiation

Radioactive decay: The spontaneous and random disintegration of an unstable nucleus, resulting in the emission of radiation and the formation of a different nuclide.

Properties of Alpha, Beta, and Gamma Radiation

Property Alpha (α) Beta-minus (β⁻) Beta-plus (β⁺) Gamma (γ)
Identity Helium-4 nucleus (²₄He) Electron (⁰₋₁e) Positron (⁰₊₁e) Electromagnetic photon
Charge +2e −1e +1e 0
Mass (u) 4 ~1/1836 ~1/1836 0
Speed ~5% of c Up to ~99% of c Up to ~99% of c c (speed of light)
Ionising ability Very high (~10⁴ ion pairs/mm) Moderate (~10² ion pairs/mm) Moderate Very low
Penetrating power Low (stopped by paper/skin) Medium (stopped by ~3 mm aluminium) Medium High (reduced by thick lead/concrete)
Deflection in fields Slight deflection (large mass) Large deflection (opposite to α) Large deflection (same as α) None
Ionising ability and penetrating power are inversely related. Alpha particles ionise strongly, so they lose energy quickly and cannot penetrate far. Gamma photons ionise weakly, so they can pass through large thicknesses of material.

11.2 Nuclear Decay Equations

Alpha Decay

The nucleus emits a helium-4 nucleus. The nucleon number decreases by 4 and the proton number decreases by 2.

ᴬ_Z X → ᴬ⁻⁴_(Z−2) Y + ⁴₂ He

Example: ²²⁶₈₈Ra → ²²²₈₆Rn + ⁴₂He

Beta-minus (β⁻) Decay

A neutron in the nucleus converts to a proton, emitting an electron and an antineutrino. The nucleon number is unchanged; the proton number increases by 1.

ᴬ_Z X → ᴬ_(Z+1) Y + ⁰₋₁e + ν̄ₑ

At the quark level: n → p + e⁻ + ν̄ₑ (a down quark changes to an up quark via the weak interaction).

Beta-plus (β⁺) Decay

A proton in the nucleus converts to a neutron, emitting a positron and a neutrino. The nucleon number is unchanged; the proton number decreases by 1.

ᴬ_Z X → ᴬ_(Z−1) Y + ⁰₊₁e + νₑ

Gamma Emission

After alpha or beta decay, the daughter nucleus may be in an excited state. It de-excites by emitting a gamma photon. Neither nucleon number nor proton number changes.

Always check that both nucleon number (top) and proton number (bottom) are conserved in decay equations. Do not forget the neutrino or antineutrino in beta decay.

11.3 Radioactive Decay — Half-life and Activity

Half-life (t½): The time taken for half of the radioactive nuclei in a sample to decay, or equivalently, the time for the activity of a sample to fall to half its initial value.
Activity (A): The rate of decay — the number of disintegrations per unit time. Measured in becquerels (Bq), where 1 Bq = 1 decay per second.
Decay constant (λ): The probability of a given nucleus decaying per unit time. Units: s⁻¹.

Key Equations

A = λN

Activity equals the decay constant multiplied by the number of undecayed nuclei.

λ = ln 2 / t½
N = N₀ e−λt
A = A₀ e−λt

These show that radioactive decay is exponential. The number of undecayed nuclei (and the activity) decreases by the same fraction in equal time intervals.

Graphical Representation

  • A plot of N (or A) against time gives an exponential decay curve.
  • A plot of ln(N) against time gives a straight line with gradient = −λ.
  • After n half-lives, the remaining fraction is (½)ⁿ.
If asked to determine half-life from a graph, read off the time for the activity (or count rate) to halve. Take multiple readings and average them for accuracy. Remember to subtract background count rate if given.

11.4 Nuclear Fission

Nuclear fission: The splitting of a heavy nucleus (e.g., uranium-235 or plutonium-239) into two smaller daughter nuclei of roughly equal mass, releasing energy, neutrons, and gamma radiation.

Chain Reactions

Each fission event releases 2–3 neutrons. These neutrons can go on to cause further fission events, leading to a chain reaction.

  • Uncontrolled chain reaction: Each fission causes more than one subsequent fission on average — exponential growth (nuclear weapon).
  • Controlled chain reaction: Exactly one neutron per fission event causes another fission — steady energy output (nuclear reactor).

Critical Mass

The minimum mass of fissile material needed to sustain a chain reaction. Below this mass, too many neutrons escape without causing further fissions.

Nuclear Reactor Components

Component Function
Fuel rods Contain fissile material (e.g., enriched uranium-235)
Moderator Slows down fast neutrons to thermal speeds so they are more likely to cause fission (e.g., water, graphite)
Control rods Absorb excess neutrons to maintain a steady rate of reaction (e.g., boron, cadmium). Raised to increase power, lowered to decrease it.
Coolant Transfers thermal energy from the reactor core to generate steam for turbines (e.g., water, CO₂, liquid sodium)

11.5 Nuclear Fusion

Nuclear fusion: The combining of two light nuclei to form a heavier nucleus, releasing energy. This is the process that powers stars.

Conditions for Fusion

  • Extremely high temperature (~10⁷–10⁸ K) — nuclei need sufficient kinetic energy to overcome the electrostatic (Coulomb) repulsion between positive charges.
  • High density/pressure — to increase the probability of collisions between nuclei.
  • Confinement time — nuclei must be held together long enough for fusion to occur.

Fusion in Stars

  • Proton–proton (pp) chain: The dominant fusion process in stars like the Sun (T ~ 1.5 × 10⁷ K). Four protons fuse (in a series of steps) to form one helium-4 nucleus, releasing energy, positrons, and neutrinos.
  • CNO cycle: Dominates in stars more massive than ~1.3 M☉ (higher core temperatures). Carbon, nitrogen, and oxygen act as catalysts in a cycle that also converts four protons to helium-4.

11.6 Binding Energy

Binding energy: The minimum energy required to completely separate all the nucleons in a nucleus. Equivalently, the energy released when individual nucleons come together to form a nucleus.
Mass defect (Δm): The difference between the total mass of the individual (separated) nucleons and the actual mass of the nucleus. Δm = Zm_p + Nm_n − M_nucleus.

Mass–Energy Equivalence

E = mc²

The binding energy is related to the mass defect by E = Δm × c². Mass and energy are interchangeable: when nucleons bind together, mass is "lost" and converted to energy.

Useful conversion: 1 u = 931.5 MeV/c²

Binding Energy per Nucleon Curve

  • A plot of binding energy per nucleon (BE/A) against nucleon number (A).
  • The peak occurs at iron-56 (and nickel-62), which has the highest BE/A (~8.8 MeV per nucleon) and is therefore the most stable nucleus.
  • Fusion of light nuclei (A < 56) moves up the curve — energy is released.
  • Fission of heavy nuclei (A > 56) also moves up the curve — energy is released.
  • Both processes produce products with higher BE/A (more stable), so energy is released.
Energy released = total BE of products − total BE of reactants (when using binding energies). Alternatively, energy released = (mass of reactants − mass of products) × c². Be consistent with which method you use.

Calculating Energy Released

For a nuclear reaction:

  1. Calculate the total mass of reactants and the total mass of products.
  2. Find the mass defect: Δm = mass of reactants − mass of products.
  3. Convert to energy: E = Δm × c² (or use Δm in u and multiply by 931.5 MeV).
If the mass of products is less than reactants, the reaction releases energy (exothermic). If the mass of products is greater, the reaction requires energy input (endothermic).
