Topic 7 — Electric and Magnetic Fields

7.1 Electric Fields

An electric field is a region of space where a charged particle experiences a force. Electric field lines show the direction of force on a positive test charge.

Radial Fields

A radial field is produced by a point charge (or a charged sphere). The field lines radiate outward from a positive charge and inward toward a negative charge. The field strength decreases with distance from the charge according to an inverse-square law.

Uniform Fields

A uniform field exists between two parallel charged plates. The field lines are parallel and equally spaced. The field strength is the same at all points between the plates.

E = V / d

where V is the potential difference between the plates and d is the separation.

Coulomb's Law

The force between two point charges is given by Coulomb's law:

F = kQ₁Q₂ / r² = (1 / 4πε₀) × Q₁Q₂ / r²

where k = 1/(4πε₀) = 8.99 × 10⁹ N m² C⁻² and ε₀ = 8.85 × 10⁻¹² F m⁻¹ is the permittivity of free space.

The force is attractive between unlike charges and repulsive between like charges.

Electric Field Strength

Electric field strength (E) at a point is the force per unit positive charge placed at that point.
E = F / Q   (unit: N C⁻¹ or V m⁻¹)

For a radial field around a point charge Q:

E = kQ / r² = Q / (4πε₀r²)

For a uniform field between parallel plates:

E = V / d

Electric Potential

Electric potential (V) at a point is the work done per unit positive charge in bringing a small test charge from infinity to that point.
V = kQ / r = Q / (4πε₀r)

Key points about electric potential:

  • Potential is a scalar quantity
  • Potential is zero at infinity
  • Positive charges create positive potential; negative charges create negative potential
  • Work done moving charge q through potential difference ΔV: W = qΔV

Equipotentials

Equipotential lines (or surfaces) connect points of equal potential. They are always perpendicular to field lines. No work is done moving a charge along an equipotential.

The electric field strength is the negative of the potential gradient: E = −dV/dr. On a V-r graph, the gradient at any point gives E at that distance.

7.2 Capacitance

Capacitance (C) is the charge stored per unit potential difference across the capacitor.
C = Q / V   (unit: farad, F)

Parallel Plate Capacitor

C = ε₀εᵣA / d

where A is the area of overlap of the plates, d is the separation, ε₀ is the permittivity of free space, and εᵣ is the relative permittivity (dielectric constant) of the material between the plates.

Capacitors in Combination

Arrangement Formula Notes
Parallel C_total = C₁ + C₂ + C₃ + ... Same p.d. across each; charges add
Series 1/C_total = 1/C₁ + 1/C₂ + 1/C₃ + ... Same charge on each; p.d.s add

Energy Stored in a Capacitor

The energy stored in a charged capacitor is the area under the Q-V graph (a straight line through the origin):

E = ½QV = ½CV² = ½Q²/C

Charging and Discharging

When a capacitor charges or discharges through a resistor, the charge, voltage, and current change exponentially.

Discharging:

Q = Q₀ e^(−t/RC)
V = V₀ e^(−t/RC)
I = I₀ e^(−t/RC)

Charging:

Q = Q₀ (1 − e^(−t/RC))
V = V₀ (1 − e^(−t/RC))

The current during charging still decays: I = I₀ e^(−t/RC)

Time Constant

The time constant (τ = RC) is the time taken for the charge (or voltage or current) to fall to 1/e (≈ 37%) of its initial value during discharge, or to rise to (1 − 1/e) ≈ 63% of its final value during charging.

After 5 time constants (5RC), the capacitor is considered fully charged or fully discharged (>99%).

To find the time constant from a graph, read off the time at which V (or Q) has fallen to 0.37 of its initial value. Alternatively, use ln(V) vs t — the gradient equals −1/RC.

7.3 Magnetic Fields

Magnetic flux density (B) is a measure of the strength of a magnetic field. It is defined as the force per unit length per unit current on a conductor carrying current perpendicular to the field.
Unit: tesla (T), where 1 T = 1 kg s⁻² A⁻¹

Force on a Current-Carrying Conductor

A straight conductor of length L carrying current I at angle θ to a uniform magnetic field B experiences a force:

F = BIL sin θ

The direction of the force is given by Fleming's left-hand rule:

  • First finger: Field (B)
  • Second finger: Current (I, conventional)
  • Thumb: Thrust (Force)

Force on a Moving Charge

A charged particle moving through a magnetic field experiences a force:

F = BQv sin θ

where Q is the charge, v is the velocity, and θ is the angle between v and B.

The force is always perpendicular to the velocity, so it does no work on the charge and cannot change its speed — only its direction. A charged particle moving perpendicular to a uniform magnetic field follows a circular path:

BQv = mv²/r   →   r = mv / (BQ)

Magnetic Flux

Magnetic flux (Φ) through a surface is the product of the magnetic flux density and the area perpendicular to the field.
Φ = BA cos θ   (unit: weber, Wb, where 1 Wb = 1 T m²)

Flux linkage for a coil of N turns: NΦ = BAN cos θ

7.4 Electromagnetic Induction

Faraday's law: The magnitude of the induced EMF is equal to the rate of change of magnetic flux linkage through the circuit.
ε = −d(NΦ)/dt
Lenz's law: The direction of the induced EMF (and hence the induced current) is such that it opposes the change in flux that produces it. This is represented by the negative sign in Faraday's law.

Lenz's law is a consequence of conservation of energy — the induced current creates a magnetic field that opposes the change causing it, so work must be done to maintain the change.

Methods of inducing an EMF:

  • Moving a conductor through a magnetic field
  • Changing the area of a circuit in a magnetic field
  • Rotating a coil in a magnetic field
  • Changing the magnetic flux density (e.g., moving a magnet towards a coil)
When applying Faraday's law, identify what is changing: is it B, A, or θ? For a rotating coil, Φ = BAN cos(ωt), so ε = BANω sin(ωt). The maximum EMF is ε₀ = BANω.
