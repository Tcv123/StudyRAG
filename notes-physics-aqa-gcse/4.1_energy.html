4.1 Energy

AQA GCSE Physics (8463) — Paper 1

4.1.1 Energy Changes in a System

Energy is a property of an object or system. It is not a substance — it is a quantity (measured in joules, J) that allows us to predict what changes can happen in a system. Energy can be stored in various ways and transferred between stores. The total amount of energy in a closed system never changes — this is the principle of conservation of energy.

AQA GCSE Physics uses the modern "stores and pathways" model. Rather than saying energy is "converted" from one type to another, we say energy is transferred from one store to another via a pathway. This language may feel unusual, but it is the language examiners expect — answers using it score more marks than older "kinetic energy becomes heat energy" phrasings.

The Eight Energy Stores

All the energy in the universe is held in one of these eight stores. You must be able to identify the store(s) involved in any situation.

StoreWhat is it?Example
KineticEnergy stored in a moving object. Depends on mass and speed.A car driving, a falling book, a runner, a bullet in flight.
ChemicalEnergy stored in the bonds between atoms and molecules. Released in chemical reactions.Food, fuels (petrol, coal, wood), batteries, explosives.
Gravitational PotentialEnergy stored in an object raised up in a gravitational field.A book on a high shelf, water in a reservoir, a skier at the top of a slope.
Elastic PotentialEnergy stored in a stretched or compressed elastic object.A stretched spring, a drawn bow, a squashed rubber ball, a wound clockwork toy.
Thermal (Internal)Energy stored in the random movement of the particles making up an object. Hotter object = greater thermal store.A hot drink, a warm radiator, the inside of an oven.
NuclearEnergy stored in the nucleus of an atom. Released in fission, fusion or radioactive decay.Uranium fuel rods in a reactor, the Sun's core, radioactive isotopes.
ElectrostaticEnergy stored between two electric charges that attract or repel.Charged thunder clouds, a charged balloon sticking to a wall, two charged spheres.
MagneticEnergy stored between two magnetic poles that attract or repel.Two bar magnets held apart, a fridge magnet pulled off the door.
Examiners often mark down answers that use old-fashioned terms such as "heat energy", "sound energy", "light energy", "movement energy" or "electrical energy". These are not energy stores — they are pathways (ways energy is transferred). Always use one of the eight store names above when describing where energy is held.

The Four Energy Pathways (Transfers)

Energy moves between stores via one of these four pathways:

PathwayDescriptionExample
MechanicallyA force does work on an object over a distance.Pushing a box along the floor (chemical → kinetic + thermal).
ElectricallyA current flows through a circuit — charges do work.A kettle heating water (chemical at power station → thermal of water).
By heatingEnergy flows from a hotter to a colder region by conduction, convection, or radiation.Hot coffee cooling down in a cold room (thermal of coffee → thermal of surroundings).
By radiationEnergy carried by waves — light, sound, infrared, other EM waves.Sunlight warming the ground (nuclear in Sun → thermal of ground).

Principle of Conservation of Energy

Energy cannot be created or destroyed. It can only be transferred usefully, stored, or dissipated. In a closed system, the total energy before any event is equal to the total energy after the event — the energy has simply moved between stores.

A closed system is one in which no energy (and no matter) enters or leaves the boundary of the system. In practice, truly closed systems are rare — almost every real process loses some energy to the surroundings as thermal energy — but we treat systems as closed when the boundary is carefully chosen (e.g., a sealed, insulated flask).

Throughout the whole event, the total energy stays the same. At the start, all the energy is in the gravitational potential store. In mid-fall, some is GPE and some is kinetic. At the moment of impact, virtually all of it has been transferred to the thermal stores of the floor, the book, and the surrounding air, plus a little carried away as a sound wave. Add it all up — you get back the original GPE.

Key Equations in 4.1.1

You must know and be able to apply all of these. AQA provides them in the equation sheet except where noted — but memorising them saves time in the exam.

Kinetic energy: Ek = ½ m v2
where Ek = kinetic energy in joules (J), m = mass in kilograms (kg), v = speed in metres per second (m/s).
Gravitational potential energy: ΔEp = m g h
where ΔEp = change in GPE in joules (J), m = mass in kg, g = gravitational field strength (9.8 N/kg on Earth), h = change in height in metres (m).
Elastic potential energy: Ee = ½ k e2
where Ee = elastic potential energy in joules (J), k = spring constant in newtons per metre (N/m), e = extension in metres (m). Assumes the spring has not been stretched beyond its limit of proportionality.
Work done: W = F d
where W = work done in joules (J), F = force in newtons (N), d = distance moved in the direction of the force in metres (m). 1 joule = 1 newton-metre.
Thermal energy (specific heat capacity): ΔE = m c Δθ
where ΔE = energy transferred in joules (J), m = mass in kg, c = specific heat capacity in J/kg °C, Δθ = temperature change in °C (or K — it is the same size of interval).

Worked Example 1 — Kinetic Energy

Question: A 1500 kg car is driving at 15 m/s. Calculate its kinetic energy.

Given:   m = 1500 kg,   v = 15 m/s
Formula: E_k = ½ m v²
Working: E_k = 0.5 × 1500 × 15²
         E_k = 0.5 × 1500 × 225
         E_k = 750 × 225
         E_k = 168 750 J   (or 168.75 kJ)

Answer:  168 750 J (to 5 s.f.)

Notice that because speed is squared, doubling the speed gives four times the kinetic energy. This is why braking distances grow so rapidly at high speed.

Worked Example 2 — Gravitational Potential Energy

Question: A 70 kg person climbs a 2 m ladder. How much
          gravitational potential energy do they gain?

Given:   m = 70 kg,   g = 9.8 N/kg,   h = 2 m
Formula: ΔE_p = m g h
Working: ΔE_p = 70 × 9.8 × 2
         ΔE_p = 1372 J

Answer:  1372 J

Worked Example 3 — Energy Conservation (Falling Object)

Question: A 0.5 kg ball is dropped from a height of 1.8 m.
          Assuming no air resistance, what is its speed just
          before it hits the ground?

Strategy: All GPE is transferred to KE (closed system, no losses).
         ΔE_p = E_k  →  mgh = ½mv²
         v² = 2gh
         v  = √(2 × 9.8 × 1.8)
         v  = √35.28
         v  = 5.94 m/s

Answer:  5.94 m/s (to 3 s.f.)
Always check your units. Mass must be in kg (not g), speed in m/s (not km/h or mph), and height in metres (not cm). A common error is forgetting to square v in the kinetic energy formula. Another is using g = 10 N/kg when AQA specifically states 9.8 N/kg for GCSE Physics (separate science). Show your working line-by-line — method marks are awarded even if the final answer is wrong.

4.1.2 Conservation and Dissipation of Energy

In reality, no energy transfer is 100% efficient — some energy is always dissipated (spread out) to the surroundings, usually as thermal energy. This wasted energy is still there — it has not been destroyed — but it is no longer useful for the purpose we wanted.

Useful energy: the energy transferred to the store(s) we want.
Wasted (dissipated) energy: energy transferred to stores we did not want — usually the thermal stores of the surroundings, and often some sound.

Examples of useful vs wasted energy transfers:

  • Electric kettle: useful = thermal store of water. Wasted = thermal store of the kettle body, sound of bubbling.
  • Car engine: useful = kinetic store of the car. Wasted = thermal (hot engine, exhaust gases) and sound.
  • Filament light bulb: useful = light (radiation). Wasted = thermal — around 95% of the input electrical energy is dissipated as heat, which is why filament bulbs have largely been replaced by LEDs.
  • Loudspeaker: useful = sound (radiation). Wasted = thermal from the coil and magnet.

Efficiency

Efficiency is the proportion of input energy that is transferred usefully.

efficiency = useful output energy transfer ÷ total input energy transfer

It can also be calculated using power:
efficiency = useful output power ÷ total input power

Efficiency is a ratio, so it has no units. It is expressed as a decimal (0 to 1) or multiplied by 100 to give a percentage (0% to 100%).

Worked Example 4 — Efficiency

Question: An electric motor is supplied with 100 J of electrical
          energy. It transfers 60 J usefully as kinetic energy.
          Calculate its efficiency.

Given:   Useful output = 60 J,   Total input = 100 J
Formula: efficiency = useful output ÷ total input
Working: efficiency = 60 ÷ 100
                    = 0.6
                    = 60%

Where did the other 40 J go?
  → 40 J dissipated as thermal energy (friction in bearings,
     heating of coils) and some as sound.

Sankey Diagrams

A Sankey diagram shows energy transfers visually. The width of each arrow is proportional to the amount of energy it represents. Wasted branches peel off to the side (usually downwards) — the remaining useful energy continues to the right. Sankey diagrams make it easy to see at a glance how efficient a device is.

Reducing Unwanted Energy Transfers

Because dissipated energy is wasted, engineers go to great lengths to reduce it. You must know at least the following methods:

ProblemSolutionHow it works
Friction between moving partsLubrication (e.g., engine oil, grease, graphite)A thin film of oil between surfaces reduces the force of friction, so less work is done against friction and less energy is dissipated as thermal energy. Also reduces wear on the parts.
Air resistance / drag on vehiclesStreamlined / aerodynamic shapeSmooth, curved shapes let air flow around the object with less turbulence. Less force needed to push through the air — less energy wasted as thermal energy of the air.
Heat loss from a house through wallsCavity wall insulation — filling the gap between two brick walls with foam or mineral woolThe trapped air in the insulation has a very low thermal conductivity. Heat escapes much more slowly by conduction.
Heat loss through the roofLoft insulation (glass wool / mineral wool laid between joists)Traps pockets of air (low thermal conductivity) above the ceiling. Reduces conduction and convection losses — hot air rises, so lofts are the biggest single source of heat loss in a poorly insulated house.
Heat loss through windowsDouble glazing — two panes of glass with a gap filled with a low-conductivity gas (argon) or vacuumThe gas has a much lower thermal conductivity than glass alone — heat conducts through far more slowly. A vacuum is even better because there are no particles to conduct at all.
Heat loss through gaps around doors/windowsDraught excludersSeal the gaps so warm air cannot leave by convection and cold air cannot enter.
Thermal conductivity is a measure of how quickly a material conducts thermal energy. A material with low thermal conductivity (e.g., mineral wool, foam, air) is a good insulator — it transfers heat slowly. A material with high thermal conductivity (e.g., copper, aluminium) is a good conductor.

The rate of cooling of a building is affected by the thickness and thermal conductivity of its walls. Thicker walls with lower-conductivity materials lose heat more slowly — the building stays warm for longer once heated.

When asked about reducing heat loss, state both the method and the physics. For example: "Cavity wall insulation fills the gap between the two walls with foam. The foam has a low thermal conductivity, so energy is transferred by conduction more slowly, reducing heat loss." Just saying "cavity wall insulation" on its own will score only 1 mark instead of 2.

4.1.3 Specific Heat Capacity

Different substances need different amounts of energy to warm them up by the same amount. Water, for instance, needs a great deal of energy to heat — this is why oceans moderate climate so effectively.

The specific heat capacity of a substance is the amount of energy required to raise the temperature of 1 kg of the substance by 1 °C.

Symbol: c. Units: J/kg °C (joules per kilogram per degree Celsius).
Equation: ΔE = m c Δθ

where ΔE = change in thermal energy (J), m = mass (kg), c = specific heat capacity (J/kg °C), Δθ = change in temperature (°C).

The Greek letter θ (theta) is used to represent temperature in AQA Physics, and Δ (delta) means "change in". So Δθ means "change in temperature" — final temperature minus initial temperature.

Typical Specific Heat Capacities

Substancec (J/kg °C)Notes
Water4200Unusually high — why oceans take a long time to warm up and cool down.
Ice2100Solid water has half the heat capacity of liquid water.
Oil (cooking)2000Heats up faster than water — useful in frying.
Concrete880Used in night-storage heaters.
Aluminium900Pans made of aluminium heat up quickly.
Iron450Heats up about 9× faster than the same mass of water.
Copper385Common material for heating pipes — heats quickly, conducts well.
Lead130Very low heat capacity — warms very quickly for its mass.

Worked Example 5 — Heating Water

Question: How much energy is needed to heat 2 kg of water from
          20 °C to 80 °C? (c_water = 4200 J/kg °C)

Given:   m = 2 kg,   c = 4200 J/kg °C,   Δθ = 80 − 20 = 60 °C
Formula: ΔE = m c Δθ
Working: ΔE = 2 × 4200 × 60
            = 504 000 J
            = 504 kJ

Answer:  504 000 J  (504 kJ)

Worked Example 6 — Rearranging for Temperature Change

Question: A 0.5 kg block of aluminium is given 9000 J of thermal
          energy. What is its temperature rise? (c = 900 J/kg °C)

Given:   m = 0.5 kg,   c = 900 J/kg °C,   ΔE = 9000 J
Formula: ΔE = m c Δθ    →    Δθ = ΔE ÷ (m c)
Working: Δθ = 9000 ÷ (0.5 × 900)
            = 9000 ÷ 450
            = 20 °C

Answer:  Temperature rises by 20 °C.

Why Water's High Specific Heat Capacity Matters

Water has a very high specific heat capacity (4200 J/kg °C) compared with most other common substances. Practical consequences include:

  • Climate: oceans absorb huge amounts of energy from the Sun without warming much. Coastal climates are therefore milder — summers are cooler and winters are warmer than inland.
  • Storage heaters: night-storage heaters are filled with concrete or water to store lots of thermal energy overnight (using cheap off-peak electricity) and release it slowly during the day.
  • Central heating: water is pumped through radiators because it carries a lot of energy per kilogram.
  • Cooling systems: car engines use water-based coolant because it can absorb a great deal of thermal energy before boiling.
  • Living organisms: because our bodies are mostly water, we resist rapid temperature changes — this is vital for homeostasis.
A common exam mistake is confusing specific heat capacity (energy to warm 1 kg of a substance by 1 °C) with specific latent heat (energy to change the state of 1 kg without temperature change). They are different. For GCSE separate physics, c only applies while the substance stays in the same state — during melting or boiling, temperature is constant and you need the latent-heat equation instead.

4.1.4 Power

Power is the rate at which energy is transferred, or equivalently the rate at which work is done.

P = E / t     or     P = W / t

where P = power in watts (W), E or W = energy transferred / work done in joules (J), t = time in seconds (s).

The watt is named after James Watt, the 18th-century Scottish engineer who improved the steam engine. 1 watt = 1 joule per second. A 100 W light bulb transfers 100 joules of energy every second.

Useful multiples:

  • 1 kilowatt (kW) = 1000 W — a typical electric kettle is around 2–3 kW.
  • 1 megawatt (MW) = 1 000 000 W — a single wind turbine is typically 2–5 MW.
  • 1 gigawatt (GW) = 109 W — large nuclear power stations output 1–3 GW.

Worked Example 7 — Energy Transferred by a Light Bulb

Question: A 60 W light bulb is left on for 5 minutes. How much
          energy is transferred?

Given:   P = 60 W,   t = 5 min = 300 s
Formula: P = E / t   →   E = P × t
Working: E = 60 × 300
           = 18 000 J
           = 18 kJ

Answer:  18 000 J  (18 kJ)

Worked Example 8 — Power of a Person Climbing Stairs

Question: A person of mass 60 kg climbs 30 steps, each 0.2 m high,
          in 10 seconds. Calculate (a) the work done against gravity,
          and (b) their average power.

Given:   m = 60 kg,   number of steps × height = 30 × 0.2 = 6 m
         g = 9.8 N/kg,   t = 10 s

(a) Work done = force × distance = weight × height gained
    Weight = m × g = 60 × 9.8 = 588 N
    W = F × d = 588 × 6 = 3528 J

(b) Power   = energy / time
    P = 3528 ÷ 10
      = 352.8 W

Answer:  (a) 3528 J    (b) 352.8 W (≈ 350 W)

For comparison: a professional cyclist sustains ~400 W for
an hour; an elite sprinter peaks briefly above 2000 W.
If a question asks for the useful power output, make sure you use the useful energy output — not the total energy input. If the device is 60% efficient and uses 100 W from the mains, its useful power output is only 60 W. Units: always convert minutes and hours to seconds before substituting into P = E / t. Forgetting this is a very common source of lost marks.

4.1.5 National and Global Energy Resources

Energy resources are used to generate electricity, for transport, and for heating. They fall into two broad categories:

Renewable resources are replenished at the same rate (or faster) than they are used. They will not run out on any human timescale.

Non-renewable resources have finite supplies that will eventually be exhausted because they are used far faster than they form.

UK Electricity Generation Mix — Changing Over Time

The UK's electricity mix has changed dramatically since 1990. Coal used to supply about 65% of the UK's electricity; today it is below 2% and due to be phased out altogether. Gas and renewables (especially wind) have replaced it. The UK generated its last tonne of coal-fired electricity in October 2024 — the first G7 country to phase out coal entirely.

Environmental Issues

Different energy sources cause different environmental problems:

  • Burning fossil fuels (coal, oil, gas) releases carbon dioxide (CO2), a greenhouse gas that traps thermal energy radiated from Earth's surface, causing global warming and climate change.
  • Burning coal (and to a lesser extent oil) releases sulfur dioxide (SO2), which dissolves in rain to form acid rain, damaging forests, lakes, and stonework.
  • Oil spills from tankers or rigs devastate marine and coastal wildlife and take decades to clean up.
  • Nuclear power produces radioactive waste that remains dangerous for thousands of years and must be stored safely. Accidents (Chernobyl 1986, Fukushima 2011) can contaminate large areas.
  • Hydroelectric dams flood valleys, destroying habitats and displacing communities; they can also release methane from decaying vegetation.
  • Wind farms have visual and noise impacts and can kill birds and bats, though the toll is small compared with cats or buildings.
  • Biofuels compete with food crops and may drive deforestation if rainforests are cleared for plantations.
  • Solar panel manufacture uses energy and rare materials; end-of-life recycling is still developing.

Reliability vs Environmental Impact — the Energy Trilemma

Policymakers must balance three competing demands, often called the energy trilemma:

  1. Security & reliability — the lights must stay on even when there is no wind and no sun.
  2. Affordability — electricity must not be so expensive that consumers or industry suffer.
  3. Sustainability — reducing CO2 emissions to meet climate targets.

No single resource satisfies all three perfectly. A realistic low-carbon electricity system uses a mix of resources: renewables for most generation, nuclear for reliable low-carbon baseload, gas for flexible backup during calm/dark periods, and increasingly large-scale storage (batteries, pumped hydro) to shift energy from times of surplus to times of shortage.

Long-answer ("6-mark") questions about energy resources almost always reward a balanced answer — you must discuss both advantages and disadvantages, consider environmental and economic impact, and reach a justified conclusion. A one-sided answer ("renewables are better, end of story") will cap at a middling grade. Always write: "advantage… however, disadvantage… overall…" with specific physics reasoning.
Know the difference between energy resource (the raw source, e.g. coal, uranium, wind) and the generation method (how electricity is produced, e.g. boiler + steam turbine, PV panel). Also know that all generation methods except PV and fuel cells involve turning a turbine connected to a generator — the only real difference between a coal, nuclear, gas, wind, tidal, and geothermal power station is what turns the turbine.
