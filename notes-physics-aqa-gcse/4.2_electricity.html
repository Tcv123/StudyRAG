4.2.1 Standard Circuit Symbols

You must be able to recognise, draw, and understand all of these circuit symbols. The exam often includes circuits you have not seen before — reading them correctly depends on knowing your symbols.

A circuit symbol is a standardised graphical representation of a component. Using standard symbols allows circuits to be drawn, shared, and understood by anyone, anywhere, regardless of language. All symbols in the grid above are defined by British Standards (BS 3939) and used worldwide.

4.2.2 Electric Current

Electric current is the rate of flow of electric charge. It is measured in amperes (A).

I = Q / t

where I = current in amperes (A), Q = charge in coulombs (C), t = time in seconds (s).

One ampere means one coulomb of charge flowing past a point in the circuit every second. A smartphone charger delivers around 2 A; a kettle draws around 13 A; a small LED might use only 10 mA (milliamps = 0.01 A).

In a series circuit, there is only one path for the current. The same current flows through every component — charge flows out of one end of a battery, through all the components in sequence, and back into the other end.

This is a key fact: in a series loop, I is identical at every point. If you measure the current through a lamp, then measure it again after the lamp, you will get the same value. Charge cannot accumulate anywhere — it must enter and leave every component at the same rate.

Worked Example 1 — Charge and Current

Question: A 2 A current flows for 30 seconds. How much charge
          has been transferred?

Given:   I = 2 A,   t = 30 s
Formula: I = Q / t   →   Q = I × t
Working: Q = 2 × 30
           = 60 C

Answer:  60 coulombs (C)
Remember that 1 coulomb is quite a large amount of charge. A typical AA battery contains about 2000 C (roughly 1 amp-hour, or 1 Ah). Always use seconds, not minutes or hours, in the formula Q = I t unless the question specifically gives time in other units.

4.2.3 Potential Difference and Resistance

Potential difference (voltage) is the energy transferred per unit charge. Symbol: V, measured in volts (V).

V = W / Q

where V = potential difference in volts (V), W = energy transferred (or work done) in joules (J), Q = charge in coulombs (C). Note: 1 V = 1 J/C.

A 12 V battery transfers 12 joules of energy to every coulomb of charge that flows through it. A 230 V mains socket transfers 230 joules per coulomb. This is why high voltages are dangerous — they transfer large amounts of energy to charge carriers (and, if you touch them, to you).

Resistance is a measure of how much a component opposes the flow of current. Symbol: R, measured in ohms (Ω).
Ohm's Law: For a component at constant temperature,
V = I R

where V = potential difference (V), I = current (A), R = resistance (Ω).

Ohm's Law is an experimental relationship, not a law of nature. It works for many materials (copper wire, a resistor) at constant temperature. But it fails for filament lamps (resistance increases as it heats), diodes (resistance depends on direction), and many other real devices.

4.2.4 I-V Characteristics

An I-V characteristic graph plots current (I) on the vertical axis and voltage (V) on the horizontal axis. The shape of the graph reveals how the resistance of a component changes with voltage.

Key Points on I-V Characteristics

A fixed resistor is ohmic — its I-V graph is a straight line through the origin. Its resistance is constant and does not change with voltage or current (provided temperature is held constant).
A filament lamp is non-ohmic — its I-V graph curves upward (increasing gradient). As the lamp gets brighter, its filament heats up, its resistance increases, and more voltage is needed to drive the same current. At low voltages, the filament is cool and resistance is low; at high voltages, it is hot and resistance is high.
A diode is a one-way valve for current. When biased forward (positive end toward positive terminal), it conducts readily (low resistance, steep curve). When biased reverse (negative end toward positive terminal), it blocks current almost completely (very high resistance, flat curve at zero current). A diode turns on sharply at a threshold voltage (about 0.6 V for silicon).

Light-Dependent Resistor (LDR)

An LDR (or photoresistor) changes resistance depending on the light falling on it. In darkness, its resistance is very high (mega-ohms); in bright light, it drops to a few hundred ohms. This is used in automatic lighting systems, security lights, and camera light meters.

Thermistor

A thermistor is a resistor whose resistance changes with temperature. There are two types: NTC (negative temperature coefficient) thermistors have resistance that decreases with temperature (the common type); PTC thermistors increase resistance with temperature (rare, used in self-regulating heaters). For GCSE, assume NTC unless stated otherwise.

4.2.5 Series and Parallel Circuits

Series Circuits

In a series circuit, components are connected one after another in a single loop. There is only one path for the current.
  • Current: The same current flows through every component. I = I1 = I2 = I3
  • Voltage: The supply voltage is divided among the components. Vsupply = V1 + V2 + V3
  • Resistance: Resistances add. Rtotal = R1 + R2 + R3 + …
total = R1 + R2 = 3 + 6 = 9 Ω I = V / Rtotal = 9 / 9 = 1 A (same through both) V1 = I × R1 = 1 × 3 = 3 V; V2 = 1 × 6 = 6 V

Parallel Circuits

In a parallel circuit, components are connected across each other so that there are multiple paths for the current.
  • Voltage: The same voltage appears across each component. V = V1 = V2 = V3
  • Current: The total current is divided among the branches. Itotal = I1 + I2 + I3 + …
  • Resistance: The reciprocal of total resistance is the sum of reciprocals: 1/Rtotal = 1/R1 + 1/R2 + 1/R3 + …. This means Rtotal is less than the smallest branch resistor.
total = 1/R1 + 1/R2 = 1/4 + 1/12 = 3/12 + 1/12 = 4/12 = 1/3 Rtotal = 3 Ω (less than the smallest branch!) Itotal = V / Rtotal = 12 / 3 = 4 A I1 = 12 / 4 = 3 A; I2 = 12 / 12 = 1 A; Check: 3 + 1 = 4 ✓

Worked Example 3 — Series Resistance

Question: Three resistors of 5 Ω, 10 Ω, and 15 Ω are connected
          in series to a 60 V supply. Calculate the total
          resistance and the current.

Given:   R₁ = 5 Ω,   R₂ = 10 Ω,   R₃ = 15 Ω,   V = 60 V
Formula: R_total = R₁ + R₂ + R₃
Working: R_total = 5 + 10 + 15
                 = 30 Ω
         I = V / R_total
           = 60 / 30
           = 2 A

Answer:  R_total = 30 Ω,   I = 2 A (through all three)

Worked Example 4 — Parallel Resistance and Current Division

Question: Two resistors, 4 Ω and 12 Ω, are connected in parallel
          to a 12 V supply. Find the total resistance and the
          current through each branch.

Given:   R₁ = 4 Ω,   R₂ = 12 Ω,   V = 12 V
Formula: 1/R_total = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂
Working: 1/R_total = 1/4 + 1/12
                   = 3/12 + 1/12
                   = 4/12
                   = 1/3
         R_total = 3 Ω

         I₁ = V / R₁ = 12 / 4 = 3 A
         I₂ = V / R₂ = 12 / 12 = 1 A
         I_total = I₁ + I₂ = 3 + 1 = 4 A

         Check: I_total = V / R_total = 12 / 3 = 4 A ✓

Answer:  R_total = 3 Ω,   I₁ = 3 A,   I₂ = 1 A
Common error: students forget that adding resistors in parallel gives a resistance less than any single branch. Intuition fails here because you might think "more wires = more resistance"; in fact, "more paths = easier flow = less resistance". Always check: after calculating R_total in parallel, verify it is smaller than the smallest branch resistor. If it is not, you have made an error.

4.2.6 Mains Electricity

UK Mains Supply

The UK mains electricity supply is alternating current (a.c.) at 230 volts and 50 Hz (fifty complete cycles per second).

Alternating current means the voltage and current reverse direction 100 times per second (50 Hz cycle = 2 reversals per cycle). This is different from direct current (d.c.) from a battery, which flows only one way. A.c. is used for the mains because it is efficient to transmit over long distances via transformers (we will see this in section 4.2.8).

Three-Wire Plug and Cable

A mains cable has three separate conductors, each with a different purpose and colour (in the UK):

WireColourVoltagePurpose
LiveBrownAlternates between +230 V and −230 V (at 50 Hz)Carries the current to the device. Always dangerous if touched.
NeutralBlueApproximately 0 V (ground reference)Completes the circuit, carrying current back. Normally safe to touch (but not recommended).
EarthGreen + Yellow (striped)Approximately 0 V (ground reference)Safety wire. Normally carries no current. If the live wire touches the metal casing, earth provides a low-resistance path to ground, triggering a fuse or circuit breaker.

Electrical Safety: Fuses and Circuit Breakers

A fuse is a safety device containing a thin wire that melts if the current exceeds a safe value. Common ratings in the UK are 3 A (for low-power devices), 13 A (for household appliances), and 32 A (for cookers). When the wire melts, the circuit is broken and current stops.

A fuse protects against two main dangers:

  1. Overload: If a device draws too much current (e.g., a short circuit), the fuse melts and disconnects the device before wires overheat and catch fire.
  2. Earth fault: If the live wire touches the metal casing (due to damaged insulation), current flows directly to earth via the earth wire and metal casing. The resistance is very low, so current is enormous, and the fuse melts almost instantly. This disconnects the device, leaving the casing at 0 V and safe to touch.
A circuit breaker (or residual current device, RCD) is an automatic switch that cuts off the supply if current flows to earth (i.e. if the live and neutral currents become unequal). Modern homes increasingly use RCDs instead of fuses because they work faster and can be reset (no need to replace a wire each time).
Double insulation (shown by the symbol ⧠ on a device) means the casing is made of a non-conducting material (plastic, not metal). There is no risk of touching live metal, so an earth wire is not necessary. These devices typically have only two wires (live and neutral) in the plug.

4.2.7 Energy and Power in Electrical Circuits

Electrical energy is transferred whenever current flows through a potential difference. The rate at which energy is transferred is power.

Electrical power: P = V I

where P = power in watts (W), V = potential difference (V), I = current (A).

A 230 V kettle drawing 13 A consumes P = 230 × 13 = 2990 W (about 3 kW). A USB phone charger at 5 V, 2 A uses P = 5 × 2 = 10 W.

Using Ohm's law, power can also be expressed as:

P = I² R     or     P = V² / R

The first form shows why thicker wires are used for high currents (to reduce I² R heating losses). The second shows why high voltage is efficient for long-distance transmission (power loss ∝ 1/V²).
Electrical energy: E = P t = V I t = Q V

where E = energy in joules (J), t = time in seconds (s), Q = charge in coulombs (C).

Worked Example 5 — Mains Power Calculation

Question: A kettle is rated 230 V, 13 A. Calculate its power
          rating and the energy transferred in 30 seconds.

Given:   V = 230 V,   I = 13 A,   t = 30 s
Formula: P = V I
         E = P t
Working: P = 230 × 13
           = 2990 W (≈ 3.0 kW)
         E = 2990 × 30
           = 89 700 J (≈ 90 kJ or 0.090 MJ)

Answer:  Power = 2990 W (3.0 kW),   Energy = 89 700 J (90 kJ)

Worked Example 6 — Energy Consumption and Cost

Question: A 2 kW electric heater runs for 30 minutes. Calculate
          the energy used in joules and in kilowatt-hours (kWh).
          If electricity costs £0.24 per kWh, what is the cost
          of running the heater?

Given:   P = 2 kW = 2000 W,   t = 30 min = 1800 s

(a) Energy in joules:
    E = P t = 2000 × 1800 = 3 600 000 J = 3.6 MJ

(b) Energy in kilowatt-hours:
    t = 30 min = 0.5 hours
    E = P t = 2 kW × 0.5 h = 1 kWh

(c) Cost:
    Cost = 1 kWh × £0.24/kWh = £0.24

Answer:  E = 3.6 MJ (or 3 600 000 J)
         E = 1 kWh
         Cost = £0.24
Examiners often test conversion between joules and kilowatt-hours. Remember: 1 kWh = 1000 W × 3600 s = 3 600 000 J. To convert MJ to kWh: 1 MJ = 1 000 000 J = 1 000 000 ÷ 3 600 000 = 0.278 kWh. Electricity bills use kWh; physics calculations usually use joules, so be ready to convert both ways.

4.2.8 The National Grid

Electrical power generated at power stations must be distributed to millions of homes and businesses across the country. The National Grid is a network of power lines and transformers that makes this possible.

Why High Voltage Transmission?

Electricity is transmitted at very high voltages (hundreds of thousands of volts) because this reduces energy loss:

Power loss in a cable is P_loss = I² R. If power is fixed at P = V I, then transmitting at high voltage means low current. Since power loss depends on I², a small reduction in current gives a large reduction in losses. For example, doubling the voltage halves the current and quarters the power loss.

A transformer uses a changing magnetic field to transfer energy between two coils. It can only work with a.c. (if the current is constant d.c., the magnetic field doesn't change and no energy is transferred to the secondary coil).
Step-up transformer: increases voltage and decreases current (same power, V₁ I₁ = V₂ I₂).
Step-down transformer: decreases voltage and increases current.

4.2.9 Static Electricity (Triple Only)

This section is for Triple Science (separate physics) students only. Foundation Science students are not examined on static electricity at GCSE.

How Charge is Generated by Friction

Static electricity is the accumulation of electric charge that is at rest (not flowing). It is generated when two materials are rubbed together. Electrons transfer from one material to the other:
  • The material that loses electrons becomes positively charged.
  • The material that gains electrons becomes negatively charged.

Example: when you rub a balloon on your hair, electrons transfer from the hair to the balloon. The balloon becomes negatively charged and attracts the hair. Your hair also becomes positively charged and repels the balloon's negative charge — but the attraction to the balloon's negative charge (pulling) is stronger than the repulsion (pushing) because the balloon is closer, so the hair sticks.

Electric Forces: Attraction and Repulsion

Coulomb's Law (concept only): Like charges repel each other; unlike charges attract. The force is proportional to the magnitude of the charges and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them.

Electric Field

An electric field is a region of space where a charged object experiences a force. Electrical potential energy is stored in the field.

Electric field patterns are shown using field lines:

  • Field lines point away from a positive charge.
  • Field lines point toward a negative charge.
  • The closer together the field lines, the stronger the field.
  • The direction of the force on a positive charge is along the field line (in the direction of the arrow).
  • The direction of the force on a negative charge is opposite to the field line.

Sparks and Electrical Discharge

If two charged objects are brought close together, the electric field becomes very strong near the gap. When the field strength exceeds about 3 million volts per metre, the air is ionised (electrons are knocked loose from atoms), and a spark forms. Charge jumps across the gap in a thin plasma channel.

A lightning strike is a giant spark: clouds become charged by friction with ice particles during storms, and when the potential difference exceeds millions of volts, a spark bridges the gap to the ground or another cloud.

For the final section on static electricity, expect questions on: (i) how friction transfers electrons and generates charge; (ii) forces between charges (like repel, unlike attract); (iii) field line diagrams (radial around point charges, uniform between plates); (iv) why sparks form (field strength ionises air). Know the direction of field lines (out of +, into −) and always draw them with arrows.
