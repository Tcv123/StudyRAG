1.1 Planning Experiments

Developing a Hypothesis

A hypothesis is a testable statement that proposes an explanation for an observed phenomenon. In biology, hypotheses must be based on existing scientific knowledge and should make clear, measurable predictions. A good hypothesis identifies both the independent variable (the factor you change) and the dependent variable (what you measure), while also suggesting the direction of the expected outcome.

Null Hypothesis: A statement that proposes no significant difference or relationship between the variables being tested. It serves as the default assumption against which statistical tests are applied. If the probability of obtaining results by chance is less than 5% (p < 0.05), the null hypothesis is rejected.

Selecting Appropriate Apparatus and Techniques

Choosing suitable equipment is fundamental to experimental design. Consider the resolution, precision, and accuracy of measurement instruments. For example, when measuring volumes of solutions for enzyme experiments, a graduated pipette (accuracy to 0.1 cm3) is more appropriate than a measuring cylinder (accuracy to 1 cm3) when small volumes need precise delivery.

Techniques should be matched to the biological system under investigation. Light microscopy is suitable for observing cell structure at magnifications up to x1500, whereas electron microscopy is needed for ultrastructure. Colorimetry is appropriate for quantifying colour changes in enzyme reactions, while chromatography separates mixtures of biological molecules such as photosynthetic pigments.

Designing Valid Experiments

Validity refers to whether the experiment actually tests what it claims to test. To ensure validity, all control variables must be identified and kept constant, only the independent variable should change between experimental groups, and the dependent variable must be measured using a method that genuinely reflects the biological process under investigation.

A control experiment provides a baseline for comparison. Positive controls confirm that the experimental setup can detect the expected result. Negative controls confirm that no confounding factors are producing the observed effect. For instance, in testing the Benedict's reagent with glucose solutions, a negative control uses distilled water and a positive control uses a known concentration of glucose.

When describing experimental design in exams, always state: the independent variable and how it is changed, the dependent variable and how it is measured, at least three control variables and how each is kept constant, and how you would ensure reliability (repeats, sample size).

Continuous, Discrete, and Categoric Variables

Continuous variables can take any numerical value within a range, such as temperature (37.2 degrees Celsius), mass (4.56 g), or time (120.5 seconds). Discrete variables can only take whole-number values, such as the number of cells counted in a haemocytometer grid or the number of organisms in a quadrat. Categoric variables are described by labels or categories rather than numbers, such as blood group type or species name.

Controlling Variables in Biological Systems

Biological systems present unique challenges for variable control because living organisms show natural variation. Strategies include using organisms of the same species, age, and sex; acclimatising organisms before experiments begin; randomising the allocation of organisms to treatment groups; and using sufficiently large sample sizes to account for individual variation.

Environmental controls are maintained using equipment such as water baths (temperature control to plus or minus 0.5 degrees Celsius), pH buffers (maintaining constant hydrogen ion concentration), and controlled-environment chambers for light intensity and photoperiod experiments.

1.3 Recording and Presenting Results

Recording Raw Data

Results tables should be constructed before the experiment begins. The independent variable occupies the left column, arranged in a logical order (increasing values for continuous data). The dependent variable is recorded in subsequent columns, with space for repeat measurements and calculated means. Column headings must include the quantity being measured and its unit, separated by a forward slash (e.g., Temperature / degrees Celsius).

Measurements should be recorded to the same number of decimal places as the resolution of the measuring instrument allows. For example, if using a balance accurate to 0.01 g, all mass readings should be recorded to two decimal places, including trailing zeros (e.g., 5.20 g, not 5.2 g).

Presenting Data Graphically

The choice of graph depends on the type of data collected:

  • Line graphs are used when both variables are continuous. Points are plotted and connected with a line of best fit (curved or straight) or joined point-to-point for time-series data.
  • Bar charts are used when the independent variable is categoric or discrete. Bars are separated by gaps and drawn with equal widths.
  • Histograms display frequency distributions of continuous data. Bars are adjacent (no gaps) because the data forms a continuous scale.
  • Scatter diagrams show the relationship between two continuous variables when testing for correlation.

All graphs require: a descriptive title, labelled axes with units, appropriate scales that use at least 75% of the available space, accurately plotted points, and a key if multiple data sets are shown.

Significant Figures and Decimal Places

Calculated values (such as means or rates) should be expressed to one more significant figure than the raw data. When calculating a mean from raw data recorded to two decimal places, the mean should be given to three significant figures. Rates are calculated as change in dependent variable divided by time, with appropriate units (e.g., cm3 per minute, arbitrary units per second).

When drawing graphs in exams, use a sharp pencil, plot points as small crosses (not dots), and ensure your scale is linear and does not skip values. Examiners award separate marks for each element: axes labels, units, scale, plotting accuracy, and line of best fit.

1.4 Analysis and Evaluation

Calculating Rates of Reaction

Rates of biological processes are determined by measuring the quantity of product formed (or substrate consumed) per unit time. The initial rate is the most meaningful measurement in enzyme kinetics because substrate concentration has not yet decreased significantly. The initial rate is found by drawing a tangent to the curve at time zero and calculating the gradient of that tangent.

For gas collection experiments, rate = volume of gas collected divided by time taken. For colorimetry experiments, rate = change in absorbance divided by time. Always express rates with compound units such as cm3 s-1 or arbitrary units min-1.

Statistical Tests

Statistical analysis determines whether observed differences or correlations are statistically significant (unlikely to have arisen by chance). The choice of test depends on the type of data and what is being investigated:

TestPurposeData Type
Student's t-testCompares means of two groupsContinuous, normally distributed
Chi-squared testTests for significant difference between observed and expected frequenciesCategoric (frequency data)
Spearman's rank correlationTests for correlation between two variablesOrdinal or continuous data that is not normally distributed

For all tests, calculate the test statistic, determine degrees of freedom, and compare the calculated value against the critical value at the 0.05 significance level. If the calculated value exceeds the critical value (for t-test and chi-squared) or falls within the critical region (for Spearman's), the null hypothesis is rejected.

Evaluating Experimental Procedures

Accuracy refers to how close a measured value is to the true value. Accuracy is improved by calibrating instruments, using appropriate equipment, and eliminating systematic errors. Precision describes how close repeated measurements are to each other (reproducibility). High precision means low spread around the mean, reflected in a small standard deviation.

Systematic errors affect all measurements in the same direction (e.g., a balance that always reads 0.02 g too high). They reduce accuracy but not precision. Random errors cause readings to scatter unpredictably around a true value (e.g., slight variations in reaction time when using a stopwatch). They reduce precision and are minimised by taking multiple repeats and calculating means.

Standard Deviation: A measure of the spread of data around the mean. A small standard deviation indicates that data points cluster closely around the mean (high precision). Approximately 68% of data falls within one standard deviation of the mean, and 95% falls within two standard deviations in a normal distribution.

Identifying Anomalous Results

Anomalous results (outliers) are data points that do not fit the overall pattern. They may result from measurement errors, contamination, or uncontrolled variables. Anomalies should be identified, excluded from mean calculations, and the experiment should be repeated to obtain a replacement value. However, anomalies should not be discarded without justification, as they may represent genuine biological variation.

1.5 Evaluation of Results and Conclusions

Drawing Valid Conclusions

Conclusions must be supported by the data collected and should directly relate back to the original hypothesis. State whether the data supports or refutes the hypothesis, reference specific data values or trends, and explain the biology underlying the observed pattern. Avoid overgeneralising from limited data sets and acknowledge limitations of the experimental design.

Correlation does not imply causation. Observing that two variables change together does not prove that one causes the other. A causal relationship requires a plausible biological mechanism, consistent results across multiple studies, and ideally a controlled experiment that isolates the proposed cause.

Evaluating Methodology

A thorough evaluation considers: whether control variables were adequately maintained, whether the range and intervals of the independent variable were appropriate, whether enough repeats were conducted for reliable means, whether the sample size was sufficient to represent the population, and whether the measurement technique had adequate sensitivity and resolution.

Suggest specific, practical improvements rather than vague statements. Instead of writing that the experiment should be repeated more times, specify the number of additional repeats needed and explain how this would improve the reliability of the mean values. Instead of stating that better equipment should be used, name the specific instrument and explain why its greater resolution or sensitivity would improve data quality.

1.6 Practical Techniques

Microscopy

Light microscopy requires preparation of thin sections or squashes, staining with appropriate dyes (e.g., iodine for starch, methylene blue for nuclei, aceto-orcein for chromosomes), and careful focusing from low to high power. Eyepiece graticules calibrated with stage micrometers allow measurement of cell dimensions. Magnification is calculated as image size divided by actual size.

Preparing temporary mounts involves placing the specimen on a glass slide, adding a drop of stain or mounting medium, and lowering a coverslip at an angle to exclude air bubbles. For root tip squashes, meristematic tissue is softened in warm hydrochloric acid before staining and squashing to separate cells for observation of mitotic stages.

Dissection

Biological dissection reveals internal structures of organisms. Heart dissection exposes the four chambers, valves, and coronary vessels. Flower dissection identifies reproductive structures for pollination studies. Dissection requires careful use of scalpels, scissors, and mounted needles, with appropriate risk assessment regarding sharps and biological hazards.

Sampling Techniques

Ecological sampling uses quadrats (for sessile organisms in defined areas), transects (for studying changes across environmental gradients), and mark-release-recapture (for estimating mobile animal populations using the Lincoln index). Random sampling eliminates bias through the use of random number generators to determine sampling positions. Systematic sampling along transects reveals zonation patterns.

Rates of Enzyme-Controlled Reactions

Enzyme activity is measured by monitoring either the rate of product formation or substrate disappearance. Methods include gas collection using a gas syringe or inverted measuring cylinder (for catalase producing oxygen), colorimetry measuring colour change over time (for amylase digesting starch), and continuous sampling with indicator tests (such as iodine tests at timed intervals to determine the time for complete starch digestion).

For practical questions, always describe the specific measurements you would take, the equipment you would use to take them, and how you would process the raw data to reach a conclusion. Generic answers such as "measure the results" will not gain marks.

Aseptic Technique

Working with microorganisms requires aseptic technique to prevent contamination of cultures and to protect the investigator. Key procedures include sterilising equipment by autoclaving (121 degrees Celsius for 15 minutes), flaming the necks of bottles and inoculating loops, working near a Bunsen flame to create convection currents that carry airborne contaminants away, taping Petri dish lids (but not sealing completely to allow aerobic conditions), and incubating cultures at 25 degrees Celsius in school laboratories to prevent growth of human pathogens.

Chromatography

Thin-layer chromatography and paper chromatography separate mixtures of biological molecules based on their relative solubility in the mobile phase versus their affinity for the stationary phase. Rf values (distance moved by substance divided by distance moved by solvent front) allow identification of unknown substances by comparison with known standards. Two-way chromatography improves separation of complex mixtures such as amino acids from protein hydrolysis.
