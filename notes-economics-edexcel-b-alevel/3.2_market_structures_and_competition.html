2. Perfect Competition

Perfect competition: A theoretical market structure with many buyers and sellers, homogeneous products, perfect information, no barriers to entry or exit, and firms that are price takers. It serves as a benchmark for evaluating other market structures.

2.1 Assumptions

  • Very large number of small firms — no single firm can influence the market price.
  • Homogeneous (identical) products — consumers see no difference between firms' output.
  • Perfect information — all buyers and sellers know all prices, qualities, and production methods.
  • No barriers to entry or exit — firms can freely enter when profits are high and leave when they are low.
  • Firms are price takers — they accept the market price determined by industry supply and demand.
  • Profit maximisation is the objective.

2.2 Short Run — Supernormal Profit

In the short run, a perfectly competitive firm can earn supernormal profit if market price is above ATC at the profit-maximising output (MC = MR).

Figure 1: Perfect competition — short-run supernormal profit. The firm produces at MC = MR (Q*), earning supernormal profit (shaded area).

2.3 Long Run — Normal Profit

Supernormal profits attract new entrants (no barriers). Industry supply increases, pushing down market price until all firms earn only normal profit. In long-run equilibrium: P = MC = ATC (at the minimum of ATC).

  • Allocative efficiency is achieved: P = MC (price reflects marginal cost of production — resources are allocated to their highest-valued use).
  • Productive efficiency is achieved: Production at the minimum of ATC (lowest possible cost per unit).
Evaluation: Perfect Competition
  • Purely theoretical — no real market perfectly matches all assumptions.
  • Achieves static efficiency (allocative and productive) but may lack dynamic efficiency — normal profits mean little surplus for R&D and innovation.
  • Homogeneous products mean no variety or choice for consumers.
  • Useful as a benchmark to evaluate how other market structures perform in comparison.

3. Monopoly

Pure monopoly: A single firm is the sole seller in a market with no close substitutes. In UK competition law, a firm has monopoly power if it has 25% or more market share (legal definition). A pure monopoly (100% market share) is rare — most real-world monopolies are dominant firms with very high market share.

3.1 Sources of Monopoly Power (Barriers to Entry)

  • Economies of scale: The incumbent's low costs make it unprofitable for smaller entrants (natural monopoly).
  • Legal barriers: Patents (20 years in the UK), copyright, licenses, and franchises granted by government.
  • Control of essential resources: De Beers historically controlled most of the world's diamond supply.
  • Brand loyalty: Established brands make it difficult for new entrants to attract customers.
  • Sunk costs: High irrecoverable costs of entry deter potential competitors.
  • Network effects: The value of a product increases with the number of users (e.g., social media platforms).
  • Predatory pricing: Incumbents may temporarily lower prices below cost to drive out entrants (illegal but hard to prove).
  • Vertical integration: Control of supply chain can exclude competitors.

Case Study: Google's Market Dominance
Google holds approximately 92% of the global search engine market. In 2024, a US federal court ruled that Google had maintained an illegal monopoly in online search through exclusive deals paying Apple and Samsung to make Google the default search engine on their devices (paying Apple alone an estimated 26 billion per year). Google argues that its dominance reflects a superior product — users choose Google because it is better, not because they are locked in. This case illustrates the tension between monopoly power achieved through innovation versus monopoly power maintained through anti-competitive practices.

3.4 Natural Monopoly

Natural monopoly: An industry where the minimum efficient scale (MES) is so large relative to market demand that only one firm can produce at the lowest cost. The fixed/infrastructure costs are so enormous that duplication would be wasteful. The LRAC curve is still falling at the level of market demand.

Examples: Water supply (Thames Water), rail network (Network Rail), electricity transmission (National Grid).

Natural monopolies are typically regulated by the government or an independent regulator (e.g., Ofwat for water, Ofgem for energy) to prevent exploitation of monopoly power.

4. Oligopoly

Oligopoly: A market structure dominated by a small number of large firms, each with significant market share. Key feature: interdependence — each firm's pricing and output decisions depend on, and are influenced by, the actions and expected reactions of rivals.

4.1 Characteristics

  • High concentration ratio: A few firms hold most of the market (e.g., the 4-firm concentration ratio for UK supermarkets is over 65%).
  • Interdependence: Firms must consider rivals' likely reactions before making decisions — this is what makes oligopoly unique and complex.
  • High barriers to entry: Economies of scale, brand loyalty, sunk costs, and predatory pricing deter new entrants.
  • Non-price competition: Firms often compete through advertising, branding, product innovation, and quality rather than price.
  • Price rigidity: Prices tend to be stable (sticky) — explained by the kinked demand curve model.

4.2 The Kinked Demand Curve

The kinked demand curve model (Sweezy) explains price rigidity in oligopoly:

  • If a firm raises its price, rivals do not follow — the firm loses customers and revenue (elastic demand above the kink).
  • If a firm lowers its price, rivals match the cut — no significant gain in market share (inelastic demand below the kink).
  • Result: firms have little incentive to change price — price is stable at the kink point.
Figure 3: The kinked demand curve — explains price rigidity in oligopoly. The gap in MR means MC can shift within the gap without changing the profit-maximising price or output.

4.3 Collusion

Overt (Formal) Collusion — Cartels

Cartel: A formal agreement between firms to fix prices, restrict output, or divide up markets. Cartels are illegal in the UK and EU under the Competition Act 1998 and Article 101 TFEU.

Tacit (Informal) Collusion

Firms may coordinate behaviour without explicit agreement:

  • Price leadership: One dominant firm sets the price and others follow (e.g., the largest petrol station in an area sets the price).
  • Parallel pricing: Firms independently reach similar prices by observing each other's behaviour.

Factors Encouraging Collusion

  • Few firms in the market (easier to coordinate).
  • Similar cost structures.
  • High barriers to entry (no outsiders to undercut the agreement).
  • Homogeneous products.
  • Stable market conditions.

Factors Causing Collusion to Break Down

  • Incentive to cheat — an individual firm can increase profit by secretly undercutting (prisoner's dilemma).
  • Difficulty monitoring compliance.
  • Different cost structures or objectives between firms.
  • New entrants disrupting the market.
  • Economic downturns increasing the temptation to cut prices.
  • Competition authority investigations and fines.

4.4 Game Theory and the Prisoner's Dilemma

Game theory analyses strategic decision-making under interdependence. The prisoner's dilemma shows why firms may not collude even when it would benefit them collectively:

Firm B: High PriceFirm B: Low Price
Firm A: High PriceA: 10m, B: 10m (both cooperate)A: 2m, B: 15m (A loses out)
Firm A: Low PriceA: 15m, B: 2m (B loses out)A: 5m, B: 5m (both compete)

The dominant strategy for each firm is to choose the low price — regardless of what the rival does, each firm does better by choosing low price. The Nash equilibrium is (Low, Low) at 5m each, even though both would be better off cooperating at (High, High) with 10m each. This explains why cartels tend to be unstable.

Case Study: UK Fuel Price Oligopoly
The UK petrol market is a classic oligopoly — dominated by a few large supermarket chains (Tesco, Asda, Sainsbury's, Morrisons) and oil company forecourts (BP, Shell, Esso). The CMA investigated the market in 2023 and found evidence of "rocket and feather" pricing — prices shoot up quickly when wholesale costs rise but fall slowly when they decline. The CMA also found that the margin between wholesale and retail fuel prices had widened significantly. While no formal collusion was proven, the oligopolistic structure and price interdependence allow firms to maintain higher margins than would exist in a competitive market. The CMA recommended greater price transparency through a real-time fuel price database.

5. Monopolistic Competition

Monopolistic competition: A market structure with many firms, each producing slightly differentiated products. Firms have some degree of market power (they can set their own price due to brand loyalty and product differences) but face competition from close substitutes. Low barriers to entry and exit.

5.1 Characteristics

  • Many buyers and sellers.
  • Product differentiation — each firm's product is slightly different (branding, quality, location, service).
  • Low barriers to entry and exit.
  • Each firm faces a downward-sloping demand curve (some price-setting power).
  • Non-price competition is important (advertising, branding, customer service).

5.2 Short Run vs Long Run

Short run: Firms can earn supernormal profit (just like a monopolist — produce where MC = MR, charge from the AR curve).

Long run: Supernormal profits attract new entrants. This shifts each existing firm's demand curve to the left (their market share falls) until only normal profit is earned. In long-run equilibrium, the demand (AR) curve is tangent to the ATC curve — the firm earns exactly normal profit.

Figure 4: Monopolistic competition — long-run equilibrium. AR is tangent to ATC at the profit-maximising output (MC = MR). Only normal profit is earned.

5.3 Efficiency in Monopolistic Competition

  • Not allocatively efficient: P > MC (firms have some market power).
  • Not productively efficient: Not producing at the minimum of ATC (excess capacity — could produce more at lower cost).
  • However, consumers benefit from product variety and choice — which has value even if it comes at a slightly higher cost.

6. Contestable Markets

Contestable market: A market where there is freedom of entry and exit, and where sunk costs are low or zero. The threat of potential competition disciplines incumbent firms, forcing them to behave competitively even if the market is currently concentrated.

6.1 Key Conditions

  • No barriers to entry or exit.
  • Low or zero sunk costs — costs that cannot be recovered on exit (e.g., advertising spend, specialised equipment).
  • Perfect information — potential entrants know about profit opportunities.
  • Hit-and-run entry: Firms can enter quickly, earn supernormal profit, and exit before the incumbent responds.

6.2 Implications

In a perfectly contestable market, even a monopolist would be forced to charge a competitive price and earn only normal profit, because any supernormal profit would attract "hit-and-run" entrants. The threat of competition is as powerful as actual competition.

Case Study: Airline Deregulation and Contestability
The airline industry became more contestable following deregulation in the US (1978) and EU (1990s). Low-cost carriers like Ryanair and EasyJet entered routes previously dominated by flag carriers (British Airways, Lufthansa). Sunk costs in aviation are relatively low — aircraft can be leased and redeployed to different routes. However, access to airport slots (especially at Heathrow) remains a significant barrier to entry, limiting contestability on the most profitable routes. This shows that real markets are imperfectly contestable — the degree of contestability varies by route and airport.

Exam Tip: When comparing market structures, always discuss efficiency. Perfect competition achieves static efficiency but may lack dynamic efficiency. Monopoly is statically inefficient but may be dynamically efficient (Schumpeter). Always evaluate — the "best" market structure depends on which type of efficiency you prioritise.

8. The Labour Market

Demand for labour: A derived demand — firms demand labour not for its own sake but for the output it produces. The demand curve for labour is the firm's marginal revenue product (MRP) curve.

MRP = Marginal Physical Product (MPP) × Marginal Revenue (MR)

8.1 Wage Determination in a Competitive Labour Market

In a perfectly competitive labour market, wages are determined by the intersection of labour supply and labour demand. The equilibrium wage is the market-clearing wage where Qs of labour = Qd of labour.

Figure 5: Competitive labour market — wage determined at the intersection of labour supply and demand (MRP).

8.2 Factors Affecting Demand for Labour

  • Demand for the final product (derived demand) — higher product demand increases MRP.
  • Productivity of labour — more productive workers have higher MRP.
  • Price of substitute factors — cheaper machinery may replace labour.
  • Wage rate — higher wages reduce quantity demanded (movement along the curve).

8.3 Factors Affecting Supply of Labour

  • Wage rate — higher wages attract more workers (but backward-bending at very high wages).
  • Non-monetary factors — job satisfaction, working conditions, location, status.
  • Qualifications and skills required — restricts supply in skilled occupations.
  • Barriers to entry — professional qualifications, licensing (e.g., doctors, lawyers).
  • Migration — immigration increases labour supply; emigration decreases it.
  • Population and demographics — ageing population reduces working-age supply.

8.4 Wage Differentials

Wages differ across occupations and sectors due to:

  • Skills and qualifications: Doctors and lawyers earn more due to long training (restricted supply) and high MRP.
  • Compensating wage differentials: Dangerous or unpleasant jobs may pay more to attract workers (e.g., oil rig workers).
  • Discrimination: Gender and racial pay gaps persist despite legislation.
  • Monopsony power: Where there is a dominant employer, wages may be below the competitive level.
  • Trade union power: Unionised workers tend to earn more than non-unionised equivalents.

8.5 The National Minimum/Living Wage

The UK introduced the National Minimum Wage in 1999 (currently the National Living Wage for over-21s, set at 11.44/hr in 2024). Economic theory suggests a minimum wage above equilibrium creates unemployment (excess supply of labour). However, empirical evidence is mixed — moderate minimum wage increases have little negative employment effect, particularly in monopsony labour markets where wages were already below competitive levels.

Case Study: UK Gender Pay Gap
The UK gender pay gap (median) stood at approximately 14.3% in 2023 (women earn on average 14.3% less than men per hour). Since 2017, firms with 250+ employees must report their gender pay gap. Causes include: occupational segregation (women disproportionately in lower-paid sectors like care and retail), the "motherhood penalty" (career breaks and part-time work reduce earnings progression), underrepresentation of women in senior positions, and historical discrimination. Government interventions include the Equality Act 2010, shared parental leave, and mandatory pay gap reporting, but progress in closing the gap has been slow.
Evaluation: Labour Market Intervention
  • Minimum wages may increase productivity by forcing firms to invest in training and technology.
  • Monopsony employers may be able to absorb minimum wage increases without cutting jobs.
  • Very high minimum wages could cause significant unemployment, especially among young and low-skilled workers.
  • Trade unions can improve wages and conditions but may also create unemployment if they push wages above market-clearing levels.
  • Labour market flexibility vs security — the "flexicurity" debate (e.g., Denmark combines flexible hiring/firing with generous unemployment benefits and training).
