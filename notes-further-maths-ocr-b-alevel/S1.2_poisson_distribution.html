S1.2: Poisson Distribution

1. Definition

\(X\sim\operatorname{Po}(\lambda)\) if \(P(X=r)=\dfrac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^r}{r!}\), \(r=0,1,2,\ldots\)

2. Conditions for Poisson Model

  • Events occur independently.
  • Constant mean rate over time/space.
  • Events occur singly.
  • Probability of event in short interval \(\propto\) interval length.

3. Mean and Variance

\(E(X)=\lambda,\quad \operatorname{Var}(X)=\lambda\)

Mean equals variance — characteristic feature. In data, variance much different from mean suggests Poisson is inappropriate.

4. Sum of Independent Poissons

Independent \(X\sim\operatorname{Po}(\lambda_1)\), \(Y\sim\operatorname{Po}(\lambda_2)\) \(\Rightarrow\) \(X+Y\sim\operatorname{Po}(\lambda_1+\lambda_2)\).

5. Scaling the Rate

If events occur at rate \(\lambda\) per unit time, count in \(t\) units \(\sim\operatorname{Po}(\lambda t)\).

Calls at 3/min. Probability of at least 5 calls in 2 minutes.
\(\lambda t=6\). \(P(Y\ge5)=1-P(Y\le4)\) from tables.

6. Poisson Approximation to Binomial

\(B(n,p)\approx\operatorname{Po}(np)\) when \(n\) large and \(p\) small. Rule: \(n>50, np<10\).

7. Hypothesis Tests on \(\lambda\)

  1. State \(H_0:\lambda=\lambda_0\), \(H_1\) (1- or 2-tailed).
  2. Under \(H_0\), compute \(P(X\ge\text{observed})\) (or \(\le\)).
  3. Compare with significance level \(\alpha\).
  4. Conclude in context.
Newsagent averages 5 enquiries/hour. Today he records 12. Significant at 5%?
\(H_0:\lambda=5\), \(H_1:\lambda>5\). \(P(X\ge12|\lambda=5)=0.0055<0.05\). Reject \(H_0\).

8. Poisson Process (Context)

Inter-arrival times are exponential with parameter \(\lambda\). Mean wait between events \(=1/\lambda\).

Exam Tip: Always state distribution under \(H_0\) explicitly (e.g. "Under \(H_0\), \(X\sim\operatorname{Po}(5)\)") and give the p-value — not just "significant/not significant".
