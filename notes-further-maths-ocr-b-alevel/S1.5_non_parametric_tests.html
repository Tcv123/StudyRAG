S1.5: Non-parametric Tests

1. What Are Non-parametric Tests?

Tests that make few or no assumptions about the underlying population distribution. Useful when data is ordinal, when parametric assumptions (e.g., normality) fail, or sample size is small.

2. Sign Test (Paired Data)

Null \(H_0\): median of differences = 0.

  1. For each pair, note sign of difference (+, −, or 0: discard zeros).
  2. Let \(n'\)=number of non-zero differences. Under \(H_0\), number of positives \(\sim B(n',0.5)\).
  3. Compute \(p\)-value (tail probability of observed count).
  4. Compare with \(\alpha\).
10 pairs, 7 positive, 0 zero. One-tailed 5%:
\(P(X\ge7|B(10,0.5))=0.172\). Not significant.

3. Wilcoxon Signed-Rank Test (Paired)

Uses magnitude information as well as signs.

  1. Compute differences \(d_i\). Discard zeros.
  2. Rank \(|d_i|\) (average ties).
  3. Attach signs back; sum the positive ranks (\(T_+\)) and negative (\(T_-\)).
  4. Test statistic \(T=\min(T_+,T_-)\).
  5. Compare with critical value from Wilcoxon tables.

Under \(H_0\)

\(E(T_+) = \tfrac{n(n+1)}{4},\quad \operatorname{Var}(T_+) = \tfrac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{24}\)

For large \(n\), \(T_+\) is approximately normal.

4. Wilcoxon Rank-Sum (Mann–Whitney) Test

For two independent samples of sizes \(n_1,n_2\).

  1. Combine all \(n_1+n_2\) observations; rank 1 to \(n_1+n_2\).
  2. Let \(R_1\) = sum of ranks in sample 1.
  3. \(U=R_1-\tfrac{n_1(n_1+1)}{2}\); use tables for critical \(U\).

6. Comparison with Parametric Tests

  • Non-parametric are robust to outliers, non-normality.
  • Usually less powerful than \(t\)-tests when parametric assumptions hold.
  • Ties handled with average ranks.

7. Exam Strategy

  • State hypotheses in terms of medians (not means) for non-parametric tests.
  • Show calculation of ranks/signs clearly.
  • Use correct table: Wilcoxon signed-rank, Mann–Whitney, or sign-test via Binomial.
  • Decide: reject iff test statistic \(\le\) critical value (tables give lower tail).
