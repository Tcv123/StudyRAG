M1.4: Circular Motion

1. Angular Measure and Angular Velocity

For a particle moving on a circle of radius \(r\):
  • Arc length \(s = r\theta\) (with \(\theta\) in radians).
  • Angular velocity \(\omega = \dot{\theta}\) (rad/s).
  • Linear speed \(v = r\omega\).
  • Period \(T = 2\pi/\omega\), frequency \(f = 1/T = \omega/(2\pi)\).

2. Centripetal Acceleration and Force

A particle moving in a circle at constant speed \(v\) has acceleration directed towards the centre:

\(a = \dfrac{v^2}{r} = r\omega^2, \quad v\omega\)  (equivalent forms)

The force required to produce this acceleration is the centripetal force, always directed towards the centre:

\(F = \dfrac{mv^2}{r} = mr\omega^2.\)
OCR Exam Tip: "Centripetal force" is not a separate force — it's the net inward force. For a conical pendulum, that's the horizontal component of tension; for a car on a banked track, the horizontal component of the normal reaction (plus friction).

3. Horizontal Circular Motion Examples

Object on a Horizontal Turntable

Friction provides the centripetal force. For slipping not to occur: \(\mu_s\, mg \ge mr\omega^2\), giving \(\omega_{\max} = \sqrt{\mu_s g/r}\).

Conical Pendulum

A particle on a string of length \(\ell\) moves in a horizontal circle of radius \(r = \ell\sin\theta\), where \(\theta\) is the angle from the vertical. Resolving vertically and horizontally:

\(T\cos\theta = mg, \quad T\sin\theta = mr\omega^2 = m\ell\sin\theta\,\omega^2.\)

Divide: \(\tan\theta = r\omega^2/g = \ell\omega^2\sin\theta/g\), i.e. \(\cos\theta = g/(\ell\omega^2)\).

Car on a Banked Track

On a track banked at angle \(\alpha\), with no friction the "design speed" is given by

\(\tan\alpha = \dfrac{v^2}{rg}.\)

4. Vertical Circular Motion

Now speed is not constant — there's a tangential component of gravity. Use conservation of energy to find \(v\) at any point:

\(\tfrac{1}{2}m v^2 + mgh = \text{constant}.\)

The centripetal equation still holds, using the instantaneous speed:

\(F_{\text{centripetal}} = \dfrac{mv^2}{r}.\)

Particle on the Inside of a Smooth Vertical Circle

At angle \(\theta\) from the lowest point, the inward forces are the tension/normal \(T\) and a component of gravity:

\(T - mg\cos\theta = \dfrac{mv^2}{r}\)  (below the horizontal)
\(T + mg\cos(\pi - \theta) = \dfrac{mv^2}{r}\)  (above the horizontal, adjusting signs)

At the top: \(T + mg = mv_{\text{top}}^2 / r\). For the string/normal not to go slack: \(v_{\text{top}}^2 \ge gr\).

A particle of mass \(m\) is attached to a string of length \(r\) and projected horizontally from the lowest point with speed \(u\). Find the minimum \(u\) for it to complete a full vertical circle.
Energy at top: \(\tfrac{1}{2}mv^2 + mg(2r) = \tfrac{1}{2}mu^2\).
Condition at top: \(v^2 \ge gr\).
So \(u^2 \ge gr + 4gr = 5gr\), i.e. \(u \ge \sqrt{5gr}\).

Particle on Outside of a Smooth Sphere

Normal reaction acts outward. At angle \(\theta\) from the top:

\(mg\cos\theta - R = \dfrac{mv^2}{r}.\)

Particle leaves the sphere when \(R = 0\), i.e. when \(v^2 = gr\cos\theta\). Combined with energy conservation, this gives the angle of departure.

5. Three Common Questions

  1. Given the speed/angular speed, find tension/normal (set up the radial equation).
  2. Given a condition (string slack, particle leaves surface), find the critical speed/angle.
  3. Use energy conservation between two positions to find speed at the second.

6. Exam Style Strategy

  • Always sketch showing forces acting at the point of interest.
  • Choose axes along and perpendicular to the radial direction.
  • For vertical motion, use energy for speed, force equation for tension/normal.
  • For "just complete" a circle: set \(T = 0\) (or \(R = 0\)) at the top; don't set \(v = 0\).
