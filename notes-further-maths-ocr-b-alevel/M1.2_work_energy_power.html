M1.2: Work, Energy and Power

1. Work Done by a Force

For a constant force \(\mathbf{F}\) producing a displacement \(\mathbf{s}\), the work done is \[W = \mathbf{F}\cdot \mathbf{s} = Fs\cos\theta\] where \(\theta\) is the angle between force and displacement. Units: joules (J) = N m.

Variable Force Along a Line

\(W = \int_{x_1}^{x_2} F(x)\,dx.\)

Work Done Against Gravity

Lifting mass \(m\) a vertical distance \(h\): \(W = mgh\).

2. Kinetic Energy

\(\operatorname{KE} = \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2\)

Work–Energy Theorem

The total work done on an object equals its change in kinetic energy: \[W_{\text{total}} = \Delta(\operatorname{KE}) = \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2 - \tfrac{1}{2}mu^2.\]
A 2 kg block is pushed 5 m along a rough floor (\(\mu = 0.3\)) by a 20 N horizontal force, starting from rest. Find its final speed.
Friction = \(0.3 \cdot 2g = 5.88\) N. Net force = \(20 - 5.88 = 14.12\) N.
Work done = \(14.12 \cdot 5 = 70.6\) J = \(\tfrac{1}{2}(2)v^2 \Rightarrow v^2 = 70.6 \Rightarrow v \approx 8.40\) m/s.

3. Gravitational Potential Energy

\(\operatorname{GPE} = mgh\)  (relative to a reference level)

Taking a convenient zero level (e.g. the ground) makes life easy. GPE is positive above the level, negative below.

4. Elastic Potential Energy (Springs and Strings)

For a spring or elastic string of modulus \(\lambda\) and natural length \(\ell_0\) at extension \(x\):

\(\operatorname{EPE} = \dfrac{\lambda x^2}{2\ell_0}.\)

Equivalently, for a Hookean spring of stiffness \(k\): \(\operatorname{EPE} = \tfrac{1}{2}kx^2\).

5. Conservation of Mechanical Energy

If only conservative forces (gravity, springs, elastic strings) act, total mechanical energy is conserved: \[\operatorname{KE} + \operatorname{PE} = \text{constant}.\]

Use this to find speeds without needing the dynamical equations directly.

A ball of mass 0.2 kg is dropped from 2 m. Find its speed just before hitting the ground.
\(mg h = \tfrac{1}{2}m v^2 \Rightarrow v = \sqrt{2gh} = \sqrt{2 \cdot 9.8 \cdot 2} = \sqrt{39.2} \approx 6.26\) m/s.

6. Work–Energy with Friction (Non-Conservative)

When friction acts, energy is not conserved — some is lost to heat. The energy equation becomes:

\(\operatorname{KE}_{\text{final}} + \operatorname{PE}_{\text{final}} = \operatorname{KE}_{\text{init}} + \operatorname{PE}_{\text{init}} - W_{\text{friction}}.\)

7. Power

Power is the rate of doing work. Units: watt (W) = J/s. \[P = \dfrac{dW}{dt} = \mathbf{F}\cdot \mathbf{v} = Fv\cos\theta.\]

Constant Power Problems

For a vehicle travelling at speed \(v\) on a level road, driving force \(F\) satisfies \(P = Fv\). Combined with Newton's second law (\(F - R = ma\), where \(R\) is resistance):

\(\dfrac{P}{v} - R = ma.\)
A 1000 kg car's engine delivers 20 kW. Resistance = 400 N. Find (i) top speed on level road; (ii) acceleration at 30 m/s.
Top speed: \(a = 0 \Rightarrow P/v = R \Rightarrow v = 20000/400 = 50\) m/s.
At 30 m/s: \(20000/30 - 400 = 1000a \Rightarrow a = (666.7 - 400)/1000 = 0.267\) m/s².

8. Inclined Planes

Include a \(\sin\theta\) component of gravity along the slope. For motion up the slope with driving force \(F\), resistance \(R\):

\(F - R - mg\sin\theta = ma.\)

Down the slope: gravity helps — reverse the sign of the \(mg\sin\theta\) term in the direction of motion.

9. Exam Style Strategy

  • Identify which energy forms are present (KE, GPE, EPE, work by external forces, friction losses).
  • Decide: can I use conservation of energy, or do I need \(F = ma\)?
  • For power questions, remember \(P = Fv\) — this often connects top speed and resistance.
  • On inclines, sketch with forces resolved along and perpendicular to the slope.
