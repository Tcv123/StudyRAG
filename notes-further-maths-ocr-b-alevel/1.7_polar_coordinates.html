1.7: Polar Coordinates

1. The Polar Coordinate System

In polar coordinates a point is given by \((r, \theta)\) where
  • \(r\) is the distance from the pole (origin),
  • \(\theta\) is the angle measured anticlockwise from the initial line (positive \(x\)-axis).
OCR convention: \(r \ge 0\).

Conversion Cartesian ↔ Polar

\(x = r\cos\theta, \quad y = r\sin\theta\)
\(r = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}, \quad \tan\theta = \dfrac{y}{x}\)  (choose \(\theta\) in the correct quadrant)

2. Sketching Polar Curves

To sketch \(r = f(\theta)\), tabulate key \(\theta\) values (\(0, \pi/6, \pi/4, \pi/3, \pi/2, \ldots\)) and plot. Some useful standard curves:

Worked Sketch — The Cardioid \(r = 1 + \cos\theta\)

Key points: \(\theta = 0 \Rightarrow r = 2\); \(\theta = \pi/2 \Rightarrow r = 1\); \(\theta = \pi \Rightarrow r = 0\) (the pole).

Cardioid \(r = 1 + \cos\theta\).

3. Tangents at the Pole

If \(r = 0\) when \(\theta = \alpha\), then the line \(\theta = \alpha\) is tangent to the curve at the pole.

Tangents at the pole for \(r = \sin 2\theta\).
\(r = 0\) when \(\sin 2\theta = 0\), i.e. \(\theta = 0, \pi/2, \pi, 3\pi/2\). These four half-lines are the pole tangents — the curve is a four-petal rose.

4. Area Enclosed by a Polar Curve

The area bounded by \(r = f(\theta)\), \(\theta = \alpha\), and \(\theta = \beta\) is \[A = \dfrac{1}{2}\int_{\alpha}^{\beta} r^2\,d\theta = \dfrac{1}{2}\int_{\alpha}^{\beta} [f(\theta)]^2\,d\theta.\]
Area enclosed by the cardioid \(r = 1 + \cos\theta\).
Symmetry: compute area for \(\theta \in [0, \pi]\) and double.
\(A = \tfrac{1}{2} \cdot 2\int_0^{\pi}(1 + \cos\theta)^2\,d\theta = \int_0^{\pi}(1 + 2\cos\theta + \cos^2\theta)\,d\theta\)
\(= \int_0^{\pi}(1 + 2\cos\theta + \tfrac{1 + \cos 2\theta}{2})\,d\theta\)
\(= \left[\tfrac{3}{2}\theta + 2\sin\theta + \tfrac{1}{4}\sin 2\theta\right]_0^{\pi} = \tfrac{3\pi}{2}.\)
OCR Exam Tip: Be careful about the limits of integration — they should trace out the region once. For symmetric curves, use symmetry to simplify.

5. Tangents in Cartesian Form

From \(x = r\cos\theta\), \(y = r\sin\theta\), the gradient is:

\(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{dy/d\theta}{dx/d\theta} = \dfrac{\dot{r}\sin\theta + r\cos\theta}{\dot{r}\cos\theta - r\sin\theta}\)

Horizontal tangents: \(dy/d\theta = 0\). Vertical tangents: \(dx/d\theta = 0\).

6. Areas Between Two Polar Curves

For the region inside \(r = g(\theta)\) and outside \(r = f(\theta)\), with \(g \ge f\) on \([\alpha, \beta]\):

\(A = \tfrac{1}{2}\int_{\alpha}^{\beta} \left(g(\theta)^2 - f(\theta)^2\right)d\theta.\)

Find intersection points by solving \(f(\theta) = g(\theta)\) — remember to consider the pole separately.

7. Exam Style Strategy

  • Always sketch first — it reveals symmetry and tells you whether the region is traced once.
  • State your limits carefully (e.g. "for \(-\pi/2 \le \theta \le \pi/2\) the full cardioid is covered").
  • For "area between two curves", find all intersection angles, including the pole.
  • Standard integrals you will need: \(\int \cos^2\theta\,d\theta\), \(\int \sin^2\theta\,d\theta\), \(\int \cos n\theta\,d\theta\) — memorise or derive via double-angle formulas.
