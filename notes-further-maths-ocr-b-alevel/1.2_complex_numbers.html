1.2: Complex Numbers

1. The Imaginary Unit and Complex Numbers

Imaginary unit: \(i\) is defined so that \(i^2 = -1\), giving \(i = \sqrt{-1}\).

Complex number: a number of the form \(z = a + bi\) where \(a, b \in \mathbb{R}\). The set of all complex numbers is denoted \(\mathbb{C}\).

Real part: \(\operatorname{Re}(z) = a\).   Imaginary part: \(\operatorname{Im}(z) = b\) (the real coefficient of \(i\), not \(bi\)).

Every real number is a complex number with \(\operatorname{Im}(z) = 0\); if \(a = 0\) and \(b \neq 0\), the number \(bi\) is called purely imaginary.

Powers of \(i\)

The powers of \(i\) cycle with period 4:

\(n\)\(i^1\)\(i^2\)\(i^3\)\(i^4\)\(i^5\)\(i^6\)
\(i^n\)\(i\)\(-1\)\(-i\)\(1\)\(i\)\(-1\)

For \(i^n\): divide \(n\) by 4 and use the remainder — \(0\to1\), \(1\to i\), \(2\to -1\), \(3\to -i\).

2. Arithmetic of Complex Numbers

Addition and Subtraction

\((a+bi) \pm (c+di) = (a\pm c) + (b\pm d)i\)

Multiplication

Expand using FOIL then substitute \(i^2 = -1\):

\((a+bi)(c+di) = (ac - bd) + (ad + bc)i\)

Complex Conjugate

The complex conjugate of \(z = a + bi\) is \(z^* = \bar{z} = a - bi\).

Key properties:

  • \(z + \bar{z} = 2a = 2\operatorname{Re}(z)\)  (real)
  • \(z - \bar{z} = 2bi = 2i\operatorname{Im}(z)\)  (purely imaginary)
  • \(z\bar{z} = a^2 + b^2 = |z|^2\)  (real and non-negative)
  • \(\overline{z_1 + z_2} = \bar{z_1} + \bar{z_2}\)  and  \(\overline{z_1 z_2} = \bar{z_1}\,\bar{z_2}\)

Division

Rationalise the denominator by multiplying numerator and denominator by the conjugate of the denominator:

\(\dfrac{a+bi}{c+di} = \dfrac{(a+bi)(c-di)}{c^2 + d^2}\)
Worked example: Simplify \(\dfrac{3+2i}{1-i}\).
Multiply by \(\dfrac{1+i}{1+i}\):   \(\dfrac{(3+2i)(1+i)}{(1-i)(1+i)} = \dfrac{3 + 3i + 2i + 2i^2}{1 - i^2} = \dfrac{1 + 5i}{2} = \tfrac{1}{2} + \tfrac{5}{2}i\).

3. The Argand Diagram

Complex numbers are represented as points (or vectors from the origin) in the plane, plotting \(\operatorname{Re}(z)\) horizontally and \(\operatorname{Im}(z)\) vertically.

Modulus and Argument

Modulus: \(|z| = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}\) — the distance from the origin to the point \((a, b)\).

Argument: \(\arg z = \theta\) — the angle from the positive real axis, measured anticlockwise. The principal argument is the value with \(-\pi < \theta \le \pi\).
OCR convention: OCR uses the principal argument in the range \(-\pi < \arg z \le \pi\). Always state your range and check the quadrant — using \(\arctan(b/a)\) without thinking gives the wrong angle in quadrants 2 and 3.

4. Modulus-Argument and Exponential Form

\(z = r(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta) = r\,e^{i\theta}\)

where \(r = |z|\) and \(\theta = \arg z\). The exponential form follows from Euler's formula:

\(e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta\)
Convert \(z = -1 + i\sqrt{3}\) to mod-arg form.
\(|z| = \sqrt{1 + 3} = 2\). Second quadrant, so \(\arg z = \pi - \arctan(\sqrt{3}/1) = \pi - \pi/3 = 2\pi/3\).
So \(z = 2(\cos\tfrac{2\pi}{3} + i\sin\tfrac{2\pi}{3}) = 2e^{i 2\pi/3}\).

Multiplication and Division in Polar/Exponential Form

Multiplication adds arguments, division subtracts them:

\(r_1 e^{i\theta_1} \cdot r_2 e^{i\theta_2} = r_1 r_2\, e^{i(\theta_1 + \theta_2)}\)
\(\dfrac{r_1 e^{i\theta_1}}{r_2 e^{i\theta_2}} = \dfrac{r_1}{r_2}\, e^{i(\theta_1 - \theta_2)}\)
  • \(|z_1 z_2| = |z_1||z_2|\),  \(\arg(z_1 z_2) = \arg z_1 + \arg z_2\)
  • \(|z_1/z_2| = |z_1|/|z_2|\),  \(\arg(z_1/z_2) = \arg z_1 - \arg z_2\)
OCR Exam Tip: Exponential form is usually the cleanest for multiplication, division and powers. Convert to mod-arg form first, then apply the rule, then convert back if needed.

5. De Moivre's Theorem

De Moivre's theorem: For any \(n \in \mathbb{Z}\), \[(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta)^n = \cos(n\theta) + i\sin(n\theta)\] Equivalently, \((e^{i\theta})^n = e^{in\theta}\).

This is proved by induction for \(n \ge 0\), and the result for negative \(n\) follows using \((e^{i\theta})^{-1} = e^{-i\theta}\).

Uses of de Moivre

  1. Powers of complex numbers: \(z^n = r^n e^{in\theta}\).
  2. Multiple-angle identities: expand \((\cos\theta + i\sin\theta)^n\) by the binomial theorem, then equate real and imaginary parts.
  3. \(n\)th roots: see §7.
Show that \(\cos 3\theta = 4\cos^3\theta - 3\cos\theta\).
By de Moivre, \(\cos 3\theta + i\sin 3\theta = (\cos\theta + i\sin\theta)^3\).
Expand: \((c + is)^3 = c^3 + 3c^2(is) + 3c(is)^2 + (is)^3 = c^3 - 3cs^2 + i(3c^2 s - s^3)\).
Real part: \(\cos 3\theta = c^3 - 3cs^2 = c^3 - 3c(1 - c^2) = 4c^3 - 3c\).  ∎

Using \(z + 1/z\) and \(z - 1/z\)

If \(z = e^{i\theta}\) then:

\(z + z^{-1} = 2\cos\theta, \quad z - z^{-1} = 2i\sin\theta\)
\(z^n + z^{-n} = 2\cos(n\theta), \quad z^n - z^{-n} = 2i\sin(n\theta)\)

These are powerful for expressing \(\cos^n\theta\) or \(\sin^n\theta\) as a sum of cosines/sines of multiple angles (needed for integration).

Express \(\cos^4\theta\) in terms of \(\cos 4\theta, \cos 2\theta, 1\).
\((2\cos\theta)^4 = (z + z^{-1})^4 = z^4 + 4z^2 + 6 + 4z^{-2} + z^{-4}\)
\(= (z^4 + z^{-4}) + 4(z^2 + z^{-2}) + 6 = 2\cos 4\theta + 8\cos 2\theta + 6\).
So \(16\cos^4\theta = 2\cos 4\theta + 8\cos 2\theta + 6 \implies \cos^4\theta = \tfrac{1}{8}\cos 4\theta + \tfrac{1}{2}\cos 2\theta + \tfrac{3}{8}\).

6. Roots of Polynomial Equations

Fundamental Theorem of Algebra: every polynomial of degree \(n \ge 1\) with complex coefficients has exactly \(n\) roots in \(\mathbb{C}\) (counting multiplicity).
Conjugate root theorem: if a polynomial has real coefficients and \(z = p + qi\) is a root, then \(\bar{z} = p - qi\) is also a root. Complex roots always occur in conjugate pairs.
Given that \(2+i\) is a root of \(z^3 - 5z^2 + 11z - 15 = 0\), find the other roots.
By the conjugate root theorem, \(2-i\) is also a root. The quadratic factor is \((z-(2+i))(z-(2-i)) = z^2 - 4z + 5\).
Divide: \(z^3 - 5z^2 + 11z - 15 = (z^2 - 4z + 5)(z - 3)\). So the third root is \(z = 3\).

7. Roots of Unity and nth Roots

The \(n\)th roots of unity are the solutions of \(z^n = 1\). There are exactly \(n\) of them, given by \[z_k = e^{2\pi i k / n}, \quad k = 0, 1, 2, \ldots, n-1.\]

On an Argand diagram, the \(n\)th roots of unity lie on the unit circle at the vertices of a regular \(n\)-gon, one vertex at \(z = 1\).

The fifth roots of unity — vertices of a regular pentagon on the unit circle.

nth Roots of a General Complex Number

To solve \(z^n = w\) where \(w = R\,e^{i\phi}\):

\(z_k = R^{1/n}\,e^{i(\phi + 2\pi k)/n}, \quad k = 0, 1, \ldots, n-1\)

All \(n\) roots have the same modulus \(R^{1/n}\) and arguments differing by \(2\pi/n\) — they lie equally spaced on a circle of radius \(R^{1/n}\).

Find all solutions of \(z^4 = -16\).
Write \(-16 = 16\,e^{i\pi}\). Then \(z_k = 2\,e^{i(\pi + 2\pi k)/4} = 2\,e^{i\pi(1 + 2k)/4}\) for \(k = 0, 1, 2, 3\).
Arguments: \(\pi/4,\; 3\pi/4,\; 5\pi/4,\; 7\pi/4\) (or equivalently \(-3\pi/4,\; -\pi/4\)).
So \(z = \sqrt{2}(1 \pm i),\; \sqrt{2}(-1 \pm i)\).
OCR Exam Tip: Always sketch the roots on an Argand diagram — it prevents sign errors and makes the symmetry obvious. Quote arguments in the principal range \(-\pi < \theta \le \pi\) unless the question asks otherwise.

Sum of Roots of Unity

For \(n \ge 2\), the sum of all \(n\)th roots of unity is zero:

\(\displaystyle \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} e^{2\pi i k/n} = 0\)

Proof: this is a geometric series with ratio \(e^{2\pi i/n} \neq 1\), sum \(= \dfrac{1 - e^{2\pi i}}{1 - e^{2\pi i/n}} = 0\).

Let \(z = x + iy\) and interpret \(|z - w|\) as the distance between \(z\) and \(w\) in the plane.

Sketch the locus \(|z - 2 + i| = 3\) and find its Cartesian equation.
Rewrite: \(|z - (2 - i)| = 3\). Circle, centre \((2, -1)\), radius \(3\).
Cartesian: \((x - 2)^2 + (y + 1)^2 = 9\).
Find the Cartesian equation of \(|z - 3| = |z + i|\).
Distance from \((3, 0)\) equals distance from \((0, -1)\) — the perpendicular bisector of the segment joining these two points.
Let \(z = x + iy\): \((x-3)^2 + y^2 = x^2 + (y+1)^2\).
Expand: \(-6x + 9 = 2y + 1 \implies y = 4 - 3x\).
OCR Exam Tip: For loci questions always (a) sketch first to visualise, (b) then do the algebra. Note whether the locus is a line, circle, ray, or region — and whether endpoints are included (strict vs. non-strict inequality).

10. Exam Style Strategy

  • Algebra in Cartesian form — best for addition, subtraction, basic multiplication/division.
  • Mod-arg / exponential form — best for powers, roots, multiple-angle identities.
  • Argand diagrams — always sketch when loci, roots of unity or multiple roots are involved.
  • Check your principal argument range and remember the quadrant rules.
  • Conjugate pairs — if a real-coefficient polynomial has one complex root, you get a second for free.
