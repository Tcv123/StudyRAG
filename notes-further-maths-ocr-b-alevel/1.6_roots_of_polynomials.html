1.6: Roots of Polynomials

1. Vieta's Formulae (Roots-Coefficient Relations)

Quadratic \(ax^2+bx+c=0\), roots \(\alpha,\beta\)

\(\alpha+\beta=-\dfrac{b}{a},\quad \alpha\beta=\dfrac{c}{a}\)

Cubic \(ax^3+bx^2+cx+d=0\), roots \(\alpha,\beta,\gamma\)

\(\sum\alpha=-\dfrac{b}{a},\quad\sum\alpha\beta=\dfrac{c}{a},\quad\alpha\beta\gamma=-\dfrac{d}{a}\)

Quartic \(ax^4+bx^3+cx^2+dx+e=0\), roots \(\alpha,\beta,\gamma,\delta\)

\(\sum\alpha=-\dfrac{b}{a},\;\sum\alpha\beta=\dfrac{c}{a},\;\sum\alpha\beta\gamma=-\dfrac{d}{a},\;\alpha\beta\gamma\delta=\dfrac{e}{a}\)

The sign pattern is \(-,+,-,+,\ldots\). \(\sum\alpha\), \(\sum\alpha\beta\) denote the elementary symmetric polynomials.

2. Symmetric Function Identities

For quadratic roots:

  • \(\alpha^2+\beta^2=(\alpha+\beta)^2-2\alpha\beta\)
  • \(\dfrac{1}{\alpha}+\dfrac{1}{\beta}=\dfrac{\alpha+\beta}{\alpha\beta}\)
  • \(\alpha^3+\beta^3=(\alpha+\beta)^3-3\alpha\beta(\alpha+\beta)\)
  • \((\alpha-\beta)^2=(\alpha+\beta)^2-4\alpha\beta\)

For cubic roots:

  • \(\sum\alpha^2=(\sum\alpha)^2-2\sum\alpha\beta\)
  • \(\sum\alpha^3=(\sum\alpha)^3-3(\sum\alpha)(\sum\alpha\beta)+3\alpha\beta\gamma\)
Given \(\alpha+\beta=4,\alpha\beta=3\), find \(\alpha^2+\beta^2\).
\(=(4)^2-2(3)=16-6=10\).

3. Transforming the Roots

To form a polynomial whose roots are transformed versions of the original, use substitution.

Shift: roots \(\alpha+k,\beta+k,\ldots\)

Let \(y=x+k\), so \(x=y-k\). Substitute into the original polynomial.

Given \(\alpha,\beta\) roots of \(x^2-5x+6=0\), find quadratic with roots \(\alpha+2,\beta+2\).
Sub \(y=x+2\), \(x=y-2\): \((y-2)^2-5(y-2)+6=0\). Expand: \(y^2-9y+20=0\).

Reciprocal: roots \(1/\alpha,1/\beta,\ldots\)

Sub \(y=1/x\) then \(x=1/y\). For polynomial coefficients \(a_n,\ldots,a_0\), this reverses the coefficients.

Cubic with roots reciprocals of \(x^3-3x^2+2x-1=0\).
Reverse coefficients: \(-y^3+2y^2-3y+1=0\), or \(y^3-2y^2+3y-1=0\).

Scale: roots \(k\alpha,k\beta,\ldots\)

Sub \(y=kx\), \(x=y/k\).

4. Worked Examples

Given roots \(\alpha,\beta,\gamma\) of \(x^3-6x^2+11x-6=0\), find \(\sum\alpha^2\).
\(\sum\alpha=6,\sum\alpha\beta=11,\alpha\beta\gamma=6\). \(\sum\alpha^2=36-22=14\).
Given \(\alpha,\beta\) roots of \(x^2-7x+2=0\), form quadratic with roots \(\alpha^2,\beta^2\).
\(\alpha^2+\beta^2=49-4=45\); \((\alpha\beta)^2=4\). Quadratic: \(y^2-45y+4=0\).

5. Higher-Order Roots (Newton's Identities)

Let \(S_n=\alpha^n+\beta^n+\cdots\). For quadratic: \(S_1=\alpha+\beta\), \(S_2=(\alpha+\beta)^2-2\alpha\beta\), and recursively \(S_n=(\alpha+\beta)S_{n-1}-\alpha\beta\,S_{n-2}\).

For \(x^2-3x+1=0\), find \(\alpha^5+\beta^5\).
\(S_0=2,S_1=3,S_2=9-2=7,S_3=3(7)-1(3)=18,S_4=3(18)-7=47,S_5=3(47)-18=123\).

6. Exam Strategy

  • Memorise the sign pattern and Vieta\'s formulae for quadratic, cubic, quartic.
  • Symmetric-function identities: derive them once; re-use.
  • Transformations via substitution are usually safer than re-deriving sum/product of new roots.
  • Give exact answers; don\'t approximate.
