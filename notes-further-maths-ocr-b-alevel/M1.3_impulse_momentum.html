M1.3: Impulse and Momentum

1. Momentum and Impulse

Momentum: \(\mathbf{p} = m\mathbf{v}\)  (kg m/s; vector).
Impulse: \(\mathbf{J} = \int \mathbf{F}\,dt = \Delta \mathbf{p} = m\mathbf{v} - m\mathbf{u}\)  (N s).

The impulse of a force equals the change in momentum it produces — a direct consequence of \(\mathbf{F} = m\mathbf{a}\) integrated in time.

Conservation of Momentum

For an isolated system (no external forces), total momentum is conserved:

\(\sum m_i \mathbf{u}_i = \sum m_i \mathbf{v}_i.\)

2. One-Dimensional Collisions

Two particles of masses \(m_1, m_2\) travelling along a straight line with velocities \(u_1, u_2\) collide, emerging with velocities \(v_1, v_2\).

\(m_1 u_1 + m_2 u_2 = m_1 v_1 + m_2 v_2\)  (conservation of momentum)

Newton's Experimental Law of Restitution

For a collision between two bodies along the line of centres: \[\text{speed of separation} = e \times \text{speed of approach}\] i.e. \(v_2 - v_1 = -e(u_2 - u_1)\), where \(0 \le e \le 1\) is the coefficient of restitution.

\(e = 1\) → perfectly elastic (kinetic energy conserved). \(e = 0\) → perfectly inelastic (objects stick together).

Kinetic Energy

Total KE \(= \tfrac{1}{2}m_1 v_1^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}m_2 v_2^2\). In general, KE is lost in collisions unless \(e = 1\).

\(\text{KE lost} = \tfrac{1}{2}\dfrac{m_1 m_2}{m_1 + m_2}(u_1 - u_2)^2\,(1 - e^2)\)
A 2 kg ball moving at 5 m/s collides with a stationary 3 kg ball. Coefficient of restitution \(e = 0.4\). Find the velocities after the collision.
Momentum: \(2(5) + 3(0) = 2v_1 + 3v_2 \Rightarrow 10 = 2v_1 + 3v_2\).
Restitution: \(v_2 - v_1 = 0.4(5 - 0) = 2 \Rightarrow v_2 = v_1 + 2\).
Substitute: \(10 = 2v_1 + 3(v_1 + 2) = 5v_1 + 6 \Rightarrow v_1 = 0.8\) m/s, \(v_2 = 2.8\) m/s.

3. Collisions with a Smooth Wall

Impact is perpendicular to the wall, so the parallel component of velocity is unchanged; the perpendicular component reverses and is multiplied by \(e\):

\(v_\perp = -e\,u_\perp, \qquad v_\| = u_\|.\)

For a ball bouncing vertically with \(e < 1\), successive heights form a geometric sequence with ratio \(e^2\).

A ball dropped from height \(h\) bounces to height \(eh^2\).
Actually, with \(e\) the coefficient of restitution on a flat floor, if dropped from \(h\), it hits with speed \(v = \sqrt{2gh}\) and leaves with speed \(ev\), reaching height \(h' = (ev)^2 / (2g) = e^2 h\). So successive heights are \(h, e^2 h, e^4 h, \ldots\).

4. Two-Dimensional Collisions (Oblique Impact)

For a ball hitting a smooth wall obliquely, resolve the velocity into components parallel and perpendicular to the wall:

  • Parallel: unchanged.
  • Perpendicular: magnitude multiplied by \(e\), direction reversed.

For collisions between two spheres, use conservation of momentum along the line of centres (with restitution) and perpendicular to it (unchanged, assuming smooth spheres).

5. Impulse as a Vector

In 2D/3D problems, impulse \(\mathbf{J} = \Delta(m\mathbf{v})\) is a vector. Write components separately.

A ball of mass 0.5 kg has velocity \((4, -3)\) m/s before impact and \((-2, -3)\) m/s after. Find the impulse.
\(\mathbf{J} = 0.5((-2, -3) - (4, -3)) = 0.5(-6, 0) = (-3, 0)\) N s.
Magnitude: \(|\mathbf{J}| = 3\) N s, directed along the negative \(x\)-axis.

6. Multi-Body Collisions

With three (or more) bodies, apply conservation of momentum and restitution to each collision in turn, using the post-first-collision velocities as inputs to the next.

7. Exam Style Strategy

  • Draw a clear diagram showing velocities before and after, with consistent sign convention.
  • Write momentum conservation and Newton's law of restitution as two equations — solve simultaneously.
  • For oblique impacts, resolve velocities perpendicular and parallel to the relevant surface/line of centres.
  • Check the answer makes physical sense — directions consistent, KE not gained.
