1.7: Polar Coordinates

1. Definition and Conversion

Polar coordinates: A point \(P\) is described by \((r, \theta)\) where:
  • \(r \geq 0\) is the distance from the origin (pole)
  • \(\theta\) is the angle measured anticlockwise from the initial line (positive \(x\)-axis)
Conversion formulae: $$x = r\cos\theta, \quad y = r\sin\theta$$ $$r^2 = x^2 + y^2, \quad \tan\theta = \frac{y}{x}$$

3. Sketching Polar Curves

Method:

  1. Find where \(r = 0\) (curve passes through pole)
  2. Find maximum and minimum values of \(r\)
  3. Check symmetry: curve is symmetric about initial line if \(r(-\theta) = r(\theta)\); about \(\theta = \pi/2\) if \(r(\pi-\theta)=r(\theta)\)
  4. Build a table of values for key angles: \(0, \pi/6, \pi/4, \pi/3, \pi/2, \pi, \ldots\)
  5. Sketch, noting the direction of tracing as \(\theta\) increases
AQA Exam Tip: AQA mark schemes for sketching award marks for: shape (1), correct intercepts on initial line (1), correct behaviour at pole/origin (1), and any labelled maximum values. Always label the maximum value of \(r\) on your sketch.

4. Area in Polar Coordinates

Area swept out by the radius vector: $$A = \frac{1}{2}\int_{\alpha}^{\beta} r^2\,d\theta$$ (the area of the sector from angle \(\alpha\) to \(\beta\)).

Area between two polar curves: $$A = \frac{1}{2}\int_{\alpha}^{\beta} (r_1^2 - r_2^2)\,d\theta$$ where \(r_1 > r_2\) in \([\alpha, \beta]\).
Example: Find the total area enclosed by the cardioid \(r = a(1+\cos\theta)\).

The cardioid is traced out for \(\theta \in [0, 2\pi]\), but using symmetry about the initial line:
\(A = 2 \times \dfrac{1}{2}\int_0^{\pi} a^2(1+\cos\theta)^2\,d\theta = a^2\int_0^{\pi}(1+2\cos\theta+\cos^2\theta)\,d\theta\)

Using \(\cos^2\theta = \dfrac{1+\cos2\theta}{2}\):
\(= a^2\int_0^{\pi}\left(\dfrac{3}{2} + 2\cos\theta + \dfrac{\cos2\theta}{2}\right)d\theta\)
\(= a^2\left[\dfrac{3\theta}{2} + 2\sin\theta + \dfrac{\sin2\theta}{4}\right]_0^{\pi} = a^2\!\left(\dfrac{3\pi}{2}\right) = \dfrac{3\pi a^2}{2}\)
Example: Find the area of one loop of the rose curve \(r = 2\cos(2\theta)\).

One petal exists where \(r \geq 0\), e.g. \(-\pi/4 \leq \theta \leq \pi/4\):
\(A = \dfrac{1}{2}\int_{-\pi/4}^{\pi/4} 4\cos^2(2\theta)\,d\theta = 2\int_0^{\pi/4}(1+\cos4\theta)\,d\theta\)
\(= 2\left[\theta + \dfrac{\sin4\theta}{4}\right]_0^{\pi/4} = 2\left(\dfrac{\pi}{4} + 0\right) = \dfrac{\pi}{2}\)
AQA Exam Tip: Always identify the correct limits carefully. For a cardioid or lima\c{c}on, the full curve is \([0, 2\pi]\). For one loop of a rose, find where \(r = 0\) to determine limits. Write down your limits explicitly — AQA marks this.

5. Tangents to Polar Curves

To find \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\) for a polar curve:

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy/d\theta}{dx/d\theta} \quad \text{where } x = r\cos\theta,\ y = r\sin\theta$$
$$\frac{dy}{d\theta} = \frac{dr}{d\theta}\sin\theta + r\cos\theta, \qquad \frac{dx}{d\theta} = \frac{dr}{d\theta}\cos\theta - r\sin\theta$$
Example: Find the tangent at the pole for \(r = a(1+\cos\theta)\).

At the pole, \(r = 0 \Rightarrow 1 + \cos\theta = 0 \Rightarrow \theta = \pi\).

\(\dfrac{dr}{d\theta} = -a\sin\theta\)
At \(\theta = \pi\): \(\dfrac{dy}{d\theta} = (-a\sin\pi)(\sin\pi) + 0\cdot\cos\pi = 0\)
\(\dfrac{dx}{d\theta} = (-a\sin\pi)(\cos\pi) - 0 = 0\)

Indeterminate — use L'Hopital or note the tangent direction is \(\theta = \pi\), i.e. horizontal.

Tangents at the Pole

The curve \(r=f(\theta)\) passes through the pole when \(f(\theta_0)=0\). The tangent at the pole has direction \(\theta=\theta_0\) (i.e.\ slope \(\tan\theta_0\)).

Example: Find the tangent lines at the pole for \(r=\cos 2\theta\). At the pole: \(\cos 2\theta=0 \Rightarrow \theta = \pi/4, 3\pi/4, 5\pi/4, 7\pi/4\). Tangent lines: \(y=x\), \(y=-x\), \(y=x\), \(y=-x\) (i.e.\ two distinct tangents \(y=x\) and \(y=-x\)).

Area Between Two Polar Curves

The area between \(r=f(\theta)\) (outer) and \(r=g(\theta)\) (inner) for \(\alpha\leq\theta\leq\beta\) is:

$$A = \frac{1}{2}\int_\alpha^\beta \left[f(\theta)^2 - g(\theta)^2\right]d\theta$$
Example: Find the area inside \(r=2\) but outside \(r=1+\cos\theta\). Intersections: \(1+\cos\theta=2 \Rightarrow \cos\theta=1 \Rightarrow \theta=0\). Also \(r=1+\cos\theta=0\) at \(\theta=\pi\). For \(\theta\in(0,\pi)\), the outer curve is \(r=2\) for \(\theta\in(0,\pi/2)\) and inner is \(r=1+\cos\theta\)... In fact: \(1+\cos\theta\leq2\) always since \(\cos\theta\leq1\), so \(r=2\) is always outer (except touching at \(\theta=0\)). $$A = \frac{1}{2}\int_0^{2\pi}(4-(1+\cos\theta)^2)d\theta = \frac{1}{2}\int_0^{2\pi}(3-2\cos\theta-\cos^2\theta)d\theta$$ $$= \frac{1}{2}\left[3(2\pi)-0-\pi\right] = \frac{1}{2}(5\pi) = \frac{5\pi}{2}$$

AQA Exam Technique — Polar Coordinates

Key tips:
  • Sketching: Always calculate \(r\) at \(\theta=0, \pi/2, \pi, 3\pi/2\) and mark these on the diagram. Note when \(r=0\) (pole).
  • Area formula: \(A=\frac{1}{2}\int r^2\,d\theta\). The limits must cover one complete loop. For a rose \(r=\cos n\theta\), one petal spans \(\pi/n\) radians.
  • Gradient: Use \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}=\dfrac{dy/d\theta}{dx/d\theta}\) where \(x=r\cos\theta\), \(y=r\sin\theta\). Differentiate fully.
  • Conversion: To convert \(r=f(\theta)\) to Cartesian, multiply both sides by \(r\) (to replace \(r\cos\theta=x\), \(r\sin\theta=y\), \(r^2=x^2+y^2\)).
  • Between curves: Find intersections carefully — also check whether the origin (pole) is a common point.

Polar to Cartesian Conversions — Worked Examples

Conversion formulae: \(x = r\cos\theta,\; y = r\sin\theta,\; r^2=x^2+y^2,\; \tan\theta = y/x\).
Example 1: Convert \(r = 3\cos\theta\) to Cartesian.
Multiply both sides by \(r\): \(r^2 = 3r\cos\theta \Rightarrow x^2+y^2 = 3x \Rightarrow \left(x-\tfrac{3}{2}\right)^2+y^2 = \tfrac{9}{4}\).
This is a circle with centre \(\left(\tfrac{3}{2},0\right)\) and radius \(\tfrac{3}{2}\).
Example 2: Convert \(r = \dfrac{2}{1-\cos\theta}\) to Cartesian.
\(r(1-\cos\theta)=2 \Rightarrow r - r\cos\theta = 2 \Rightarrow \sqrt{x^2+y^2} - x = 2\).
\(\sqrt{x^2+y^2} = x+2 \Rightarrow x^2+y^2=(x+2)^2 = x^2+4x+4 \Rightarrow y^2=4x+4\).
Example 3: Convert Cartesian \(x^2+y^2=4\) to polar.
\(r^2=4 \Rightarrow r=2\) (circle of radius 2 centred at origin).

Polar Curves — Further Examples

Spiral of Archimedes: \(r = a\theta\) — radius increases linearly with angle.
Reciprocal spiral: \(r = a/\theta\) — radius decreases as angle increases.
Conic sections in polar: \(r = \dfrac{l}{1+e\cos\theta}\) where \(l\) is semi-latus rectum, \(e\) eccentricity. Circle: \(e=0\); ellipse: \(01\).
Example — Sketching \(r = 1+2\cos\theta\) (limaçon with inner loop):
At \(\theta=0\): \(r=3\). At \(\theta=\pi/2\): \(r=1\). At \(\theta=2\pi/3\): \(r=0\) (pole).
For \(2\pi/3 < \theta < 4\pi/3\): \(r < 0\) — this traces an inner loop.
Key feature: inner loop occurs when \(r=0\), i.e. \(\cos\theta = -1/2\).

Area in Polar Coordinates — Full Examples

Example 1 — Area of one loop of \(r = \cos 2\theta\):
One loop: \(-\pi/4 \le \theta \le \pi/4\) (where \(\cos 2\theta \ge 0\)).
\(A = \dfrac{1}{2}\displaystyle\int_{-\pi/4}^{\pi/4}\cos^2 2\theta\,d\theta = \dfrac{1}{2}\int_{-\pi/4}^{\pi/4}\dfrac{1+\cos 4\theta}{2}\,d\theta\)
\(= \dfrac{1}{4}\left[\theta+\dfrac{\sin 4\theta}{4}\right]_{-\pi/4}^{\pi/4} = \dfrac{1}{4}\cdot\dfrac{\pi}{2} = \dfrac{\pi}{8}\)
Example 2 — Area enclosed by \(r = 2+2\cos\theta\) (cardioid):
Full loop: \(\theta\) from \(0\) to \(2\pi\).
\(A = \dfrac{1}{2}\displaystyle\int_0^{2\pi}(2+2\cos\theta)^2\,d\theta = \dfrac{1}{2}\int_0^{2\pi}4(1+\cos\theta)^2\,d\theta\)
\(= 2\displaystyle\int_0^{2\pi}(1+2\cos\theta+\cos^2\theta)\,d\theta = 2\int_0^{2\pi}\left(\tfrac{3}{2}+2\cos\theta+\tfrac{\cos 2\theta}{2}\right)d\theta\)
\(= 2\left[\dfrac{3\theta}{2}+2\sin\theta+\dfrac{\sin 2\theta}{4}\right]_0^{2\pi} = 2\cdot 3\pi = 6\pi\)
Example 3 — Area between two curves \(r=1\) and \(r=1+\cos\theta\):
They intersect where \(1=1+\cos\theta \Rightarrow \cos\theta=0 \Rightarrow \theta=\pm\pi/2\).
Area between curves (upper half, doubled): \(A = 2\cdot\dfrac{1}{2}\displaystyle\int_0^{\pi/2}\left[(1+\cos\theta)^2-1\right]d\theta\)
\(= \displaystyle\int_0^{\pi/2}(2\cos\theta+\cos^2\theta)\,d\theta = \left[2\sin\theta+\dfrac{\theta}{2}+\dfrac{\sin 2\theta}{4}\right]_0^{\pi/2} = 2+\dfrac{\pi}{4}\)

Tangents to Polar Curves — Full Examples

\(x = r\cos\theta,\; y = r\sin\theta\).
\(\dfrac{dy}{d\theta} = \dfrac{dr}{d\theta}\sin\theta + r\cos\theta,\qquad \dfrac{dx}{d\theta} = \dfrac{dr}{d\theta}\cos\theta - r\sin\theta\)
Gradient: \(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{dy/d\theta}{dx/d\theta}\). Horizontal tangent: \(dy/d\theta=0\). Vertical tangent: \(dx/d\theta=0\).
Example — Tangents to \(r = 1+\cos\theta\) at the pole:
At the pole, \(r=0 \Rightarrow 1+\cos\theta=0 \Rightarrow \theta=\pi\).
Tangent at pole is the line \(\theta=\pi\), i.e. the negative x-axis.
Example — Horizontal tangent to \(r = 1+\cos\theta\):
\(\dfrac{dy}{d\theta} = -\sin\theta\cdot\sin\theta+(1+\cos\theta)\cos\theta = \cos\theta+\cos^2\theta-\sin^2\theta = 2\cos^2\theta+\cos\theta-1\).
Set to zero: \((2\cos\theta-1)(\cos\theta+1)=0 \Rightarrow \cos\theta=\tfrac{1}{2}\) or \(\cos\theta=-1\).
At \(\theta=\pi/3\): \(r=\tfrac{3}{2}\), point \(\left(\tfrac{3}{4},\tfrac{3\sqrt{3}}{4}\right)\). Horizontal tangent here.

Symmetry of Polar Curves

SymmetryCondition
About initial line (\(x\)-axis)\(r(-\theta)=r(\theta)\), i.e. \(r\) is even function of \(\theta\)
About \(\theta=\pi/2\) (\(y\)-axis)\(r(\pi-\theta)=r(\theta)\)
About pole (origin)\(r(\pi+\theta)=r(\theta)\) or \(r(-\theta)=-r(\theta)\)
Example: \(r=\cos 2\theta\) is symmetric about both axes (since \(\cos(-2\theta)=\cos 2\theta\) and \(\cos(2(\pi-\theta))=\cos(2\pi-2\theta)=\cos 2\theta\)). So it suffices to sketch one quadrant and reflect.

Arc Length in Polar Coordinates

Arc length of polar curve \(r=f(\theta)\) from \(\alpha\) to \(\beta\): \[L = \int_\alpha^\beta \sqrt{r^2 + \left(\dfrac{dr}{d\theta}\right)^2}\,d\theta\]
Example — Arc length of cardioid \(r = a(1+\cos\theta)\):
\(\dfrac{dr}{d\theta} = -a\sin\theta\).
\(r^2+\left(\dfrac{dr}{d\theta}\right)^2 = a^2(1+\cos\theta)^2 + a^2\sin^2\theta = a^2(1+2\cos\theta+\cos^2\theta+\sin^2\theta) = a^2(2+2\cos\theta)\).
Using \(2+2\cos\theta = 4\cos^2(\theta/2)\):
\(L = \displaystyle\int_0^{2\pi}a\cdot 2|\cos(\theta/2)|\,d\theta = 4a\int_0^{\pi}\cos u\,du\cdot 2 = 8a\).

Finding Areas Using Symmetry

Example — Area of lemniscate \(r^2 = 4\cos 2\theta\):
Exists when \(\cos 2\theta \ge 0\), i.e. \(-\pi/4 \le \theta \le \pi/4\) and \(3\pi/4 \le \theta \le 5\pi/4\).
By symmetry, total area = 4 × (area for \(0 \le \theta \le \pi/4\)):
\(A = 4\cdot\dfrac{1}{2}\displaystyle\int_0^{\pi/4}4\cos 2\theta\,d\theta = 8\left[\dfrac{\sin 2\theta}{2}\right]_0^{\pi/4} = 8\cdot\dfrac{1}{2} = 4\)
Example — Area inside \(r=3\cos\theta\) but outside \(r=1+\cos\theta\):
Find intersections: \(3\cos\theta = 1+\cos\theta \Rightarrow 2\cos\theta=1 \Rightarrow \theta=\pm\pi/3\).
\(A = \dfrac{1}{2}\displaystyle\int_{-\pi/3}^{\pi/3}\left[(3\cos\theta)^2-(1+\cos\theta)^2\right]d\theta\)
\(= \dfrac{1}{2}\int_{-\pi/3}^{\pi/3}(9\cos^2\theta - 1 - 2\cos\theta - \cos^2\theta)\,d\theta\)
\(= \dfrac{1}{2}\int_{-\pi/3}^{\pi/3}(8\cos^2\theta - 2\cos\theta - 1)\,d\theta\)
\(= \dfrac{1}{2}\int_{-\pi/3}^{\pi/3}(4+4\cos 2\theta - 2\cos\theta - 1)\,d\theta\)
\(= \dfrac{1}{2}\left[3\theta+2\sin 2\theta-2\sin\theta\right]_{-\pi/3}^{\pi/3} = \dfrac{1}{2}\left(2\pi+2\sqrt{3}-2\sqrt{3}\right) = \pi\)

Polar Curves — Detailed Sketching Technique

Step-by-step method for sketching \(r = f(\theta)\):
  1. Identify the type (cardioid, rose, limaçon, circle, etc.) from the form.
  2. Find when \(r=0\): these are the tangent lines at the pole.
  3. Find the maximum value of \(r\) and the angles where it occurs.
  4. Make a table of \(r\) at key angles: \(0, \pi/6, \pi/4, \pi/3, \pi/2, \pi, 3\pi/2\).
  5. Check symmetry to halve the work.
  6. Note: if \(r < 0\) is obtained, the curve is in the opposite direction.
Sketching \(r = 2\sin 3\theta\) (three-petal rose):
\(r=0\) when \(\sin 3\theta=0 \Rightarrow \theta=0, \pi/3, 2\pi/3, \pi\).
Maximum \(r=2\) when \(\sin 3\theta=1 \Rightarrow \theta=\pi/6, \pi/2, 5\pi/6\).
Three petals, at angles \(\pi/6, \pi/2, 5\pi/6\) from the initial line. Symmetric about \(\theta=\pi/2\).

Equations of Lines in Polar Form

  • Half-line at angle \(\alpha\): \(\theta = \alpha\)
  • Line perpendicular to initial line at distance \(a\): \(r\cos\theta = a\) (i.e. \(x=a\))
  • Line parallel to initial line at height \(b\): \(r\sin\theta = b\) (i.e. \(y=b\))
  • General line: \(r(a\cos\theta + b\sin\theta) = c\)

Complex Numbers and Polar Form — Connection

The polar form of a complex number \(z = x+iy\) is \(z = r e^{i\theta} = r(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta)\), directly using polar coordinates \((r,\theta)\).
Multiplying two complex numbers in polar form: \(r_1 e^{i\theta_1}\cdot r_2 e^{i\theta_2} = r_1 r_2 e^{i(\theta_1+\theta_2)}\) — multiply moduli, add arguments. This is the geometric origin of the polar area and rotation connection.

Worked Example — Tangent Lines at the Pole

At the pole (\(r=0\)), the tangent direction is \(\theta = \theta_0\) where \(r(\theta_0)=0\). If the curve passes through the pole at angle \(\theta_0\), the tangent line is \(\theta = \theta_0\).
Example — \(r = \sin 2\theta\):
\(r=0\) when \(\sin 2\theta = 0 \Rightarrow \theta = 0, \pi/2, \pi, 3\pi/2\).
Tangent lines at the pole: \(\theta=0\) (the initial line), \(\theta=\pi/2\), \(\theta=\pi\), \(\theta=3\pi/2\).
These are the x and y axes — the four-petal rose is tangent to the coordinate axes at the origin.

Polar Gradient — Worked Calculation

Find the gradient of \(r = 1+\cos\theta\) at \(\theta = \pi/2\):
\(x = r\cos\theta = (1+\cos\theta)\cos\theta = \cos\theta+\cos^2\theta\).
\(y = r\sin\theta = (1+\cos\theta)\sin\theta = \sin\theta+\sin\theta\cos\theta\).
\(\dfrac{dx}{d\theta} = -\sin\theta - 2\sin\theta\cos\theta = -\sin\theta(1+2\cos\theta)\).
\(\dfrac{dy}{d\theta} = \cos\theta + \cos^2\theta - \sin^2\theta = \cos\theta+\cos 2\theta\).
At \(\theta=\pi/2\): \(\dfrac{dy}{d\theta} = 0 + \cos\pi = -1\). \(\dfrac{dx}{d\theta} = -1\cdot(1+0)=-1\).
Gradient \(= \dfrac{-1}{-1} = 1\).

Converting Between Polar and Parametric Forms

Polar curve \(r=f(\theta)\) can be expressed in parametric form as: \[x(\theta) = f(\theta)\cos\theta,\qquad y(\theta) = f(\theta)\sin\theta\] This allows standard calculus techniques (area, arc length) to be applied directly.
Example: Express \(r = 2\cos\theta\) in parametric form and verify it is a circle.
\(x = 2\cos^2\theta,\; y = 2\cos\theta\sin\theta = \sin 2\theta\).
Alternatively, from Cartesian form: \(r=2\cos\theta \Rightarrow x^2+y^2=2x \Rightarrow (x-1)^2+y^2=1\). Circle with centre \((1,0)\), radius \(1\). ✓

Polar Coordinates — Common Errors

  • Wrong limits: For one petal of \(r=\cos n\theta\), use the range where \(r\ge 0\) — from where \(r=0\) to the next zero. Do NOT use \(0\) to \(2\pi\) for a single loop.
  • Missing factor of \(\frac{1}{2}\): The area formula is \(\frac{1}{2}\int r^2\,d\theta\), not \(\int r^2\,d\theta\).
  • Negative \(r\): When \(r < 0\), the point is in the opposite direction. This traces a second copy — include it only if it produces a distinct curve.
  • Cartesian conversion: Multiply \(r=f(\theta)\) through by \(r\) before substituting \(r^2=x^2+y^2\) and \(r\cos\theta=x\).
  • Intersections: Polar intersections may not be visible from solving \(r_1=r_2\) — the pole may be a common point reached at different \(\theta\) values.

Polar Coordinates — Further Worked Examples

Example — Show that \(r = 2a\sin\theta\) is a circle and find its radius:
Multiply by \(r\): \(r^2 = 2ar\sin\theta \Rightarrow x^2+y^2 = 2ay \Rightarrow x^2+(y-a)^2 = a^2\).
Circle with centre \((0,a)\) and radius \(|a|\).
Example — Find points where \(r = 4\cos\theta\) and \(r = 4\sin\theta\) intersect:
Setting equal: \(\cos\theta = \sin\theta \Rightarrow \tan\theta = 1 \Rightarrow \theta = \pi/4\).
At \(\theta=\pi/4\): \(r = 4\cos(\pi/4) = 2\sqrt{2}\). Cartesian: \((\sqrt{2}\cdot 2\sqrt{2}/\sqrt{2}, \ldots) = (2,2)\).
Also check the pole: \(r=0\) for \(r=4\cos\theta\) when \(\theta=\pi/2\); for \(r=4\sin\theta\) when \(\theta=0\). So the pole is also a common point.
Example — Volume of revolution using polar form:
The curve \(r=a\) (circle) rotated about initial line gives sphere. Volume:
\(V = \pi\displaystyle\int_{-a}^{a}(a^2-x^2)\,dx = \pi\left[a^2x - \dfrac{x^3}{3}\right]_{-a}^{a} = \dfrac{4\pi a^3}{3}\) (standard result).

  • Cardioid \(r=a(1+\cos\theta)\): total area \(=\dfrac{3\pi a^2}{2}\), total arc length \(=8a\).
  • Circle \(r=2a\cos\theta\): area \(=\pi a^2\) (as expected for radius \(a\)).
  • Rose \(r=a\cos n\theta\): area of each petal \(=\dfrac{\pi a^2}{4n}\); total area \(=\dfrac{\pi a^2}{4}\) (all petals combined, regardless of \(n\)).
  • Lemniscate \(r^2=a^2\cos 2\theta\): total area \(=a^2\).
  • At the pole: the tangent direction \(\theta=\theta_0\) where \(r(\theta_0)=0\).
  • Cartesian gradient: \(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{r'+r\cos\theta\cdot(1/\sin\theta)}{r'-r\sin\theta\cdot(1/\cos\theta)}\) — or better, compute \(dy/d\theta\) and \(dx/d\theta\) separately.
  • When converting \(r=f(\theta)\) to Cartesian, if you cannot simplify the expression, leave it in the form \(r^2 = \ldots\) with \(r^2=x^2+y^2\) substituted — this is acceptable in AQA answers.
  • The formula sheet gives the area formula \(\tfrac{1}{2}\int_\alpha^\beta r^2\,d\theta\) — always double-check your limits are in radians and that they describe exactly one complete region.
  • For AQA proof questions involving polar curves: begin from \(x=r\cos\theta,\;y=r\sin\theta\) and differentiate, showing all intermediate steps for full marks.
