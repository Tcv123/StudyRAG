M6: Projectiles

1. Key Principle: Independence of Motion

In projectile motion, the horizontal and vertical components of motion are completely independent:

  • Horizontal: constant velocity (no air resistance assumed)
  • Vertical: constant downward acceleration \(g = 9.8\ \text{m/s}^2\)
This means you apply the constant acceleration (suvat) equations separately to each direction.

2. Setting Up — Initial Velocity Components

A projectile is launched with speed \(u\) at angle \(\theta\) above the horizontal:

$$u_x = u\cos\theta \qquad u_y = u\sin\theta$$

Standard axes: x positive to the right (horizontal), y positive upward (vertical).

Projectile Path Diagram

Projectile parabola: initial angle θ, range R, maximum height H. At peak, vertical velocity = 0, only horizontal velocity \(u\cos\theta\) remains.

3. Equations of Motion

Horizontal (x)Vertical (y)
Acceleration\(a_x = 0\)\(a_y = -g = -9.8\ \text{m/s}^2\)
Initial velocity\(u_x = u\cos\theta\)\(u_y = u\sin\theta\)
Velocity at time t\(v_x = u\cos\theta\)\(v_y = u\sin\theta - gt\)
Position at time t\(x = u\cos\theta \cdot t\)\(y = u\sin\theta \cdot t - \tfrac{1}{2}gt^2\)

(Taking the launch point as the origin, upward positive.)

4. Key Results

Time of Flight

Set \(y = 0\) (return to launch height) and solve for \(t\):

$$u\sin\theta \cdot t - \tfrac{1}{2}gt^2 = 0 \implies t(u\sin\theta - \tfrac{1}{2}gt) = 0$$ $$T = \frac{2u\sin\theta}{g}$$

Discard \(t = 0\) (launch instant). The non-zero solution is the time of flight \(T\).

Maximum Height

At maximum height, vertical velocity \(v_y = 0\):

$$t_{max} = \frac{u\sin\theta}{g} \qquad H = \frac{u^2\sin^2\theta}{2g}$$

Range

Horizontal distance when \(y = 0\) (substitute \(T\) into \(x\)):

$$R = \frac{u^2\sin 2\theta}{g}$$

Maximum range occurs when \(\sin 2\theta = 1\), i.e., \(\theta = 45°\): \(R_{max} = \dfrac{u^2}{g}\)

Symmetry: The same range \(R\) is achieved by two complementary angles, \(\theta\) and \((90° - \theta)\). For example, 30° and 60° give the same range.

5. Trajectory Equation (Eliminating t)

From horizontal: \(t = \dfrac{x}{u\cos\theta}\). Substitute into the vertical equation:

$$y = x\tan\theta - \frac{gx^2}{2u^2\cos^2\theta}$$

This is the equation of the parabolic path. It is useful for questions giving a point on the trajectory (e.g., a specific \((x,y)\) coordinate) and asking you to find \(u\), \(\theta\), or whether the projectile clears an obstacle.

6. Projectile from a Height

When launched from a height \(h\) above the ground (e.g., from a cliff):

  • Set origin at launch point. Ground is at \(y = -h\).
  • Set \(y = -h\) in \(y = u\sin\theta \cdot t - \tfrac{1}{2}gt^2\) to find time of landing.
  • Range = \(x = u\cos\theta \cdot t\) using the landing time.
Example: A ball is thrown from a cliff top 45 m high, with speed 20 m/s at 30° above horizontal. Find (a) the time of flight, (b) the horizontal range.

\(u_x = 20\cos30° = 17.32\ \text{m/s}\), \(u_y = 20\sin30° = 10\ \text{m/s}\)
Taking upward as positive, ground at \(y = -45\):
\(-45 = 10t - \tfrac{1}{2}(9.8)t^2\)
\(4.9t^2 - 10t - 45 = 0\)
\(t = \dfrac{10 \pm \sqrt{100 + 4(4.9)(45)}}{9.8} = \dfrac{10 \pm \sqrt{982}}{9.8}\)
Taking positive root: \(t = \dfrac{10 + 31.34}{9.8} = 4.22\ \text{s}\)
Range: \(x = 17.32 \times 4.22 = 73.1\ \text{m}\)

7. Speed and Direction at Any Point

At time \(t\):

  • Speed: \(v = \sqrt{v_x^2 + v_y^2} = \sqrt{(u\cos\theta)^2 + (u\sin\theta - gt)^2}\)
  • Direction (angle to horizontal): \(\phi = \arctan\!\left(\dfrac{v_y}{v_x}\right)\) — negative means below horizontal

Common Errors:
  • Using \(g = 9.8\) but forgetting to halve it in \(\frac{1}{2}gt^2\).
  • Confusing the direction of \(g\): it acts downward, so in the vertical equation with upward positive it is \(-g\), not \(+g\).
  • Discarding both roots when solving \(y = 0\): always keep the non-zero positive \(t\).
  • Using suvat in the horizontal direction with \(a = g\) — horizontal acceleration is zero (no air resistance).
  • For projectile from a height, always set \(y = -h\) (below launch point), not \(y = 0\).
