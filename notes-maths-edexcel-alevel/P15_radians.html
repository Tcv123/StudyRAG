P15: Radians

1. Definition of a Radian

1 radian is the angle subtended at the centre of a circle when the arc length equals the radius. $$\pi \text{ radians} = 180°$$

When arc length = radius, the angle at centre = 1 radian

2. Converting Between Degrees and Radians

$$\text{Degrees} \to \text{Radians}: \times \frac{\pi}{180}$$
$$\text{Radians} \to \text{Degrees}: \times \frac{180}{\pi}$$

3. Arc Length and Sector Area

$$\text{Arc length: } l = r\theta$$
$$\text{Sector area: } A = \frac{1}{2}r^2\theta$$
$$\text{Segment area: } A = \frac{1}{2}r^2(\theta - \sin\theta)$$
Segment area = Area of sector − Area of triangle
$$A_{\text{segment}} = \frac{1}{2}r^2\theta - \frac{1}{2}r^2\sin\theta = \frac{1}{2}r^2(\theta - \sin\theta)$$

Perimeter of Sector and Segment

  • Perimeter of sector = arc length + 2 radii = \(r\theta + 2r\)
  • Perimeter of segment = arc length + chord length = \(r\theta + 2r\sin\!\left(\dfrac{\theta}{2}\right)\)
Example: A sector has radius 8 cm and angle \(\dfrac{\pi}{3}\) rad. Find its arc length, area, and perimeter.

Arc length: \(l = 8 \times \dfrac{\pi}{3} = \dfrac{8\pi}{3}\) cm
Sector area: \(A = \dfrac{1}{2}(8)^2 \times \dfrac{\pi}{3} = \dfrac{32\pi}{3}\) cm²
Perimeter: \(\dfrac{8\pi}{3} + 2(8) = \dfrac{8\pi}{3} + 16\) cm
Exam Tip: All arc length and area formulas require \(\theta\) to be in radians. If the angle is given in degrees, convert first.

4. Small Angle Approximations

For small \(\theta\) (in radians): $$\sin\theta \approx \theta \qquad \cos\theta \approx 1 - \frac{\theta^2}{2} \qquad \tan\theta \approx \theta$$

These approximations become more accurate as \(\theta \to 0\). They are derived from the Maclaurin series (not required to derive in exam, but should be able to apply).

Example: Show that for small \(\theta\), \(\dfrac{\sin 3\theta}{1 - \cos 2\theta} \approx \dfrac{3}{\theta}\).

Numerator: \(\sin 3\theta \approx 3\theta\)
Denominator: \(1 - \cos 2\theta \approx 1 - \left(1 - \dfrac{(2\theta)^2}{2}\right) = 2\theta^2\)
Ratio: \(\dfrac{3\theta}{2\theta^2} = \dfrac{3}{2\theta}\)

Note: This gives \(\dfrac{3}{2\theta}\), not \(\dfrac{3}{\theta}\) — always work through the algebra carefully.
Exam Tip: When using small angle approximations, substitute immediately without rearranging first. Always check which approximation applies to which trig function in the expression.
