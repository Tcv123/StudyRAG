P19: Numerical Methods

1. Locating Roots — Change of Sign Method

Change of sign principle: If \(f(a)\) and \(f(b)\) have opposite signs (i.e. \(f(a) \cdot f(b) < 0\)) and \(f\) is continuous on \([a,b]\), then by the Intermediate Value Theorem there is at least one root in \((a, b)\).

Procedure

  1. Evaluate \(f(a)\) and \(f(b)\)
  2. If signs differ, state: "There is a sign change in \([a,b]\), so by the IVT there is a root in this interval."
  3. To narrow down: evaluate \(f\) at the midpoint and repeat (bisection)
Example: Show that \(f(x) = x^3 - x - 1\) has a root between 1 and 2.

\(f(1) = 1 - 1 - 1 = -1 < 0\)
\(f(2) = 8 - 2 - 1 = 5 > 0\)
Since \(f\) is continuous and there is a sign change, there is a root in \((1, 2)\).

Limitations of the change of sign method

  • Repeated roots: \(f(x) = (x-2)^2\) touches zero but does not change sign — no sign change detected even though \(x=2\) is a root.
  • Discontinuities: If \(f\) has a vertical asymptote in \([a,b]\), a sign change may be detected even with no root (e.g. \(f(x) = \tan x\) near \(x = \pi/2\)).
  • Multiple roots in interval: The method only guarantees at least one root — there could be more (e.g. two roots, net sign unchanged).
Exam tip: Always state "sign change and \(f\) is continuous on \([a,b]\)" when justifying a root. Do not just say "one positive, one negative" — the continuity argument is required for full marks.

2. Fixed-Point Iteration

To solve \(f(x) = 0\), rearrange to the form \(x = g(x)\) and iterate:

$$x_{n+1} = g(x_n)$$ Starting from an initial estimate \(x_0\), compute \(x_1, x_2, x_3, \ldots\) until convergence.

Choosing a rearrangement

Different rearrangements of the same equation give different iterative formulae. Some converge; others diverge. A rearrangement \(x = g(x)\) converges near a root \(\alpha\) if:

$$|g'(\alpha)| < 1$$

If \(|g'(\alpha)| > 1\), the iteration diverges.

Example: Solve \(x^3 - x - 1 = 0\) using \(x_{n+1} = (x_n + 1)^{1/3}\), starting at \(x_0 = 1.3\).

\(x_1 = (1.3 + 1)^{1/3} = (2.3)^{1/3} \approx 1.3200\)
\(x_2 = (1.3200 + 1)^{1/3} \approx 1.3247\)
\(x_3 \approx 1.3252\)
Converging to root \(\approx 1.3247\).

Staircase and Cobweb Diagrams

The iteration can be visualised by plotting \(y = g(x)\) and \(y = x\). Starting from \(x_0\), draw vertical and horizontal lines alternately between the two curves.

A staircase diagram arises when \(0 < g'(\alpha) < 1\) (monotone convergence). A cobweb diagram (oscillating inward spiral) arises when \(-1 < g'(\alpha) < 0\).

3. Newton-Raphson Method

Newton-Raphson formula: $$x_{n+1} = x_n - \frac{f(x_n)}{f'(x_n)}$$ This is a special case of fixed-point iteration with \(g(x) = x - \dfrac{f(x)}{f'(x)}\). It converges much faster than basic iteration when it works.

Geometrically: at \(x_n\), draw the tangent to \(y = f(x)\). The next estimate \(x_{n+1}\) is where this tangent crosses the \(x\)-axis.

Example: Use Newton-Raphson to find the root of \(f(x) = x^3 - 2x - 5\), starting at \(x_0 = 2\).

\(f'(x) = 3x^2 - 2\)
\(x_1 = 2 - \dfrac{f(2)}{f'(2)} = 2 - \dfrac{8-4-5}{12-2} = 2 - \dfrac{-1}{10} = 2.1\)
\(f(2.1) = 9.261 - 4.2 - 5 = 0.061\)
\(x_2 = 2.1 - \dfrac{0.061}{f'(2.1)} = 2.1 - \dfrac{0.061}{11.23} \approx 2.0946\)
The root is approximately \(2.0946\).

Failure of Newton-Raphson

Newton-Raphson fails or behaves poorly when:

  • The initial estimate \(x_0\) is near a stationary point (\(f'(x_0) \approx 0\)) — the tangent is nearly horizontal and \(x_1\) is far from the root.
  • The function has oscillatory behaviour — the iterates may cycle without converging.
  • The starting point is on the wrong side of a discontinuity.
Exam tip: Questions often ask you to explain why Newton-Raphson fails for a given starting point. The answer is almost always: "The gradient \(f'(x_0)\) is very small (close to zero) because \(x_0\) is near a stationary point, so the tangent is nearly horizontal and gives a very poor next estimate."

4. Trapezium Rule (Numerical Integration)

Approximates \(\displaystyle\int_a^b f(x)\,dx\) by dividing the area into trapezoids of equal width \(h = \dfrac{b-a}{n}\):

$$\int_a^b f(x)\,dx \approx \frac{h}{2}\bigl[y_0 + 2(y_1 + y_2 + \cdots + y_{n-1}) + y_n\bigr]$$ where \(h = \dfrac{b-a}{n}\), \(y_i = f(a + ih)\).
Example: Estimate \(\displaystyle\int_0^1 e^{x^2}\,dx\) using 4 strips.

\(h = 0.25\), \(x\)-values: \(0, 0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 1\)
\(y_0 = e^0 = 1\), \(y_1 = e^{0.0625} \approx 1.0645\), \(y_2 = e^{0.25} \approx 1.2840\), \(y_3 = e^{0.5625} \approx 1.7551\), \(y_4 = e^1 \approx 2.7183\)
\(I \approx \dfrac{0.25}{2}[1 + 2(1.0645 + 1.2840 + 1.7551) + 2.7183]\) \(= 0.125[1 + 2(4.1036) + 2.7183] = 0.125 \times 11.9255 \approx 1.491\)

Overestimate or underestimate?

  • If \(f\) is convex (concave up, \(f'' > 0\)): trapezium rule overestimates
  • If \(f\) is concave (concave down, \(f'' < 0\)): trapezium rule underestimates
  • More strips = smaller \(h\) = better approximation

5. Error in Numerical Methods

To verify a root \(\alpha\) lies in an interval, show a sign change in \(f\) over that interval at the required decimal place accuracy. For example, to confirm \(\alpha = 1.324\) to 3 d.p., show \(f(1.3235) < 0\) and \(f(1.3245) > 0\) (or vice versa).
