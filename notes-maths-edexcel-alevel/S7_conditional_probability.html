Key identity: \(P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)\)

2. Conditional Probability

Conditional probability \(P(A|B)\) is the probability that event A occurs given that event B has already occurred. $$P(A|B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}, \quad \text{provided } P(B) \neq 0$$

Rearranging gives the Multiplication Rule:

$$P(A \cap B) = P(A|B) \times P(B) = P(B|A) \times P(A)$$
Example: In a class, \(P(\text{passed Maths}) = 0.7\), \(P(\text{passed both Maths and Physics}) = 0.42\). Find the probability that a student passed Physics given they passed Maths.

\(P(\text{Physics} | \text{Maths}) = \dfrac{P(\text{Physics} \cap \text{Maths})}{P(\text{Maths})} = \dfrac{0.42}{0.7} = 0.6\)

3. Independence

Events A and B are independent if the occurrence of one does not affect the probability of the other: $$P(A|B) = P(A) \quad \Leftrightarrow \quad P(A \cap B) = P(A) \times P(B)$$
Exam tip: To test independence, check whether \(P(A \cap B) = P(A) \times P(B)\). If it equals, the events are independent. Do not just look at a Venn diagram visually — always check the numbers.

4. Law of Total Probability

If B and B' are complementary events (partition the sample space):

$$P(A) = P(A|B) \cdot P(B) + P(A|B') \cdot P(B')$$

This is useful when A can occur via two mutually exclusive routes.

5. Bayes' Theorem

Bayes' theorem allows you to reverse the conditioning — find \(P(B|A)\) when you know \(P(A|B)\): $$P(B|A) = \frac{P(A|B) \cdot P(B)}{P(A)}$$ where \(P(A)\) is found using the law of total probability.
Medical Testing Example: A disease affects 1% of the population. A test has 95% sensitivity (correctly identifies disease) and 90% specificity (correctly identifies no disease). Given a positive test, find the probability the person actually has the disease.

Let D = has disease, T = positive test.
\(P(D) = 0.01\), \(P(D') = 0.99\)
\(P(T|D) = 0.95\) (sensitivity), \(P(T|D') = 0.10\) (false positive rate = 1 − specificity)

\(P(T) = P(T|D)P(D) + P(T|D')P(D')\)
\(P(T) = 0.95 \times 0.01 + 0.10 \times 0.99 = 0.0095 + 0.099 = 0.1085\)

\(P(D|T) = \dfrac{P(T|D) \cdot P(D)}{P(T)} = \dfrac{0.95 \times 0.01}{0.1085} = \dfrac{0.0095}{0.1085} \approx 0.0876\)

Despite a positive test, there is only an 8.76% chance of having the disease — due to the low base rate.

7. Tree Diagrams

Tree diagrams show sequences of events with conditional probabilities on the branches. Multiply along branches for joint probabilities; add for mutually exclusive outcomes.

Quality Control Example: A factory has two machines, M1 and M2. M1 produces 60% of items; M2 produces 40%. The defect rates are 3% (M1) and 5% (M2). Find the probability a randomly selected item is defective.

\(P(\text{defective}) = P(D|M_1)P(M_1) + P(D|M_2)P(M_2)\)
\(= 0.03 \times 0.6 + 0.05 \times 0.4 = 0.018 + 0.020 = 0.038\)

Given a defective item, find \(P(M_1|\text{defective})\):
\(P(M_1|D) = \dfrac{0.03 \times 0.6}{0.038} = \dfrac{0.018}{0.038} \approx 0.474\)
