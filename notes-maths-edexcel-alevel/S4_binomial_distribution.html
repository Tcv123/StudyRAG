S4: Statistical Distributions — Binomial

1. Conditions for a Binomial Distribution

A random variable \(X\) follows a binomial distribution \(X \sim B(n, p)\) when ALL four conditions hold:

  1. Fixed number of trials: \(n\) is known in advance.
  2. Two outcomes only: Each trial results in either a "success" (S) or "failure" (F).
  3. Constant probability: The probability of success \(p\) is the same for every trial.
  4. Independent trials: The outcome of one trial does not affect any other trial.
Exam Tip: When asked to state assumptions, write all four conditions in context. For example: "Each customer either buys or doesn't buy (two outcomes); the probability a customer buys remains constant at 0.4; purchases are independent of each other; there are a fixed 20 customers."

2. Binomial Formula

If \(X \sim B(n, p)\), then: $$P(X = r) = \binom{n}{r} p^r (1-p)^{n-r}, \quad r = 0, 1, 2, \ldots, n$$ where \(\binom{n}{r} = \frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}\) is the binomial coefficient.
Example: \(X \sim B(6, 0.4)\). Find \(P(X = 2)\).
\(P(X=2) = \binom{6}{2}(0.4)^2(0.6)^4 = 15 \times 0.16 \times 0.1296 = 0.3110\) (4 d.p.)

3. Mean, Variance and Standard Deviation

$$E(X) = np$$
$$\text{Var}(X) = np(1-p) = npq \quad \text{where } q = 1-p$$
$$\sigma = \sqrt{np(1-p)}$$
Example: \(X \sim B(20, 0.35)\)
\(E(X) = 20 \times 0.35 = 7\)
\(\text{Var}(X) = 20 \times 0.35 \times 0.65 = 4.55\)
\(\sigma = \sqrt{4.55} \approx 2.133\)

4. Cumulative Probabilities

Use your calculator's binomial CDF (cumulative distribution function) to find \(P(X \leq r)\). Key conversions:

Required ProbabilityHow to Calculate
\(P(X \leq r)\)Direct from CDF
\(P(X < r)\)\(P(X \leq r-1)\)
\(P(X \geq r)\)\(1 - P(X \leq r-1)\)
\(P(X > r)\)\(1 - P(X \leq r)\)
\(P(a \leq X \leq b)\)\(P(X \leq b) - P(X \leq a-1)\)
Exam Tip: Be very careful with strict vs non-strict inequalities. \(P(X \geq 5)\) means \(1 - P(X \leq 4)\), NOT \(1 - P(X \leq 5)\).

7. Modelling with the Binomial Distribution

Before applying \(B(n, p)\), check all four conditions hold in context. Then identify \(n\) and \(p\) from the problem.

Example: A spinner has probability 0.25 of landing on red. It is spun 15 times. Let \(X\) = number of times it lands on red.
Model: \(X \sim B(15, 0.25)\) — fixed 15 spins, two outcomes (red/not red), constant probability 0.25, spins are independent.
\(P(X \geq 5) = 1 - P(X \leq 4)\)
Using calculator: \(P(X \leq 4) = 0.6865\)
\(P(X \geq 5) = 1 - 0.6865 = 0.3135\)
Exam Tip: If sampling without replacement from a small finite population, the binomial model is not strictly appropriate (probabilities change). However, if the population is large relative to the sample, the binomial is used as an approximation and the assumption of independence is stated.
