S1: Statistical Sampling

1. Population, Census and Sample

Population: The entire group of individuals or items being studied. A population can be finite (e.g. all students in a school) or infinite (e.g. all possible tosses of a coin).
Census: A survey of the entire population. Every member is included.
Sample: A subset of the population selected for study. Findings from the sample are used to make inferences about the whole population.

2. Sampling Frames and Key Terms

Sampling frame: A complete list of all members of the population from which a sample can be drawn (e.g. an electoral register, school roll).
  • Sample unit: Each individual member of the population.
  • Representative sample: A sample that accurately reflects the characteristics of the whole population.
  • Random sample: A sample in which every member of the population has an equal chance of being selected.

3. Sampling Methods

Simple Random Sampling

Every member of the population has an equally likely chance of being selected. Requires a complete sampling frame. Each member is given a number; numbers are generated randomly (random number table or calculator).

  • Lottery method: names in a hat, draw without replacement.
  • Random number generator: assign numbers 1 to N; generate n random numbers.
Example: A school has 600 students. A simple random sample of 30 is required. Number all students 001–600 and use a random number generator to select 30 distinct numbers.

Systematic Sampling

Members are selected at regular intervals from a sampling frame. The sampling interval is:

$$k = \frac{N}{n}$$

where \(N\) = population size, \(n\) = sample size. Choose a random starting point between 1 and \(k\), then select every \(k\)th member.

Example: Population size 500, sample size 25. \(k = 500/25 = 20\). Random start between 1 and 20, say 7. Sample: 7, 27, 47, 67, …
Exam Tip: Systematic sampling requires a sampling frame. It can introduce bias if there is a periodic pattern in the list that coincides with the sampling interval.

Stratified Sampling

The population is divided into non-overlapping groups (strata) based on a shared characteristic (e.g. year group, gender). A proportional number is then selected from each stratum using simple random sampling.

$$\text{Sample from stratum} = \frac{\text{stratum size}}{N} \times n$$
Example: A school has 400 Year 12 and 200 Year 13 students. A stratified sample of 60 is needed.
Year 12: \(\frac{400}{600} \times 60 = 40\) students
Year 13: \(\frac{200}{600} \times 60 = 20\) students

Opportunity (Convenience) Sampling

The researcher samples whoever is available at the time. No sampling frame is required.

  • Advantage: Quick, cheap, easy to carry out.
  • Disadvantage: Highly likely to be biased; not representative of the whole population.

Quota Sampling

The researcher divides the population into groups and sets quotas for each. Individuals are then selected non-randomly until each quota is filled.

  • Advantage: Ensures representation of key subgroups; no sampling frame needed.
  • Disadvantage: Non-random selection within quotas introduces bias; not truly random.

5. Bias in Sampling

Bias: A systematic error that causes the sample to be unrepresentative of the population. A biased sample consistently over- or under-estimates the true value.
  • Selection bias: Certain groups are more likely to be chosen (e.g. only sampling people in a particular location).
  • Non-response bias: People who don't respond may differ systematically from those who do.
  • Question wording bias: Leading questions nudge respondents towards certain answers (e.g. "Don't you agree that…?").
  • Volunteer bias: People who choose to participate may not be representative.
Exam Tip: In context questions, identify why a sampling method is biased. For example, "standing outside a gym to survey exercise habits" would over-represent frequent exercisers.

6. Choosing and Justifying a Sampling Method

Exam questions often ask you to: (a) identify the sampling method used, or (b) suggest an appropriate method and give a reason.

  • Use stratified when the population has distinct subgroups that should be proportionally represented.
  • Use systematic when a sampling frame exists and simplicity is important.
  • Use simple random when complete fairness is needed and the population is manageable.
  • Use opportunity or quota when no sampling frame exists and speed is prioritised (acknowledge the bias).
Worked Example: A factory produces 2000 bolts per day: 800 from Machine A, 700 from Machine B, and 500 from Machine C. A quality control sample of 40 is needed. Suggest an appropriate sampling method and calculate how many from each machine.

Method: Stratified sampling, because the bolts come from different machines (subgroups) and we want proportional representation.
Machine A: \(\frac{800}{2000} \times 40 = 16\)
Machine B: \(\frac{700}{2000} \times 40 = 14\)
Machine C: \(\frac{500}{2000} \times 40 = 10\)
Total: \(16 + 14 + 10 = 40\) ✓
