M7: Further Forces — Friction and Inclines

1. The Friction Law

Friction is a force that opposes relative motion (or the tendency of motion) between two surfaces in contact.

$$F \leq \mu R$$ where \(F\) is the frictional force, \(\mu\) is the coefficient of friction, and \(R\) is the normal reaction.

At limiting equilibrium (particle about to move, or sliding): \(F = \mu R\)
When not at the point of sliding: \(F < \mu R\) (static friction, unknown value — find by resolving)
  • \(\mu\) is dimensionless: typically \(0 \leq \mu \leq 1\) for most surfaces, but can be greater than 1.
  • \(\mu = 0\) means the surface is smooth (frictionless).
  • Friction always acts to oppose the motion or tendency of motion.

2. Particle on an Inclined Plane

For a particle of mass \(m\) on a slope inclined at angle \(\alpha\) to the horizontal:

  • Resolve perpendicular to slope: \(R = mg\cos\alpha\)
  • Resolve along slope: depends on motion/equilibrium
$$R = mg\cos\alpha \qquad F_{max} = \mu R = \mu mg\cos\alpha$$

3. Particle Sliding Down a Slope

If the particle slides down the slope, friction acts up the slope.

$$\text{Along slope: } mg\sin\alpha - F = ma$$ $$\text{Perp to slope: } R - mg\cos\alpha = 0 \implies R = mg\cos\alpha$$ $$F = \mu R = \mu mg\cos\alpha$$ $$\text{So: } a = g(\sin\alpha - \mu\cos\alpha)$$

4. Particle Moving Up a Slope

If the particle moves up the slope, friction acts down the slope (opposing motion).

$$\text{Along slope (taking up as positive): } -mg\sin\alpha - F = ma$$ $$a = -g(\sin\alpha + \mu\cos\alpha)$$

The deceleration is greater when moving up than when sliding down — the friction adds to gravity on the way up and opposes it on the way down.

5. Equilibrium on a Slope

For a particle in equilibrium on a slope:

  • If the particle is on the verge of sliding down: \(mg\sin\alpha = \mu mg\cos\alpha \implies \tan\alpha = \mu\)
  • This gives the maximum angle before sliding: \(\alpha_{max}\) where \(\tan\alpha_{max} = \mu\)
Angle of Friction \(\lambda\): \(\tan\lambda = \mu\)
If the slope angle \(\alpha \leq \lambda\), the particle remains in equilibrium. If \(\alpha > \lambda\), the particle slides.

6. Particle on a Rough Horizontal Surface with a Force at an Angle

If a force \(P\) at angle \(\theta\) above horizontal is applied to a particle of mass \(m\) on a rough surface (\(\mu\)):

  • Vertically: \(R + P\sin\theta = mg \implies R = mg - P\sin\theta\)
  • Horizontally: \(P\cos\theta - F = ma\)
  • At limiting equilibrium: \(F = \mu R = \mu(mg - P\sin\theta)\)
Key: When a force has an upward component, the normal reaction R decreases, which also decreases the maximum friction. Always recalculate R after resolving vertically.

7. Connected Particles on a Slope

Two particles connected by a string over a pulley at the top of a slope:

  • Particle A (mass \(m_1\)) on the slope at angle \(\alpha\), rough (\(\mu\)).
  • Particle B (mass \(m_2\)) hanging freely.
  • If B descends (pulling A up the slope):
For A (up the slope, friction acts down): \(T - m_1 g\sin\alpha - \mu m_1 g\cos\alpha = m_1 a\)
For B (descending): \(m_2 g - T = m_2 a\)
Add: \(m_2 g - m_1 g\sin\alpha - \mu m_1 g\cos\alpha = (m_1 + m_2)a\)
$$a = \frac{m_2 g - m_1 g(\sin\alpha + \mu\cos\alpha)}{m_1 + m_2}$$

8. Worked Example — Full Solution

A 5 kg particle rests on a rough plane inclined at 30° to the horizontal. The coefficient of friction between the particle and the plane is 0.4. A force P acts up the slope. Find the minimum P to keep the particle in equilibrium.

Resolve perpendicular to slope: \(R = 5 \times 9.8 \times \cos30° = 49 \times \tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2} = 42.43\ \text{N}\)
Friction (max, acting up slope since particle tends to slide down): \(F = \mu R = 0.4 \times 42.43 = 16.97\ \text{N}\)
Resolve along slope (up positive): \(P + F - mg\sin30° = 0\)
\(P = mg\sin30° - F = 49 \times 0.5 - 16.97 = 24.5 - 16.97 = 7.53\ \text{N}\)

(If P were absent and friction insufficient: particle would slide down. The minimum P is when friction is at its maximum value acting up the slope.)

9. Key Formulae Summary

FormulaUse
\(F \leq \mu R\)Friction law (always)
\(F = \mu R\)Limiting equilibrium / sliding
\(R = mg\cos\alpha\)Normal reaction on slope angle α
\(a = g(\sin\alpha - \mu\cos\alpha)\)Acceleration down rough slope
\(\tan\alpha = \mu\)Max slope angle for equilibrium
\(\tan\lambda = \mu\)Angle of friction
Common Errors:
  • Friction direction error — friction always opposes the motion (or tendency of motion), not just "down the slope" always.
  • Using \(R = mg\) on a slope — always resolve perpendicular: \(R = mg\cos\alpha\).
  • Forgetting to update R when an angled force is applied (R changes).
  • Using \(F = \mu R\) when the particle is not at limiting equilibrium — if the particle is stationary and no information says it is about to slip, friction may be less than \(\mu R\); find F by resolving.
  • Sign error when friction reverses direction (e.g., particle pushed up vs sliding down — opposite friction directions give different accelerations).
