4.4.1 Photosynthesis

Photosynthesis is the process used by plants, algae and some bacteria to make glucose from carbon dioxide and water. Light energy is transferred to chemical energy in glucose.
Word equation:
carbon dioxide + water → glucose + oxygen (light energy)

Balanced symbol equation:
6CO₂ + 6H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂

Photosynthesis is endothermic — energy is taken in from the light. It happens in chloroplasts (containing chlorophyll), mostly in palisade mesophyll cells of leaves.

Limiting Factors

A limiting factor is the factor (in shortest supply) that controls the rate of photosynthesis at a given moment. The three main limiting factors are light intensity, CO₂ concentration and temperature. Chlorophyll amount can also limit rate.

Temperature

Enzymes control photosynthesis, so rate rises with temperature to an optimum (~25–30 °C) then falls sharply as enzymes denature above ~40–45 °C.

Inverse Square Law for Light

Light intensity ∝ 1 / d²

Doubling the distance from a lamp reduces intensity to 1/4.
Example: at 10 cm → 100 bubbles/min. At 20 cm → 100 ÷ 4 = 25 bubbles/min.

Use of Photosynthesis in Agriculture

Greenhouses control limiting factors to increase crop yield:

  • Lamps extend day length and raise light intensity.
  • Heaters raise temperature; paraffin heaters also release CO₂ from combustion.
  • CO₂ generators increase CO₂ concentration.

The farmer must weigh the cost of each measure against extra yield/profit.

4.4.2 Respiration

Respiration is the process that transfers energy from glucose to cells. It is exothermic — energy is released. Respiration occurs continuously in all living cells.

Energy released by respiration is used for:

  • Muscle contraction (movement).
  • Building larger molecules (protein synthesis).
  • Active transport (e.g. mineral uptake in roots).
  • Maintaining body temperature in mammals and birds.

Aerobic Respiration

glucose + oxygen → carbon dioxide + water (energy released)
C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O

Takes place mostly in mitochondria. Releases much more energy per glucose molecule than anaerobic respiration.

Anaerobic Respiration in Muscles

glucose → lactic acid

Occurs when oxygen cannot be delivered fast enough during intense exercise. Lactic acid builds up in muscles, contributing to fatigue.

Anaerobic Respiration in Plants and Yeast (Fermentation)

glucose → ethanol + carbon dioxide

Used in brewing (ethanol) and baking (CO₂ makes dough rise; ethanol evaporates during cooking).

4.4.3 Response to Exercise

During exercise, muscles respire more quickly:

  • Heart rate ↑ — delivers O₂ and glucose faster; removes CO₂.
  • Breathing rate ↑ and depth increase — more O₂ taken in, more CO₂ breathed out.
  • When demand exceeds supply, muscles respire anaerobically → lactic acid.

Oxygen Debt

Oxygen debt — the amount of extra oxygen needed after exercise to oxidise the accumulated lactic acid to CO₂ and water. This is why breathing and heart rate remain elevated after hard exercise.

Lactic acid is transported in the blood to the liver, where it is converted back to glucose using oxygen.

4.4.4 Metabolism

Metabolism is the sum of all the chemical reactions taking place in a cell or organism. Metabolism includes reactions that build large molecules from smaller ones and reactions that break down larger molecules.
ProcessExample
Converting glucose → starch/glycogen/celluloseEnergy storage or cell wall formation
Combining glucose + nitrate → amino acids → proteinsGrowth and repair
Converting glucose + fatty acids → lipidsStored in seeds/animal fat
Respiration (glucose → CO₂ + H₂O)Releases energy
Breakdown of excess proteinsForms urea for excretion (deamination in liver)
Remember: photosynthesis is endothermic; respiration is exothermic. Plants both photosynthesise and respire — at high light intensity they produce more O₂ than they use, giving a net release of O₂.

Summary — 4.4

  • Photosynthesis: 6CO₂ + 6H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ (endothermic).
  • Limiting factors: light, CO₂, temperature (chlorophyll too).
  • Light intensity follows inverse square law (I ∝ 1/d²).
  • Uses of glucose: respiration, starch, cellulose, amino acids, fats.
  • Aerobic respiration: much energy; anaerobic (muscle): lactic acid, little energy.
  • Yeast fermentation: glucose → ethanol + CO₂.
  • Exercise: heart rate, breathing rate, and depth increase; oxygen debt repaid afterwards.
  • Metabolism: all chemical reactions in a cell/organism.
