4.5 Homeostasis and Response

AQA GCSE Biology (8461) — Paper 2

4.5.1 Homeostasis

Homeostasis is the regulation of the internal conditions of a cell or organism to maintain optimum conditions for function, in response to internal and external change. It maintains optimum conditions for enzyme action and all cell processes.

In humans, the main conditions controlled are:

  • Body temperature (~37 °C)
  • Blood glucose concentration
  • Water & ion content (osmotic balance)

Components of a Control System

4.5.2 The Nervous System

The nervous system enables humans to react to their surroundings and coordinate behaviour. Nerve impulses travel along neurones, carrying electrical signals.

Reflex Arc

A reflex action is a rapid, automatic response that does not involve conscious thought. Reflex arcs protect the body (e.g. withdrawing from a hot object, pupil reflex).

Stimulus → receptor → sensory neurone → relay neurone (in CNS/spinal cord) → motor neurone → effector (muscle/gland) → response.

Synapses

A synapse is the gap between two neurones. An electrical impulse triggers the release of neurotransmitter chemicals, which diffuse across the gap and trigger a new impulse in the next neurone.

4.5.4 The Eye (Separate Biology)

Key parts: cornea, iris, pupil, lens, ciliary muscles, suspensory ligaments, retina, optic nerve.

Accommodation

  • Distant object: ciliary muscles relax, suspensory ligaments tight, lens thin — less refraction.
  • Near object: ciliary muscles contract, suspensory ligaments slacken, lens thick — more refraction.

Eye Defects

  • Myopia (short sight) — eyeball too long; image forms in front of retina. Corrected by concave/diverging lens.
  • Hyperopia (long sight) — eyeball too short; image forms behind retina. Corrected by convex/converging lens.

4.5.7 Control of Blood Glucose

The pancreas detects blood glucose concentration and releases:
• Insulin when blood glucose is too high (after a meal) — causes cells to take up glucose and store as glycogen.
• Glucagon when blood glucose is too low — causes liver to break down glycogen back to glucose.

4.5.8 Water & Nitrogen Balance (Separate Biology)

Water is gained from drinks, food, and respiration; lost in urine, sweat and exhaled air. Ions and urea must also be excreted.

Formation of Urea

Excess amino acids are broken down in the liver (deamination) producing toxic ammonia, which is converted to urea and transported in blood to the kidneys for excretion.

The Kidneys

Kidneys filter blood and reabsorb useful substances (glucose, selected ions, water as needed) while excreting urea and excess ions/water as urine.

ADH and Water Balance

Concentrated blood (dehydration) → osmoreceptors in hypothalamus → pituitary releases ADH → kidney collecting ducts become more permeable → more water reabsorbed → less, more concentrated urine.

IVF (Separate Biology)

Used to treat infertility:

  1. Mother given FSH and LH to stimulate egg maturation.
  2. Eggs collected and fertilised with sperm in the lab.
  3. Embryos develop for a few days.
  4. One or two embryos implanted in uterus.

Success rates are low (~30%); drugs can cause side effects; ethical concerns about unused embryos; risk of multiple births.

4.5.10 Hormones in Plants (Separate Biology)

Auxin is a hormone produced in shoot/root tips that controls growth in response to light (phototropism) and gravity (gravitropism).

  • Shoot: light from one side — auxin moves to the shaded side — cells there elongate more — shoot bends towards light (positive phototropism).
  • Root: horizontal — auxin accumulates on lower side. In roots, high auxin inhibits elongation — root bends down (positive gravitropism).

Commercial Uses of Plant Hormones

  • Auxins — selective weedkillers, rooting powders, tissue culture.
  • Gibberellins — end seed dormancy, promote flowering, make larger fruits.
  • Ethene — ripens fruit during storage and transport.
For 6-mark questions on homeostasis, always name the stimulus, receptor, coordination centre, effector and response — and explain the negative feedback loop that restores the set point.

Summary — 4.5

  • Homeostasis maintains optimum conditions (temp, glucose, water) using negative feedback.
  • Nervous system: fast, targeted. Reflex arc = sensory → relay → motor.
  • Endocrine system: slower, long-lasting. Pituitary = master gland.
  • Blood glucose: insulin (lowers, stores as glycogen) vs glucagon (raises).
  • Menstrual cycle: FSH → egg maturation; LH surge → ovulation; oestrogen & progesterone prepare lining.
  • Contraception: hormonal (pill), barrier (condom), IUD, sterilisation.
  • Plant tropisms controlled by auxin.
