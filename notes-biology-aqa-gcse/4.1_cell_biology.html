4.1.1 Cell Structure

Cells are the smallest unit of life. All living things are made of cells. Most cells are so small they can only be viewed under a microscope. Biology divides all cells into two broad groups: eukaryotic (cells with a nucleus — plants, animals, fungi, protists) and prokaryotic (cells without a true nucleus — bacteria).

Prokaryotic Cells (Bacteria)

Prokaryotes are much smaller than eukaryotic cells (0.1–5 μm vs 10–100 μm) and have simpler internal organisation.

  • No nucleus — their DNA is a single loop in the cytoplasm.
  • Plasmids — small rings of extra DNA. Often carry antibiotic resistance genes.
  • No membrane-bound organelles. Ribosomes are present.
  • Surrounded by a cell wall (not made of cellulose).
  • Some have a flagellum (tail for movement) or a slime capsule (protection).
When comparing cells, learn the three key features of eukaryotic cells: (1) a true nucleus, (2) genetic material enclosed inside it, (3) membrane-bound organelles. Prokaryotes lack all three.

Specialised Cells

During an organism's development, cells differentiate into specialised forms with specific functions. Most animal cells do this early; plant cells retain the ability to differentiate throughout life.

4.1.2 Microscopy

Light vs Electron Microscope

FeatureLight microscopeElectron microscope
SourceLightBeam of electrons

Cost & useCheap, simple, can view living cellsExpensive, complex, cannot view living cells
Magnification = how much larger the image looks.
Resolution = how far apart two points can be and still be distinguished as separate. Electron microscopes have much better resolution — they can reveal ribosomes, inner mitochondrial membranes and detailed structure.
Magnification equation:
Magnification = size of image ÷ size of real object
(keep units the same, e.g. both in μm)

Standard Form and Units

  • 1 millimetre (mm) = 1 × 10⁻³ m = 1000 μm
  • 1 micrometre (μm) = 1 × 10⁻⁶ m = 1000 nm
  • 1 nanometre (nm) = 1 × 10⁻⁹ m

Worked example: A cell has a real diameter of 20 μm. On a photograph it measures 80 mm. Calculate the magnification.

Step 1 — same units: 80 mm = 80 000 μm.
Step 2 — divide: 80 000 ÷ 20 = ×4000.

4.1.3 Cell Division by Mitosis

Mitosis is the division of a body cell to produce two genetically identical diploid daughter cells. It is used for growth, repair of damaged tissue and asexual reproduction.

Mitosis is part of the cell cycle, which has three main phases:

  1. Interphase — cell grows, organelles duplicate, DNA replicates so each chromosome has two sister chromatids.
  2. Mitosis — the nucleus divides: chromosomes line up, then sister chromatids are pulled apart to opposite poles.
  3. Cytokinesis — cytoplasm and cell membrane divide giving two daughter cells.

Binary Fission in Bacteria

Bacteria reproduce asexually by binary fission: the DNA loop is copied, the cell elongates and splits in two. In ideal conditions a bacterium can divide every 20 minutes, producing millions within a day.

Bacterial growth: number of bacteria = 2n, where n = number of divisions.
Example: 1 bacterium dividing every 20 min; after 2 h (120 min) → n = 6 divisions → 26 = 64 bacteria.

4.1.4 Stem Cells

A stem cell is an undifferentiated cell that can divide by mitosis to form more of the same type of cell and can differentiate into specialised cells.

Therapeutic Cloning

An embryo is produced with the same genes as the patient, so stem cells obtained from it would not be rejected by the immune system. Advantages: personalised treatment, no rejection. Disadvantages: ethical concerns (destroying embryos), risk of tumours, high cost.

AQA expects balanced evaluation of stem cell use. Learn one benefit (e.g. paralysis, type 1 diabetes) and one objection (destruction of embryos / religious/ethical views) — you must mention both for full marks in a "discuss" question.

4.1.5 Transport In and Out of Cells

Diffusion

Diffusion — the net movement of particles from a region of higher concentration to a region of lower concentration, down a concentration gradient. It is passive (no energy required).

Factors affecting diffusion rate: steeper concentration gradient, higher temperature, larger surface area, thinner membrane.

Osmosis

Osmosis — the net movement of water across a partially permeable membrane from a more dilute solution (higher water concentration) to a more concentrated solution (lower water concentration).

Active Transport

Active transport moves substances against their concentration gradient (low → high). It requires energy from respiration (ATP) and uses carrier proteins in the membrane.

Two examples:

  • Root hair cells absorb mineral ions from the soil even when ion concentration is higher inside the cell than out.
  • Small intestine absorbs glucose into the blood when blood glucose is already higher.

Key Definitions — 4.1 Summary

  • Diffusion — net movement of particles down a concentration gradient.
  • Osmosis — diffusion of water across a partially permeable membrane.
  • Active transport — movement against a gradient, requires ATP.
  • Mitosis — cell division producing two identical diploid cells.
  • Differentiation — how cells become specialised during development.
  • Stem cell — undifferentiated cell capable of becoming different cell types.
  • Magnification = image size ÷ real size.
