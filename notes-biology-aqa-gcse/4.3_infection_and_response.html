4.3.1 Communicable Diseases

Pathogens are microorganisms that cause communicable (infectious) diseases — diseases that can be passed between individuals. Pathogens reproduce rapidly inside the host. Four main types: bacteria, viruses, fungi, protists.
TypeHow it causes diseaseExample
BacteriaReproduce rapidly; some produce toxins that damage cellsSalmonella, TB, gonorrhoea
VirusEnter host cells, take over machinery to make copies, then burst (lyse) the cellMeasles, HIV, influenza, TMV
FungusGrow on skin/surfaces; produce sporesRose black spot, athlete's foot
ProtistSingle-celled eukaryotes; often spread by vectorsMalaria (Plasmodium)

Ways Pathogens Spread

  • Direct contact (skin/fluids) — HIV, athlete's foot.
  • Air (droplets) — flu, measles (coughs/sneezes).
  • Water or food — cholera, salmonella.
  • Vectors (other organisms) — mosquitoes carry malaria parasite.

4.3.2 Human Defences Against Pathogens

Non-Specific Defences (First Line)

  • Skin — physical barrier; produces antimicrobial secretions; clots seal wounds.
  • Nose — hairs and mucus trap pathogens.
  • Trachea & bronchi — mucus traps particles; cilia sweep them up to the throat.
  • Stomach — hydrochloric acid kills most pathogens in swallowed mucus/food.

White Blood Cells (Second Line)

Immunity and Vaccination

Vaccination introduces a small amount of dead/inactive/weakened pathogen (or its antigens) into the body. White blood cells produce antibodies and memory lymphocytes which remain. On later exposure, the secondary immune response is rapid and large — destroying the pathogen before symptoms develop.

Herd Immunity

If a high proportion of a population is vaccinated, the pathogen cannot spread easily. Those who cannot be vaccinated (young babies, immunocompromised) are indirectly protected.

4.3.3 Antibiotics and Painkillers

  • Antibiotics kill bacteria inside the body (e.g. penicillin). They do not kill viruses because viruses live and reproduce inside host cells.
  • Painkillers (aspirin, paracetamol) treat symptoms only — they do not kill pathogens.

Antibiotic Resistance

Random mutations sometimes give bacteria resistance. When antibiotics are used, non-resistant bacteria die and resistant ones survive and reproduce — natural selection. Resistance spreads as the bacteria divide and via plasmids.

Reducing resistance:

  • Only prescribe antibiotics when needed.
  • Patients must complete the full course.
  • Limit use in farming.
  • Developing new antibiotics — slow and expensive.

Stages of Drug Testing

  1. Preclinical — tested on cells, tissues and animals for efficacy & toxicity.
  2. Phase 1 — small group of healthy volunteers, low dose — safety.
  3. Phase 2 — larger group of patients — effectiveness & optimal dose.
  4. Phase 3 — large scale double-blind, randomised, placebo-controlled trial.
  5. Phase 4 — monitoring after approval for rare/long-term effects.
Placebo — dummy treatment; controls for the placebo effect.
Double-blind — neither the doctor nor the patient knows which is being given; controls bias.
Randomised — patients assigned to groups randomly.

4.3.5 Monoclonal Antibodies (Separate Biology)

Monoclonal antibodies are identical antibodies produced from a single clone of lymphocytes (hybridoma cells), targeting one specific antigen.

How They Are Made

  1. Mouse is injected with the antigen → mouse produces lymphocytes with antibodies.
  2. Lymphocytes are collected and fused with tumour (myeloma) cells — this creates hybridoma cells, which divide rapidly and produce antibodies.
  3. Hybridomas are screened; selected cells are cloned.
  4. Cultured in large numbers to produce large quantities of antibodies.

Uses

  • Pregnancy tests — detect the hormone hCG in urine.
  • Diagnosing disease — detecting specific molecules in blood or tissue.
  • Treating cancer — antibodies target antigens on cancer cells; may carry drugs, radiation or block growth signals.
  • Research — locating particular molecules in cells.
Monoclonal antibodies have more side effects than originally expected, are expensive to produce, and are produced in mice — raising ethical/allergy issues. They are still not as widely used in treatment as once hoped. Mention all of these when evaluating.

4.3.6 Plant Diseases and Defences (Separate Biology)

Detecting Plant Disease

  • Visual symptoms: stunted growth, spots, discolouration, decay, abnormal growths, malformed stems/leaves, presence of pests.
  • Laboratory tests: DNA analysis; monoclonal antibodies.
  • Gardening manuals, websites, testing kits.

Plant Mineral Deficiencies

  • Nitrate deficiency → stunted growth (no amino acids → no proteins).
  • Magnesium deficiency → chlorosis (yellow leaves; no chlorophyll).

Summary — 4.3

  • Pathogens: bacteria, viruses, fungi, protists. Key named examples.
  • Human defences: skin, mucus/cilia, stomach acid, white blood cells (phagocytosis, antibodies, antitoxins).
  • Vaccines use memory cells — herd immunity protects those not vaccinated.
  • Antibiotics target bacteria; overuse → resistance (natural selection).
  • Drug discovery: willow bark (aspirin), foxglove (digitalis), Penicillium (penicillin).
  • Trials: preclinical → Phase 1–3 (double-blind, placebo-controlled) → Phase 4.
  • Monoclonal antibodies: hybridoma cells → pregnancy tests, cancer treatment.
