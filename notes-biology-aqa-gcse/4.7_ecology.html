AQA GCSE Biology (8461) — Paper 2

4.7.1 Adaptations, Interdependence and Competition

Ecosystem — the interaction of a community of living (biotic) organisms with the non-living (abiotic) parts of their environment.
Community — all the populations of different species living in the same habitat.
Population — all the individuals of one species in a habitat.

Interdependence

Species in a community rely on each other for food, shelter, pollination and seed dispersal. A stable community is one where all the species and environmental factors are in balance so that population sizes remain roughly constant. Removing one species can affect many others.

Adaptations

Organisms may be adapted in different ways:

  • Structural — body features (e.g. thick fur on polar bears).
  • Behavioural — actions (e.g. bird migration, hibernation).
  • Functional — internal chemistry (e.g. slow metabolism in camels; producing venom).

Extremophiles

Organisms living in extreme conditions — high temperature (deep-sea hydrothermal vents), high pressure, high salt concentration. Many are bacteria or archaea.

4.7.2 Organisation of an Ecosystem

Producers (green plants and algae) photosynthesise to make glucose — the start of most food chains. Consumers eat other organisms — primary (eat producers), secondary, tertiary and apex predators.

Efficiency of Biomass Transfer

Efficiency = (biomass at higher level ÷ biomass at previous level) × 100

Example: 10 000 kJ producer, 900 kJ primary consumer → efficiency = 900 ÷ 10 000 × 100 = 9%.

Decomposers and Decay

Bacteria and fungi decompose dead organisms and waste, releasing nutrients (N, C, P) back to soil. Conditions that speed decay:

  • Warmth — up to enzyme optimum.
  • Moisture — water needed for microbial activity.
  • Oxygen — aerobic decomposition is fastest.

Gardeners use compost heaps to decompose plant waste into a fertiliser. Anaerobic conditions (e.g. slurry, landfill) produce biogas (mainly methane) which can be used as a fuel.

4.7.3 The Carbon, Water and Nitrogen Cycles

The Water Cycle

Driven by the Sun. Water evaporates from oceans, lakes and plants (transpiration); vapour condenses into clouds; returns to land as precipitation; flows back to the sea.

The Carbon Cycle

The Nitrogen Cycle (Separate Biology)

  • Nitrogen-fixing bacteria (in soil and root nodules of legumes) convert N₂ → ammonium.
  • Decomposers break down proteins/waste to ammonium.
  • Nitrifying bacteria convert ammonium → nitrates (usable by plants).
  • Denitrifying bacteria (waterlogged soils) convert nitrates → N₂ (lost back to atmosphere).

4.7.4 Biodiversity and the Effect of Human Interaction

Biodiversity is the variety of all the different species of organisms on Earth, or within an ecosystem. High biodiversity helps ensure ecosystem stability by reducing the dependence of one species on another for food, shelter, and the maintenance of the physical environment.

Human Impacts on Biodiversity

  • Population growth — more resources needed; more waste.
  • Land use — building, farming, quarrying and waste disposal reduce habitats.
  • Deforestation — reduces CO₂ uptake, destroys habitat, soil erosion.
  • Pollution — air (SO₂, acid rain), water (fertilisers, sewage, toxins), land (landfill, pesticides).
  • Peat bog destruction — releases stored carbon; destroys unique habitats.
  • Global warming — species distribution and migration disrupted.

Global Warming

Greenhouse gases (CO₂, methane, water vapour) trap infrared radiation. Human activity (burning fossil fuels, deforestation, cattle and paddy fields producing methane) raises concentrations.

Consequences: rising sea levels from melting ice; loss of habitats (e.g. polar ecosystems); changes in species distribution; more extreme weather; impacts on agriculture.

Maintaining Biodiversity

  • Breeding programmes for endangered species.
  • Protecting and regenerating rare habitats and hedgerows.
  • Reducing deforestation and CO₂ emissions.
  • Recycling resources, reducing landfill.
  • Conservation of species in nature reserves and zoos.

4.7.5 Food Security and Sustainable Food Production (Separate Biology)

Factors Threatening Food Security

  • Increasing birth rate / population.
  • Changing diets (more meat & dairy as countries develop).
  • New pests and pathogens.
  • Environmental changes affecting yields.
  • Cost of agricultural inputs.
  • Conflict affecting availability of food/water.

Intensive Farming

Restricts energy loss (keeping animals warm, limiting movement). High yields per area but concerns about animal welfare and environmental impact.

Sustainable Fisheries

Quotas on catch size; control of net mesh size (to let young fish escape); marine protected areas. Without controls, overfishing can collapse populations.

Biotechnology

  • GM crops with improved yields/resistance.
  • Insulin from genetically modified bacteria.
  • Mycoprotein (Fusarium) as a vegetarian protein source grown in fermenters.
For a "suggest why" question on biodiversity, link to at least two specific services: pollination, pest control, climate regulation, nutrient recycling, medicines. Vague answers like "nature is important" score no marks.

Summary — 4.7

  • Levels: cell → tissue → organ → organism → population → community → ecosystem.
  • Biotic factors (food, predators) vs abiotic (temp, light, pH).
  • Adaptations: structural, behavioural, functional; extremophiles.
  • Food chains start with producers; ~10% biomass transfer between levels.
  • Decomposers recycle nutrients; rate depends on temp, moisture, oxygen.
  • Carbon cycle: photosynthesis in, respiration + combustion out.
  • Human impact reduces biodiversity; conservation needed.
  • Food security depends on climate, pests, population, diet, economics.
