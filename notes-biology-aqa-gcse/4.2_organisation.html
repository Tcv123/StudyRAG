4.2 Organisation

AQA GCSE Biology (8461) — Paper 1

Multicellular organisms are organised into levels:
cell → tissue → organ → organ system → organism.
A tissue is a group of similar cells working together; an organ is a group of different tissues working together; an organ system is multiple organs working together.

4.2.2 Enzymes and Digestion

Enzymes

Enzymes are biological catalysts — they speed up chemical reactions in the body without being used up. They are made of protein.
Each enzyme has a specific active site whose shape is complementary to its substrate (lock and key model).

Effect of Temperature and pH on Enzymes

Temperature: Rate increases with temperature up to an optimum. Above the optimum, the bonds holding the active site break; the enzyme is denatured and rate drops sharply.

pH: Each enzyme works best at a specific pH. Extreme pH causes denaturation.

Bile

Bile is made in the liver, stored in the gallbladder and released into the small intestine. It has two functions:

  • Neutralises acid from the stomach — providing optimum pH for small intestine enzymes.
  • Emulsifies fats — breaks large fat droplets into small ones, increasing surface area for lipase.

4.2.3 The Heart and Blood Vessels

Humans have a double circulatory system: blood passes through the heart twice per circuit — once to the lungs (pulmonary) and once to the body (systemic).

Pathway of Blood Through the Heart

Deoxygenated blood: Vena cava → Right atrium → Right ventricle → Pulmonary artery → Lungs.
Oxygenated blood: Pulmonary vein → Left atrium → Left ventricle → Aorta → Body.

Blood Components

  • Red blood cells — contain haemoglobin which binds O₂. Biconcave, no nucleus — maximises surface area and space for haemoglobin.
  • White blood cells — defend the body. Phagocytes engulf pathogens; lymphocytes produce antibodies.
  • Platelets — small cell fragments that produce clots to seal wounds.
  • Plasma — yellow liquid carrying cells, nutrients, hormones, urea, CO₂ and heat.

Heart Rate Equation

Cardiac output (cm³/min) = stroke volume (cm³) × heart rate (bpm)
Example: stroke volume 70 cm³, heart rate 75 bpm → 70 × 75 = 5250 cm³/min.

Coronary Heart Disease (CHD) & Treatments

CHD occurs when fatty deposits (atheroma) build up in the coronary arteries that supply the heart muscle, reducing blood flow. It can lead to angina or a heart attack.

4.2.4 Non-Communicable Disease

Non-communicable diseases cannot spread between people. Risk factors are things that raise your chance of disease. Risk factors for CHD: smoking, high-fat diet, obesity, inactivity, genetics, high alcohol intake.

Cancer

  • Benign tumour — grows in one place and does not invade other tissues (usually not life-threatening).
  • Malignant tumour — cells break off and spread in the blood to form secondary tumours (metastasis) — cancerous.

Lifestyle risk factors: smoking, UV exposure, alcohol, poor diet, obesity. Genetic risk factors also exist (e.g. BRCA1/2 genes for breast/ovarian cancer).

Transpiration

Transpiration is the loss of water vapour from plant leaves. Water evaporates from mesophyll cells and diffuses out through the stomata. This pulls more water up the xylem from the roots.

Factors that increase transpiration rate:

  • Higher temperature (faster evaporation)
  • Higher light intensity (stomata more open)
  • More air movement/wind (removes water vapour)
  • Lower humidity (steeper gradient)

Rate of water uptake can be measured with a potometer. The distance an air bubble moves in a set time gives the rate.

Stomata and Guard Cells

Stomata are openings, mainly on the underside of leaves. Guard cells flank each pore:

  • When turgid (full of water) — stomata open to allow gas exchange.
  • When flaccid — stomata close, reducing water loss during drought.
A classic 6-mark question is: "Describe and explain how a leaf is adapted for efficient photosynthesis." Structure your answer by naming each tissue AND giving the adaptation (e.g. "Palisade mesophyll cells are column-shaped and packed with chloroplasts to maximise light absorption").

Summary — 4.2

  • Organisation: cells → tissues → organs → systems → organism.
  • Enzymes follow lock-and-key; activity peaks at optimum T and pH; denature when bonds break.
  • Digestive enzymes: amylase (starch), protease (protein), lipase (fat).
  • Bile emulsifies fat and neutralises stomach acid.
  • Heart has 4 chambers; left ventricle is thicker (pumps to body).
  • Arteries — thick muscular walls, high pressure; veins — valves, low pressure; capillaries — 1-cell-thick walls for exchange.
  • Leaf: palisade (light) + spongy (gas exchange) + stomata; xylem up, phloem around.
  • CHD treated with statins, stents, transplants or artificial hearts.
