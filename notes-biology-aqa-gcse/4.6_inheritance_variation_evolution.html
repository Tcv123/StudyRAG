Meiosis

Gametes are produced by meiosis. Meiosis halves the chromosome number (diploid → haploid) and produces four genetically different daughter cells. Fertilisation restores the diploid number.

4.6.2 DNA and the Genome

The genome is the entire genetic material of an organism. DNA is a polymer made of two strands forming a double helix, contained in chromosomes in the nucleus. A gene is a small section of DNA that codes for a particular sequence of amino acids, and therefore a specific protein.

Each DNA strand is made of nucleotides. Four bases: A, T, C, G — they pair A–T and C–G (complementary base pairs).

Protein Synthesis (Separate Biology)

The sequence of bases in a gene determines the order of amino acids in a protein. Each set of three bases (codon) codes for one amino acid. mRNA carries the code to ribosomes where amino acids are joined.

Sex Determination

Females XX, males XY. Egg cells always carry X; sperm cells carry X or Y. Sex is determined by the father's gamete (Y = male; X = female), giving a 50:50 ratio.

4.6.4 Variation and Mutation

Variation within a species can be caused by: genes (inherited), environment (conditions during life), or a combination of both.

Mutations are random changes in DNA. Most mutations have little or no effect on the phenotype. A few alter the protein — these can be harmful, neutral, or beneficial (the raw material of evolution).

4.6.5 Evolution and Natural Selection

The theory of evolution by natural selection (Darwin) states that all species of living things have evolved from simple life forms that first developed more than 3 billion years ago. Individuals with traits best suited to the environment are more likely to survive and reproduce — their alleles become more common over generations.

Key steps:

  1. Variation — individuals differ due to genes/mutations.
  2. Competition — more offspring are produced than survive.
  3. Survival of the fittest — best-adapted survive.
  4. Reproduction — survivors pass their alleles to offspring.
  5. Over many generations, favourable alleles increase in frequency → evolution.

Evidence for Evolution

  • Fossils show change in species over time and transitional forms (e.g. Archaeopteryx).
  • Antibiotic-resistant bacteria — observable evolution in our lifetime (MRSA, drug-resistant TB).
  • Comparative anatomy — similar limb structures in mammals suggest common ancestor.
  • DNA evidence — closer species share more base sequence similarity.

Speciation (Separate Biology)

New species form when populations become isolated (geographically or reproductively), experience different selection pressures, and accumulate enough genetic differences that they can no longer interbreed to produce fertile offspring.

Lamarck vs Darwin

Lamarck proposed inheritance of acquired characteristics (e.g. giraffes stretch their necks and pass longer necks to offspring). This is wrong — changes to body cells are not passed to gametes. Darwin's theory is consistent with genetics: the mechanism is natural selection acting on heritable variation.

4.6.6 Selective Breeding and Genetic Engineering

Selective Breeding (Artificial Selection)

Humans choose parents with desirable traits to breed. Over many generations those traits become common. Examples: dairy cows (high milk), disease-resistant wheat, domestic dogs with specific temperaments.

Disadvantage: reduced gene pool (inbreeding) — populations may be less able to resist new diseases or environmental change.

Genetic Engineering

Transferring a gene from one organism to another so the recipient expresses a desired trait. Example: human insulin gene inserted into bacteria using restriction enzymes and ligase. Bacteria grow in fermenters and produce large amounts of insulin.

Other GM applications:

  • Herbicide-resistant crops (e.g. soya, maize).
  • Pest-resistant crops (Bt maize).
  • Improved nutrition (golden rice with β-carotene → vitamin A).
  • Potential gene therapy to treat inherited disorders.

Concerns: effects on wild species, transfer of genes to wild plants, long-term health effects, economics and accessibility.

4.6.7 Cloning (Separate Biology)

  • Tissue culture — small plant pieces grown in nutrient medium produce clones.
  • Cuttings — simple, low-tech cloning of plants.
  • Embryo transplants — split an embryo into several clones before implantation.
  • Adult cell cloning — nucleus from an adult body cell → empty egg cell → embryo → surrogate (Dolly the sheep).

4.6.8 Classification

Linnaean system: Kingdom → Phylum → Class → Order → Family → Genus → Species. The binomial name uses genus and species (e.g. Homo sapiens).

Three-Domain System (Woese)

Molecular evidence (rRNA) divides life into:

  1. Archaea — primitive bacteria (often in extreme environments).
  2. Bacteria — true bacteria.
  3. Eukaryota — protists, fungi, plants, animals.

Evolutionary Trees

Based on comparing characteristics and DNA. Show branching relationships, with a shared branch point representing a common ancestor.
