3.2.5 Fiscal Policy and Supply-Side Policies

1. What Is Fiscal Policy?

Fiscal policy refers to the use of government spending (G) and taxation (T) to influence the level of aggregate demand (AD) and achieve macroeconomic objectives such as economic growth, low unemployment, low and stable inflation, and a satisfactory balance of payments position. Fiscal policy is determined by the Chancellor of the Exchequer and announced in the annual Budget (usually in the autumn) and the Spring Statement.

The two main instruments of fiscal policy are:

  1. Government spending (G) — directly injects demand into the circular flow; an increase in G raises AD.
  2. Taxation (T) — a withdrawal from the circular flow; a reduction in T raises disposable income and therefore consumption (C), increasing AD.
AD = C + I + G + (X − M)

Fiscal policy affects AD directly through changes in G and indirectly through changes in T (which alter C and I).

2. Government Spending

Total government spending in the UK (known as Total Managed Expenditure, TME) is divided into three categories:

2.1 Current Spending (Day-to-Day Spending)

Spending on goods and services that are consumed in the current period and do not create long-term assets. This is the largest component of government spending.

  • NHS wages and running costs
  • Teacher and police salaries
  • Defence spending on operations
  • Administrative costs of government departments

2.2 Capital Spending (Investment)

Spending on assets that provide benefits over many years. This adds to the economy's productive capacity and is a form of investment.

  • Building new hospitals, schools, and roads
  • Railway and broadband infrastructure (e.g., HS2, 5G rollout)
  • Military equipment
  • Scientific research facilities

2.3 Transfer Payments

Payments made by the government for which no goods or services are received in return. They redistribute income from taxpayers to recipients.

  • State pensions
  • Jobseeker's Allowance / Universal Credit
  • Child Benefit
  • Disability benefits
Key Point: Transfer payments are not included in the calculation of GDP because they do not represent payment for currently produced goods or services. However, they do affect AD indirectly because recipients spend the money they receive, boosting consumption (C).

3. Taxation

3.1 Direct vs Indirect Taxes

Direct taxes are levied on income and wealth and are paid directly to HMRC by the taxpayer. The legal incidence and economic incidence fall on the same person.

Indirect taxes are levied on expenditure (spending on goods and services). They are collected by the seller on behalf of HMRC. The legal incidence falls on the seller, but the economic incidence (burden) can be shared between buyer and seller depending on price elasticity of demand and supply.

Common Mistake: Students often confuse marginal and average tax rates. A progressive tax means the average rate rises with income — the marginal rate (rate on the next pound earned) is always higher than the average rate for a progressive tax. VAT is regressive not because the rate changes, but because lower-income households spend a higher proportion of their income.

3.3 Adam Smith's Canons of Taxation

In The Wealth of Nations (1776), Adam Smith set out four principles of a good tax system:

CanonMeaningApplication
Equity (Fairness)Taxes should be based on ability to pay — those with higher incomes should pay more.Justifies progressive income tax. Regressive taxes like VAT can be seen as violating this principle.
EconomyThe cost of collecting the tax should be low relative to the revenue it raises.PAYE (Pay As You Earn) is efficient to collect. Some small taxes cost more to administer than they raise.
ConvenienceIt should be easy and straightforward for the taxpayer to pay.VAT is convenient (built into prices). Self-assessment for the self-employed is less convenient.
CertaintyTaxpayers should know clearly when, where, and how much they must pay. Tax rules should be transparent.Complex tax codes with many allowances and exemptions can violate this principle.

Modern economists often add further criteria:

  • Flexibility — the tax system should be easy to adjust when economic conditions change
  • Efficiency — taxes should minimise distortions to economic behaviour (deadweight loss should be small)

4. The Budget Balance and National Debt

4.1 Budget Deficit and Surplus

Budget deficit — occurs when government spending exceeds tax revenue in a given financial year (G > T). The government must borrow to finance the gap, typically by selling government bonds (gilts).

Budget surplus — occurs when tax revenue exceeds government spending (T > G). The surplus can be used to repay existing debt.

Balanced budget — occurs when G = T (extremely rare in practice).
Budget Balance = Tax Revenue (T) − Government Spending (G)

4.2 Cyclical vs Structural Deficit

Cyclical DeficitStructural Deficit
The part of the deficit caused by the economic cycle.The part of the deficit that exists even when the economy is at full employment.
In a recession: tax revenue falls (lower incomes, profits, spending) and spending rises (more benefit claimants). This creates or widens the deficit.Reflects a fundamental, long-term imbalance between government spending commitments and revenue-raising capacity.
Self-correcting: the cyclical deficit disappears automatically as the economy recovers and moves back to trend output.Requires deliberate policy action to correct — spending cuts, tax rises, or structural reforms.

Exam Tip: When discussing whether a government should reduce its deficit, always distinguish between the cyclical and structural components. Cutting spending during a recession to reduce a cyclical deficit may be counterproductive (it deepens the recession). It makes more sense to target the structural deficit during a period of growth.

4.3 National Debt

National debt (public sector net debt) is the total accumulated stock of government borrowing over time. It is the sum of all past budget deficits minus any surpluses. UK national debt exceeded 100% of GDP following the COVID-19 pandemic.

It is important to distinguish:

  • Deficit = a flow concept (borrowing in a single year)
  • Debt = a stock concept (total accumulated borrowing)

4.4 Significance of National Debt

ConcernExplanation
Debt interest paymentsA large debt means significant annual interest payments. In 2023-24, UK debt interest exceeded £100bn. This is an opportunity cost — money spent on interest cannot fund public services or tax cuts.
Intergenerational equityFuture generations inherit the obligation to service and repay debt they did not benefit from. However, they also inherit the assets funded by borrowing (infrastructure, education).
Crowding outGovernment borrowing increases demand for loanable funds, potentially pushing up interest rates. This may reduce (crowd out) private sector investment. However, this depends on the state of the economy — crowding out is less likely in a recession when private demand for borrowing is low.

Evaluation: The significance of national debt depends on several factors: (1) the debt-to-GDP ratio matters more than the absolute figure; (2) who holds the debt — debt held domestically (UK citizens holding gilts) is less of a concern than debt held by foreign creditors; (3) what the borrowing was used for — borrowing for productive investment (infrastructure) may pay for itself through higher future growth; (4) the interest rate environment — low rates make even large debts more manageable.

5. Expansionary vs Contractionary Fiscal Policy

5.1 Expansionary Fiscal Policy

An increase in government spending and/or a reduction in taxation, designed to increase AD. Used to stimulate economic growth and reduce unemployment, typically during a recession or period of low growth.

  • Increases AD (shifts AD curve to the right)
  • Leads to a budget deficit (or a larger deficit)
  • Multiplier effect: the initial injection creates further rounds of spending
  • Risks: demand-pull inflation (if the economy is near capacity), higher national debt, crowding out of private investment

5.2 Contractionary Fiscal Policy (Austerity)

A reduction in government spending and/or an increase in taxation, designed to reduce AD. Used to reduce inflation, improve the budget position, or reduce an unsustainable deficit.

  • Reduces AD (shifts AD curve to the left)
  • Moves the budget towards surplus
  • Negative multiplier effect: reduced spending leads to further falls in income and output
  • Risks: higher unemployment, lower growth, reduced public services, social costs

Case Study: UK Austerity 2010-2015

Following the 2008 financial crisis, the UK deficit reached approximately 10% of GDP. The Coalition government (2010) implemented a programme of fiscal consolidation (austerity) to reduce the structural deficit. Key measures included a public sector pay freeze, cuts to welfare benefits, reductions in local government funding, a rise in VAT from 17.5% to 20%, and reduced capital spending. The deficit fell significantly (to around 4% of GDP by 2015), but critics argued austerity slowed the recovery, increased inequality, and damaged public services — particularly the NHS and local councils. GDP growth was sluggish from 2010-12, and the OBR estimated austerity reduced GDP growth by approximately 1% per year. Supporters argued it restored fiscal credibility and kept gilt yields low.
Case Study: COVID-19 Furlough Scheme (2020-2021)

The UK government's Coronavirus Job Retention Scheme (furlough) paid 80% of employees' wages (up to a cap of £2,500/month) for workers who could not work due to lockdowns. At its peak, nearly 9 million jobs were furloughed. The scheme cost approximately £70 billion. The government also provided business grants, VAT cuts for hospitality, the Eat Out to Help Out scheme, and extended Universal Credit by £20/week. This was one of the largest peacetime fiscal expansions in UK history. The deficit reached over £300 billion (around 15% of GDP) in 2020-21. The policy is credited with preventing mass unemployment, but contributed to national debt exceeding 100% of GDP for the first time since the 1960s.

6. Automatic Stabilisers and Discretionary Fiscal Policy

6.1 Automatic Stabilisers

Automatic stabilisers are features of the tax and benefit system that automatically adjust to offset fluctuations in the economic cycle, without any deliberate government action. They dampen the amplitude of the business cycle.

There are two main automatic stabilisers:

Progressive Income Tax

  • In a boom: Incomes rise, pushing people into higher tax brackets. A larger proportion of income is taken in tax, reducing disposable income and dampening consumption growth. This helps prevent the economy from overheating.
  • In a recession: Incomes fall, people drop into lower tax brackets or below the tax-free allowance. A smaller proportion of income is taken in tax, cushioning the fall in disposable income and supporting consumption.

Means-Tested Welfare Benefits

  • In a recession: Unemployment rises, so benefit spending automatically increases (more claimants for Universal Credit, JSA). This injects spending into the economy, partially offsetting the fall in AD.
  • In a boom: Unemployment falls, so benefit spending automatically decreases. This acts as a withdrawal, dampening AD growth.
Evaluation of Automatic Stabilisers: They act immediately with no time lag (unlike discretionary policy). They reduce the severity of recessions and booms without requiring political decision-making. However, they can only dampen the cycle, not eliminate it. They may also be weakened if the tax system is made less progressive or if benefits are cut. Countries with larger welfare states (e.g., Scandinavian countries) tend to have stronger automatic stabilisers.

6.2 Discretionary Fiscal Policy

Discretionary fiscal policy refers to deliberate, one-off changes to government spending or taxation designed to influence AD. Unlike automatic stabilisers, these require active decisions by the government.

Examples of discretionary fiscal policy:

  • A stimulus package during a recession (e.g., COVID-19 support measures)
  • A temporary VAT cut to boost consumer spending
  • An increase in income tax to reduce a budget deficit
  • A programme of infrastructure investment to boost growth

Discretionary policy is subject to time lags:

  1. Recognition lag — time to identify the problem (GDP data published with a delay)
  2. Decision lag — time for political debate and legislative approval
  3. Implementation lag — time to put the policy into practice (building infrastructure takes years)
  4. Impact lag — time for the policy to have its full effect on AD and the economy
Exam Tip: The time lag argument is one of the most important criticisms of discretionary fiscal policy. By the time a stimulus takes effect, the economy may have already recovered — making the stimulus inflationary. This is why monetary policy (which has shorter implementation lags) is often preferred for fine-tuning the economy.

7. The Fiscal Multiplier

The multiplier effect means that an initial change in injections (such as government spending) leads to a larger final change in national income. This occurs because one person's spending becomes another's income, generating successive rounds of spending.

Multiplier (k) = 1 / (1 − MPC) = 1 / MPW

Where MPC = marginal propensity to consume, and MPW = marginal propensity to withdraw (MPW = MPS + MPT + MPM — the sum of marginal propensities to save, tax, and import).

For example, if MPC = 0.8, the multiplier = 1 / (1 − 0.8) = 5. This means a £1 billion increase in G leads to a £5 billion increase in national income.

Factors Affecting the Size of the Multiplier

  • MPC / MPW: A higher MPC (lower MPW) means a larger multiplier. Poorer households tend to have a higher MPC, so spending targeted at lower earners has a larger multiplier.
  • Spare capacity: The multiplier is larger when there is a negative output gap (spare capacity). If the economy is at or near full capacity, additional spending generates inflation rather than output.
  • Openness of the economy: A more open economy (high imports) has a smaller multiplier as spending leaks abroad.
  • Confidence: If consumers and firms are pessimistic, they may save rather than spend any additional income, reducing the multiplier.
Evaluation: The multiplier can work in reverse — a cut in government spending can cause a larger-than-expected fall in national income (negative multiplier). This is a key argument against austerity during a recession. Empirical estimates suggest the fiscal multiplier in the UK is typically between 0.5 and 1.5, depending on economic conditions. The OBR estimated the spending multiplier during the 2010 austerity was around 0.6-1.0.

8. The Laffer Curve

The Laffer Curve illustrates the theoretical relationship between tax rates and total tax revenue collected by the government. It suggests that there is an optimal tax rate (t*) that maximises revenue. Beyond this rate, higher taxes reduce revenue because they discourage economic activity, reduce the tax base, and encourage tax avoidance and evasion.
Figure 2: The Laffer Curve — at 0% and 100% tax rates, revenue is zero. Points A and B may generate similar revenue despite very different tax rates. Beyond t*, rate increases are counterproductive.

8.1 Why Revenue Falls Beyond t*

  • Reduced work incentive: Very high marginal tax rates discourage additional work effort — the substitution effect (choosing leisure over work) dominates the income effect.
  • Tax avoidance and evasion: High rates make it more worthwhile to hire accountants, use tax havens, or conceal income.
  • Capital flight: Businesses and wealthy individuals may relocate to countries with lower tax rates.
  • Reduced enterprise: High corporate taxes discourage investment and new business creation, shrinking the tax base.
  • Shadow economy: High taxes may drive economic activity underground (informal economy), reducing taxable output.

8.2 Evaluation of the Laffer Curve

Strengths:
  • Theoretically sound — at the extremes (0% and 100%), revenue is indeed zero.
  • Highlights the trade-off between tax rates and the tax base.
  • Provides a justification for examining whether tax rates are optimal.
  • Supported by some historical evidence (e.g., UK top-rate cut from 83% to 60% in 1979 increased top-rate revenue).

Weaknesses:
  • The revenue-maximising rate (t*) is unknown — it varies by country, tax type, time period, and economic conditions. There is no reliable way to identify where an economy sits on the curve.
  • Oversimplified — assumes a single smooth relationship, but in reality the curve may be asymmetric, have multiple peaks, or be very flat over a wide range of rates.
  • Used selectively — politicians have used the Laffer Curve to justify tax cuts that primarily benefit the wealthy, even without evidence that rates are above t*.
  • Mixed empirical evidence — the Reagan tax cuts in the US (1980s) were partly justified by the Laffer Curve, but the US deficit increased significantly, suggesting rates were not above t*.
  • Ignores equity — even if lower taxes raise more revenue, society may value progressive taxation for fairness reasons.
Case Study: The Laffer Curve Debate — Reagan Tax Cuts (1981)

President Reagan cut the top US income tax rate from 70% to 50% (and later to 28%) based partly on supply-side arguments including the Laffer Curve. Proponents (notably Arthur Laffer and supply-side economists) argued the cuts would stimulate so much economic activity that total tax revenue would increase. In practice, the US economy did grow strongly in the mid-1980s, but federal tax revenue as a share of GDP fell, and the budget deficit tripled. Critics argue this shows the US was on the normal (left) side of the Laffer Curve, meaning the tax cuts reduced revenue. Supporters counter that the economic growth would have been even weaker without the cuts, and that spending increases (particularly defence) were the main cause of the deficit.

9. Supply-Side Policies

Supply-side policies are government policies designed to increase the productive potential of the economy by improving the efficiency and quality of the factors of production (land, labour, capital, enterprise). They aim to shift the long-run aggregate supply (LRAS) curve to the right, enabling the economy to produce more output at every price level. This allows for non-inflationary, sustainable economic growth.

Supply-side policies are divided into two broad categories:

  1. Market-based (free-market) policies — reduce government intervention, increase competition, and improve incentives through deregulation, tax cuts, and privatisation.
  2. Interventionist policies — involve government actively investing in or directing the economy to address market failures and improve productive capacity.

10. Market-Based (Free-Market) Supply-Side Policies

These policies aim to improve incentives, increase competition, and reduce the role of the state in the economy. They are associated with classical/monetarist/New Right economics.

Case Study: Thatcher's Supply-Side Reforms (1979-1990)

Margaret Thatcher's government implemented the most significant programme of market-based supply-side reforms in modern UK history. Key policies included: privatisation of over 50 state-owned enterprises (raising over £50 billion); trade union reform through a series of Employment Acts that required pre-strike ballots, banned secondary picketing, and limited closed shops; income tax cuts (top rate from 83% to 40%); deregulation of financial markets (the 1986 Big Bang); and cuts to welfare benefits to sharpen work incentives.

Outcomes: Productivity growth improved, particularly in manufacturing. Inflation fell from over 18% (1980) to around 4% by 1983. The financial sector grew significantly, making London a global financial centre. However, unemployment reached over 3 million in the early 1980s as traditional industries (coal, steel, manufacturing) declined. Income inequality increased sharply — the Gini coefficient rose from 0.25 to 0.34 during the 1980s. De-industrialisation caused lasting regional deprivation in the North, Midlands, and Wales. The legacy remains deeply contested.

11. Interventionist Supply-Side Policies

These policies involve the government actively intervening to correct market failures and improve productive capacity. They are associated with Keynesian economics and the view that markets alone may underinvest in education, infrastructure, and innovation.

12. Evaluation of Supply-Side Policies

12.1 Advantages

  • Non-inflationary growth: By expanding productive capacity, supply-side policies allow the economy to grow without generating demand-pull inflation.
  • Address root causes: Unlike demand-side policies which manage symptoms (unemployment, inflation), supply-side policies tackle the underlying causes of poor economic performance.
  • Improve international competitiveness: A more productive economy can produce goods more cheaply, improving the trade balance in the long run.
  • Reduce the natural rate of unemployment: By improving incentives and skills, supply-side policies reduce structural and frictional unemployment (shift the LRPC left).
  • Long-lasting benefits: Once embedded (e.g., better infrastructure, more skilled workforce), the benefits persist for years or decades.
  • Can improve both equity and efficiency: Interventionist policies (education, healthcare) can simultaneously boost productivity and reduce inequality.

12.2 Disadvantages and Limitations

  • Time lags: Most supply-side policies take years or even decades to have full effect. Education reforms take a generation. Infrastructure projects take decades from planning to completion. This makes them unsuitable for addressing short-run problems.
  • Cost: Interventionist policies require significant government spending, increasing the deficit or requiring higher taxes elsewhere. There is a large opportunity cost.
  • Inequality: Market-based policies (tax cuts, deregulation, welfare cuts) tend to increase income and wealth inequality. The benefits of tax cuts are concentrated on higher earners.
  • Uncertainty of outcomes: There is no guarantee that supply-side policies will achieve their intended effects. Education spending does not automatically produce the skills employers need.
  • Cannot solve demand deficiency: In a recession caused by a collapse in AD, supply-side policies alone will not restore spending and reduce unemployment. You cannot solve a demand problem by expanding supply.
  • Political resistance: Reforms such as welfare cuts, privatisation, or trade union restrictions may face strong political and social opposition, making implementation difficult.
  • Government failure: Interventionist policies risk government failure — the state may lack the information or capability to identify the best investments, leading to wasted resources (picking losers).
  • Environmental costs: Deregulation and growth-focused policies may come at the expense of environmental protection.
Market-Based vs Interventionist — Key Contrast:

Market-based policies tend to be cheaper for the government (tax cuts aside) and can be implemented relatively quickly (deregulation, privatisation). However, they tend to increase inequality and may reduce worker/consumer protection.

Interventionist policies address market failures (underinvestment in education, infrastructure, R&D) and can reduce inequality. However, they are expensive, subject to long time lags, and risk government failure.

In practice, most governments use a combination of both approaches. The optimal mix depends on the nature of the supply-side problems and the government's ideological position.

13. Evaluation of Fiscal Policy

13.1 Strengths of Fiscal Policy

  • Targeted: Unlike monetary policy (which affects the whole economy via interest rates), fiscal policy can be directed at specific sectors, regions, or groups (e.g., investment in the North, tax cuts for low earners, spending on the NHS).
  • Powerful multiplier effects: Government spending has a direct impact on AD, and the multiplier means the total effect is larger than the initial injection. This is especially powerful in a recession when there is spare capacity.
  • Addresses inequality: Progressive taxation and targeted spending can reduce income and wealth inequality. Transfer payments support the poorest in society.
  • Automatic stabilisers: These work immediately and without political delay, smoothing the business cycle.
  • Effective at the zero lower bound: When interest rates are at or near 0% (as in 2009-2021), monetary policy becomes less effective (liquidity trap). Fiscal policy remains potent because it directly injects spending into the economy.
  • Can provide public goods: Fiscal policy funds goods and services that the market would not provide (defence, streetlighting, public parks).

13.2 Weaknesses of Fiscal Policy

  • Time lags: Recognition, decision, implementation, and impact lags mean fiscal policy may be poorly timed. A stimulus may arrive after the recession is over, causing inflation.
  • Crowding out: Government borrowing may increase interest rates, reducing private sector investment and consumption, partially offsetting the stimulus. (Keynesian economists argue crowding out is negligible in a recession.)
  • Political influence: Spending and tax decisions may be driven by electoral considerations (e.g., pre-election tax cuts) rather than economic need. The political business cycle distorts policy.
  • Forecasting errors: Fiscal policy relies on economic forecasts which are often inaccurate. If the government misjudges the state of the economy, fiscal policy may be destabilising.
  • Irreversibility and ratchet effect: Once spending programmes are created, they are politically difficult to reverse. Government spending tends to ratchet upwards over time.
  • Sustainability: Persistent deficits lead to rising national debt. If debt becomes unsustainable, the government may lose market confidence, face higher borrowing costs, or be forced into sudden, damaging austerity.
  • Ricardian equivalence: Consumers may anticipate that today's government borrowing will require future tax increases. They may therefore save more now to pay future taxes, offsetting the expansionary effect of fiscal policy. (This theory is contested — evidence suggests consumers are not fully rational in this way.)
  • Inflationary risk: Expansionary fiscal policy near full capacity can cause demand-pull inflation without significantly increasing output.

14. Keynesian vs Classical/Monetarist Debate

The role of fiscal policy is one of the most important dividing lines in macroeconomics:

Keynesian ViewClassical / Monetarist / New Right View
Role of fiscal policy Active fiscal policy is essential to manage AD and stabilise the economy. Markets do not self-correct quickly enough. Fiscal policy should be minimal. Markets are self-correcting in the long run. Government intervention causes more problems than it solves.
In a recession Government should increase spending and/or cut taxes to boost AD and close the output gap. The multiplier makes this effective. Government should not intervene. The economy will self-correct through flexible wages and prices. Fiscal stimulus causes crowding out and inflation.
Budget deficits Deficits are acceptable and desirable in recessions (counter-cyclical policy). The budget should be balanced over the economic cycle, not in every year. Deficits are harmful — they lead to rising debt, higher interest rates, and burden future generations. Governments should aim for balanced budgets or surpluses.
Supply-side approach Interventionist: government should invest in education, infrastructure, and healthcare to boost productivity. Market failures justify an active state. Market-based: deregulation, tax cuts, privatisation, and trade union reform. Reducing the size of the state improves efficiency and incentives.
Crowding out Minimal in a recession (spare capacity, low interest rates). Government spending fills the gap left by weak private demand. Significant: government borrowing competes with the private sector for funds, raising interest rates and reducing private investment.
Multiplier Large (especially in recession). Fiscal policy is highly effective at boosting output and employment. Small or zero. Leakages (saving, tax, imports) and crowding out mean fiscal stimulus has little lasting effect on output.
Key figure John Maynard Keynes Milton Friedman, Friedrich Hayek, Adam Smith
Exam Tip: AQA examiners reward answers that consider both sides of this debate. A strong evaluation will acknowledge that the effectiveness of fiscal policy depends on: the state of the economy (recession vs boom), the size of the output gap, the level of existing debt, the interest rate environment, and the specific policies used. Avoid one-sided conclusions.

15. The Policy Mix

In practice, governments do not rely on a single type of policy. Effective macroeconomic management requires a combination of monetary, fiscal, and supply-side policies working together. This is called the policy mix.

15.1 How Policies Complement Each Other

ScenarioAppropriate Policy Mix
Deep recession (e.g., COVID-19, 2008 crisis) Monetary: Cut interest rates to near zero, quantitative easing.
Fiscal: Expansionary — increase G, cut T, furlough schemes, stimulus payments.
Supply-side: Training programmes to prevent long-term unemployment (scarring effects).
High inflation, overheating economy Monetary: Raise interest rates to reduce borrowing and spending.
Fiscal: Contractionary — reduce G, raise T to reduce AD.
Supply-side: Policies to increase capacity (investment, competition) so the economy can meet demand without inflation.
Low growth, low productivity (secular stagnation) Monetary: Low rates to support investment.
Fiscal: Infrastructure investment, R&D spending (productive fiscal expansion).
Supply-side: Education reform, deregulation, industrial strategy to boost long-run productive capacity.
Supply-side shock (e.g., energy crisis) Monetary: Careful — raising rates may worsen the recession, but may be needed to prevent inflation expectations becoming embedded.
Fiscal: Targeted support for affected households and firms (energy price cap, windfall taxes).
Supply-side: Diversify energy sources, invest in renewables, reduce dependence on imports.

15.2 Potential Conflicts

  • Fiscal expansion + monetary tightening: If the government increases spending while the central bank raises interest rates, the policies work against each other. The fiscal stimulus boosts AD, but higher rates dampen it.
  • Short-run vs long-run: Expansionary fiscal policy may boost AD in the short run but increase debt, constraining future fiscal space. Supply-side policies build long-run capacity but cannot address short-run demand deficiency.
  • Equity vs efficiency: Market-based supply-side policies may boost efficiency but worsen inequality. Redistributive fiscal policy may improve equity but reduce incentives.
Key Evaluation Point: The strongest exam answers argue that demand-side and supply-side policies are complementary, not substitutes. In a recession, fiscal and monetary stimulus provides the short-run boost needed to restore confidence and employment. Supply-side policies then ensure that when the economy recovers, it has the productive capacity to grow without inflation. Neither approach is sufficient on its own — the best policy mix depends on the specific diagnosis of the economy's problems.
