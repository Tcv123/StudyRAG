Module 1 — Development of Practical Skills in Chemistry

1.1 Planning Experiments

Hypothesis: A testable prediction based on scientific knowledge or prior observations that proposes a relationship between variables.

Variables

Every experiment involves three types of variables that must be clearly identified during the planning stage:

  • Independent variable: The variable you deliberately change (e.g. concentration of acid).
  • Dependent variable: The variable you measure as a result (e.g. volume of gas produced).
  • Control variables: All other variables that must be kept constant to ensure a fair test (e.g. temperature, volume of reactant, catalyst presence).

Risk Assessments

A risk assessment identifies hazards associated with the chemicals and procedures, evaluates the likelihood and severity of harm, and outlines precautions to minimise risk. You should reference standard hazard symbols and safety data sheets (SDS).

Selecting Apparatus

You must choose apparatus appropriate to the measurement required. Consider the resolution (smallest scale division) and the range of the instrument. For measuring volumes of solutions, a burette (resolution 0.05 cm³) is more precise than a measuring cylinder (resolution 0.5 cm³). For measuring masses, a balance reading to 0.01 g or 0.001 g is typical.

Exam Tip: When asked to describe an experimental procedure, always state the specific apparatus you would use and why. For example, state "use a burette (resolution ±0.05 cm³)" rather than simply "measure the volume."

1.2 Recording and Presenting Results

Recording Data

Raw data should be recorded in a clearly labelled table with appropriate headings that include units. Headings should follow the format: Quantity / Unit (e.g. "Volume / cm³"). All readings should be recorded to the same number of decimal places, consistent with the precision of the instrument.

Significant Figures

Calculated results should be given to the same number of significant figures as the least precise measurement used. For example, if a mass is measured to 3 significant figures and a volume to 4, the final answer should be reported to 3 significant figures.

Significant figures: The number of meaningful digits in a measurement, starting from the first non-zero digit. For example, 0.00452 has 3 significant figures.

Graphs

Graphs should have:

  • Labelled axes with units
  • An appropriate scale that uses at least half the available graph paper
  • Data points plotted accurately as small crosses
  • A line of best fit (straight or curved as appropriate), which does not need to pass through every point
  • A descriptive title

1.3 Analysis and Evaluation

Accuracy, Precision and Reliability

Accuracy: How close a measured value is to the true or accepted value.
Precision: How close repeated measurements are to each other, regardless of whether they are close to the true value. A precise set of results has a small spread.
Reliability: Results are reliable if they can be consistently reproduced under the same conditions by different experimenters.

Types of Error

Error TypeDescriptionEffectHow to Reduce
Random errorUnpredictable fluctuations in readingsReduces precision; scatter around the true valueTake repeat measurements and calculate a mean
Systematic errorConsistent error in the same direction every timeReduces accuracy; shifts all readingsCalibrate instruments; improve technique
Zero errorInstrument does not read zero when it shouldSystematic shift of all readingsCheck and reset the instrument before use

Percentage Error and Uncertainty

Percentage error = (|experimental value − true value| / true value) × 100%
Percentage uncertainty = (absolute uncertainty / measured value) × 100%

For a burette with an uncertainty of ±0.05 cm³ per reading, two readings are taken (initial and final), so the total uncertainty is ±0.10 cm³. If the titre is 25.00 cm³, the percentage uncertainty is (0.10 / 25.00) × 100 = 0.40%.

Exam Tip: When combining uncertainties, add absolute uncertainties for addition/subtraction and add percentage uncertainties for multiplication/division.

Evaluating Procedures

In evaluation questions, you should identify the most significant sources of error, suggest improvements to reduce those errors, and assess whether the results support the hypothesis. Consider whether anomalous results should be excluded from mean calculations and explain why.

1.4 Practical Techniques

Titrations

Titration: A technique used to determine the concentration of a solution by reacting it with a solution of known concentration until the equivalence point is reached.

Standard procedure for an acid-base titration:

  1. Rinse the burette with the solution it will contain (e.g. the acid). Fill and record the initial reading.
  2. Use a pipette (rinsed with the solution it will contain) to transfer a fixed volume (e.g. 25.0 cm³) of the other solution into a conical flask.
  3. Add a few drops of a suitable indicator (e.g. methyl orange for strong acid/weak base, phenolphthalein for weak acid/strong base).
  4. Add the solution from the burette slowly, swirling the flask, until the indicator changes colour permanently. Record the final burette reading.
  5. Perform a rough titration first, then repeat to obtain at least two concordant results (within 0.10 cm³).
  6. Calculate the mean titre from concordant results only.
Exam Tip: The conical flask is rinsed with deionised water only (not with the solution), because adding extra solution would change the amount of substance present. The pipette and burette are rinsed with their respective solutions to avoid dilution.

Distillation

Simple distillation separates a solvent from a solution (e.g. water from salt water). The solution is heated, the solvent evaporates, passes through a condenser where it cools and condenses, and is collected in a receiving flask. This is suitable when the boiling points of the components differ by more than about 25 °C.

Fractional distillation separates a mixture of miscible liquids with similar boiling points (e.g. ethanol and water). A fractionating column is packed with glass beads to provide a large surface area. The liquid with the lowest boiling point reaches the top of the column first and is collected. A thermometer at the top monitors which fraction is being collected.

Reflux

Reflux: A technique for heating a reaction mixture continuously without losing volatile components. Vapour rises, condenses in a vertical condenser attached to the flask, and drips back into the mixture.

Reflux is used when a reaction requires prolonged heating (e.g. oxidation of a primary alcohol with acidified potassium dichromate to form a carboxylic acid). An anti-bumping granule is added to ensure smooth boiling. The flask is heated using a water bath or electric heater rather than a naked flame when flammable substances are involved.

Recrystallisation

Recrystallisation purifies an impure solid by exploiting differences in solubility at different temperatures:

  1. Dissolve the impure solid in the minimum volume of hot solvent.
  2. Filter the hot solution to remove any insoluble impurities (this is called hot filtration using a pre-heated funnel).
  3. Allow the filtrate to cool slowly. The desired product crystallises out as it becomes less soluble at lower temperatures. Impurities remain dissolved because they are present in small amounts.
  4. Filter the crystals using vacuum (Buchner) filtration for faster and drier collection.
  5. Wash the crystals with a small volume of cold solvent to remove residual impurities.
  6. Dry the crystals between filter papers or in a desiccator.

Chromatography

Chromatography separates components of a mixture based on their different affinities for a stationary phase and a mobile phase.

Thin-layer chromatography (TLC): A small spot of sample is placed on a TLC plate (stationary phase: silica or alumina on a glass or plastic plate). The plate is placed in a beaker with a shallow layer of solvent (mobile phase). The solvent rises by capillary action, carrying components at different rates. An Rf value is calculated for each spot:

Rf = distance moved by spot / distance moved by solvent front

Paper chromatography works on the same principle but uses paper as the stationary phase. Both techniques can be used to identify unknown substances by comparing Rf values with those of known standards run on the same plate or paper.

Exam Tip: The pencil baseline must be used (not pen) because ink would dissolve in the solvent and interfere with results. The solvent level must be below the baseline so spots are not dissolved directly into the solvent.

Gravimetric Analysis

Gravimetric analysis: A quantitative technique that determines the amount of a substance by measuring mass changes, often involving precipitation, filtration, drying and weighing.

A typical gravimetric procedure involves adding an excess of a reagent to precipitate the desired ion from solution, then filtering, washing, drying and weighing the precipitate. For example, determining the sulfate content of a solution by adding excess barium chloride to precipitate barium sulfate.

Ba²+(aq) + SO₄²−(aq) → BaSO₄(s)

The mass of the dried precipitate is used to calculate the moles and hence the mass or concentration of the sulfate ions originally present.

1.5 Practical Endorsement (PAG Summary)

The OCR A specification includes a set of Practical Activity Groups (PAGs) that must be completed to gain the practical endorsement. These assess your ability to follow written instructions, use appropriate apparatus, make and record observations, and analyse results. Key PAGs include:

  • PAG 1: Moles determination (e.g. measuring molar gas volume, determining the formula of a hydrated salt by heating to constant mass)
  • PAG 2: Titrations (acid-base and redox titrations)
  • PAG 3: Enthalpy changes (e.g. calorimetry experiments measuring temperature changes)
  • PAG 4: Rates of reaction (measuring how rate changes with concentration or temperature)
  • PAG 5: Qualitative tests (identifying ions using test-tube reactions)
  • PAG 6: Organic preparations (synthesis, purification, and testing of organic products)
Exam Tip: Questions about practical skills appear across all papers, not just in a dedicated practical paper. You may be asked to plan experiments, analyse data, evaluate methods, or suggest improvements to procedures. Always link your answers to specific apparatus and techniques.

1.6 Working with Data

Calculating Means

When calculating a mean, exclude any anomalous results first. An anomalous result is one that does not fit the pattern of the other data. You should identify and explain why a result might be anomalous (e.g. misreading a burette, contamination, incomplete reaction).

Percentage Yield and Atom Economy

Percentage yield = (actual yield / theoretical yield) × 100%
Atom economy = (Mr of desired product / sum of Mr of all products) × 100%

Percentage yield is always less than 100% in practice due to incomplete reactions, side reactions, and losses during purification (e.g. during transfer, filtration, or evaporation). Atom economy is a theoretical measure of how efficiently a reaction uses its atoms. A high atom economy means less waste, which is important in green chemistry and industrial processes.

Green chemistry: An approach to chemistry that seeks to reduce waste, use safer solvents and reagents, improve atom economy, and minimise environmental impact.
