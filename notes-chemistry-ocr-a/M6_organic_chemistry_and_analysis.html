Module 6 — Organic Chemistry and Analysis

6.1 Aromatic Chemistry

Structure of Benzene

Benzene (C6H6): A planar, cyclic molecule with six carbon atoms in a ring. Each carbon is sp2 hybridised, forming three sigma bonds (to two adjacent carbons and one hydrogen). The remaining p orbital on each carbon overlaps with its neighbours to form a delocalised pi system above and below the plane of the ring.

Evidence against the Kekule (cyclohexatriene) model:

  • Bond lengths: All C–C bonds in benzene are identical (0.139 nm), intermediate between a C–C single bond (0.154 nm) and a C=C double bond (0.134 nm). Kekule's model predicts alternating lengths.
  • Enthalpy of hydrogenation: The experimental value for benzene is less exothermic than three times the value for cyclohexene, indicating benzene is more stable by approximately 150 kJ mol−1 (the delocalisation energy or resonance energy).
  • Resistance to addition: Benzene undergoes substitution rather than addition reactions, preserving the stable delocalised system.

Electrophilic Substitution

Electrophilic substitution: A reaction in which an electrophile attacks the delocalised pi electron system of the benzene ring, substituting for a hydrogen atom. The delocalised ring is preserved.

Nitration

Reagents: concentrated nitric acid and concentrated sulfuric acid. Temperature: below 55°C (to avoid further substitution).

The electrophile is the nitronium ion (NO2+), generated by the reaction:

HNO3 + H2SO4 → NO2+ + HSO4− + H2O

Mechanism: The NO2+ electrophile attacks the delocalised pi system, forming a positively charged intermediate. A proton is then lost and the delocalised system is restored. The sulfuric acid is regenerated (it acts as a catalyst).

Nitration is used to produce nitrobenzene, which can be reduced to form phenylamine (aniline) using tin and concentrated HCl, followed by NaOH. Nitration is also important in the manufacture of explosives and dyes.

Friedel-Crafts Acylation

Reagents: an acyl chloride (e.g. CH3COCl) with an AlCl3 catalyst. The electrophile is the acylium ion (CH3CO+), generated when AlCl3 accepts a lone pair from the chlorine of the acyl chloride:

CH3COCl + AlCl3 → CH3CO+ + AlCl4−

The acylium ion attacks the benzene ring via the same electrophilic substitution mechanism. The product is an aromatic ketone (e.g. phenylethanone). AlCl3 is a Lewis acid catalyst and is regenerated at the end.

Friedel-Crafts Alkylation

Reagents: a halogenoalkane (e.g. CH3Cl) with an AlCl3 catalyst. The electrophile is a carbocation formed when AlCl3 polarises the C–Cl bond. The product is an alkylarene (e.g. methylbenzene).

Exam Tip: In Friedel-Crafts mechanisms, always show the role of the AlCl3 catalyst in generating the electrophile, and show its regeneration at the end. The catalyst is a halogen carrier (Lewis acid).

6.2 Nitrogen Compounds

Amines

Amine: An organic compound derived from ammonia (NH3) by replacing one or more hydrogen atoms with alkyl or aryl groups. Primary amines have one alkyl group (RNH2), secondary have two (R2NH), and tertiary have three (R3N).

Preparation of primary amines:

  • From haloalkanes: React with excess concentrated ammonia in ethanol, heated in a sealed tube. Excess NH3 ensures the primary amine is the main product and reduces further substitution.
  • From nitriles: Reduction with LiAlH4 in dry ether, or H2/Ni catalyst. For example: CH3CN → CH3CH2NH2.
  • From nitrobenzene: Reduction with tin and concentrated HCl, then NaOH to liberate the free amine (phenylamine).

Amines act as bases because the lone pair on nitrogen can accept a proton:

CH3NH2 + H2O ⇌ CH3NH3+ + OH−

Amines also act as nucleophiles because the lone pair on nitrogen can attack electrophilic carbon atoms.

Amino Acids

Amino acid: A molecule containing both an amine group (–NH2) and a carboxylic acid group (–COOH). In alpha-amino acids, both groups are attached to the same carbon atom. At neutral pH, amino acids exist as zwitterions (internal salts) with a positive NH3+ group and a negative COO− group.

Chirality

Chiral centre: A carbon atom bonded to four different groups. Molecules with a chiral centre exist as two non-superimposable mirror images called enantiomers. Enantiomers rotate plane-polarised light in opposite directions. A racemic mixture (racemate) contains equal amounts of both enantiomers and shows no net optical rotation.
Exam Tip: All alpha-amino acids (except glycine, where R = H) have a chiral centre. Draw 3D representations using wedge and dash bonds to show chirality clearly.

Amides

Amides contain the –CONH2 functional group (or –CONHR, –CONR2). They are formed by the reaction of a carboxylic acid with an amine under heating, or more efficiently by reacting an acyl chloride with an amine at room temperature.

6.3 Polymers

Condensation Polymers

Condensation polymerisation: Monomers with two functional groups join together with the loss of a small molecule (usually water) at each link. Unlike addition polymerisation, a by-product is formed.

Polyesters

Formed by the condensation reaction between a diol (two –OH groups) and a dicarboxylic acid (two –COOH groups). An ester linkage (–COO–) is formed at each join, and water is eliminated.

nHO–R–OH + nHOOC–R'–COOH → (–O–R–OOC–R'–CO–)n + 2nH2O

Example: PET (polyethylene terephthalate) is made from ethane-1,2-diol and benzene-1,4-dicarboxylic acid.

Polyamides

Formed by the condensation of a diamine (two –NH2 groups) and a dicarboxylic acid. An amide (peptide) linkage (–CONH–) is formed at each join.

nH2N–R–NH2 + nHOOC–R'–COOH → (–NH–R–NHCO–R'–CO–)n + 2nH2O

Example: Nylon-6,6 is made from 1,6-diaminohexane and hexanedioic acid. Proteins are natural polyamides made from amino acids joined by peptide bonds.

Biodegradability and Disposal

  • Addition polymers (e.g. poly(ethene)) are non-biodegradable. They persist in landfill for hundreds of years. They can be recycled mechanically or used as feedstock for cracking.
  • Condensation polymers (polyesters, polyamides) can be hydrolysed (broken down by water, especially in acidic or alkaline conditions). This makes them more biodegradable than addition polymers, though the rate varies.
  • Biodegradable polymers such as PLA (polylactic acid) are made from renewable resources and can be composted.
Exam Tip: Be able to identify the monomer(s) from a section of a polymer chain and vice versa. For condensation polymers, remember to add back the water molecule that was lost when identifying monomers.

6.5 Carbonyl Compounds

Aldehydes and Ketones

Both contain the C=O (carbonyl) group. Aldehydes have the carbonyl at the end of the carbon chain (–CHO); ketones have it in the middle of the chain.

Nucleophilic Addition

Nucleophilic addition: A nucleophile attacks the electron-deficient (δ+) carbon of the C=O group. The pi bond breaks and the electrons shift to the oxygen, forming an alkoxide intermediate, which is then protonated.

Reaction with HCN (hydrogen cyanide): produces a hydroxynitrile (cyanohydrin). The nucleophile is CN−, which attacks the δ+ carbonyl carbon. The alkoxide intermediate is protonated by HCN. This reaction extends the carbon chain by one carbon and introduces a chiral centre, producing a racemic mixture.

CH3CHO + HCN → CH3CH(OH)CN
Exam Tip: In the nucleophilic addition mechanism with HCN, use KCN as the source of CN− in the presence of dilute acid (to generate small amounts of HCN in situ). This avoids handling the highly toxic HCN gas directly.

2,4-Dinitrophenylhydrazine (2,4-DNP) Test

2,4-DNP reacts with any carbonyl compound (aldehyde or ketone) to form an orange/yellow precipitate called a 2,4-dinitrophenylhydrazone. The precipitate can be purified by recrystallisation and its melting point measured and compared with data tables to identify the specific carbonyl compound. This is a test for the presence of a C=O group (but does not distinguish aldehydes from ketones).

6.6 Carboxylic Acids and Derivatives

Carboxylic Acids

Carboxylic acids contain the –COOH group. They are weak acids, dissociating partially in water to release H+ ions. They react with bases, carbonates, and reactive metals in typical acid reactions.

Esters

Esters are formed by the reaction of a carboxylic acid with an alcohol (Fischer esterification, using conc. H2SO4 catalyst, reflux) or more effectively by reacting an acyl chloride with an alcohol (vigorous reaction at room temperature, producing HCl as by-product).

Acyl Chlorides

Acyl chloride: A compound with the functional group –COCl. Acyl chlorides are highly reactive and undergo nucleophilic addition-elimination reactions readily at room temperature.

6.7 Chromatography

Thin-Layer Chromatography (TLC)

A quick qualitative technique. A spot of sample is placed on a silica/alumina plate and developed in a suitable solvent. Components separate based on their relative affinity for the stationary phase (silica) and mobile phase (solvent). Rf values are calculated and compared with standards for identification.

Gas Chromatography (GC)

The sample is vaporised and carried by an inert gas (e.g. nitrogen or helium) through a long coiled column. Components separate based on their boiling points and affinity for the stationary phase (liquid coated on the column walls). A detector produces a chromatogram showing retention time (x-axis) and signal intensity (y-axis). The area under each peak is proportional to the amount of that component.

High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC)

Similar principle to GC but uses a liquid mobile phase pumped at high pressure through a column packed with fine solid particles (stationary phase). HPLC is used for non-volatile or thermally unstable compounds that cannot be analysed by GC. UV detection is commonly used.

Exam Tip: GC-MS combines gas chromatography with mass spectrometry. GC separates the components of a mixture, then each component enters the mass spectrometer for identification via its fragmentation pattern and molecular ion peak. This is a powerful technique for identifying unknown compounds in complex mixtures.

6.8 NMR Spectroscopy

Carbon-13 (13C) NMR

13C NMR: A technique that detects the different chemical environments of carbon atoms in a molecule. Each chemically distinct carbon produces a single peak at a characteristic chemical shift (δ, in ppm). The number of peaks equals the number of non-equivalent carbon environments.

For example, ethanol (CH3CH2OH) has two peaks in its 13C NMR spectrum because it has two distinct carbon environments.

Proton (1H) NMR

1H NMR: A technique that detects the different chemical environments of hydrogen atoms (protons) in a molecule. It provides information about the number of proton environments, the relative number of protons in each environment, and the number of neighbouring protons.

Chemical Shift

The position of a peak on the x-axis, measured in ppm relative to the reference compound TMS (tetramethylsilane, Si(CH3)4), which is set at δ = 0. TMS is used because: it produces a single sharp peak (all 12 H atoms are equivalent), it is inert and volatile (easily removed), and it absorbs at a higher field than most organic protons.

Integration

The area under each peak (shown as a step or integration trace) is proportional to the number of protons in that environment. For example, if the integration ratio is 3:2:1, this tells you there are protons in the ratio 3:2:1 in those environments (e.g. CH3 : CH2 : CH).

Spin-Spin Splitting (Coupling)

A peak is split into (n + 1) sub-peaks, where n is the number of non-equivalent protons on adjacent carbon atoms. This follows the n + 1 rule.

Number of Adjacent H (n)Splitting PatternName
01 peakSinglet (s)
12 peaksDoublet (d)
23 peaksTriplet (t)
34 peaksQuartet (q)

O–H and N–H protons do not usually show splitting and appear as singlets. They can be identified using a D2O shake: adding deuterium oxide causes O–H and N–H peaks to disappear because the protons exchange with deuterium.

Exam Tip: In combined analytical technique questions, use all available data together: molecular formula (from mass spec M+), functional groups (from IR), degree of unsaturation (from molecular formula), carbon environments (13C NMR), proton environments with splitting and integration (1H NMR). Work systematically and eliminate possibilities.

Combined Techniques — Strategy

  1. Determine the molecular formula from the mass spectrum (M+ peak) and elemental analysis.
  2. Calculate the degree of unsaturation (also called index of hydrogen deficiency, IHD): IHD = (2C + 2 + N − H − X) / 2, where C, H, N, X are the numbers of carbon, hydrogen, nitrogen, and halogen atoms. An IHD of 4 strongly suggests a benzene ring.
  3. Identify functional groups from the IR spectrum (look for broad O–H, sharp C=O, etc.).
  4. Count distinct carbon environments from 13C NMR.
  5. Analyse 1H NMR: count proton environments, use integration to find ratios, use splitting to determine neighbours.
  6. Propose a structure consistent with all the data and verify that it explains every observation.
