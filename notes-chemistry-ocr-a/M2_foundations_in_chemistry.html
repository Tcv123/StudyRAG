2.1 Atoms, Ions and Compounds

Atomic Structure

Atom: The smallest particle of an element that can take part in a chemical reaction, consisting of a nucleus (protons and neutrons) surrounded by electrons.
ParticleRelative MassRelative ChargeLocation
Proton1+1Nucleus
Neutron10Nucleus
Electron1/1836 (negligible)−1Shells/orbitals around nucleus

Atomic number (Z) = number of protons. Mass number (A) = number of protons + neutrons. In a neutral atom, the number of electrons equals the number of protons.

Isotopes: Atoms of the same element (same number of protons) with different numbers of neutrons, and therefore different mass numbers. Isotopes have identical chemical properties but different physical properties (e.g. density, rate of diffusion).

Relative Atomic Mass

Relative atomic mass (Ar): The weighted mean mass of an atom of an element compared to 1/12 of the mass of a carbon-12 atom.
Ar = Σ(isotopic mass × percentage abundance) / 100

Relative atomic masses can be determined using a mass spectrometer. The mass spectrum shows m/z values (mass-to-charge ratio) on the x-axis and relative abundance on the y-axis.

Ions and Ionic Compounds

Ions form when atoms gain or lose electrons. Metals lose electrons to form positive cations (e.g. Na→Na+ + e−). Non-metals gain electrons to form negative anions (e.g. Cl + e−→Cl−). Ionic compounds are held together by strong electrostatic attractions between oppositely charged ions in a giant ionic lattice.

2.2 Amount of Substance

Mole: The amount of substance that contains 6.022 × 1023 particles (atoms, molecules, ions, etc.). This number is the Avogadro constant, NA.

Molar Mass

n = m / M

Where n = amount in moles, m = mass in grams, M = molar mass in g mol−1. The molar mass is numerically equal to the relative formula mass (Mr).

Empirical and Molecular Formulae

Empirical formula: The simplest whole-number ratio of atoms of each element in a compound.
Molecular formula: The actual number of atoms of each element in one molecule of a compound. It is a whole-number multiple of the empirical formula.

To find the empirical formula from percentage composition: divide each percentage by the element's Ar, then divide all results by the smallest to get the simplest ratio. To find the molecular formula, divide the Mr by the empirical formula mass to find the multiplier.

Exam Tip: If the ratio after dividing by the smallest is not a whole number (e.g. 1:1.5), multiply all values by the smallest integer needed (e.g. multiply by 2 to get 2:3).

Moles in Solutions

n = c × V

Where c = concentration in mol dm−3 and V = volume in dm3. Remember: to convert cm3 to dm3, divide by 1000.

Moles in Gases

At room temperature and pressure (RTP: 20 °C, 1 atm), one mole of any gas occupies approximately 24.0 dm3.

n = V / 24.0     (at RTP, V in dm3)

The Ideal Gas Equation

pV = nRT

Where p = pressure in Pa, V = volume in m3, n = moles, R = 8.314 J K−1 mol−1, T = temperature in K. Always convert units: cm3 → m3 (divide by 106), dm3 → m3 (divide by 1000), °C → K (add 273).

Exam Tip: The most common error with pV = nRT is forgetting to convert units. Always check: pressure in Pa, volume in m3, temperature in K.

Concentration in g dm−3

concentration (g dm−3) = concentration (mol dm−3) × Mr

2.3 Acid-Base and Redox Reactions

Acids and Bases

Acid: A proton (H+) donor. In aqueous solution, acids release H+ ions.
Base: A proton (H+) acceptor. A soluble base is called an alkali and releases OH− ions in aqueous solution.

Oxidation States (Oxidation Numbers)

Oxidation state: A number assigned to an atom to indicate the degree of oxidation or reduction it has undergone. It represents the number of electrons that an atom has lost, gained, or shared unequally.

Rules for assigning oxidation states:

  • Uncombined elements: 0
  • Simple ions: equal to the charge (Na+ = +1, Cl− = −1)
  • Oxygen: usually −2 (except in peroxides where it is −1, and in OF2 where it is +2)
  • Hydrogen: usually +1 (except in metal hydrides where it is −1)
  • Fluorine: always −1
  • The sum of oxidation states in a neutral compound = 0; in a polyatomic ion = charge of the ion

Redox Reactions

Oxidation: Loss of electrons / increase in oxidation state.
Reduction: Gain of electrons / decrease in oxidation state.
Remember: OIL RIG — Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain.

A redox reaction involves simultaneous oxidation and reduction. The substance that is oxidised is the reducing agent; the substance that is reduced is the oxidising agent.

Writing Redox Half-Equations and Ionic Equations

To write a balanced redox equation:

  1. Write separate half-equations for oxidation and reduction.
  2. Balance atoms other than O and H first.
  3. Balance O by adding H2O.
  4. Balance H by adding H+.
  5. Balance charge by adding electrons.
  6. Multiply half-equations so electrons cancel, then combine.
Exam Tip: Always check that your final equation is balanced for both atoms and charge. State symbols should be included where possible.

2.4 Electrons and Bonding

Electron Configuration

Electrons occupy orbitals in order of increasing energy. Each orbital holds a maximum of 2 electrons with opposite spins. The order of filling is: 1s, 2s, 2p, 3s, 3p, 4s, 3d, 4p.

Sub-shellNumber of OrbitalsMax ElectronsShape
s12Spherical
p36Dumbbell
d510Complex (clover-leaf)

For d-block elements, the 4s sub-shell fills before 3d but is lost first when forming ions. For example, Fe is [Ar] 3d6 4s2, but Fe2+ is [Ar] 3d6 (the two 4s electrons are removed first). Chromium and copper are exceptions: Cr is [Ar] 3d5 4s1 and Cu is [Ar] 3d10 4s1 due to the extra stability of half-filled and fully-filled d sub-shells.

Exam Tip: When writing the electron configuration of d-block ions, always remove the 4s electrons before removing any 3d electrons.

Ionic Bonding

Ionic bond: The strong electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions in an ionic lattice, formed by the transfer of electrons from a metal to a non-metal.

Properties of ionic compounds: high melting and boiling points (strong electrostatic forces), conduct electricity when molten or dissolved (ions are free to move), do not conduct when solid (ions are fixed in position), soluble in polar solvents like water.

Covalent Bonding

Covalent bond: A shared pair of electrons between two atoms, where both nuclei are attracted to the shared electron pair.

A dative (coordinate) bond is a covalent bond where both electrons in the shared pair come from the same atom. Shown as an arrow from the donor atom to the acceptor (e.g. in NH4+, the nitrogen donates a lone pair to H+).

Shapes of Molecules — VSEPR Theory

Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion (VSEPR) theory predicts molecular shapes based on the principle that electron pairs around a central atom repel each other and arrange themselves as far apart as possible. Lone pairs repel more strongly than bonding pairs.

Electronegativity and Bond Polarity

Electronegativity: The ability of an atom to attract the bonding pair of electrons in a covalent bond towards itself. It increases across a period and up a group.

If there is a difference in electronegativity between bonded atoms, the bond is polar. A large difference (>1.7) typically results in ionic bonding. A small difference gives a polar covalent bond. If the molecule is symmetrical (e.g. CO2, CCl4), the dipoles cancel and the molecule is non-polar overall, even if individual bonds are polar.

Metallic Bonding

Metallic bond: The strong electrostatic attraction between positive metal ions (cations) and a sea of delocalised electrons.

Properties: high melting points (strong forces), good electrical and thermal conductivity (delocalised electrons are free to move), malleable and ductile (layers of ions can slide over each other without breaking bonds).

Intermolecular Forces

Intermolecular forces act between molecules and are much weaker than covalent bonds. They determine physical properties such as boiling point and solubility.

Never say "bonds are broken" when discussing boiling — intermolecular forces are overcome, not covalent bonds.
