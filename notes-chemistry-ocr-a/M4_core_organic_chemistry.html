Types of Isomerism

Structural isomers: Molecules with the same molecular formula but different structural arrangements of atoms. Three types: chain isomerism (different carbon skeleton), position isomerism (functional group at different positions), and functional group isomerism (different functional groups).
Stereoisomers: Molecules with the same structural formula but a different arrangement of atoms in space. E/Z isomerism (a type of geometrical isomerism) occurs in alkenes when there is restricted rotation around the C=C bond and each carbon of the double bond has two different substituents.

E/Z isomers are distinguished using Cahn-Ingold-Prelog (CIP) priority rules based on atomic number. Z (zusammen, "together") has the higher priority groups on the same side; E (entgegen, "opposite") has them on opposite sides.

Exam Tip: For E/Z isomerism, you need restricted rotation (C=C) AND two different groups on each carbon of the double bond. If one carbon has two identical groups, E/Z isomers cannot exist.

4.2 Alkanes

Alkanes: Saturated hydrocarbons with only single C–C and C–H bonds. They are relatively unreactive due to the strength of C–C and C–H bonds and their non-polar nature.

Combustion

Complete combustion (excess oxygen) produces CO2 and H2O:

CH4(g) + 2O2(g) → CO2(g) + 2H2O(l)

Incomplete combustion (limited oxygen) produces CO and/or C (soot) along with H2O. Carbon monoxide is toxic because it binds irreversibly to haemoglobin, reducing the blood's ability to carry oxygen.

Free Radical Substitution

Alkanes react with halogens (Cl2, Br2) in the presence of UV light via a free radical mechanism. This has three stages:

1. Initiation: UV light provides the energy to break the Cl–Cl bond homolytically, producing two chlorine radicals.

Cl2 → 2Cl•

2. Propagation: A chain reaction in which one radical produces another. Each step generates a new radical.

Cl• + CH4 → •CH3 + HCl
•CH3 + Cl2 → CH3Cl + Cl•

3. Termination: Two radicals combine, removing radicals from the system.

Cl• + Cl• → Cl2     •CH3 + Cl• → CH3Cl     •CH3 + •CH3 → C2H6

Limitations: further substitution can occur (producing CH2Cl2, CHCl3, CCl4), so a mixture of products is obtained. This makes it unsuitable for preparing specific products in good yield.

Exam Tip: Always use the correct curly arrow notation: single-headed (fishhook) arrows for radical mechanisms showing the movement of one electron, and double-headed arrows for ionic mechanisms showing the movement of a pair of electrons.

4.3 Alkenes

Alkenes: Unsaturated hydrocarbons containing at least one C=C double bond. The double bond consists of a sigma bond (head-on overlap) and a pi bond (sideways overlap of p orbitals). The pi bond is an area of high electron density, making alkenes susceptible to attack by electrophiles.

Test for Unsaturation

Add bromine water to the sample. If a C=C is present, the orange/brown bromine water is decolourised as Br2 adds across the double bond.

Electrophilic Addition Reactions

Electrophilic addition: A reaction in which an electrophile is attracted to the electron-rich C=C double bond, breaking the pi bond and forming two new sigma bonds.

Mechanism of electrophilic addition with HBr:

  1. The electron-rich C=C double bond attracts the electrophile H–Br.
  2. The H–Br bond is polarised (δ+H–Brδ−). The pi electrons attack the δ+ H, forming a new C–H bond. The H–Br bond breaks heterolytically, forming Br−.
  3. A carbocation intermediate forms on the other carbon.
  4. The Br− ion acts as a nucleophile and attacks the carbocation, forming a C–Br bond.

Markownikoff's Rule

When HBr adds to an asymmetric alkene, the major product is the one where the H adds to the carbon already bonded to more hydrogen atoms. This is because the secondary (or tertiary) carbocation intermediate is more stable than the primary one. The greater stability arises from the inductive (electron-donating) effect of alkyl groups, which stabilises the positive charge.

Addition Polymers

Addition polymerisation: Many small alkene molecules (monomers) join together to form a long-chain polymer by opening their C=C double bonds to form C–C single bonds. No other substance is produced.
nCH2=CH2 → (–CH2–CH2–)n

Addition polymers such as poly(ethene) and poly(propene) are non-biodegradable because of the strong C–C backbone. Disposal is a significant environmental concern; recycling and feedstock recovery are more sustainable options.

Exam Tip: To produce an aldehyde from a primary alcohol, you must distil the product immediately as it forms. If you reflux instead, the aldehyde is further oxidised to a carboxylic acid.

Elimination

Heating an alcohol with concentrated sulfuric acid or passing alcohol vapour over hot aluminium oxide (Al2O3) removes water to form an alkene:

CH3CH2OH → CH2=CH2 + H2O   (conc. H2SO4 or Al2O3 catalyst, heat)

Esterification

An alcohol reacts with a carboxylic acid in the presence of a concentrated sulfuric acid catalyst to form an ester and water. This is a reversible reaction.

CH3COOH + CH3CH2OH ⇌ CH3COOCH2CH3 + H2O

4.5 Haloalkanes

Nucleophilic Substitution

Nucleophile: An electron pair donor that attacks a positively charged or electron-deficient carbon atom. Examples: OH−, NH3, CN−.

The carbon bonded to the halogen is electron-deficient (δ+) because halogens are more electronegative. Nucleophiles attack this carbon.

Reactivity of Haloalkanes

The rate of hydrolysis depends on the strength of the C–X bond. Bond strength decreases down the group: C–F > C–Cl > C–Br > C–I. Therefore, iodoalkanes react fastest and fluoroalkanes react slowest. This is a bond enthalpy effect, not an electronegativity effect.

Ozone Depletion

Chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs) were widely used as refrigerants and propellants. In the stratosphere, UV radiation breaks the C–Cl bond homolytically to produce chlorine radicals that catalytically destroy ozone:

Cl• + O3 → ClO• + O2
ClO• + O3 → Cl• + 2O2

The chlorine radical is regenerated, so one CFC molecule can destroy thousands of ozone molecules. CFCs have been replaced by HCFCs and HFCs, which break down in the lower atmosphere before reaching the ozone layer.

4.6 Organic Synthesis

Organic synthesis involves planning a series of reactions to convert a starting material into a desired product. You should be able to suggest suitable reagents and conditions for each step and recognise how different functional groups can be interconverted.

Exam Tip: Learn the key reaction pathways connecting alkanes, alkenes, haloalkanes, alcohols, aldehydes, ketones, carboxylic acids, and esters. Be prepared to describe multi-step syntheses with full reagents and conditions at each step.

4.7 Analytical Techniques (AS)

Mass Spectrometry

A mass spectrometer determines the relative molecular mass (Mr) of a compound. The molecular ion peak (M+) has the highest m/z value (ignoring isotope peaks) and corresponds to the intact molecule minus one electron. Fragmentation patterns provide information about the structure.

Infrared (IR) Spectroscopy

IR spectroscopy identifies functional groups by measuring which frequencies of infrared radiation are absorbed by a molecule. Bonds absorb IR radiation and vibrate (stretch or bend) at characteristic frequencies.

The fingerprint region (below 1500 cm−1) is unique to each compound and can be matched against a database for positive identification.

Exam Tip: In IR spectroscopy questions, you will be given a data table of characteristic absorptions. Look for the presence or absence of key peaks: a broad O–H around 3300 cm−1 (alcohol), a very broad O–H around 2500–3300 cm−1 (carboxylic acid), or a sharp C=O around 1700 cm−1 (carbonyl). The disappearance or appearance of peaks confirms a reaction has occurred.
