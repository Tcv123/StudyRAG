Module 3 — Periodic Table and Energy

3.1 Periodicity

Periodicity: The repeating pattern of physical and chemical properties of elements across each period of the periodic table, caused by the repeating pattern of electron configurations.

Trends Across Period 3 (Na to Ar)

PropertyTrend Across Period 3Explanation
Atomic radiusDecreases Na → ArIncreasing nuclear charge pulls electrons closer; same shielding (same number of inner shells)
First ionisation energyGenerally increasesIncreasing nuclear charge with same shielding means more energy needed to remove an electron
Melting pointIncreases Na→Al, then Si very high, then drops sharply for P, S, Cl, ArNa, Mg, Al: metallic bonding strengthens (more delocalised electrons, smaller ionic radius). Si: giant covalent (very strong). P4, S8, Cl2, Ar: simple molecular (weak London forces)
ElectronegativityIncreases Na → ClGreater nuclear charge attracts bonding electrons more strongly

First Ionisation Energy Anomalies

First ionisation energy: The energy required to remove one electron from each atom in one mole of gaseous atoms to form one mole of gaseous 1+ ions.
X(g) → X+(g) + e−

Two anomalies occur across Period 3:

  • Mg → Al drop: Al has its outer electron in a 3p orbital, which is higher in energy and further from the nucleus than the 3s orbital of Mg. The 3p electron is also partially shielded by the 3s2 electrons. Less energy is needed to remove it.
  • P → S drop: Sulfur has a paired electron in one of its 3p orbitals (3p4). The repulsion between the two paired electrons makes it easier to remove one compared to removing an unpaired electron from phosphorus (3p3, which has a stable half-filled arrangement).
Exam Tip: When explaining ionisation energy anomalies, always mention the specific sub-shell involved, and explain the factor (shielding by a sub-shell or electron pairing repulsion) that makes removal easier.

3.2 Group 2 — The Alkaline Earth Metals

Trends Down Group 2

PropertyTrendExplanation

First ionisation energyDecreasesOuter electrons are further from the nucleus with more shielding, despite increasing nuclear charge
ReactivityIncreasesOuter electrons are lost more easily (lower ionisation energy)
Melting pointGenerally decreases (with some anomalies)Metallic bond weakens as ionic radius increases and charge density decreases

Reactions of Group 2 Elements

With water: Group 2 metals react with water to form a metal hydroxide and hydrogen gas. Reactivity increases down the group.

Mg(s) + 2H2O(l) → Mg(OH)2(aq) + H2(g)   (very slow with cold water)
Ca(s) + 2H2O(l) → Ca(OH)2(aq) + H2(g)   (steady reaction)
Ba(s) + 2H2O(l) → Ba(OH)2(aq) + H2(g)   (vigorous)

With dilute acid: All Group 2 metals react vigorously with acids.

Mg(s) + 2HCl(aq) → MgCl2(aq) + H2(g)

Thermal Decomposition of Group 2 Carbonates and Nitrates

Group 2 carbonates decompose on heating to form the metal oxide and carbon dioxide:

CaCO3(s) → CaO(s) + CO2(g)

The temperature required for decomposition increases down the group. This is because larger cations polarise the carbonate ion less, making it harder to decompose. MgCO3 decomposes at a relatively low temperature, while BaCO3 requires a very high temperature.

Solubility Trends

Hydroxides: Solubility increases down Group 2 (Mg(OH)2 is sparingly soluble; Ba(OH)2 is soluble). Sulfates: Solubility decreases down Group 2 (MgSO4 is soluble; BaSO4 is insoluble).

Disproportionation

Disproportionation: A reaction in which the same element is simultaneously oxidised and reduced.

Chlorine reacts with cold dilute sodium hydroxide in a disproportionation reaction:

Cl2(aq) + 2NaOH(aq) → NaCl(aq) + NaClO(aq) + H2O(l)

Chlorine starts at oxidation state 0. In NaCl it is −1 (reduced) and in NaClO it is +1 (oxidised). Sodium hypochlorite (NaClO) is the active ingredient in bleach and is used in water treatment to kill bacteria.

3.5 Enthalpy Changes

Enthalpy change (ΔH): The heat energy change at constant pressure during a chemical reaction. Exothermic reactions have negative ΔH (heat released); endothermic reactions have positive ΔH (heat absorbed).

Standard Enthalpy Definitions

TermSymbolDefinition
Standard enthalpy of combustionΔcH°Enthalpy change when one mole of a substance undergoes complete combustion under standard conditions (298 K, 100 kPa), all reactants and products in standard states
Standard enthalpy of formationΔfH°Enthalpy change when one mole of a compound is formed from its elements in their standard states under standard conditions
Standard enthalpy of neutralisationΔneutH°Enthalpy change when an acid and a base react to form one mole of water under standard conditions

Calorimetry

q = mcΔT

Where q = heat energy (J), m = mass of solution (g), c = specific heat capacity (4.18 J g−1 K−1 for water), ΔT = temperature change (K or °C). To find the enthalpy change per mole, divide q by the number of moles of the limiting reagent.

Exam Tip: In calorimetry calculations, assume the density of the solution is 1.00 g cm−3 and the specific heat capacity is 4.18 J g−1 K−1 (same as water) unless told otherwise. Remember that calorimetry gives inaccurate results because of heat loss to the surroundings.

Hess's Law

Hess's Law: The total enthalpy change for a reaction is independent of the route taken, provided the initial and final conditions are the same.

Using enthalpies of formation:

ΔrH° = ΣΔfH°(products) − ΣΔfH°(reactants)

Using enthalpies of combustion:

ΔrH° = ΣΔcH°(reactants) − ΣΔcH°(products)

Bond Enthalpies

Mean bond enthalpy: The average energy needed to break one mole of a specific type of bond in gaseous molecules, averaged across a range of different compounds.
ΔrH° ≈ Σ(bond enthalpies broken) − Σ(bond enthalpies formed)

Bond enthalpy calculations are only approximate because mean bond enthalpies are averages and the exact energy depends on the molecular environment.

3.6 Reaction Rates

Rate of reaction: The change in concentration of a reactant or product per unit time.

Collision Theory

For a reaction to occur, particles must collide with sufficient energy (greater than or equal to the activation energy, Ea) and with the correct orientation. Factors that increase rate:

  • Increasing concentration: More particles per unit volume means more frequent collisions.
  • Increasing temperature: Particles have greater kinetic energy, so more collisions exceed Ea; collision frequency also increases slightly.
  • Increasing surface area: More particles are exposed for collision (applies to solids).
  • Using a catalyst: Provides an alternative reaction pathway with a lower Ea.

Boltzmann Distribution

The Boltzmann distribution shows the spread of molecular energies at a given temperature. Key features: starts at the origin, rises steeply to a peak (the most probable energy), then tails off asymptotically at high energies (never touches the x-axis). At higher temperatures, the curve shifts right, flattens, and broadens. The area under the curve (total number of molecules) remains constant. More molecules have energy greater than or equal to Ea, so the rate increases.

Exam Tip: When drawing Boltzmann distributions, ensure the curve starts at the origin, the peak shifts right and down at higher temperature, and the area under both curves is equal. Label Ea on the x-axis and shade the area to the right of it.

Catalysts

Catalyst: A substance that increases the rate of a reaction by providing an alternative reaction pathway with a lower activation energy, without being consumed in the reaction.

On a Boltzmann distribution, a catalyst is shown by a new, lower Ea line. More molecules now have energy exceeding this lower activation energy, so the rate increases. The distribution curve itself does not change.

3.7 Chemical Equilibrium

Dynamic equilibrium: A state reached in a closed system when the rate of the forward reaction equals the rate of the reverse reaction. The concentrations of reactants and products remain constant but are not necessarily equal. Both reactions continue to occur.

Le Chatelier's Principle

Le Chatelier's Principle: If a system at equilibrium is subjected to a change in conditions (concentration, pressure, or temperature), the position of equilibrium shifts to counteract the change and partially restore the original conditions.
ChangeEffect on EquilibriumEffect on Kc
Increase concentration of reactantShifts to the right (towards products)No change
Increase pressure (gaseous equilibrium)Shifts to the side with fewer moles of gasNo change
Increase temperature (exothermic forward)Shifts to the left (endothermic direction)Kc decreases
Increase temperature (endothermic forward)Shifts to the rightKc increases
Add a catalystNo change in positionNo change

Equilibrium Constant Kc

For the reaction: aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD

Kc = [C]c[D]d / [A]a[B]b

Kc is only affected by temperature. A large Kc means the equilibrium lies to the right (products favoured). A small Kc means the equilibrium lies to the left (reactants favoured). Changes in concentration, pressure, or adding a catalyst do not change Kc.

Exam Tip: When calculating Kc, always use equilibrium concentrations (not initial). Set up an ICE table (Initial, Change, Equilibrium) to find equilibrium concentrations. Include the units of Kc, which depend on the overall change in moles.
