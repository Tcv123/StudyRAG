1.6: Further Vectors

1. Scalar (Dot) Product — Review

Dot product: For vectors \(\mathbf{a}\) and \(\mathbf{b}\): $$\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = |\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|\cos\theta$$ where \(\theta\) is the angle between them. In component form (3D): $$\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b} = a_1b_1 + a_2b_2 + a_3b_3$$ Vectors are perpendicular iff \(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b} = 0\).

2. Vector (Cross) Product

Cross product: For vectors \(\mathbf{a}\) and \(\mathbf{b}\) in 3D: $$\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b} = |\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|\sin\theta\,\hat{\mathbf{n}}$$ where \(\hat{\mathbf{n}}\) is the unit vector perpendicular to both \(\mathbf{a}\) and \(\mathbf{b}\) (right-hand rule).

Component formula: $$\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b} = \begin{vmatrix}\mathbf{i}&\mathbf{j}&\mathbf{k}\\a_1&a_2&a_3\\b_1&b_2&b_3\end{vmatrix} = (a_2b_3-a_3b_2)\mathbf{i} - (a_1b_3-a_3b_1)\mathbf{j} + (a_1b_2-a_2b_1)\mathbf{k}$$

Properties of the Cross Product

  • \(\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b} = -(\mathbf{b}\times\mathbf{a})\) — anti-commutative
  • \(\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{a} = \mathbf{0}\)
  • \(\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b} = \mathbf{0}\) iff \(\mathbf{a}\) and \(\mathbf{b}\) are parallel
  • \(|\mathbf{i}\times\mathbf{j}| = |\mathbf{j}\times\mathbf{k}| = |\mathbf{k}\times\mathbf{i}| = 1\); \(\mathbf{i}\times\mathbf{j} = \mathbf{k}\), \(\mathbf{j}\times\mathbf{k} = \mathbf{i}\), \(\mathbf{k}\times\mathbf{i} = \mathbf{j}\)
  • Distributive: \(\mathbf{a}\times(\mathbf{b}+\mathbf{c}) = \mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b} + \mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{c}\)
Example: Find \(\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b}\) where \(\mathbf{a} = \begin{pmatrix}1\\2\\3\end{pmatrix}\) and \(\mathbf{b} = \begin{pmatrix}4\\5\\6\end{pmatrix}\).

\(\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b} = \begin{vmatrix}\mathbf{i}&\mathbf{j}&\mathbf{k}\\1&2&3\\4&5&6\end{vmatrix}\)
\(= (2\cdot6-3\cdot5)\mathbf{i} - (1\cdot6-3\cdot4)\mathbf{j} + (1\cdot5-2\cdot4)\mathbf{k}\)
\(= (12-15)\mathbf{i} - (6-12)\mathbf{j} + (5-8)\mathbf{k} = -3\mathbf{i}+6\mathbf{j}-3\mathbf{k} = \begin{pmatrix}-3\\6\\-3\end{pmatrix}\)

Area Using the Cross Product

$$\text{Area of parallelogram} = |\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b}|$$ $$\text{Area of triangle} = \frac{1}{2}|\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b}|$$

3. Scalar Triple Product

Scalar triple product: $$[\mathbf{a},\mathbf{b},\mathbf{c}] = \mathbf{a}\cdot(\mathbf{b}\times\mathbf{c}) = \begin{vmatrix}a_1&a_2&a_3\\b_1&b_2&b_3\\c_1&c_2&c_3\end{vmatrix}$$ The volume of a parallelepiped with edges \(\mathbf{a}\), \(\mathbf{b}\), \(\mathbf{c}\) is \(|\mathbf{a}\cdot(\mathbf{b}\times\mathbf{c})|\).

Vectors \(\mathbf{a}\), \(\mathbf{b}\), \(\mathbf{c}\) are coplanar iff \(\mathbf{a}\cdot(\mathbf{b}\times\mathbf{c}) = 0\).
Example: Find the volume of the parallelepiped with edges \(\mathbf{a} = \mathbf{i}+2\mathbf{j}\), \(\mathbf{b} = 3\mathbf{j}+\mathbf{k}\), \(\mathbf{c} = \mathbf{i}+\mathbf{j}+\mathbf{k}\).

\(\mathbf{a}\cdot(\mathbf{b}\times\mathbf{c}) = \begin{vmatrix}1&2&0\\0&3&1\\1&1&1\end{vmatrix} = 1(3-1)-2(0-1)+0 = 2+2 = 4\)
Volume \(= |4| = 4\).

4. Equations of Lines in 3D

Vector equation of a line: $$\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{a} + \lambda\mathbf{d}$$ where \(\mathbf{a}\) is a position vector of a point on the line and \(\mathbf{d}\) is the direction vector.

Cartesian (symmetric) form: $$\frac{x-a_1}{d_1} = \frac{y-a_2}{d_2} = \frac{z-a_3}{d_3}$$

Angle Between Two Lines

The acute angle \(\theta\) between lines with directions \(\mathbf{d}_1\) and \(\mathbf{d}_2\):

$$\cos\theta = \frac{|\mathbf{d}_1\cdot\mathbf{d}_2|}{|\mathbf{d}_1||\mathbf{d}_2|}$$

5. Equations of Planes

Vector equation of a plane: \(\mathbf{r}\cdot\mathbf{n} = d\), where \(\mathbf{n}\) is the normal vector and \(d = \mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{n}\) for any point \(\mathbf{a}\) on the plane.

Cartesian equation: \(n_1 x + n_2 y + n_3 z = d\)

Angle Between a Line and a Plane

The angle \(\phi\) between a line with direction \(\mathbf{d}\) and a plane with normal \(\mathbf{n}\):

$$\sin\phi = \frac{|\mathbf{d}\cdot\mathbf{n}|}{|\mathbf{d}||\mathbf{n}|}$$

(Note: the angle between a line and a plane is the complement of the angle between the line and the normal.)

Angle Between Two Planes

The angle between two planes equals the angle between their normals \(\mathbf{n}_1\) and \(\mathbf{n}_2\):

$$\cos\theta = \frac{|\mathbf{n}_1\cdot\mathbf{n}_2|}{|\mathbf{n}_1||\mathbf{n}_2|}$$

6. Distance Formulae

Distance from a Point to a Plane

$$d = \frac{|n_1 x_0 + n_2 y_0 + n_3 z_0 - D|}{|\mathbf{n}|}$$ where \((x_0, y_0, z_0)\) is the point and \(n_1 x + n_2 y + n_3 z = D\) is the plane.

Distance Between Two Parallel Lines

Let \(\ell_1: \mathbf{r} = \mathbf{a} + \lambda\mathbf{d}\) and \(\ell_2: \mathbf{r} = \mathbf{b} + \mu\mathbf{d}\). The distance is:

$$d = \frac{|(\mathbf{b}-\mathbf{a})\times\mathbf{d}|}{|\mathbf{d}|}$$

Shortest Distance Between Two Skew Lines

For skew lines \(\ell_1: \mathbf{r} = \mathbf{a} + \lambda\mathbf{d}_1\) and \(\ell_2: \mathbf{r} = \mathbf{b} + \mu\mathbf{d}_2\):

$$d = \frac{|(\mathbf{b}-\mathbf{a})\cdot(\mathbf{d}_1\times\mathbf{d}_2)|}{|\mathbf{d}_1\times\mathbf{d}_2|}$$
Example: Find the shortest distance between the skew lines
\(\ell_1: \mathbf{r} = \begin{pmatrix}1\\0\\0\end{pmatrix} + \lambda\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\\1\end{pmatrix}\) and \(\ell_2: \mathbf{r} = \begin{pmatrix}0\\1\\0\end{pmatrix} + \mu\begin{pmatrix}0\\1\\1\end{pmatrix}\).

\(\mathbf{d}_1\times\mathbf{d}_2 = \begin{pmatrix}1\\0\\1\end{pmatrix}\times\begin{pmatrix}0\\1\\1\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}0-1\\0-1\\1-0\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}-1\\-1\\1\end{pmatrix}\)
\(|\mathbf{d}_1\times\mathbf{d}_2| = \sqrt{3}\)

\(\mathbf{b}-\mathbf{a} = \begin{pmatrix}-1\\1\\0\end{pmatrix}\)
\((\mathbf{b}-\mathbf{a})\cdot\begin{pmatrix}-1\\-1\\1\end{pmatrix} = 1 - 1 + 0 = 0\)

Shortest distance \(= 0/\sqrt{3} = 0\). (Lines actually intersect — check if skew by testing for solution to \(\ell_1 = \ell_2\).)

7. Intersections

Line and Plane

Substitute the line equation \(\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{a} + \lambda\mathbf{d}\) into the plane equation \(\mathbf{r}\cdot\mathbf{n} = D\) and solve for \(\lambda\).

Two Planes

Two non-parallel planes intersect in a line. Find the direction as \(\mathbf{n}_1\times\mathbf{n}_2\); find a point by setting one variable to a convenient value and solving the system.

Three Planes — Analysis

A system of 3 planes can have:

  • Unique intersection point: \(\det(\text{coefficient matrix}) \neq 0\)
  • A line of intersection: Two planes intersect in a line that lies on the third
  • No intersection (inconsistent): Various configurations — triangular prism, or all parallel, etc.
AQA Exam Tip: For 3-plane systems, AQA often gives a parameter \(k\) in one equation and asks for the value(s) of \(k\) that give different types of intersection. Use \(\det A = 0\) for the singular case, then test consistency.
Example: Find the point of intersection of the line \(\mathbf{r} = \begin{pmatrix}1\\2\\-1\end{pmatrix}+\lambda\begin{pmatrix}1\\-1\\2\end{pmatrix}\) and the plane \(2x+y-z=3\).

Substitute: \(2(1+\lambda)+(2-\lambda)-(-1+2\lambda) = 3\)
\(2+2\lambda+2-\lambda+1-2\lambda = 3\)
\(5 - \lambda = 3 \Rightarrow \lambda = 2\)

Point: \(\begin{pmatrix}1+2\\2-2\\-1+4\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}3\\0\\3\end{pmatrix}\)

9. Practice Checklist

  • Can you compute dot products and cross products in component form?
  • Can you find the area of a triangle given three vertices using the cross product?
  • Can you find and use the scalar triple product?
  • Can you find angles between lines, between a line and a plane, and between two planes?
  • Can you find the distance from a point to a plane?
  • Can you find the intersection of a line with a plane?
  • Can you analyse when a 3-plane system has no solution, a line of solutions, or a unique solution?
