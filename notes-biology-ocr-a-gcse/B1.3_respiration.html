1. What is Respiration?

Respiration is the chemical process that takes place in every cell of every living organism, transferring energy from glucose to a usable form: ATP (adenosine triphosphate). It is an exothermic reaction — energy is released, often as heat. Do not confuse respiration with breathing! Breathing is the movement of air in and out of the lungs. Respiration is the cellular process that uses oxygen and glucose.

Energy released by respiration is used by the cell for:

  • Building larger molecules from smaller ones (e.g. proteins from amino acids)
  • Muscle contraction
  • Active transport across cell membranes
  • Maintaining body temperature in mammals and birds (warm-blooded animals)
  • Keeping the heart and other organs working

2. Aerobic Respiration

Aerobic respiration uses oxygen to break down glucose, releasing the maximum amount of energy. It occurs in the mitochondria.
Word: glucose + oxygen → carbon dioxide + water (+ energy)
Symbol: C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O (+ energy / ATP)

3. Anaerobic Respiration

Anaerobic respiration happens when there is not enough oxygen to keep up with energy demand (e.g. during intense exercise). It releases far less energy per molecule of glucose than aerobic respiration because glucose is not fully broken down.

In animals (incl. humans)

glucose → lactic acid (+ a small amount of energy)

Lactic acid builds up in muscles, causing fatigue and a burning sensation. After intense exercise, the body needs to take in extra oxygen to break down the lactic acid (the oxygen debt). This is why you continue to breathe heavily after stopping exercise.

In plants and yeast — fermentation

glucose → ethanol + carbon dioxide (+ a small amount of energy)

This anaerobic process in yeast is called fermentation. It is used commercially to make:

  • Bread — CO₂ released by yeast makes the dough rise. The ethanol evaporates during baking.
  • Beer / wine — ethanol gives the alcoholic drink its alcohol content. The CO₂ produces fizz.

4. Exercise — Heart Rate, Breathing Rate, Oxygen Debt

During exercise:

  • Heart rate increases → more blood pumped per minute → more oxygen and glucose delivered to muscles, more CO₂ removed.
  • Breathing rate and depth increase → more oxygen taken in, more CO₂ removed.
  • Muscles use more oxygen for aerobic respiration. If demand exceeds supply, anaerobic respiration begins.
Oxygen debt: the extra oxygen needed AFTER exercise to break down the lactic acid that built up during anaerobic respiration. Lactic acid is broken down (in the liver) to CO₂ and water.

5. Metabolism and Metabolic Rate

Metabolism = the sum of all chemical reactions occurring in cells (including respiration). Metabolic rate = the rate at which these reactions take place — usually measured by oxygen consumption or heat production.

Metabolic rate is affected by:

  • Activity — exercise increases metabolic rate.
  • Body temperature — fever raises metabolic rate.
  • Genetics, age, sex, body composition — more muscle mass = higher BMR.
  • Hormones e.g. thyroxine increases metabolic rate.

6. Required Practical Link

Linked PAG: investigating the rate of respiration in living organisms. Often uses germinating peas / small invertebrates with a respirometer measuring O₂ consumption (a coloured liquid moves towards the organisms as O₂ is used). See PAG notes for full method.

Common exam mistake: writing "energy is produced by respiration". Respiration transfers or releases energy from glucose. Energy cannot be created or destroyed.
