1. Surface Area to Volume Ratio

Small organisms (e.g. bacteria, single-celled algae) have a large surface area to volume ratio (SA:V), so substances can diffuse in/out fast enough to meet their needs. As organisms get larger, SA:V decreases — diffusion alone is too slow. Multicellular organisms therefore need specialised exchange surfaces and transport systems.

2. Adaptations of Exchange Surfaces

Effective exchange surfaces — like the alveoli in lungs, villi in the small intestine, or gills in fish — share these features:

  • Large surface area (folds, villi, alveoli) → more particles cross per second
  • Thin / one-cell thick walls → short diffusion distance
  • Good blood supply (in animals) → maintains a steep concentration gradient
  • Ventilation (where appropriate) e.g. breathing or gill movement → maintains concentration gradient
  • Moist surface → gases dissolve so they can diffuse

3. The Heart and Circulatory System

Humans have a double circulatory system — blood passes through the heart TWICE for every full circuit. This separates oxygenated and deoxygenated blood, allows higher pressure to body tissues, and is more efficient.

4. The Lungs and Gas Exchange

The lungs are adapted for gas exchange:

  • Many alveoli → huge surface area (~70 m² in adult humans)
  • Walls one cell thick + close to capillaries (1 cell thick) → very short diffusion distance
  • Excellent blood supply from pulmonary capillaries → maintains concentration gradient (CO₂ removed, O₂ taken away)
  • Ventilation by ribs/diaphragm → keeps fresh air arriving at alveoli
  • Moist lining → gases dissolve to diffuse across cell membranes

Transpiration = the loss of water vapour from a plant, mainly through the stomata in the leaves. As water evaporates, more water is pulled up the xylem from the roots. This continuous flow is the transpiration stream.

Factors affecting rate of transpiration

  • Temperature ↑ — water molecules have more KE → faster evaporation
  • Humidity ↓ — drier air → bigger water concentration gradient → faster transpiration
  • Wind speed ↑ — removes humid air around the leaf → maintains gradient
  • Light intensity ↑ — stomata open wider for photosynthesis → more water lost

Guard cells

Each stoma is bounded by two guard cells. When water enters them they swell and bow outwards, opening the stoma. When they lose water, they go floppy and close the stoma — limiting water loss. Most stomata are on the lower surface of the leaf to reduce direct sun exposure and water loss.
