1. The Nervous System Overview

The nervous system uses electrical impulses to allow rapid responses to changes in the environment. It is divided into the central nervous system (CNS) — brain and spinal cord — and the peripheral nervous system (PNS) — all the nerves connecting the CNS to the rest of the body.

The basic flow of information is:

stimulus → receptor → sensory neurone → CNS → motor neurone → effector → response

  • Stimulus = a change in the environment (e.g. heat, light, sound, pressure, chemical)
  • Receptor = a specialised cell that detects the stimulus (e.g. light receptors in the retina)
  • Sensory neurone = carries impulse from receptor to CNS
  • CNS = the brain and spinal cord; processes information and coordinates a response
  • Relay neurone (in CNS) = passes the signal between sensory and motor neurones
  • Motor neurone = carries impulse from CNS to effector
  • Effector = a muscle (which contracts) or a gland (which secretes a hormone)

2. Neurones

TypeFunctionSpecial features
Sensory neuroneCarries impulse from receptor → CNSLong dendrons; cell body off to the side
Relay neuroneConnects sensory and motor neurones inside CNSShort axon and dendrons; located in CNS
Motor neuroneCarries impulse from CNS → effectorLong axon; many dendrites; ends at muscle/gland

Adaptations of a neurone

  • Long axon — carries the impulse over a long distance
  • Many dendrites — connect to many other neurones
  • Myelin sheath (a fatty insulator) — speeds up impulse and prevents short-circuit between neurones
  • Many mitochondria at the synapse — release energy for neurotransmitter release

3. Synapses

A synapse is a small gap between two neurones. Electrical impulses cannot jump the gap, so the message is carried across by chemicals called neurotransmitters.
  1. Electrical impulse arrives at the end of the pre-synaptic neurone.
  2. Vesicles containing neurotransmitter fuse with the membrane and release neurotransmitter into the synaptic gap.
  3. Neurotransmitter diffuses across the gap.
  4. Neurotransmitter binds to receptors on the post-synaptic neurone — triggering a new electrical impulse.
  5. Neurotransmitter is broken down or reabsorbed (so the signal stops).

4. The Reflex Arc

A reflex action is a rapid, automatic, involuntary response to a stimulus that protects the body from damage (e.g. pulling your hand away from a hot object). It does NOT involve the conscious brain — the path is shorter, so the response is faster.

Studying the brain: scientists can map function by studying patients with brain damage in specific areas, by stimulating brain electrically during surgery, or by using MRI scans to see which areas are active during specific tasks.
