1. Biodiversity

Biodiversity = the variety of living organisms in an ecosystem. It includes the variety of species, the genetic variety within each species, and the variety of ecosystems present. High biodiversity makes ecosystems more stable and resilient.

Biodiversity matters because:

  • Ecosystems with more species are more stable — better at resisting disease or environmental change
  • Provides essential ecosystem services (pollination, water purification, soil formation, oxygen, climate regulation)
  • Source of medicines (many drugs come from plants, fungi, microbes)
  • Cultural/aesthetic value

Mark-release-recapture (Lincoln Index)

Capture, mark, release a sample of organisms. Wait for them to mix back into the population, then capture a second sample. Estimate population using:

N = (n₁ × n₂) ÷ m

where N = total population, n₁ = first sample (marked), n₂ = second sample, m = recaptured marked individuals.

Worked example

A scientist marks 50 woodlice. After release, they capture 60 woodlice; 10 are marked. Estimate the population.

N = (50 × 60) ÷ 10 = 300 woodlice.

Assumptions: no births/deaths/migration; marking does not affect survival; marked individuals mix randomly with population.

3. Indicator Species and Pollution Monitoring

An indicator species is a species whose presence (or absence) indicates the level of pollution. They are sensitive to specific pollutants.
PollutionIndicator speciesIndicates
Air (sulfur dioxide)LichensMany lichen types = clean air; few or none = polluted air
Air (SO₂)Blackspot fungus on rosesPresent = clean; absent = polluted (because SO₂ kills the fungus)
Water (low O₂ / sewage)Mayfly nymphs / stonefly nymphsPresent = clean, oxygenated water
Water (high pollution)Bloodworms / sludgewormsPresent = polluted water (low O₂)

Non-living monitoring: scientists also use sensors to measure pH, temperature, dissolved O₂, nitrate, particulates, CO₂, etc. directly.

Eutrophication

Climate change

Increased CO₂, methane and other greenhouse gases trap infrared radiation in the atmosphere, raising average temperatures (the greenhouse effect). Effects include:

  • Melting polar ice → rising sea levels
  • More extreme weather (storms, droughts, floods)
  • Shifting species ranges (some can\'t adapt → extinction)
  • Coral bleaching from warming ocean and acidification (more dissolved CO₂)

5. Conservation

Conservation = active management of the environment to protect species and ecosystems.
MethodHow it helps
Breeding programmes (in zoos)Increase numbers of endangered species — release back into wild
Seed banksPreserve genetic material of plants — useful if a species goes extinct in the wild
Protected areas (national parks, marine reserves)Habitat preserved; hunting/fishing restricted; species protected by law
Reintroduction programmesCaptive-bred individuals released to restore populations
Reducing deforestation / replantingRestores habitat; locks up carbon
Education and ecotourismRaises awareness; provides funding for conservation
Legal protection (CITES)Bans trade in endangered species

Trade-offs: conservation may conflict with farming, building, business interests. Some argue protecting nature is essential; others prioritise economic development. Solutions often involve compromise (e.g. sustainable forestry, reserves with buffer zones).

6. Sustainable Fisheries

Overfishing threatens fish populations and the wider ecosystem. Sustainable fishing techniques aim to harvest fish in a way that does not deplete the population.
MethodHow it works
Net mesh size limitsLarger mesh lets young/small fish escape so they can reproduce
Fishing quotasLimit total weight of each species caught per year
Closed seasonsNo fishing during spawning periods
Marine protected areasNo-fishing zones allow populations to recover
