3. Interdependence and Competition

Within a community, every species depends on others for things like food, shelter, pollination and seed dispersal. This is interdependence. A change in one species can affect many others. Organisms compete with each other for resources that are in short supply.
Plants compete for…Animals compete for…
Light, water, mineral ions, space (root and growing room)Food, water, mates, territory, shelter

4. Food Chains and Food Webs

A food chain shows the feeding relationships in a community. Energy is transferred along the chain from producer (a green plant or alga) → primary consumer (herbivore) → secondary consumer (carnivore) → tertiary consumer (top predator). Each step is a trophic level.

A food web shows interconnected food chains in a community — most organisms have multiple food sources and may be prey for multiple predators.

5. Predator-Prey Relationships

Predator populations and prey populations are linked. When prey numbers increase, predators have more food and reproduce more — predator numbers rise. As predator numbers rise, more prey are eaten — prey numbers fall. With less food, predator numbers also fall. This produces a cyclic (oscillating) pattern.

6. Sampling — Quadrats and Transects

Scientists sample populations because counting every organism is impractical.

Quadrats

A quadrat is a square frame of known area (e.g. 0.25 m² or 1 m²). It is placed at random positions in the habitat (random number generator for x, y coordinates) and the species inside are counted.

Estimate total number = (mean per quadrat) × (total area ÷ area of quadrat).

Worked example: 10 quadrats of 0.5 m × 0.5 m (= 0.25 m²) in a field of 1000 m². Mean = 8 daisies per quadrat. Estimate = 8 × (1000 ÷ 0.25) = 32 000 daisies.

Transects

A transect is a line stretched across an area to investigate how a population changes along an environmental gradient (e.g. distance from a pond). Quadrats are placed at regular intervals along the line.

7. Decay and Cycling of Materials

Decay is the breakdown of dead organisms and waste by microorganisms (bacteria and fungi — decomposers). Decay returns mineral ions (e.g. nitrate) to the soil, ready to be reused.

Conditions affecting rate of decay:

  • Temperature ↑ — faster (up to optimum); enzyme reactions speed up
  • Moisture ↑ — needed for enzyme reactions, dissolves food, and prevents drying out
  • Oxygen ↑ — most decomposers respire aerobically
  • pH — neutral usually best

9. The Water Cycle

Key processes: evaporation (from oceans, lakes, soil) → water vapour rises → condensation in cooler air → forms clouds → precipitation (rain/snow) → runs off into rivers and back to the sea, OR is absorbed by plants/animals and returned via transpiration (plants), respiration (water vapour exhaled), and excretion (in urine and sweat).

10. The Nitrogen Cycle

Key bacteria in the nitrogen cycle

BacteriaRole
Nitrogen-fixing bacteriaConvert atmospheric N₂ into ammonium / nitrates. Found in soil and in root nodules of legumes (peas, beans, clover). Mutualistic.
Decomposers (bacteria and fungi)Break down dead matter and waste, releasing ammonium
Nitrifying bacteriaConvert ammonium → nitrites → nitrates (NO₃⁻ — the form plants absorb)
Denitrifying bacteriaConvert nitrates → N₂ gas (returned to atmosphere). Anaerobic — common in waterlogged soils

Plants need nitrate ions to make amino acids and proteins. Without them, growth is stunted. Crop rotation and the use of fertilisers help maintain soil nitrate levels.
