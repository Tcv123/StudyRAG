1. Eukaryotic and Prokaryotic Cells

All living organisms are made of cells. Cells are the smallest unit of life and are classified into two fundamental types based on whether they contain a true nucleus: eukaryotic cells (with a membrane-bound nucleus) and prokaryotic cells (no true nucleus — DNA floats free in the cytoplasm).

Eukaryotic organisms include all animals, plants, fungi and protoctists. Their cells are typically 10–100 μm across and contain organelles enclosed in membranes. Prokaryotic organisms are bacteria and archaea — their cells are 0.1–5 μm across and contain no membrane-bound organelles.

2. Animal Cell — Structure and Function

OrganelleFunction
NucleusContains the genetic material (DNA in chromosomes); controls all activities of the cell.
CytoplasmJelly-like substance where most chemical reactions take place; contains dissolved nutrients, salts and enzymes.
Cell membranePartially permeable; controls what enters and leaves the cell.
MitochondriaSite of aerobic respiration — release energy (ATP) for cell processes.
RibosomesSite of protein synthesis; assemble amino acids into proteins.

3. Plant Cell — Additional Structures

Extra structure (plant only)Function
Cell wall (cellulose)Strengthens and supports the cell; prevents bursting in dilute solutions.
Chloroplasts (contain chlorophyll)Site of photosynthesis; absorb light energy to make glucose.
Permanent vacuoleFilled with cell sap; keeps the cell turgid and helps maintain the plant's shape.
All plant cells also contain everything an animal cell has (nucleus, cytoplasm, cell membrane, mitochondria, ribosomes). Always state the difference: plant cells have extras — cell wall, chloroplasts, permanent vacuole. Note: not every plant cell has chloroplasts (e.g. root cells).

4. Prokaryotic (Bacterial) Cell

A bacterial cell has the following components: a cell wall (peptidoglycan, NOT cellulose), cell membrane, cytoplasm, ribosomes, a single loop of chromosomal DNA, one or more small circular plasmids (carry extra genes such as antibiotic resistance), and possibly a flagellum for movement.

5. Microscopy

Light vs Electron Microscopes

FeatureLight microscopeElectron microscope
SourceLight (visible)Beam of electrons
Maximum magnification×1500×2,000,000
Resolution~200 nm~0.2 nm (1000× better)
Living specimens?YesNo — sample must be in vacuum
CostCheap; portableVery expensive; needs lab
What can be seen?Whole cells, nucleus, chloroplastsSub-cellular detail: ribosomes, cristae of mitochondria, viruses
Magnification = how much larger an object appears compared to its real size. Resolution = the smallest distance between two points that can still be seen as separate. Higher resolution → more detail.
Magnification = image size / real (actual) size

Worked example

An image of a cheek cell measures 60 mm long. The real cell is 40 μm. Find the magnification.

  1. Convert to the same units: 60 mm = 60 000 μm.
  2. Magnification = 60 000 ÷ 40 = ×1500.
Always check units. 1 mm = 1000 μm, 1 μm = 1000 nm. If image is in mm and real size in μm, multiply mm by 1000 first. Show your conversion in the answer for the marks.

6. Specialised Cells

Most cells in a multicellular organism are specialised — they have particular features that adapt them for their function. The process by which an unspecialised cell becomes specialised is called differentiation. Examples below.

Sperm cellCarry male DNA to the eggTail/flagellum (swims); many mitochondria (energy); acrosome with enzymes (penetrate egg); haploid nucleus (23 chromosomes)
Egg cell (ovum)Carry female DNA; provide nutrients to embryoLarge cytoplasm with food store; haploid nucleus; membrane changes after fertilisation to block other sperm
Ciliated epithelial cellMove mucus along airwaysCilia (hair-like) that beat in waves; many mitochondria for energy

Root hair cellAbsorb water and mineral ionsLong projection (large surface area); thin wall (short diffusion distance); many mitochondria (active transport)

Phloem cell (sieve tube)Transport sugars (sucrose)Sieve plates between cells (allow flow); companion cells supply energy

7. Required Practical Link

This topic is examined alongside PAG B1: Microscopy. You should be able to use a light microscope to observe and draw onion epidermal cells, then calculate the magnification of your drawing using the formula in section 5. See the separate Required Practicals notes for full method.

When asked to "describe how to use a light microscope": 1. Place slide on stage; clip in place. 2. Use lowest objective lens first. 3. Use coarse focus to bring stage close to lens, then move stage away while looking through eyepiece until image is clear. 4. Switch to higher magnification; use only fine focus. 5. Total magnification = eyepiece magnification × objective magnification.
