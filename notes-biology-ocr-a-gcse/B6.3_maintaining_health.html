1. Health and Disease

The WHO defines health as a state of complete physical, mental and social wellbeing — not just the absence of disease. Disease = a condition that affects normal body function. Diseases fall into two broad categories: communicable (can be passed from one organism to another) and non-communicable (cannot be passed on).
CommunicableNon-communicable
Caused by pathogens (bacteria, viruses, fungi, protists)Caused by lifestyle, genetics or environment
e.g. flu, measles, TB, malaria, athlete\'s foote.g. heart disease, type 2 diabetes, most cancers

2. Pathogens and Communicable Disease

Pathogen typeExamplesHow it spreads
BacteriaCholera, TB, food poisoning (Salmonella)Multiply rapidly; release toxins that damage cells/tissues
VirusesFlu, measles, HIV, COVID-19Reproduce inside host cells; eventually burst the cell, releasing more viruses
FungiAthlete\'s foot, ringwormHyphae grow into skin; release digestive enzymes
Protists (single-celled eukaryotes)Malaria (carried by mosquitoes)Often need a vector (e.g. mosquito) to spread between hosts

Methods of disease transmission

  • Direct contact — touching (e.g. athlete\'s foot, STIs)
  • Air — droplets from sneezes/coughs (e.g. flu, COVID-19, TB)
  • Water — contaminated water (e.g. cholera)
  • Food — contaminated food (e.g. Salmonella, E. coli)
  • Vector — animal carrier (e.g. mosquitoes carry malaria)
  • Body fluids — blood, sex (e.g. HIV)

Reducing transmission

  • Hand washing, hygiene
  • Cooking food properly; clean water supply
  • Sterilising medical instruments
  • Isolating infected people
  • Vector control (e.g. insecticides, bed nets for malaria)
  • Vaccination (see below)

4. Vaccination

A vaccine contains dead or weakened pathogens (or pathogen fragments) that are introduced into the body. Lymphocytes produce antibodies and form memory cells, so if the real pathogen later infects the body, the immune response is faster and stronger — the person is immune.

Primary vs secondary immune response

Herd immunity

If a high enough proportion of the population is vaccinated, even unvaccinated people are protected because the disease cannot spread easily — this is herd immunity. For highly contagious diseases like measles, around 95% vaccination is needed.

5. Antibiotics and Antiviral Drugs

Drug typeTargetsExamples
AntibioticBACTERIA only — kill them or stop them dividingPenicillin, amoxicillin, methicillin

AntifungalFungal infections (e.g. athlete\'s foot)Clotrimazole creams

Antibiotics do NOT work on viruses because viruses live inside host cells and use host machinery — there is no bacterial structure to target. Overuse causes antibiotic resistance (see B5.2).

6. Drug Development

New drugs are extensively tested before being released:

StageWhat\'s testedHow
1. Pre-clinicalToxicity, dosage, efficacyTested on cells, then on tissues, then on animals
2. Clinical trials Phase ISafety; dosageSmall group of healthy volunteers
3. Clinical trials Phase II/IIISafety + efficacyLarger groups including patients with the condition; usually double-blind with placebo control
4. Approval and monitoringLong-term safetyDrug released; effects in real population monitored
Placebo = a "fake" treatment (e.g. sugar pill) used to control for the psychological effect of being treated. Double-blind = neither the patient nor the doctor knows who is receiving the real drug — prevents bias.

7. Monoclonal Antibodies

Monoclonal antibodies (mAbs) are identical antibodies produced from a clone of a single B-lymphocyte. They are highly specific — they bind to one type of antigen only.

Production

  1. A mouse is injected with a target antigen.
  2. The mouse\'s lymphocytes produce antibodies.
  3. The lymphocytes are extracted and fused with tumour cells (which divide rapidly) to make hybridoma cells.
  4. Hybridoma cells divide indefinitely, producing huge quantities of identical antibodies.

Uses

UseHow
Pregnancy testsmAbs detect the hormone hCG in urine — line on stick goes blue if pregnant
Diagnosing diseasemAbs labelled with a fluorescent dye bind to specific tissues — show location of cancers
Cancer treatmentmAbs can carry drug or radiation directly to cancer cells, sparing healthy cells
ResearchIdentifying specific molecules in tissues / cells

CVD treatments

  • Lifestyle changes: stop smoking, healthier diet, more exercise — first line
  • Statins: drugs to lower cholesterol
  • Stents: mesh tubes to hold open narrowed coronary arteries
  • Bypass surgery: replace blocked artery with a vein from the leg
  • Heart transplant: in severe cases
