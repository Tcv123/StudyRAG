1. The Structure of DNA

DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid) is the molecule that stores the genetic instructions for life. It is found in the nucleus of eukaryotic cells, packaged into structures called chromosomes. A short section of DNA that codes for a particular protein is called a gene.

DNA is a double helix — two strands twisted around each other. Each strand is a chain of repeating units called nucleotides, and each nucleotide is made of a sugar (deoxyribose), a phosphate group, and one of four bases: A, T, C or G.

Complementary base pairing: A always pairs with T (held by 2 hydrogen bonds), and G always pairs with C (held by 3 hydrogen bonds). This means knowing the sequence of one strand tells you the sequence of the other.

Always remember the rule "A pairs with T, G pairs with C". A common trick: "Apple in the Tree, Car in the Garage".

From gene to protein — overview

A gene's sequence of bases codes for the order of amino acids in a protein. Three bases (a triplet or codon) code for one amino acid. There are 20 different amino acids, joined together to make different proteins. Different sequences of amino acids fold into different 3D shapes — this gives each protein its specific function.

2. Enzymes

An enzyme is a biological catalyst — a protein that speeds up a chemical reaction without being used up itself. Enzymes are needed because chemical reactions in cells happen too slowly at body temperature to keep an organism alive.

Each enzyme is a specific shape. The reactant (the substrate) fits into a region called the active site. This is the lock-and-key model: only a substrate of the right shape will fit into the enzyme's active site, just like a key into a lock. After the reaction, the products are released and the enzyme is unchanged, ready to bind another substrate.

Factors affecting enzyme activity

FactorEffect on rate
TemperatureRate increases as temperature rises (more kinetic energy → more successful collisions). Above the optimum (~37 °C in humans), the enzyme starts to denature: hydrogen bonds break, the active site changes shape and the substrate no longer fits. Rate falls rapidly.
pHEach enzyme has an optimum pH (e.g. pepsin pH 2 in the stomach; amylase pH 7 in the mouth; lipase pH 8 in the small intestine). Outside this range bonds in the active site break and the enzyme denatures.
Substrate concentrationRate increases as substrate concentration increases (more collisions). Eventually a plateau is reached — all active sites are occupied; rate is now limited by enzyme concentration.
Enzyme concentrationRate increases as enzyme concentration increases (more active sites available), provided substrate is in excess.
When asked to describe an enzyme graph: say WHAT happens (rate increases / peaks / drops). When asked to explain: relate to molecules — kinetic energy and collisions, denaturing of the active site so substrate no longer fits.

3. Calculating Rate of Reaction

Rate = amount of substance changed / time

For example, if 30 cm³ of oxygen is produced in 60 seconds, the rate is 30 ÷ 60 = 0.5 cm³/s. Practise these in the practical questions for B1.2.
