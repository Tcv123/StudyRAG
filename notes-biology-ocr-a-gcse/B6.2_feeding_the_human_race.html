1. Food Security

Food security = having enough food for everyone, available, accessible and nutritious. The global population is growing rapidly — predicted to reach ~10 billion by 2050 — placing enormous pressure on food production.

Threats to food security

  • Increasing population — more mouths to feed
  • Changing diets — wealthier populations eat more meat, requiring 10× more land than plant-based diets
  • New pests and pathogens — destroy crops
  • Climate change — extreme weather, droughts, floods, shifting growing seasons
  • Reduced biodiversity — fewer pollinators, fewer crop varieties
  • Cost of farming inputs — fuel, fertilisers, machinery
  • Conflict and political instability — disrupts trade and farming

2. Improving Food Production

Selective breeding

Plants and animals can be selectively bred for desirable traits:

  • Crops: high yield, disease resistance, drought tolerance, larger fruits
  • Livestock: faster growth, higher milk yield, better meat quality

See B5.2 for the basic process of selective breeding (choose parents → breed → select best offspring → repeat).

Genetic engineering / Genetic modification (GM)

Genetic engineering involves transferring genes from one organism into another. In agriculture, useful genes can be inserted into crops to improve yield or resistance.
ExampleModificationBenefit
Bt cornBacterial gene producing toxin against insects (Bt = Bacillus thuringiensis)Less pesticide use; reduces crop loss to insects
Golden RiceGenes from daffodil and bacterium making beta-caroteneReduces vitamin A deficiency / blindness in children in poorer countries
Herbicide-resistant soyResistance to glyphosateAllows weeds to be controlled without damaging crop
Drought-tolerant maizeGenes for water-stress toleranceHigher yield in dry regions

3. Fertilisers

Fertilisers are chemicals (or organic matter) added to soil to provide essential mineral ions for plant growth. Plants need nitrate (for amino acids/proteins), phosphate (for DNA/membranes), potassium (for enzymes), and magnesium (for chlorophyll).
MineralUse in plantSymptoms of deficiency
Nitrate (N)Amino acids → proteinsStunted growth; pale yellow leaves
Phosphate (P)DNA / membranesPoor root growth; purple young leaves
Potassium (K)Enzymes for respiration / photosynthesisYellow leaves with brown spots; poor flower/fruit growth
Magnesium (Mg)ChlorophyllYellow leaves between green veins (chlorosis)

4. Biological Control of Pests

Biological control uses a natural predator, parasite or pathogen to control a pest, instead of chemical pesticides.
ProsCons
No chemical residuesSlower than pesticides
One-off introduction may give long-term controlPredator may attack non-target species
Pests less likely to evolve resistancePredator may itself become invasive (e.g. cane toad)
Environmentally saferHard to remove if it goes wrong

Example: ladybirds released to eat aphids on rose bushes; parasitic wasps used to control whiteflies in greenhouses.

5. Hydroponics

Hydroponics = growing plants in a nutrient solution rather than in soil. Used in commercial greenhouses for tomatoes, lettuce, herbs.
ProsCons
Precise control of nutrients (no waste)Requires constant electricity/pumps
No soil-borne diseasesSetup is expensive
Can grow in any climate (greenhouses)Plants depend entirely on the system — failure = total crop loss
Less land used per kg producedLimited to certain crops

6. Cloning Plants

Two main methods:

Cuttings

  1. Cut a section of stem with leaves attached.
  2. Dip cut end in rooting powder (containing auxin).
  3. Plant in moist compost; cover to maintain humidity.
  4. New plant grows — genetically identical to parent.

Tissue culture (micropropagation)

  1. Take a few cells from the meristem of a parent plant.
  2. Place on agar containing nutrients and plant hormones (auxin + cytokinin).
  3. Cells divide and form a callus, then differentiate into roots and shoots.
  4. Tiny plantlets are transferred to soil; thousands of clones can be produced quickly.

Tissue culture is used for:

  • Mass-producing rare or endangered plants
  • Producing disease-free crops (start with sterilised tissue)
  • Quickly multiplying GM or selectively-bred individuals

7. Cloning Animals

MethodBrief description
Embryo splittingAn embryo is split before cells specialise; each fragment grows into an identical animal
Adult cell cloning (Somatic Cell Nuclear Transfer)Nucleus removed from a body cell of adult animal A, transferred into an egg cell (with its own nucleus removed) from animal B; egg stimulated to divide; embryo placed in surrogate. Famous example: Dolly the sheep (1996)
