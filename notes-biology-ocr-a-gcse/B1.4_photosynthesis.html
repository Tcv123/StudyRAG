1. What is Photosynthesis?

Photosynthesis is the process by which plants and algae use light energy (absorbed by chlorophyll) to convert carbon dioxide and water into glucose and oxygen. It is an endothermic reaction — it absorbs energy from the surroundings (light energy from the sun).
Word: carbon dioxide + water → glucose + oxygen (in light, with chlorophyll)
Symbol: 6CO₂ + 6H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂

Photosynthesis takes place in the chloroplasts of plant cells (mainly in the palisade cells of the leaf), which contain the green pigment chlorophyll. Chlorophyll absorbs light energy — mainly red and blue wavelengths, reflecting green (which is why plants look green).

2. Leaf Structure — Adaptations

AdaptationFunction
Broad and flatLarge surface area to absorb light
ThinShort diffusion distance for gases (CO₂ in, O₂ out)
Waxy cuticle (transparent)Waterproof — reduces water loss but lets light pass through
Palisade mesophyll cells (column-shaped)Packed with chloroplasts at the top — absorb the most light
Spongy mesophyll with air spacesAllow CO₂ and O₂ to diffuse easily through the leaf
Stomata (mostly on lower surface)Pores that allow gas exchange — opened/closed by guard cells
Veins (xylem and phloem)Xylem brings water; phloem carries glucose away to other parts of the plant

3. Limiting Factors of Photosynthesis

A limiting factor is a factor that is in shortest supply and therefore limits the rate of photosynthesis. The three main limiting factors are light intensity, carbon dioxide concentration and temperature.
FactorEffect
Light intensityAs light increases, rate increases (more energy for the reaction). At high light intensity, another factor becomes limiting and the rate plateaus.
CO₂ concentrationAs CO₂ increases, rate increases (more reactant). At high CO₂, another factor becomes limiting and rate plateaus.
TemperatureAs temperature increases, rate increases — enzymes work faster. Above ~40 °C, enzymes denature and rate falls sharply.
ChlorophyllPlants short of magnesium (or with leaf disease) make less chlorophyll, slowing the rate.

4. The Inverse Square Law

Inverse square law: Light intensity decreases as the square of the distance from the source. Doubling the distance gives ¼ of the light intensity.
Light intensity ∝ 1 / (distance)²

Worked example

A light source is moved from 10 cm to 20 cm from a plant. By what factor does the rate of photosynthesis decrease (assuming light is the limiting factor)?

  1. Distance is doubled (×2), so light intensity decreases by 2² = 4.
  2. Light intensity becomes 1/4. Rate decreases by a factor of 4.
In an exam practical, this means moving the lamp from 10 cm to 30 cm gives 1/9 of the light. Always work out the SQUARE of the distance ratio.

5. Uses of Glucose in Plants

Glucose made by photosynthesis is used by the plant in several ways:

  • Respiration — releases energy for cell processes
  • Converted to cellulose for cell walls (growth)
  • Combined with nitrogen ions absorbed from the soil to form amino acids (then proteins)
  • Stored as insoluble starch (in leaves and storage organs like potatoes)
  • Stored as oils/lipids in seeds (e.g. sunflowers)
