B3.3 Maintaining Internal Environments

OCR A GCSE Biology (J247) Gateway — Topic B3: Organism-level systems

1. Homeostasis

Homeostasis = the maintenance of a constant internal environment despite changes in external or internal conditions. In humans, this involves controlling body temperature, blood glucose, water levels and other variables. Homeostasis usually works by negative feedback — a change is detected and a response counters it.

The general structure of a homeostatic control system:

Receptor (detects change) → Coordination centre (CNS / endocrine) → Effector (muscle/gland) → Response → Negative feedback returns conditions to normal

2. Thermoregulation

Body temperature is monitored by the thermoregulatory centre in the hypothalamus (in the brain). It is kept close to 37 °C — the optimum for enzymes.

Mechanisms of vasodilation and vasoconstriction

Vessel responseWhenEffect
VasodilationHotArterioles supplying skin capillaries widen → more blood flows near skin → more heat lost by radiation/convection. Skin appears red.
VasoconstrictionColdArterioles supplying skin capillaries narrow → less blood flow near skin → less heat lost. Skin looks pale.
Common misconception: capillaries themselves do NOT widen or narrow. The change happens in the arterioles that supply the capillary bed. Capillaries don\'t have muscle in their walls.

3. The Kidneys and Osmoregulation

Osmoregulation = the control of water and salt concentration in the body. The kidneys are the main osmoregulatory organs. They also remove urea, a waste product from the breakdown of excess amino acids in the liver (deamination).

Kidney function — three steps

  1. Ultrafiltration — at the Bowman\'s capsule. High-pressure blood forces small molecules (water, glucose, amino acids, salt, urea) out of the blood into the nephron. Larger items (cells, large proteins) stay in the blood.
  2. Selective reabsorption — as the filtrate passes along the tubule:
    • All glucose reabsorbed back into the blood (active transport)
    • All amino acids reabsorbed
    • Most ions reabsorbed (as much as needed)
    • Most water reabsorbed (controlled by ADH)
  3. The remaining liquid — urine (water, salts, urea) — passes to the bladder via the ureter.

4. ADH and Water Balance

ADH (anti-diuretic hormone) is released by the pituitary gland. It controls how much water is reabsorbed by the kidney tubules — adjusting urine concentration to keep blood water level constant.

5. Plant Hormones and Tropisms

Plants respond to environmental stimuli by growing in particular directions. A tropism is a directional growth response. The main plant growth hormone is auxin, produced at shoot and root tips. Auxin causes shoot cells to elongate.
TropismResponseAdaptive value
Phototropism (light)Shoots grow TOWARDS light (positive); roots are negative or unresponsiveShoots get more light for photosynthesis
Gravitropism / GeotropismShoots grow AWAY from gravity (negative); roots grow WITH gravity (positive)Roots anchor and find water; shoots reach light
HydrotropismRoots grow TOWARDS waterFind water for survival

Commercial uses of plant hormones

  • Selective weed killers — synthetic auxins kill broad-leaved weeds in lawns/cereal crops without harming the grass.
  • Rooting powders — auxin to encourage cuttings to develop roots.
  • Tissue culture — combinations of auxin + cytokinin grow new plants from tissue.
  • Ripening fruit — ethene gas ripens fruit during transport (so they can be picked unripe and ripened on arrival).
  • Gibberellins — used to end seed dormancy, promote flowering, increase fruit size.
