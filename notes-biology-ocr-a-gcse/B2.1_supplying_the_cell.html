1. Diffusion

Diffusion is the net movement of particles from a region of higher concentration to a region of lower concentration (down a concentration gradient). It is a passive process — it does not require energy.

Diffusion happens because particles are constantly moving randomly. Over time, the random motion spreads particles evenly throughout a space. Examples in living systems include:

  • O₂ diffuses from alveoli into red blood cells (high → low)
  • CO₂ diffuses from blood into alveoli
  • Glucose diffuses from the small intestine into the blood
  • Urea diffuses from cells into the blood, then into the kidney

Factors affecting rate of diffusion

FactorEffect on rate
Concentration gradientSteeper gradient → faster rate
TemperatureHigher temperature → particles have more kinetic energy → faster rate
Surface areaLarger SA → more particles can cross per second → faster rate
Diffusion distanceShorter distance → faster rate

2. Osmosis

Osmosis is the net movement of water molecules across a partially permeable membrane from a region of higher water concentration (more dilute) to a region of lower water concentration (more concentrated solute). Like diffusion, osmosis is passive.

Water moves "down its water potential gradient". A solution with lots of water and little solute (e.g. distilled water) has a HIGH water potential. A concentrated sugar solution has a LOW water potential.

3. Active Transport

Active transport is the movement of substances against a concentration gradient (from low to high concentration) using energy from respiration (ATP). It uses carrier proteins in the cell membrane.

Examples:

  • Mineral ions (e.g. nitrate) absorbed into root hair cells from the soil where the ion concentration is much lower than in the cell.
  • Glucose reabsorbed from the kidney tubules back into the blood.
  • Glucose absorbed from the gut into the blood when blood glucose is already higher.

4. Mitosis and the Cell Cycle

Mitosis is the process of cell division that produces two genetically identical diploid daughter cells. It is part of the cell cycle and is used for growth, repair and asexual reproduction.

Stages of mitosis (simplified)

  1. Interphase — cell grows, organelles are copied, DNA replicates (each chromosome becomes two identical sister chromatids).
  2. Prophase — chromosomes condense and become visible.
  3. Metaphase — chromosomes line up along the equator (middle).
  4. Anaphase — sister chromatids are pulled apart to opposite poles.
  5. Telophase — two new nuclei form.
  6. Cytokinesis — cytoplasm and cell membrane divide → two identical daughter cells.

5. Stem Cells and Differentiation

A stem cell is an undifferentiated cell capable of dividing many times by mitosis to produce more cells of the same type, AND of differentiating into many specialised cell types.
SourceWhat it can doIssues
Embryonic (3–5 day embryos)Can become ANY cell type (pluripotent)Ethical: requires destruction of embryos
Adult (e.g. bone marrow)Limited — usually only certain cell types (e.g. blood cells)Ethically uncontroversial; harder to obtain large numbers
Plant meristemsCan produce any plant cell type — ALL through lifeUsed in horticulture / cloning rare or disease-free plants

Therapeutic uses: treating type 1 diabetes (replacing β-cells), paralysis (regrowing damaged nerves), leukaemia (bone marrow transplants), eye diseases (replacing damaged retinal cells).

Risks/Ethics: rejection by immune system, viral contamination, formation of tumours; embryonic source raises moral debate around the right to life of an embryo.
