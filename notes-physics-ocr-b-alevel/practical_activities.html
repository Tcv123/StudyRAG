⚗ Resistivity of a Wire

Apparatus

  • Constantan wire (~1 m, taped to ruler)
  • Ammeter, voltmeter, cell/power supply, switch
  • Micrometer screw gauge, metre ruler, crocodile clips

Method

  1. Measure wire diameter at 6+ points with micrometer. Calculate A = πd²/4.
  2. Connect ammeter in series, voltmeter in parallel across wire section.
  3. Use clips to select length L. Record V and I. Calculate R = V/I.
  4. Repeat for 6+ lengths. Plot R vs L.
  5. Gradient = ρ/A, so ρ = gradient × A.

Safety

  • Switch off between readings; low voltage; avoid touching hot wire

Analysis

R = ρL/A ⇒ gradient of R vs L = ρ/A

Expected: ρ(constantan) ≈ 4.9 × 10−7 Ω m.

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Wire heatingSystematicSwitch off between readings; low current
Contact resistanceSystematicClean clips; firm connections
Diameter variationRandomMultiple micrometer readings; use mean
Length measurement at clipRandomTape wire to ruler; align carefully
Exam Tip: Constantan is chosen because its resistance barely changes with temperature. The biggest uncertainty typically comes from the diameter (squared in the area calculation). A non-zero y-intercept suggests contact resistance.

⚗ V-I Characteristics

Apparatus

  • DC power supply with potential divider (or variable resistor)
  • Ammeter, voltmeter
  • Components to test: wire (ohmic), filament lamp, silicon diode
  • Switch, connecting wires

Method

  1. Connect the potential divider circuit for full voltage range (0 to Vmax).
  2. Place the component under test in series with the ammeter. Voltmeter in parallel across it.
  3. Vary voltage from 0 upwards. Record V and I at each setting.
  4. For the diode, reverse the connections and repeat.
  5. Plot I vs V for each component.

Safety

  • Filament lamp gets hot — do not touch
  • Low voltage (< 12 V)
  • Do not exceed maximum current rating of components

Analysis

  • Ohmic conductor: Straight line through origin (constant resistance)
  • Filament lamp: Curve — resistance increases as wire heats up
  • Diode: Very little current below ~0.6 V (threshold); rapid increase above; negligible current in reverse bias
Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Component heating changes resistanceSystematicTake readings quickly; allow cooling
Cannot reach 0 V with variable resistor aloneSystematicUse potential divider circuit
Meter resolutionRandomUse digital meters with appropriate range
Parallax on analogue metersRandomRead at eye level
Exam Tip: A potential divider allows you to vary the voltage from 0 V upwards, which is essential for accurately determining the diode threshold. A simple variable resistor in series cannot reduce V to 0 V.

⚗ Young Modulus of a Wire

Apparatus

  • Long thin wire (~2 m), G-clamp, wooden blocks
  • Micrometer, vernier scale or travelling microscope
  • Slotted masses, tape measure, safety goggles

Method

  1. Measure diameter (6+ readings). Calculate A = πd²/4.
  2. Measure original length L. Apply small initial load to straighten.
  3. Add masses in steps. Record F = mg and extension ΔL.
  4. Check elastic behaviour on unloading.
  5. Plot stress vs strain. Gradient = Young modulus E.

Safety

  • Safety goggles; cushion beneath masses; do not exceed elastic limit

Analysis

E = σ/ε = (F/A)/(ΔL/L)

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Diameter variesRandomMultiple readings; different positions/orientations
Wire kinksSystematicApply small initial load
Thermal expansionSystematicUse reference wire; stable temperature
Small extensionsRandomLong thin wire; travelling microscope
Exam Tip: Percentage uncertainty in d is doubled in the area formula (d²), making it the dominant source of error. Use a long wire to maximise extensions. The area under the linear stress-strain graph gives strain energy per unit volume.

⚗ EMF and Internal Resistance

Apparatus

  • Cell, variable resistor, ammeter, voltmeter, switch

Method

  1. Connect cell, ammeter, variable resistor in series. Voltmeter across cell.
  2. Record V and I for 6+ settings. Switch off between.
  3. Plot V vs I.

Safety

  • No short circuits; switch off between readings

Analysis

V = ε − Ir. y-intercept = ε, gradient = −r.

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Cell runs downSystematicWork quickly; switch off between readings
Meter resistancesSystematicDigital meters
FluctuationsRandomWait for stable readings
Heating effectsSystematicShort measurement bursts
Exam Tip: The x-intercept gives the short-circuit current I = ε/r. As the external resistance R increases, V approaches ε (more of the EMF is across the external component).

⚗ Stationary Waves on a String

Apparatus

  • Signal generator, vibrator, string, pulley, masses, ruler

Method

  1. Set up string between vibrator and pulley with hanging mass.
  2. Vary frequency to find fundamental and harmonics. Record f and measure L.
  3. Vary tension. Plot f vs 1/L or f vs √T.

Safety

  • Safety screen; soft pad beneath masses

Analysis

f = (1/2L)√(T/μ)

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Resonance hard to judgeRandomPaper rider method
Length measurementRandomMeasure node to node
Hanger mass omittedSystematicInclude in tension
String not uniform μRandomWeigh measured length
Exam Tip: The fundamental frequency has one antinode. Each harmonic adds one more antinode. The frequency of the nth harmonic is n times the fundamental frequency.

⚗ Determination of g by Free-Fall

Apparatus

  • Electromagnet, steel ball, trapdoor, timer, 2 m ruler, clamp stand

Method

  1. Set height s. Release ball (timer starts). Ball hits trapdoor (timer stops).
  2. Repeat 3 times; take mean t. Vary s over 6+ values.
  3. Plot s vs t². Gradient = g/2.

Safety

  • Soft pad; stable stand; clear feet

Analysis

s = ½gt² ⇒ g = 2 × gradient

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Residual magnetismSystematicDemagnetiser or mechanical release
Air resistanceSystematicSmall dense ball
Height measurementRandomMeasure bottom of ball to trapdoor
Reaction time (if manual)RandomUse electronic timer
Exam Tip: A systematic timing delay shifts all t values by a constant amount, giving a non-zero y-intercept on the s vs t² graph. The gradient is still valid. Calculate percentage error from accepted value to evaluate your result.

⚗ Investigating Interference (Double Slit)

Apparatus

  • Laser (or white light + colour filter + single slit)
  • Double slit (known separation d)
  • Screen, ruler/vernier callipers, tape measure

Method

  1. Darken room. Direct laser through double slit onto distant screen.
  2. Measure across several fringe spacings. Divide to get w (fringe spacing).
  3. Measure slit-to-screen distance D.
  4. Calculate λ = wd/D.
  5. Vary D and check λ remains consistent.

Safety

  • Never look into laser beam; "Laser in use" warning
  • Remove reflective objects

Analysis

λ = wd/D

Measure across multiple fringes to reduce uncertainty in w.

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Fringes blurredRandomUse laser for coherent source; darken room
D measurementRandomLarge D reduces % uncertainty
Fringe spacing hard to measureRandomMeasure across 10+ fringes and divide
Laser not perpendicular to slitsSystematicAlign carefully
Exam Tip: For white light, central fringe is white with coloured fringes either side (red outermost because it has the longest wavelength and hence largest fringe spacing). With a laser, all fringes are the same colour and equally spaced.

⚗ SHM with Mass-Spring and Pendulum

Apparatus

  • Spring, masses, clamp stand, stopwatch, ruler
  • String, bob, protractor, ruler

Method (Mass-Spring)

  1. Hang mass m. Displace slightly, release. Time 10 oscillations.
  2. Repeat for 6+ masses. Plot T² vs m.

Method (Pendulum)

  1. Measure L. Displace <10°, release. Time 10 oscillations.
  2. Repeat for 6+ lengths. Plot T² vs L.

Safety

  • Stable stand; clear of swinging masses

Analysis

Spring: T² = (4π²/k)m     Pendulum: T² = (4π²/g)L

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Miscounting oscillationsRandomFiducial marker; count from equilibrium
Large amplitude (pendulum)SystematicKeep θ < 10°
Reaction timeRandomTime 10+ oscillations
Spring massSystematicAdd mspring/3 as effective mass
Exam Tip: The pendulum period is independent of mass. The spring period is independent of g. Plotting T² (not T) linearises the graph. The gradient gives k or g respectively.

⚗ Specific Heat Capacity

Apparatus

  • Metal block with holes, heater, thermometer
  • Ammeter, voltmeter, power supply, stopwatch, balance, insulation

Method

  1. Weigh block. Insert heater and thermometer with oil.
  2. Record initial θ. Heat for measured time t. Record V, I, final θ.
  3. c = VIt / (mΔθ).

Safety

  • Hot block; tongs/gloves; heat mat; heater fully inserted

Analysis

c = VIt / (mΔθ)

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Heat lossSystematic (c too high)Insulate block
Poor thermal contactSystematicOil in thermometer hole
V/I fluctuationsRandomRegulated supply; monitor throughout
Thermometer lagSystematicWait for equilibrium; stir if liquid
Exam Tip: The measured c is always slightly too high because of heat losses. Insulation and a cooling correction can improve accuracy. Plotting Δθ vs t gives a straight line whose gradient = VI/(mc).

⚗ Boyle's Law

Apparatus

  • Sealed syringe or Boyle's law apparatus, pressure gauge, thermometer

Method

  1. Record initial V and P.
  2. Change V slowly. Wait for temperature equilibrium.
  3. Record V and P. Repeat for 6+ data points.
  4. Plot P vs 1/V.

Safety

  • Do not over-pressurise; safety screen

Analysis

PV = constant ⇒ P vs 1/V is linear through origin

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Temperature rise on compressionSystematicCompress slowly; wait for equilibrium
Piston frictionSystematicLubricate; tap gently
Gas leaksSystematicCheck seals
Gauge parallaxRandomRead at eye level; digital gauge
Exam Tip: Boyle's law only holds at constant temperature. If the graph of P vs 1/V does not pass through the origin, this suggests a systematic error (e.g. trapped air of unknown volume, or temperature change).

⚗ Capacitor Charge and Discharge

Apparatus

  • Capacitor (470–2200 μF), resistor, DC supply, two-way switch, datalogger

Method

  1. Charge capacitor through resistor. Record V vs t.
  2. Discharge. Record V vs t.
  3. Plot ln(V) vs t for discharge. Gradient = −1/RC.

Safety

  • Correct polarity; discharge before handling; low voltage

Analysis

V = V0e−t/RC ⇒ ln V = ln V0 − t/RC

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Voltmeter draws currentSystematicHigh-impedance datalogger
Manual timingRandomDatalogger
Capacitor leakageSystematicHigh-quality capacitor
Component toleranceSystematicMeasure actual R and C independently
Exam Tip: The time constant τ = RC. After 1τ, V falls to 37% of V0. After 5τ, V < 1% of V0 (effectively fully discharged). Choose R and C so τ is 10–30 s for manageable timing.

⚗ Force on a Current-Carrying Wire

Apparatus

  • U-magnet, wire cradle, top-pan balance, DC supply, ammeter, ruler

Method

  1. Magnet on balance. Zero. Position wire in field.
  2. Vary I. Record I and Δm. F = Δm × g.
  3. Measure wire length L in field. Plot F vs I.
  4. Gradient = BL, so B = gradient/L.

Safety

  • Low current; short bursts; magnets away from electronics

Analysis

F = BIL ⇒ gradient of F vs I = BL

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Draughts/vibrationsRandomDraught shield; stable surface
Non-uniform fieldSystematicStrong flat-faced magnets; wire at centre
Wire not perpendicularSystematicCareful alignment
Wire heatingSystematicShort bursts of current
Exam Tip: The balance reading changes because by Newton's third law, the force on the wire is equal and opposite to the force on the magnet. F = BIL only when B, I, and L are mutually perpendicular.

⚗ Inverse-Square Law for Gamma Radiation

Apparatus

  • Gamma source, GM tube + counter, metre ruler, source holder

Method

  1. Measure background (60+ s, no source).
  2. Place source at distance d. Count for 60 s. Subtract background.
  3. Repeat for 6+ distances. Take 3 readings at each.
  4. Plot corrected count rate C vs 1/d².

Safety

  • Tongs; maximise distance; minimise time; ALARA; lead-lined storage

Analysis

I ∝ 1/d² ⇒ C vs 1/d² should be linear through origin

Or plot ln(C) vs ln(d): gradient should be −2.

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Random decayRandomCount for longer; more repeats
Background fluctuationsRandomLong background count
Distance to tube centreSystematicMeasure to GM tube window
Non-point sourceSystematicUse large d values
Exam Tip: Always subtract background before plotting. If the graph curves at small d, the point-source approximation breaks down. The inverse-square law applies because gamma radiation spreads out equally in all directions from a point source.

⚗ Absorption of Radiation

Apparatus

  • Gamma source, GM tube + counter, lead sheets of known thickness, callipers

Method

  1. Measure background count rate (no source, 60+ s).
  2. Fix source-detector distance. Record count rate with no absorber.
  3. Insert lead sheets of increasing total thickness. Record corrected count rate.
  4. Repeat for 6+ thicknesses.
  5. Plot ln(corrected count rate) vs thickness x.

Safety

  • Tongs; distance; time minimised; ALARA; lead storage

Analysis

I = I0e−μx ⇒ ln(C) vs x gives gradient = −μ

Half-value thickness: x½ = ln 2 / μ ≈ 0.693 / μ.

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Random nature of decayRandomLong count times; multiple readings
Background fluctuationsRandomLong background count; multiple measurements
Absorber thickness not uniformSystematicMeasure at multiple points with callipers
Scattered radiationSystematicCollimate the beam
Exam Tip: Different types of radiation have very different absorption characteristics. Alpha is stopped by paper, beta by a few mm of aluminium, and gamma requires several cm of lead. The half-value thickness is specific to both the radiation energy and the absorber material.
