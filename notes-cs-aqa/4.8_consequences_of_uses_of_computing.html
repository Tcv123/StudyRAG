4.8 Consequences of Uses of Computing

AQA A-Level Computer Science (7517) — Paper 2

4.8.1 Moral, Ethical, Legal, and Cultural Issues

Moral issue: A matter of personal conscience — what an individual believes is right or wrong, based on their own values and beliefs. Different people may reach different conclusions on the same issue.
Ethical issue: A matter of accepted standards within a profession or society — what is considered acceptable behaviour by a group. Professional bodies (like BCS) codify ethical standards.
Legal issue: A matter governed by law — what is or is not permitted by legislation. Breaking the law has legal consequences (fines, imprisonment).
Cultural issue: A matter relating to the norms, values, and practices of a particular group or society. Technology can reinforce or undermine cultural practices.

Computing raises questions across all four areas. The distinctions matter because:

  • Something can be legal but unethical (e.g., a company legally selling user data but violating users' trust)
  • Something can be ethical but illegal (e.g., a whistleblower exposing illegal surveillance by their employer)
  • Something can be morally objectionable to some but not others (e.g., AI-generated art replacing human artists)

Examples by category:

In essay questions, clearly distinguish between moral, ethical, and legal issues — examiners award marks for correct classification. Use specific examples and name specific legislation. Consider multiple stakeholder perspectives. A good structure: state the issue, classify it, explain the different viewpoints, give a specific real-world example, and discuss possible solutions or mitigations.

4.8.2 Legislation

Data Protection Act 2018 / UK GDPR

The Data Protection Act 2018 implements the UK GDPR and governs how personal data must be handled. It applies to any organisation (data controller — determines how data is used, or data processor — processes data on behalf of a controller) that handles personal data of UK residents.

Key principles — personal data must be:

  1. Processed lawfully, fairly, and transparently — there must be a legal basis for processing (e.g., consent, legitimate interest, legal obligation, contract). The individual must know what data is collected and why.
  2. Collected for specified, explicit, and legitimate purposes — data cannot be used for purposes beyond what was originally stated (purpose limitation).
  3. Adequate, relevant, and limited to what is necessary (data minimisation) — do not collect data you do not need.
  4. Accurate and kept up to date — inaccurate data must be corrected or deleted without delay.
  5. Kept no longer than necessary (storage limitation) — define retention periods and delete data when no longer needed.
  6. Processed securely — protected against unauthorised access, loss, or damage using appropriate technical and organisational measures (integrity and confidentiality). This includes encryption, access controls, staff training, and incident response plans.

Rights of data subjects (the individuals whose data is held):

  • Right to be informed — know what data is collected, why, and how it is used (privacy notices)
  • Right of access — request a copy of all personal data held (Subject Access Request / SAR, must respond within 30 days)
  • Right to rectification — correct inaccurate or incomplete data
  • Right to erasure ("right to be forgotten") — request deletion of data when no longer needed or if consent is withdrawn
  • Right to restrict processing — limit how data is used (e.g., during a dispute about accuracy)
  • Right to data portability — receive data in a machine-readable format and transfer it to another service
  • Right to object — object to data processing for direct marketing or based on legitimate interests
  • Rights related to automated decision making and profiling — not to be subject to decisions made solely by algorithms that have significant effects (e.g., loan decisions), with the right to human review

The Information Commissioner's Office (ICO) enforces the Act and can issue fines of up to £17.5 million or 4% of annual global turnover (whichever is greater).

Example scenario: An online retailer collects customer email addresses during checkout. Under the DPA 2018, they must: tell customers at the point of collection what the data will be used for (principle 1); only use it for the stated purpose, e.g., order updates (principle 2); not store it indefinitely after the transaction is complete (principle 5); protect it with encryption and access controls (principle 6).

Computer Misuse Act 1990

The Computer Misuse Act 1990 was introduced to combat hacking and other computer crimes. It was one of the first laws specifically targeting cyber-offences.

SectionOffenceDetailMax Penalty
Section 1Unauthorised access to computer materialKnowingly accessing a computer system without permission. This includes guessing passwords, using someone else's login, or exploiting a vulnerability to gain access. Intent to access is sufficient — you do not need to succeed in stealing data.2 years imprisonment and/or fine
Section 2Unauthorised access with intent to commit a further offenceGaining access to commit another crime, such as fraud, blackmail, or identity theft. The further offence does not need to be committed — the intent is enough.5 years imprisonment and/or fine
Section 3Unauthorised modification of computer materialDeliberately modifying data without permission: deploying malware, deleting files, planting viruses, encrypting data with ransomware, modifying system files. Also covers DDoS attacks (impairing the operation of a computer).10 years imprisonment and/or fine
Section 3AMaking, supplying, or obtaining tools for computer misuseCreating, distributing, or obtaining hacking tools, malware, exploit kits, or password crackers with intent to use them for offences under Sections 1-3. Added by the Police and Justice Act 2006.2 years imprisonment and/or fine

Key point: The Act covers intent and authorisation. A security researcher who has written permission to test a system (penetration testing) is not committing an offence. Someone who accesses a system "just to look around" without permission IS committing a Section 1 offence, even if they do not steal or damage anything.

Copyright, Designs and Patents Act 1988

This Act protects the intellectual property of creators. In computing, it covers:

  • Software — the source code and object code of programs are protected as literary works. The copyright belongs to the author (or their employer if created during employment).
  • Databases — the structure and creative selection/arrangement of data
  • Digital media — music, video, images, written content
  • Website content — text, graphics, layouts, and underlying code

It is illegal to copy, distribute, modify, or sell copyrighted material without the owner's permission. This includes pirating software, sharing copyrighted music via P2P networks, using images from the internet without a licence, or copying code from a proprietary project.

Duration: Copyright typically lasts for the creator's lifetime plus 70 years.

Regulation of Investigatory Powers Act 2000 (RIPA)

RIPA governs the surveillance and investigation powers of public bodies (police, intelligence agencies, HMRC, local authorities). Key provisions:

  • Authorises the interception of communications (phone, email, internet traffic) with appropriate warrants signed by a senior official or judge
  • Allows the acquisition of communications data (metadata: who contacted whom, when, where, and for how long — but not the content of communications)
  • Permits covert surveillance (directed surveillance of a specific individual, or intrusive surveillance inside private premises)
  • Requires ISPs to maintain the capability to intercept communications when served with a warrant
  • Can require individuals to disclose encryption keys or provide plaintext when served with a Section 49 notice. Failure to comply is a criminal offence (up to 2 years, or 5 years for national security cases).

Controversy: Critics argue RIPA enables mass surveillance that infringes civil liberties. The Investigatory Powers Act 2016 ("Snoopers' Charter") extended these powers further, requiring ISPs to retain browsing histories for 12 months.

Freedom of Information Act 2000

Gives the public the right to request access to information held by public authorities (government departments, councils, NHS, schools, police, universities).

  • Public authorities must respond within 20 working days
  • They must have a publication scheme that proactively publishes certain categories of information
  • Exemptions exist for: national security, personal data (covered by DPA instead), law enforcement investigations, commercial interests, information intended for future publication, and legal professional privilege
  • If a request is refused, the applicant can appeal to the Information Commissioner's Office (ICO)
When discussing legislation, always: (1) name the specific Act and its year, (2) state what it covers with the correct legal terminology (data controller, data subject, unauthorised access), (3) explain how it applies to the scenario in the question, and (4) mention the enforcement body and potential penalties where relevant.

4.8.3 Surveillance and Privacy

Methods of Digital Surveillance

  • CCTV and facial recognition — automated identification of individuals in public spaces using AI-powered cameras. Cities like London have extensive CCTV networks.
  • Internet monitoring — tracking browsing history, search queries, online purchases, and social media activity
  • Communications interception — monitoring email, phone calls, messaging apps, and VoIP
  • Location tracking — GPS data from smartphones, mobile phone mast triangulation, Wi-Fi positioning, vehicle number plate recognition (ANPR)
  • Social media monitoring — analysing posts, connections, likes, shares, and behaviour patterns
  • Smart device data collection — IoT devices (smart speakers, smart TVs, fitness trackers, smart doorbells) continuously collect data about users' homes and habits
  • Cookies and web tracking — third-party cookies, browser fingerprinting, advertising trackers, cross-site tracking pixels
  • Workplace surveillance — email monitoring, keylogging, screen recording, productivity tracking software

4.8.4 AI and Machine Learning — Ethical Implications

Artificial Intelligence (AI): Computer systems designed to perform tasks that normally require human intelligence, such as learning, reasoning, problem-solving, perception, natural language understanding, and decision-making.
Machine Learning (ML): A subset of AI where systems learn patterns from data to improve performance on a task without being explicitly programmed with rules for that task.

Ethical Concerns

ConcernDetailReal-World Example
Bias and discriminationML models trained on biased historical data perpetuate and amplify existing societal biases in their predictions and decisions.Recruitment AI trained on historical hiring data (predominantly male applicants) discriminated against female candidates. Facial recognition systems showed higher error rates for darker-skinned individuals.
AccountabilityWhen an AI system causes harm, it can be unclear who is responsible: the developer, the company deploying it, the user, or the AI itself. Many AI models are "black boxes" — their reasoning is opaque.If a self-driving car causes an accident, who is liable? The manufacturer, the software developer, or the owner?
Job displacementAI and automation threaten to replace jobs, potentially increasing inequality. However, new job types may also emerge.Automated checkouts replacing cashiers; AI legal research tools reducing need for junior lawyers; autonomous trucks threatening driving jobs.
PrivacyAI systems often require vast amounts of personal data for training. This creates surveillance risks.Facial recognition databases built from social media photos without consent. Voice assistants recording conversations.
Autonomous weaponsAI-powered weapons (drones, targeting systems) raise questions about removing human decision-making from lethal force.Debate over "killer robots" at the UN Convention on Certain Conventional Weapons.
DeepfakesAI-generated fake images, video, and audio that are increasingly difficult to distinguish from real content.Fake political speeches, non-consensual intimate images, voice cloning for fraud.
Transparency (Explainability)Complex neural networks make decisions that even their creators cannot fully explain. This is problematic when decisions affect people's lives.A credit scoring algorithm rejects a loan application but cannot explain why in terms the applicant can understand.
AI ethics questions require balanced arguments. Always: (1) identify the stakeholders, (2) discuss benefits alongside risks, (3) consider different perspectives, (4) reference specific examples, and (5) suggest possible mitigations (regulation, transparency requirements, human oversight, bias auditing). Avoid one-sided answers.

Digital Divide: The gap between those who have effective access to modern information and communications technology and those who do not. This divide exists between countries (global digital divide), between regions within a country (rural vs urban), and between social groups (age, income, education, disability).

Consequences of the digital divide: exclusion from online services (banking, healthcare, government); reduced access to education and employment; social isolation; widening economic inequality; democratic deficit (unable to participate in online civic engagement).

4.8.7 Autonomous Vehicles — Ethical Considerations

Self-driving vehicles use AI, sensors (LIDAR, cameras, radar, ultrasonic), GPS, and HD mapping to navigate without human intervention. They are classified on a scale from Level 0 (no automation) to Level 5 (full automation in all conditions).

Ethical Dilemmas

  • The trolley problem: If a collision is unavoidable, how should the vehicle decide who to protect — the passengers, pedestrians, or other road users? Who programmes these decisions, and whose values do they encode? Different cultures may prioritise differently.
  • Liability: In an accident, who is responsible — the manufacturer, the software developer, the vehicle owner, or the AI itself? Current law struggles with this question because liability frameworks were designed for human drivers.
  • Data collection: Autonomous vehicles collect vast amounts of data about routes, passengers, surroundings, and driving patterns. Who owns this data? How long is it retained? Can it be used for surveillance?
  • Job losses: Taxi drivers, lorry drivers, bus drivers, and delivery drivers could be displaced. This affects millions of workers worldwide.
  • Algorithmic bias: Object recognition systems have shown lower accuracy in detecting pedestrians with darker skin tones, or those wearing unusual clothing, or children and wheelchair users.
  • Security: If the vehicle's software can be hacked, an attacker could potentially take control of the vehicle, creating safety and terrorism risks.

Potential Benefits

  • Reduced accidents (over 90% of road crashes are caused by human error — distraction, fatigue, impairment)
  • Increased mobility for elderly, disabled, and non-driving individuals
  • Reduced traffic congestion through optimised routing, platooning, and V2V (vehicle-to-vehicle) communication
  • Lower emissions through efficient driving patterns and electric vehicle integration
  • Productive use of commuting time (passengers can work, read, or rest)

4.8.11 Professional Standards — BCS Code of Conduct

The British Computer Society (BCS) is the Chartered Institute for IT in the UK. It publishes a Code of Conduct that sets out the professional and ethical standards expected of IT professionals. Members voluntarily agree to abide by this code.

The Four Sections of the BCS Code of Conduct

SectionKey Requirements
1. Public Interest
  • Have due regard for public health, privacy, security, and wellbeing of others
  • Have due regard for the environment and sustainability
  • Consider the rights of third parties, particularly vulnerable groups
  • Promote equal access to the benefits of IT and oppose discrimination
  • Comply with relevant legislation (DPA, CMA, etc.)
2. Professional Competence and Integrity
  • Only undertake work within your competence; do not claim expertise you lack
  • Keep professional knowledge and skills up to date through CPD (Continuing Professional Development)
  • Avoid injecting malicious code or engaging in deceptive practices
  • Respect and protect confidential information
  • Reject bribery, corruption, and unethical inducements
3. Duty to Relevant Authority
  • Carry out professional responsibilities with due care and diligence
  • Avoid conflicts of interest; declare any that arise
  • Accept professional responsibility for work done under your supervision
  • Do not disclose or misuse confidential information obtained through work
  • Do not take on work you know to be unethical
4. Duty to the Profession
  • Uphold the reputation and good standing of the IT profession
  • Seek to improve professional standards through knowledge sharing
  • Support and mentor fellow BCS members in their professional development
  • Act with integrity in all professional relationships
  • Report unethical behaviour through appropriate channels

Whistleblowing: If an IT professional becomes aware of unethical or illegal practices (e.g., a company deliberately ignoring a security vulnerability that puts users at risk), the BCS Code supports them in raising concerns through appropriate channels, even if it conflicts with employer interests. The Public Interest Disclosure Act 1998 provides legal protection for whistleblowers.

Key distinction: The BCS Code of Conduct is a professional code, not a law. Breaching it can result in disciplinary action from the BCS (e.g., loss of membership, loss of Chartered status) but not criminal prosecution. However, the activities it prohibits (e.g., injecting malicious code) may also breach actual laws (e.g., Computer Misuse Act).

For BCS Code of Conduct questions, know the four sections and be able to apply them to a scenario. For example: "A developer is asked to collect more user data than necessary. This conflicts with Section 1 (Public Interest — have due regard for privacy) and Section 2 (Professional Integrity — do not engage in deceptive practices)." Always link the specific section to the scenario.
