AQA A-Level Computer Science (7517) — Paper 2

4.8.1 Moral, Ethical, Legal, and Cultural Issues

Moral issue: A matter of personal conscience — what an individual believes is right or wrong.
Ethical issue: A matter of accepted standards within a profession or society. Professional bodies (like BCS) codify ethical standards.
Legal issue: A matter governed by law — breaking the law has legal consequences (fines, imprisonment).
Cultural issue: A matter relating to norms, values, and practices of a particular group or society.

The distinctions matter because:

  • Something can be legal but unethical (e.g., a company legally selling user data but violating trust)
  • Something can be ethical but illegal (e.g., a whistleblower exposing illegal surveillance)
  • Something can be morally objectionable to some but not others (e.g., AI-generated art replacing human artists)

4.8.2 Legislation

Data Protection Act 2018 / UK GDPR

Governs how personal data must be handled. Applies to any data controller (determines how data is used) or data processor (processes data on behalf of a controller).

Six key principles — personal data must be:

  1. Processed lawfully, fairly, and transparently — legal basis needed (consent, legitimate interest, legal obligation)
  2. Collected for specified, explicit, and legitimate purposes (purpose limitation)
  3. Adequate, relevant, and limited to what is necessary (data minimisation)
  4. Accurate and kept up to date
  5. Kept no longer than necessary (storage limitation)
  6. Processed securely (integrity and confidentiality) — encryption, access controls, staff training

Rights of data subjects:

  • Right to be informed — privacy notices explaining data collection
  • Right of access — Subject Access Request (SAR), respond within 30 days
  • Right to rectification — correct inaccurate data
  • Right to erasure ("right to be forgotten")
  • Right to restrict processing
  • Right to data portability — receive data in machine-readable format
  • Right to object — especially to direct marketing
  • Rights regarding automated decision making — right to human review of algorithmic decisions

Enforced by the ICO (Information Commissioner's Office). Fines up to £17.5 million or 4% of annual global turnover.

Computer Misuse Act 1990

SectionOffenceDetailMax Penalty
Section 1Unauthorised accessKnowingly accessing a computer without permission. Intent to access is sufficient — no need to steal data.2 years
Section 2Unauthorised access with intentGaining access to commit further crime (fraud, blackmail). The further offence need not be committed.5 years
Section 3Unauthorised modificationDeliberately modifying data: deploying malware, deleting files, ransomware, DDoS attacks.10 years

Key point: The Act covers intent and authorisation. Penetration testers with written permission are not committing an offence. Accessing "just to look around" without permission IS a Section 1 offence.

Copyright, Designs and Patents Act 1988

Protects intellectual property: software source/object code, databases, digital media, website content. Illegal to copy, distribute, modify, or sell copyrighted material without permission. Lasts creator's lifetime plus 70 years.

Regulation of Investigatory Powers Act 2000 (RIPA)

Governs surveillance powers of public bodies:

  • Authorises interception of communications with warrants
  • Allows acquisition of metadata (who, when, where — not content)
  • Permits covert surveillance
  • Can require individuals to disclose encryption keys (Section 49)
  • The Investigatory Powers Act 2016 extended powers, requiring ISPs to retain browsing histories for 12 months

Freedom of Information Act 2000

Right to request information held by public authorities. Must respond within 20 working days. Exemptions for national security, personal data, law enforcement.

Always: (1) name the Act and year, (2) use correct terminology (data controller, unauthorised access), (3) explain how it applies to the scenario, (4) mention enforcement body and penalties.

4.8.8 Professional Standards — BCS Code of Conduct

The BCS (British Computer Society) is the Chartered Institute for IT. Its Code of Conduct has four sections:

SectionKey Requirements
1. Public InterestHave due regard for public health, privacy, security, wellbeing, and the environment. Promote equal access. Comply with legislation.
2. Professional Competence & IntegrityOnly take on work within your competence. Keep skills up to date (CPD). Respect confidentiality. Reject bribery/corruption.
3. Duty to Relevant AuthorityWork with due care and diligence. Avoid conflicts of interest. Accept responsibility for supervised work. Do not take on unethical work.
4. Duty to the ProfessionUphold the profession's reputation. Share knowledge. Mentor colleagues. Report unethical behaviour through appropriate channels.

Key distinction: The BCS Code is a professional code, not law. Breach results in disciplinary action (loss of membership/Chartered status), not criminal prosecution. But prohibited activities may also breach actual laws (e.g., Computer Misuse Act).

Whistleblowing: The Code supports raising concerns about unethical practices. The Public Interest Disclosure Act 1998 protects whistleblowers legally.

4.8.10 Social Media — Cultural and Personal Impact

Positive Effects of Social Media

  • Community and connection — enables people separated by geography or disability to maintain relationships
  • Information sharing — rapid spread of news, public health campaigns, emergency alerts
  • Civic participation — organising protests, petitions, raising awareness of social issues
  • Economic opportunity — small businesses can market directly; influencers monetise content
  • Education — free tutorials, academic communities, open knowledge sharing

Negative Effects of Social Media

  • Mental health — links to anxiety, depression, low self-esteem (especially in young people); social comparison, FOMO, dopamine-driven validation
  • Misinformation / disinformation — false content spreads faster than corrections; echo chambers and filter bubbles reinforce existing beliefs
  • Addiction — deliberately engineered engagement mechanics (infinite scroll, notifications, variable rewards) exploit psychological vulnerabilities
  • Privacy erosion — extensive personal data collected and monetised through targeted advertising
  • Political polarisation — algorithms amplify outrage and division to maximise engagement

Cyberbullying

Cyberbullying: Bullying conducted through digital technology — social media, messaging apps, gaming platforms, or online forums. It includes harassment, threats, posting embarrassing content, impersonation, and exclusion.

Always mention legislation — the Online Safety Act 2023 places legal duties on platforms to prevent harmful content.

4.8.12 Worked Exam-Style Scenarios

Scenario A: Facial Recognition in Schools

A school proposes using AI facial recognition cameras to track student attendance automatically and alert parents if a student leaves the site.

Issue TypeAnalysis
LegalStudents are minors — biometric data requires explicit parental consent under DPA 2018. School must conduct a Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA). ICO must be notified if data breach occurs.
EthicalBCS Code (Public Interest) requires due regard to privacy. Is facial recognition proportionate to the need? Manual registers may suffice.
MoralDoes constant surveillance harm trust and student autonomy? Is it right to normalise biometric tracking in childhood?
CulturalCreates a culture of surveillance; students from certain ethnic groups may be disproportionately misidentified (AI bias).
StakeholdersStudents (privacy, safety); parents (protection, consent); staff (reduced admin); school governors (liability, safeguarding duty); ICO (regulatory compliance).

Scenario B: Company Sells Customer Purchase Data

An online retailer sells detailed purchase history data to insurance companies without customers' knowledge.

Issue TypeAnalysis
LegalViolates DPA 2018 Principle 1 (lawful basis required) and Principle 2 (purpose limitation). ICO can fine up to £17.5 million or 4% of global turnover. Customers have right to object and right to erasure.
EthicalBreaches BCS Code (Public Interest — duty to protect privacy). Customers have reasonable expectation data is used only for purchases.
MoralInsurance pricing based on purchase data (e.g., alcohol purchases raising premiums) could be seen as discriminatory and unfair.
MitigationObtain explicit consent; anonymise data; privacy-by-design; regular audits; whistleblower protections for staff who identify the practice.
In AQA extended-answer questions (6-12 marks), use a clear structure: (1) identify the issue and classify it (moral/ethical/legal/cultural), (2) name the specific legislation or professional code, (3) explain stakeholder perspectives, (4) suggest a balanced conclusion. Avoid one-sided answers — present the arguments for and against, then conclude. Use connectives like "however", "on the other hand", "consequently".

4.8.13 Health and Safety in Computing

The Health and Safety at Work Act 1974 (HASAWA) places a duty on employers to ensure the health, safety and welfare of all employees so far as is reasonably practicable. For computing environments this means minimising physical and psychological risks from prolonged computer use.

Physical Health Risks

Repetitive Strain Injury (RSI)Repetitive hand/wrist movements from typing or mouse use; inflammation of tendons and nerves (e.g. carpal tunnel syndrome)Ergonomic keyboards and mice; wrist rests; regular breaks (Pomodoro technique); voice recognition software
Eye strain / Computer Vision SyndromeStaring at screens for prolonged periods without breaks; glare; incorrect screen brightness or distance20-20-20 rule (every 20 min, look 20 ft away for 20 sec); anti-glare screens; correct monitor height and distance (50–70 cm); regular eye tests (employer-funded under Display Screen Equipment Regulations)

Psychological Health Risks

  • Technostress: Anxiety from constant connectivity, notification overload, and expectation of 24/7 availability
  • Burnout: Blurred work-life boundaries especially with remote/hybrid working
  • Digital fatigue: Mental exhaustion from excessive video calls (Zoom fatigue) and screen-based work
  • Workplace surveillance anxiety: Stress induced by monitoring of keystrokes, emails, and productivity metrics

Employer Responsibilities

Under the Display Screen Equipment (DSE) Regulations 1992 (updated under HASAWA), employers must:

  • Conduct DSE risk assessments for all regular screen users
  • Provide adjustable furniture and equipment
  • Offer free eye tests and contribute to corrective eyewear if needed for screen work
  • Give adequate breaks and varied tasks to prevent RSI
  • Provide training on safe workstation setup
Health and safety questions often ask you to: (1) name HASAWA 1974 and DSE Regulations 1992, (2) describe specific conditions (RSI, eye strain, back pain) with causes, (3) explain employer duties and practical mitigations, (4) distinguish physical from psychological risks. For 6-mark answers, cover at least three distinct health risks with prevention for each.

4.8.14 Privacy vs Security — The Core Tension

Many of the most important ethical debates in computing reduce to a fundamental tension: the right to privacy conflicts with the need for security. This applies to state surveillance, corporate data collection, and end-to-end encryption.

IssuePrivacy ArgumentSecurity Argument
End-to-end encryption (E2EE)Individuals have the right to private communication free from state or corporate interception; encryption protects journalists, activists, abuse victimsE2EE prevents law enforcement from accessing evidence of terrorism, child exploitation, and organised crime; governments argue backdoors are necessary
Mass surveillanceBlanket surveillance of innocent people without suspicion violates Article 8 ECHR; creates chilling effect on free speech and dissentPattern analysis across large datasets can identify threats before attacks occur; targeted surveillance is insufficient for catching sophisticated networks
Data retention by ISPsRetaining browsing history of all users is disproportionate; innocent people's data held indefinitely is a privacy violationHistorical browsing records are invaluable evidence in serious crime investigations; retrospective access requires data to have been stored
Facial recognition in publicAnonymity in public spaces is a civil liberty; misidentification harms innocents; chilling effect on protests and free assemblyQuickly identifies suspects, finds missing persons, and deters crime in public spaces

Key conclusion for essays: Most democracies accept that neither right is absolute. The test is proportionality — are surveillance powers proportionate to the threat, subject to judicial oversight, and time-limited? The ECHR Article 8 allows interference with privacy "in accordance with the law" and "necessary in a democratic society" for specified purposes including national security and crime prevention.

Privacy vs security is a classic AQA extended-answer theme. Always: (1) acknowledge both legitimate interests, (2) reference specific legislation (RIPA 2000, Investigatory Powers Act 2016, Article 8 ECHR), (3) discuss proportionality and oversight as the balancing mechanism, (4) use specific examples (Snowden revelations, encrypted messaging, CCTV). Avoid treating it as "privacy good, surveillance bad" — top answers present genuine tension.

4.8.15 Worked Extended-Answer Questions

Question 1 (6 marks): "Discuss the ethical and legal issues raised by a company using AI to automatically screen job applications and reject candidates."

Mark BandWhat Examiners Look For
5–6 marksIdentifies multiple distinct issues with specific legislation/concepts; balanced perspective; named examples; clear structure
3–4 marksIdentifies some issues with some supporting detail; may be one-sided or miss legal dimension
1–2 marksBasic points with little development; may confuse ethical and legal

Model Answer:

Using AI to screen job applications raises significant ethical issues around bias and fairness. Machine learning models trained on historical hiring data may encode past discrimination — for example, if a company historically hired mostly men for technical roles, the AI may downgrade female applicants' CVs. This perpetuates inequality even if no human decision-maker is consciously biased, making it particularly insidious.

There is also an accountability problem: if a candidate is rejected, the AI's decision may be inexplicable due to the "black box" nature of neural networks. This violates the principle of transparency and may undermine candidates' ability to challenge unfair decisions.

From a legal perspective, the Data Protection Act 2018 / UK GDPR grants individuals rights regarding automated decision-making under Article 22. Where AI makes solely automated decisions with significant effects (such as job rejection), individuals have the right to request human review and to receive a meaningful explanation. Failure to provide this could expose the company to ICO enforcement action and fines of up to £17.5 million.

The BCS Code of Conduct (Section 1 — Public Interest) requires professionals to promote equal access and avoid discrimination. A developer who knows the AI system is biased but fails to raise it may be breaching their professional obligations.

On the other hand, AI screening can remove human prejudice (interviewers showing unconscious bias towards candidates with similar backgrounds) and handle high application volumes consistently. However, these benefits depend entirely on the quality of training data and ongoing bias auditing, which many companies do not conduct rigorously.

Question 2 (8 marks): "A government proposes mandatory 'backdoors' in all encrypted messaging apps so security services can access communications with a court warrant. Discuss the arguments for and against this proposal."

Model Answer:

Arguments for: End-to-end encryption prevents law enforcement from accessing communications even with lawful court warrants. This creates what the FBI has called "going dark" — an investigative dead zone exploited by terrorists, paedophile networks, and organised criminals. The Investigatory Powers Act 2016 already permits targeted interception with judicial authorisation; backdoors would extend this capability to encrypted platforms that currently cannot comply even when ordered by courts. Proponents argue that just as physical premises can be searched with a warrant, digital communications should be accessible under the same legal framework.

Arguments against: Cryptographers and security experts argue that a backdoor for government is mathematically a backdoor for everyone. Once a vulnerability is engineered into a system, it can be discovered and exploited by hostile states, criminal hackers, and surveillance-heavy regimes. There is no "responsible vulnerability." The security of banking, healthcare, and critical infrastructure depends on the same encryption being proposed for weakening.

Furthermore, determined bad actors would simply switch to open-source encryption tools that cannot be mandated to include backdoors, meaning only law-abiding citizens' security would be reduced. Citizens have a right to private communication under Article 8 of the European Convention on Human Rights, and mass-decryptable communications would create a chilling effect on free speech and political dissent.

From a cultural perspective, different societies weigh security and privacy differently — authoritarian governments already use backdoors to suppress opposition. Introducing them in democracies risks normalising practices incompatible with civil liberties.

Conclusion: The proposal creates an irresolvable technical contradiction: secure backdoors cannot exist. Most security researchers, companies including Apple and Signal, and bodies including the NCSC (UK's cyber security agency) have concluded that the security costs outweigh the investigative benefits. Alternative approaches — improving other investigative techniques, metadata analysis, and greater international cooperation on lawful intercept — may achieve security goals without undermining encryption.

For 8-12 mark AQA questions: structure as FOR points (3–4 developed arguments with examples) → AGAINST points (3–4 developed arguments) → CONCLUSION (weigh the evidence, don't sit on the fence). Each point should name a specific law, body, or real example. Use technical vocabulary: end-to-end encryption, backdoor, Article 8 ECHR, Investigatory Powers Act, chilling effect, function creep.

Question 3 (6 marks): "Describe the health and safety risks of working with computers and explain how employers can reduce these risks."

Model Answer:

Prolonged computer use causes Repetitive Strain Injury (RSI), particularly carpal tunnel syndrome, from repeated keyboard and mouse movements causing inflammation of tendons. Employers must provide ergonomic keyboards, wrist rests, and ensure regular task rotation to reduce repetitive motion.

Eye strain results from sustained focusing on screens, often worsened by glare and incorrect screen distance. Under the Display Screen Equipment Regulations 1992 (made under HASAWA 1974), employers must offer free eye tests and fund corrective eyewear where needed specifically for screen work. Employees should be advised of the 20-20-20 rule.

Back and neck pain from poor posture and unsuitable furniture affects many desk workers. Employers must conduct DSE risk assessments and provide height-adjustable chairs, monitors at eye level, and lumbar support. Sit-stand desks reduce the risks of prolonged sedentary posture.

Psychologically, technostress and burnout arise from always-on connectivity expectations, excessive notifications, and blurred work-life boundaries — particularly in remote working. Employers can mitigate this through clear policies on out-of-hours contact, workload management, and regular breaks.

Overall, HASAWA 1974 places a legal duty on employers to ensure employees' health and safety "so far as is reasonably practicable." Failure to comply exposes employers to HSE enforcement, fines, and civil liability if employees suffer harm.
