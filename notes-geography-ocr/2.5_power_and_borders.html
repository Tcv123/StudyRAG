Power & Borders (OCR Component 2)

This topic examines the nature of political power, how it is exercised at different scales, and the significance of borders in shaping geopolitical relationships. It explores the concept of sovereignty, the characteristics and influence of superpowers, theories of power, contested borders, and the evolving role of global governance institutions.

1. Sovereignty and the Nation-State

Sovereignty: The supreme authority of a state to govern itself and make decisions within its territorial boundaries without external interference. Sovereignty has internal (control over domestic affairs) and external (recognition by other states) dimensions.
Nation-state: A political unit in which the boundaries of the state (territory governed by a single authority) coincide with those of the nation (a group of people sharing common identity, culture, language, and history). True nation-states are rare; most states contain multiple national groups.

Key Concepts

Term Definition Examples
State A defined territory with a permanent population, government, and capacity to enter into relations with other states (Montevideo Convention, 1933) France, Nigeria, Brazil
Nation A group of people who share a common identity based on ethnicity, language, culture, or history - may or may not have a state Kurds (no state), Basques, Scots
Nation-state A state where the majority of citizens share a common national identity Japan, Iceland (closely aligned); very few pure examples
Multinational state A state containing multiple national groups UK (English, Scottish, Welsh, Northern Irish), India, Russia
Stateless nation A national group without its own sovereign state Kurds (~30-40 million across Turkey, Iraq, Syria, Iran), Palestinians, Tibetans
Failed state A state that has lost the ability to provide basic governance, security, and services to its population Somalia, South Sudan, Yemen

Challenges to Sovereignty

State sovereignty in the 21st century faces multiple challenges:

  • Globalisation: TNCs operate across borders, sometimes with more economic power than states. Global financial markets can destabilise national economies.
  • Supranational organisations: EU, WTO, and UN rules can override national decision-making. EU law takes precedence over domestic law in member states.
  • International law: The ICC and R2P doctrine challenge the idea that states can do whatever they wish within their borders.
  • Technology: Cyber warfare, social media, and digital communication enable non-state actors to challenge state control of information and security.
  • Separatist movements: Catalonia, Scotland, Quebec - internal demands for independence challenge territorial integrity.
Exam Tip: Brexit is an excellent example for discussing sovereignty. The Leave campaign's argument centred on reclaiming sovereignty from the EU, but critics argue that in an interconnected world, formal sovereignty does not necessarily equate to greater real-world power or influence. Use it to discuss the tension between sovereignty and interdependence.

2. Superpowers

Superpower: A state with dominant influence in global affairs, possessing overwhelming military, economic, political, and cultural power that extends far beyond its borders. During the Cold War, the USA and USSR were the two superpowers. Today, the USA remains the sole superpower, with China as a rising challenger.
Emerging power: A state whose economic growth and increasing global influence suggest it could become a superpower in future. Often characterised by large populations, rapid industrialisation, and growing diplomatic assertiveness. Examples include China, India, and Brazil.

3. Theories of Power

Joseph Nye's Concept of Soft Power

Political scientist Joseph Nye coined the term "soft power" in 1990 to describe the ability to shape the preferences of others through attraction rather than coercion. Soft power rests on three pillars:

  1. Culture: When a nation's culture includes universally attractive values and practices (e.g., Hollywood, K-pop, British universities, French cuisine)
  2. Political values: When a nation lives up to its stated values at home and abroad (democracy, human rights, rule of law)
  3. Foreign policy: When policies are seen as legitimate and morally authoritative (multilateralism, development aid, climate leadership)
Case Study - China's Belt and Road Initiative (BRI): Launched in 2013, the BRI is China's flagship smart power initiative, combining economic investment (ports, railways, digital infrastructure across 140+ countries) with diplomatic influence. By 2024, China had invested over $1 trillion globally. Supporters argue the BRI fills critical infrastructure gaps in LICs. Critics warn of "debt-trap diplomacy" (Sri Lanka's Hambantota Port was leased to China for 99 years after the country defaulted on loans), environmental damage, and the extension of Chinese political influence. The BRI demonstrates how economic power can be leveraged for geopolitical advantage.

Geopolitical Theories

Theory Key Thinker Core Idea Relevance Today
Heartland Theory Halford Mackinder (1904) Whoever controls the Eurasian "Heartland" (Central Asia/Russia) controls the world. Land power is supreme. Explains Russian strategic anxiety about NATO expansion; China's westward BRI expansion into Central Asia
Rimland Theory Nicholas Spykman (1944) The coastal "Rimland" of Eurasia (Western Europe, Middle East, South/East Asia) is more strategically important than the Heartland. Explains US strategy of overseas bases and alliances along the Eurasian periphery
World Systems Theory Immanuel Wallerstein (1974) The world is divided into core (dominant), semi-periphery, and periphery (dependent) nations. Power and wealth flow from periphery to core. Explains persistent inequality; unequal trade relationships; dependency of LICs on HICs
Clash of Civilisations Samuel Huntington (1993) Future conflicts will be along cultural/civilisational lines rather than ideological (Cold War) or economic. Debated in context of "War on Terror", rise of political Islam, China-West tensions. Widely criticised as essentialist and reductive.

4. Contested Borders

Contested border: A boundary between states (or within a state) that is disputed, with competing claims to sovereignty over the territory. Contested borders can lead to conflict, diplomatic tensions, displacement, and human rights abuses.
Case Study - South China Sea: China claims approximately 90% of the South China Sea through its "nine-dash line" claim, overlapping with claims from the Philippines, Vietnam, Malaysia, Brunei, and Taiwan. The area is strategically vital: $3.4 trillion in trade passes through annually, it contains significant oil and gas reserves, and rich fishing grounds. China has constructed artificial islands with military facilities (airstrips, radar, missile systems). In 2016, the Permanent Court of Arbitration ruled China's claims had no legal basis under UNCLOS, but China rejected the ruling. The USA conducts "freedom of navigation" operations to challenge China's claims, raising the risk of military confrontation. This dispute illustrates how competing resource demands, national identity, and great power rivalry can turn maritime borders into flashpoints.
Case Study - Russia-Ukraine Conflict: Russia's annexation of Crimea in 2014 and full-scale invasion of Ukraine in February 2022 represents the most significant challenge to European border stability since 1945. Russia's motivations include preventing Ukraine's NATO membership, protecting ethnic Russians in eastern Ukraine, and reasserting its sphere of influence. The invasion has caused tens of thousands of casualties, displaced 14+ million people, and triggered the largest refugee crisis in Europe since WWII. Western responses include unprecedented economic sanctions, arms supplies to Ukraine, and NATO reinforcement of eastern member states. The conflict has exposed the limitations of international law and organisations in preventing aggression by a nuclear-armed permanent Security Council member.

5. Geopolitics and Global Governance

Global governance: The collective management of common problems at the international level through institutions, rules, norms, and processes. It includes formal organisations (UN, WTO, NATO, ICC) and informal arrangements (G7, G20, climate agreements).

The United Nations

The UN remains the centrepiece of global governance but faces significant criticisms:

Strengths Limitations
Universal membership (193 states) Security Council P5 veto paralyses action on major crises (Syria, Ukraine)
Legitimacy and moral authority No standing army; dependent on member states for peacekeeping troops
Specialised agencies deliver vital services (WHO, UNHCR, UNICEF) Under-funded; major powers withhold or reduce contributions
Platform for negotiation and multilateral agreements Slow bureaucracy; reform of Security Council composition blocked
Peacekeeping has prevented escalation in some conflicts Peacekeeping failures (Rwanda 1994, Srebrenica 1995) undermine credibility

NATO (North Atlantic Treaty Organisation)

Founded in 1949 as a collective defence alliance against the Soviet Union, NATO has 31 members (Finland joined 2023, Sweden 2024). Article 5 guarantees collective defence: an attack on one member is treated as an attack on all.

  • Post-Cold War role: NATO expanded eastward (Poland 1999, Baltic States 2004), intervened in Kosovo (1999), Afghanistan (2001-2021), and Libya (2011)
  • Current challenges: Russian aggression in Ukraine has reinvigorated NATO; increased defence spending demands; balancing US and European interests; Turkey's ambiguous position
  • Criticism: Russia views NATO expansion as a direct threat; some argue NATO provoked the Ukraine crisis; burden-sharing debates (US pays disproportionate share)

The WTO and Economic Governance

The WTO regulates international trade through multilateral agreements and dispute resolution. It promotes free trade but faces criticism for favouring HICs, failing to complete the Doha Development Round, and being unable to address new challenges (digital trade, state subsidies, environmental standards). The rise of bilateral/regional trade agreements (CPTPP, RCEP) challenges WTO centrality.

6. Changing Global Power Dynamics

The Shift from Unipolarity to Multipolarity

The post-Cold War unipolar moment (US dominance) is giving way to a more multipolar world order characterised by:

  • China's rise: Second-largest economy; BRI; growing military; assertive foreign policy in South China Sea, Taiwan Strait, and globally
  • BRICS expansion: Brazil, Russia, India, China, South Africa (expanded to include Egypt, Ethiopia, Iran, Saudi Arabia, UAE in 2024) - represents 46% of world population; challenge to Western-dominated institutions
  • Declining US hegemony: Withdrawal from Afghanistan; political polarisation; relative economic decline compared to Asia; questioning of alliance commitments
  • Regional powers: Turkey, Saudi Arabia, India, and others increasingly pursue independent foreign policies
  • Non-state actors: TNCs, terrorist organisations, NGOs, and social media platforms exert increasing influence on global affairs
Case Study - The Rise of China: China's transformation from an isolated, agrarian economy to the world's second-largest economy in four decades is arguably the most significant geopolitical shift since the end of the Cold War. Key milestones: joining the WTO (2001), overtaking Japan as world's 2nd largest economy (2010), launching the BRI (2013), and becoming the world's largest trading nation. China's approach combines economic soft power (infrastructure investment, trade partnerships) with growing hard power (military modernisation, South China Sea militarisation, increasing defence budget). Challenges include an ageing population, debt levels, territorial tensions with neighbours, and strategic rivalry with the USA (trade wars, technology competition, Taiwan). Whether China will become a true superpower or face constraints depends on its ability to manage these domestic and international challenges.
Exam Tip: OCR frequently asks you to evaluate whether global governance is effective. Strong answers will use specific examples to argue both sides: governance works well in some areas (trade, health emergencies, environmental standards) but fails in others (preventing war, addressing inequality, climate change targets). Always consider whose interests are served by current governance structures, and how emerging powers are creating alternative institutions.

7. Borders, Migration, and Identity

The Significance of Borders

Borders are not merely lines on a map - they are political, economic, social, and cultural constructs that shape human experience in profound ways:

  • Physical barriers: Walls and fences (US-Mexico border wall, Israel's West Bank barrier, Hungary's border fence) physically control movement
  • Economic boundaries: Tariffs, customs, and trade agreements create economic borders that affect livelihoods
  • Digital borders: Internet censorship, data sovereignty laws, and surveillance technologies create new forms of territorial control (China's Great Firewall)
  • Cultural boundaries: Language, religion, and ethnicity create invisible borders that may or may not align with political ones

Colonial Legacy and Borders

Many of today's contested borders in Africa, the Middle East, and South Asia were drawn by colonial powers with little regard for existing ethnic, linguistic, or cultural boundaries. The Berlin Conference (1884-85) divided Africa among European powers, creating states that grouped hostile ethnic groups together or split unified peoples across borders. This legacy continues to fuel conflict in many regions.

Case Study - The Sykes-Picot Agreement (1916): This secret agreement between Britain and France divided the Ottoman Empire's Middle Eastern territories into spheres of influence, creating the borders of modern Iraq, Syria, Lebanon, and Jordan. These borders grouped together Sunni, Shia, Kurdish, Christian, and other communities with little regard for local identities. The consequences include: the Kurdish question (30-40 million Kurds split across four states), sectarian conflict in Iraq and Syria, the Israel-Palestine conflict, and ongoing instability across the region. When ISIS declared its "caliphate" in 2014, it explicitly stated it was erasing the Sykes-Picot borders, demonstrating the enduring power of this colonial legacy.
Exam Tip: When discussing power and borders, always think synoptically. Link to globalisation (is the world becoming more or less borderless?), migration (how do borders affect human movement?), sovereignty (how do supranational institutions challenge state borders?), and inequality (how do borders create and maintain unequal access to resources and rights?). The strongest answers weave multiple themes together with well-chosen evidence.
