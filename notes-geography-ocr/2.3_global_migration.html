2.3 Global Migration

This topic explores the causes, patterns, and consequences of international migration. It examines migration theories, the impacts on source and host countries, and how migration is managed at national and international levels.

Key Migration Terminology

Migration: The permanent or semi-permanent movement of people from one place to another. International migration crosses national borders; internal migration occurs within a country.

Voluntary migrationA free choice to move, typically for economic or lifestyle reasons

DiasporaA scattered population whose origin lies in a different geographical area (e.g., Indian, Chinese, or Jewish diaspora)
RemittancesMoney sent by migrants back to their families in the source country

Migration Theories and Models

Lee's Migration Model (1966)

Everett Lee proposed that migration decisions are influenced by four sets of factors:

  • Push factors: Negative conditions at the origin that encourage people to leave
  • Pull factors: Positive conditions at the destination that attract people
  • Intervening obstacles: Barriers between origin and destination (distance, cost, legal restrictions, physical barriers)
  • Personal factors: Individual characteristics (age, education, family ties, risk tolerance) that affect the migration decision

Ravenstein's Laws of Migration (1885)

One of the earliest migration theories, many observations still hold:

  • Most migrants move only a short distance
  • Long-distance migrants tend to move to major cities
  • Each migration flow produces a counter-flow
  • Urban residents are less likely to migrate than rural residents
  • Females are more likely to migrate within their country; males are more likely to migrate internationally
  • Economic factors are the main cause of migration

Todaro's Model of Rural-Urban Migration

Michael Todaro (1969) argued that rural-urban migration is driven by the expected income differential between rural and urban areas, even if the migrant faces a risk of unemployment. People move because the expected urban wage (even accounting for unemployment probability) exceeds guaranteed rural income.

Exam Tip: Migration is rarely caused by a single push or pull factor — it is always a combination. Show this complexity in your essays. Also note that migration is selective: it is not the poorest who migrate internationally (they lack resources), but those with some education, savings, and networks.

Global Migration Patterns

Scale and Trends

  • Approximately 281 million international migrants globally (2020, UN) — about 3.6% of world population
  • The number has more than tripled since 1970
  • Most migrants move from developing to developed countries, but South-South migration is also significant (around 40%)
  • Top destination countries: USA (~51 million), Germany (~16 million), Saudi Arabia, Russia, UK, UAE
  • Top source countries: India, Mexico, Russia, China, Syria (due to conflict)
  • Refugees: ~100 million forcibly displaced people worldwide (2022, UNHCR); major source countries: Syria, Ukraine, Afghanistan, South Sudan

Case Study: EU Migration to the UK (Pre-Brexit)
Following EU enlargement in 2004 (A8 countries including Poland, Czech Republic joined), the UK experienced significant inward migration:
  • Scale: Net migration peaked at ~330,000/year by 2015; ~3.7 million EU nationals living in UK by 2019
  • Economic benefits: Migrants filled labour shortages in agriculture, construction, healthcare, hospitality; EU migrants paid more in taxes than they received in benefits (UCL study); GDP growth contribution
  • Social/cultural: Cultural diversity in cities; Polish became the second most spoken language; new restaurants, shops, cultural institutions
  • Tensions: Pressure on housing in some areas; perception of strain on NHS and schools; concerns about wage competition in low-skilled sectors; fuelled Brexit debate
  • Post-Brexit: Freedom of movement ended (2021); points-based immigration system introduced; labour shortages emerged in agriculture, hospitality, logistics, and healthcare
Case Study: Syrian Refugee Crisis (2011–present)
The Syrian civil war (from 2011) created the largest refugee crisis since World War II:
  • Scale: 6.8 million Syrian refugees abroad; 6.9 million internally displaced; ~13.5 million Syrians in need of humanitarian aid
  • Main host countries: Turkey (3.6 million — largest refugee population of any country); Lebanon (1.5 million, ~25% of population); Jordan (670,000); Germany (800,000+)
  • Impact on Turkey: Enormous strain on resources; most refugees live in urban areas, not camps; work in informal economy; tensions with local populations; Turkey received EU funding (~€6 billion) to host refugees
  • Impact on Lebanon: Refugee population equal to ~25% of the pre-crisis population; extreme strain on already fragile infrastructure, water, electricity; economic pressure; social tensions; no formal refugee camps (urban refugees)
  • European response: 2015 crisis when ~1 million migrants/refugees crossed Mediterranean; some countries welcomed refugees (Germany's "open door"); others closed borders (Hungary's fence); EU-Turkey deal (2016) aimed to reduce crossings; exposed divisions within EU
  • Mediterranean crossings: Thousands died attempting sea crossings in overcrowded boats; UNHCR recorded ~25,000 deaths/missing in Mediterranean since 2014

Migration and Development

Remittances

Remittances: Money sent by migrants working abroad back to their families and communities in the source country. In 2022, global remittances to LMICs reached ~$626 billion — more than three times total official development aid (ODA).

Positive impacts of remittances:

  • Direct poverty reduction — improve living standards for receiving families
  • Fund education and healthcare spending
  • Multiplier effect in local economies
  • More stable than other capital flows (FDI, aid) — continue during economic downturns

Limitations:

  • Often spent on consumption (food, housing) rather than productive investment
  • Can create inequality between families who receive remittances and those who do not
  • Dependency: communities rely on remittances rather than developing local economy
  • Transfer fees can be high (average ~6%)

International Frameworks

  • 1951 UN Refugee Convention: Defines who is a refugee and their rights; principle of non-refoulement (cannot return refugees to danger)
  • UNHCR: UN agency responsible for refugee protection and assistance
  • Global Compact on Migration (2018): Non-binding framework for international cooperation on migration; 23 objectives including protecting rights, reducing irregular migration, facilitating legal pathways
  • International Organization for Migration (IOM): Provides assistance and guidance on migration issues
Case Study: USA-Mexico Migration
One of the world's largest and most politically contentious migration corridors:
  • Scale: ~11 million Mexican-born migrants in USA; large undocumented population (estimated 5–6 million from Mexico)
  • Push factors: Poverty (especially rural areas), violence (drug cartels), limited employment, corruption
  • Pull factors: Higher wages (average US wage ~5x Mexican wage), job availability (agriculture, construction, services), family networks (established diaspora), proximity
  • Economic impact on USA: Migrants fill essential low-wage jobs; contribute $600+ billion to GDP; pay taxes; but some wage depression in low-skilled sectors
  • Remittances to Mexico: ~$60 billion/year (2022) — Mexico's largest source of foreign income, exceeding oil revenues
  • Political controversy: Trump border wall; family separation policies; DACA (Deferred Action for Childhood Arrivals) debate; migration consistently a top political issue
  • Central American transit migration: Migrants from Guatemala, Honduras, El Salvador transiting through Mexico to reach the USA, fleeing gang violence and poverty (Northern Triangle)

Climate Change and Migration

Environmental/climate migrants: People displaced by environmental change, including sea-level rise, drought, desertification, flooding, and extreme weather events. Not currently recognised as "refugees" under the 1951 Convention.
  • The World Bank estimates 216 million people could be internally displaced by climate change by 2050
  • Sea-level rise: Low-lying island nations (Tuvalu, Maldives, Kiribati) face existential threat
  • Desertification: Sahel region (sub-Saharan Africa) experiencing drought, driving southward migration
  • Extreme weather: Increased frequency of hurricanes, floods, and droughts displaces millions annually
  • Key issue: No international legal framework protects climate migrants — a major governance gap
Exam Tip: Migration questions require balanced evaluation. Always discuss impacts on both source AND host countries, consider different perspectives (economic, social, cultural, political), and use specific case study data. For essay questions, consider whether migration is positive or negative overall, and for whom. Show awareness that migration is a complex, contested issue with no simple answers.
