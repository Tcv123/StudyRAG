1.3 Hazardous Earth

This topic covers tectonic hazards (earthquakes, volcanoes, tsunamis), atmospheric hazards (tropical storms), and wildfire. It examines the physical processes that create hazards, the factors that influence their impacts, and strategies for managing risk.

Plate Tectonics Theory

Plate tectonics: The theory that Earth's lithosphere is divided into large rigid plates that move relative to one another, driven by convection currents in the mantle. Tectonic hazards (earthquakes, volcanoes) are concentrated at plate boundaries.

Lithosphere: The rigid outer layer comprising the crust and uppermost mantle (~100 km thick). Divided into tectonic plates.
Asthenosphere: The semi-molten, ductile layer beneath the lithosphere on which the plates float and move.

Evidence for Plate Tectonics

  • Continental fit: Alfred Wegener noted the jigsaw fit of continents (e.g., South America and Africa)
  • Fossil evidence: Identical fossils (Mesosaurus, Glossopteris) found on now-separated continents
  • Rock matches: Same rock types and geological structures across ocean basins
  • Palaeomagnetism: Alternating magnetic stripes in ocean floor basalt either side of mid-ocean ridges, showing sea-floor spreading
  • GPS measurements: Direct measurement of plate movement (a few cm/year)
  • Hot spots: Chains of volcanic islands (e.g., Hawaii) showing plate movement over a stationary mantle plume

Driving Mechanisms

  • Mantle convection: Heat from the core drives convection currents in the mantle; rising material pushes plates apart, sinking material pulls them together
  • Ridge push: Newly formed hot crust at mid-ocean ridges is elevated; gravity pushes the plate away from the ridge
  • Slab pull: Dense oceanic crust subducts and pulls the rest of the plate with it (considered the dominant mechanism)

Plate Boundaries

Divergent (Constructive) Boundary

Plates move apart. Rising convection currents push plates in opposite directions. Magma rises to fill the gap, creating new oceanic crust. Features: mid-ocean ridges, rift valleys, shield volcanoes, shallow earthquakes.

  • Oceanic-oceanic divergence: Mid-Atlantic Ridge (Iceland sits on this)
  • Continental-continental divergence: East African Rift Valley

Convergent (Destructive) Boundary

Plates move together. The denser plate subducts beneath the lighter one into the asthenosphere, where it melts. Features: deep ocean trenches, fold mountains, explosive composite volcanoes, deep earthquakes.

  • Oceanic-continental: Nazca Plate subducting beneath South American Plate → Andes mountains, Peru-Chile Trench
  • Oceanic-oceanic: Pacific Plate subducting beneath Philippine Plate → Mariana Trench, island arc volcanoes
  • Continental-continental: Indian Plate colliding with Eurasian Plate → Himalayas (no subduction, crust crumples upward)

Conservative (Transform) Boundary

Plates slide past each other laterally. No crust is created or destroyed. Friction causes plates to lock, then release energy as earthquakes. No volcanic activity. Example: San Andreas Fault, California.

Volcanic Hazards

Volcano: An opening in the Earth's crust through which magma, ash, and gases erupt. Volcanoes are found at constructive boundaries, destructive boundaries, and over hot spots.

Volcanic Hazards

  • Lava flows: Molten rock flowing downslope; destroys everything in its path but usually slow enough to evacuate
  • Pyroclastic flows: Fast-moving (up to 700 km/h) currents of hot gas and rock fragments; the deadliest volcanic hazard
  • Ash fall: Fine particles that can collapse roofs, contaminate water, disrupt aviation, and cause respiratory problems
  • Lahars: Volcanic mudflows created when volcanic material mixes with water (rain, melted ice); can travel far and fast
  • Volcanic gases: CO2, SO2, H2S — toxic, can cause acid rain
  • Tsunamis: Triggered by volcanic collapse or submarine eruption
  • Volcanic winter: Major eruptions inject SO2 into stratosphere, forming aerosols that reflect sunlight and cool global temperatures
Case Study: Mount Pinatubo, Philippines (1991)
One of the largest eruptions of the 20th century (VEI 6). Key details:
  • Primary effects: Pyroclastic flows covered 400 km²; ashfall collapsed roofs (up to 20 cm depth in some areas); lahars buried towns
  • Secondary effects: 847 deaths (mostly from collapsed roofs); 1 million displaced; farmland destroyed for years; global cooling of ~0.5°C for 2 years due to SO2 aerosols
  • Responses: USGS/PHIVOLCS monitoring (seismometers, gas sampling) enabled successful prediction and evacuation of 58,000 people from a 30 km exclusion zone. Clark Air Base evacuated. However, Typhoon Yunya hit at the same time, worsening lahar and ash impacts.
  • Long-term: Lahars continued for years during monsoon season, burying villages; indigenous Aeta people permanently displaced from ancestral lands

Seismic Hazards (Earthquakes)

Earthquake: A sudden release of energy in the Earth's crust that creates seismic waves. Caused by tectonic plate movement, volcanic activity, or (rarely) human activity (fracking, reservoir loading).
Focus (hypocentre): The point underground where the earthquake originates.
Epicentre: The point on the Earth's surface directly above the focus.

Earthquake Hazards

  • Ground shaking: Primary hazard; intensity depends on magnitude, depth, distance, and geology
  • Liquefaction: Saturated, loose soil behaves like liquid when shaken; buildings sink/tilt
  • Landslides: Shaking destabilises slopes, especially saturated or deforested ones
  • Tsunamis: Submarine earthquakes displace the ocean floor, generating waves that travel across oceans at up to 800 km/h
  • Secondary hazards: Fires (ruptured gas mains), flooding (broken dams/levees), disease outbreaks
Case Study: Nepal Earthquake (2015) — LIC
  • Magnitude: 7.8 Mw; shallow focus (15 km); epicentre 80 km NW of Kathmandu
  • Primary effects: ~9,000 deaths; 22,000 injured; 600,000 homes destroyed; historic temples and monuments in Kathmandu flattened
  • Secondary effects: Avalanches on Everest killed 19 climbers; landslides blocked roads; 3.5 million made homeless; waterborne disease outbreaks; schools destroyed (affecting 950,000 children)
  • Responses: International aid (over $4 billion pledged); India and China sent search and rescue teams; but remote mountain villages took days to reach. Poor infrastructure hampered response. Corruption and bureaucracy slowed reconstruction.
  • Vulnerability factors: Low income country; poor building standards (unreinforced masonry); limited emergency services; mountainous terrain; high population density in Kathmandu
Case Study: Japan Earthquake & Tsunami (2011) — HIC
  • Magnitude: 9.1 Mw (megathrust at subduction zone); shallow focus; epicentre 70 km offshore
  • Primary effects: Violent shaking for 6 minutes; tsunami waves up to 40 m high struck the coast within 30 minutes
  • Secondary effects: ~18,500 deaths (mostly from tsunami); 340,000 displaced; Fukushima nuclear plant meltdown (radiation release, 154,000 evacuated); £180 billion damage (most costly natural disaster in history)
  • Responses: Japan Meteorological Agency issued tsunami warning within 3 minutes; earthquake-resistant buildings survived shaking; $300 billion reconstruction; new 12 m sea walls built. But: sea walls were overtopped, nuclear safety questioned.
  • Why impacts were severe despite HIC status: Extreme magnitude; proximity; tsunami overwhelmed defences; nuclear risk created cascading disaster
Exam Tip: When comparing earthquake impacts in HICs vs LICs, discuss: building standards, emergency services, warning systems, infrastructure quality, governance/corruption, population density, economic resources for recovery. Always use specific data from your case studies.

Tropical Storms

Tropical storm (hurricane/cyclone/typhoon): An intense low-pressure weather system with sustained wind speeds exceeding 119 km/h (74 mph), forming over warm tropical oceans (26.5°C+). Named differently by region: hurricanes (Atlantic/NE Pacific), typhoons (NW Pacific), cyclones (Indian Ocean/S Pacific).

Formation Conditions

  • Sea surface temperature of at least 26.5°C to a depth of ~60 m
  • Located at least 5° north or south of the equator (Coriolis effect needed to initiate spin)
  • Low wind shear (consistent wind speeds at different altitudes)
  • Atmospheric instability (warm moist air rises rapidly)
  • Pre-existing low-pressure disturbance

Tropical Storm Hazards

  • Storm surge: A dome of water pushed ashore by low pressure and wind; the deadliest tropical storm hazard
  • High winds: Destroy buildings, infrastructure, power lines, trees
  • Intense rainfall & flooding: Torrential rain (up to 500 mm in 24 hours); river flooding, flash flooding
  • Landslides: Saturated slopes collapse, especially in mountainous tropical regions
  • Tornadoes: Can be spawned within the outer rainbands
Case Study: Typhoon Haiyan, Philippines (2013)
  • Category 5: Sustained winds of 315 km/h; one of the strongest storms ever recorded at landfall
  • Storm surge: Up to 5 m in Tacloban, devastating the low-lying city
  • Impacts: Over 6,300 deaths; 4 million displaced; 1 million homes damaged/destroyed; total damage ~$2.9 billion; Tacloban airport destroyed; infrastructure devastated
  • Why so severe: Extreme intensity; low-lying coastal settlements; high poverty (many could not evacuate); inadequate warning communication (many did not understand "storm surge"); high population density
  • Response: Philippines government overwhelmed initially; international aid from UN, USA, UK; UK sent HMS Daring and HMS Illustrious; rebuilding programme "Build Back Better"; improved warning systems established

Wildfire

Wildfire: An uncontrolled fire that burns through vegetation in rural or wilderness areas. Can be caused naturally (lightning) or by human activity (arson, campfires, power lines). Increasingly severe due to climate change.

Conditions for Wildfire

  • Fuel: Dry vegetation, leaf litter, deadwood (fuel load)
  • Heat/ignition: Lightning, human activity, spontaneous combustion
  • Oxygen: Wind increases oxygen supply and drives the fire
  • Weather: Hot, dry conditions with low humidity; drought; strong winds
  • Topography: Fires spread faster uphill (preheating of fuel above)

Climate Change and Wildfire

Climate change increases wildfire risk through: higher temperatures, longer dry seasons, reduced soil moisture, earlier snowmelt, drought, and increased lightning frequency. This creates a positive feedback: fires release stored carbon as CO2, which enhances warming, which increases fire risk.

Case Study: Australian Bushfires (2019–2020)
  • Scale: Over 18.6 million hectares burned across NSW, Victoria, Queensland, and South Australia (an area larger than England)
  • Causes: Record-breaking drought (worst in decades); extreme heat (Penrith reached 48.9°C); strong winds; some human ignition (arson, power lines)
  • Impacts: 34 human deaths; ~3 billion animals killed or displaced; 5,900 buildings destroyed (including 2,779 homes); $10 billion AUD economic losses; smoke blanketed Sydney for weeks (air quality 11x hazardous levels); smoke reached New Zealand and South America
  • Ecological impact: Habitat destruction for endangered species (e.g., koalas); some ecosystems may not recover as fire intensity exceeded regeneration capacity
  • Response: Military deployed (Operation Bushfire Assist); international firefighters from USA, Canada, NZ; government criticised for slow response and climate change denial
  • Climate link: Scientists concluded climate change made the fires significantly more likely through drought and extreme heat conditions

Hazard Risk and Vulnerability

Risk: The probability of a hazard occurring AND causing harm. It depends on the interaction between the hazard itself and human vulnerability/exposure.
Vulnerability: The susceptibility of a population to the impacts of a hazard, determined by economic, social, and political factors.
Risk = Hazard × Vulnerability ÷ Capacity (to cope)

Hazard Management Strategies

The Hazard Management Cycle

Prediction and Monitoring

HazardPrediction Methods
VolcanoesSeismometers (earthquake swarms); tiltmeters (ground deformation); gas monitoring (SO2 increase); satellite thermal imaging; historical records
EarthquakesCannot be reliably predicted. Seismic gap theory; historical recurrence intervals; animal behaviour (unproven). Focus on preparedness instead.

Protection and Adaptation

  • Earthquake-resistant buildings: Steel frames, cross-bracing, base isolators, deep foundations, tuned mass dampers (e.g., Taipei 101)
  • Land-use planning: Restricting development in high-risk areas (floodplains, volcanic flanks, fault lines)
  • Sea walls and levees: Protect against storm surges and tsunamis (but can be overtopped)
  • Education and drills: Japan's annual earthquake drills; "Drop, Cover, Hold On"
  • Community-based risk reduction: Local knowledge, building back better, insurance schemes
Exam Tip: For hazard management questions, evaluate the effectiveness of strategies using the Park Model (shows response and recovery over time) or the Hazard Management Cycle. Discuss short-term responses vs long-term mitigation. Always consider whether strategies are realistic for the level of development (LIC vs HIC).

Why People Live in Hazardous Areas

  • Fertile volcanic soils (e.g., Java, Indonesia)
  • Geothermal energy (e.g., Iceland)
  • Tourism and economic opportunity
  • Lack of alternative; poverty traps people in risky locations
  • Perception that disaster is unlikely (optimism bias)
  • Historical/cultural ties to place
  • Lack of hazard awareness or education
