1.1 Landscape Systems — Coastal Landscapes

Systems Approach to Landscapes

System: A set of interrelated components connected by flows of energy and materials, working together as a complex whole. Coastal landscapes operate as open systems with inputs, throughputs, outputs, and feedback loops.

Systems Terminology

  • Inputs: Energy from waves, wind, tides; sediment from rivers, cliff erosion, offshore sources; precipitation
  • Throughputs (transfers/processes): Erosion, transport (longshore drift), weathering, mass movement
  • Outputs: Sediment removed by longshore drift, dissolved minerals, offshore sediment loss
  • Stores: Beaches, sand dunes, spits, bars, mudflats
Dynamic equilibrium: A balanced state in a system where inputs and outputs are roughly equal. Any change in one component causes adjustments elsewhere to restore balance. For example, increased cliff erosion adds sediment to the beach, which then absorbs more wave energy and reduces further erosion.

Feedback Loops

  • Negative feedback: Self-regulating — a change triggers a response that counteracts the original change. Example: cliff collapse adds sediment to beach → wider beach absorbs wave energy → reduced erosion
  • Positive feedback: Self-reinforcing — a change triggers a response that amplifies the original change. Example: sea-level rise increases erosion → narrower beach → less wave energy absorption → more erosion

Waves

Wave: A transfer of energy through water caused by wind blowing over the sea surface. Waves are characterised by their height, wavelength, frequency, and type.

Wave Formation

Waves form when wind blows across the surface of the sea, transferring energy through friction. Three factors determine wave size:

  • Wind speed: Stronger winds generate larger waves
  • Wind duration: Longer blowing time builds bigger waves
  • Fetch: The distance of open water over which the wind blows. A longer fetch means larger, more powerful waves

Wave Behaviour

In deep water, water particles move in circular orbits. As waves approach the shore, the sea floor causes friction, the base of the wave slows, and the top topples forward, forming a breaker. The swash pushes water up the beach; the backwash pulls it back under gravity.

Exam Tip: When explaining wave type, always link frequency, swash/backwash balance, and the resulting beach profile. Examiners want to see the process-landform connection.

Coastal Erosion Processes

Marine Erosion

ProcessDescription
Hydraulic actionForce of water hitting the cliff face compresses air in cracks, exerting pressure that widens joints and fractures. When the wave retreats, explosive decompression occurs (cavitation).
Abrasion (corrasion)Waves hurl sand, pebbles, and rocks against the cliff face, wearing it away like sandpaper. Most effective during storms with large sediment loads.
AttritionRocks and pebbles carried by waves knock against each other, becoming smaller, smoother, and more rounded over time.
Corrosion (solution)Chemical dissolution of soluble rocks (e.g., limestone, chalk) by slightly acidic seawater containing carbonic acid.

Sub-aerial Processes

These operate on the cliff face from above, weakening rock and making it more vulnerable to marine erosion.

Weathering types:

  • Mechanical (physical) weathering: Freeze-thaw — water enters cracks, freezes and expands by 9%, widening cracks. Repeated cycles shatter rock. Also salt crystallisation in coastal areas.
  • Chemical weathering: Carbonation — rainwater + CO2 forms weak carbonic acid that dissolves calcium carbonate in limestone/chalk. Oxidation weakens iron-rich rocks.
  • Biological weathering: Plant roots grow into cracks and widen them. Burrowing organisms (piddocks) bore into rock. Algae produce acids.

Mass Movement

TypeDescriptionConditions
RockfallFragments break off steep cliff face under gravityFreeze-thaw weathering, steep bare cliffs
LandslideBlock of rock slides along a flat plane (bedding plane/fault)Saturated ground, weak rock layers
Rotational slumpCurved slip plane; material rotates as it movesClay/soft rock, heavy rainfall, undercutting
MudflowSaturated soil/clay flows downslopeHeavy rain on impermeable/clay-rich slopes
Soil creepSlow, gradual downslope movementOngoing process on most slopes

Sediment Transport

Methods of Transport

  • Traction: Large boulders and pebbles rolled along the seabed by wave action
  • Saltation: Smaller stones bounced along the seabed in a hopping motion
  • Suspension: Fine sand and silt carried within the water column
  • Solution: Dissolved minerals carried invisibly in the water

Longshore Drift (Littoral Drift)

Longshore drift: The movement of sediment along the coast by wave action. Waves approach the beach at an angle determined by the prevailing wind direction. Swash carries sediment up the beach at this angle, while backwash drags sediment straight back down the beach under gravity. This creates a zigzag movement of material along the shore.

Sediment Cells

Sediment cell (littoral cell): A self-contained stretch of coastline within which sediment movement is largely independent of adjacent cells. The coast of England and Wales is divided into 11 major sediment cells. Understanding these is crucial for coastal management because interventions in one cell should not affect another.

Coastal Deposition

Deposition occurs when the sea loses energy and can no longer carry its sediment load. This happens when:

  • Waves enter sheltered bays or behind headlands
  • Wind speed decreases
  • There is a large sediment supply (e.g., from river mouths)
  • Constructive waves dominate with strong swash
  • Longshore drift reaches a change in coastline direction

Erosional Landforms

Headlands and Bays

Form where the coast is made of alternating bands of hard and soft rock (discordant coastline). The sea erodes the weaker rock faster, forming bays, while the more resistant rock protrudes as headlands. Over time, wave refraction concentrates energy on the headlands and disperses it in the bays.

Cliff Profiles and Wave-Cut Platforms

  1. Waves attack the base of the cliff, especially between high and low tide marks
  2. Hydraulic action and abrasion create a wave-cut notch
  3. The rock above the notch becomes unsupported and collapses
  4. The cliff retreats, leaving a gently sloping wave-cut platform
  5. As the platform extends, waves lose energy crossing it, slowing further erosion

Caves, Arches, Stacks, and Stumps

  1. Cave: Hydraulic action and abrasion exploit a weakness (joint/fault) in a headland, eroding it into a cave
  2. Arch: Back-to-back caves erode through the headland to form an arch
  3. Stack: The roof of the arch collapses due to weathering and gravity, leaving an isolated column of rock
  4. Stump: Continued erosion at the base of the stack causes it to collapse, leaving a low stump visible at low tide
Case Study: Old Harry Rocks, Dorset
Located on the Purbeck coast, Old Harry Rocks are chalk stacks at the eastern end of the Jurassic Coast. Old Harry is a stack, and Old Harry's Wife (now a stump) collapsed in 1896. They demonstrate the headland erosion sequence: the Foreland headland has caves, arches, and stacks in various stages. The chalk is relatively resistant but has many joints that are exploited by hydraulic action. The site shows how geological structure (concordant/discordant coast) controls landform development.

Geo and Blow Hole

  • Geo: A narrow, steep-sided inlet formed when a cave roof collapses or a joint is eroded
  • Blow hole: A vertical shaft formed when wave pressure exploits a weakness in the cave roof; spray erupts through it during storms

Depositional Landforms

Beaches

The most common depositional landform. Sandy beaches tend to be wide and gently sloping (fine material easily moved by constructive waves). Shingle/pebble beaches are steeper because water percolates through the coarse material, reducing backwash.

Beach features: berms (ridges marking high tide), cusps (semicircular depressions), ripples, runnels

Spits

A spit is a long, narrow ridge of sand or shingle extending from the mainland into the sea at a point where the coastline changes direction. Longshore drift carries material past the change in direction. The end may curve (recurved tip) due to secondary wave action. A sheltered area behind the spit often develops into a salt marsh.

Case Study: Spurn Point, Humber Estuary
Spurn Point is a 5.5 km long sand and shingle spit extending across the mouth of the Humber Estuary. It is formed by longshore drift carrying material southward from Holderness cliffs. The spit has a recurved tip and encloses mudflats and salt marshes. It has been breached multiple times (e.g., 2013 storm surge) and is extremely dynamic, migrating westward. It is managed by the Yorkshire Wildlife Trust. The spit illustrates the link between cliff erosion (source) and deposition (store) within a sediment cell.

Bars and Tombolos

  • Bar: A spit that grows across a bay, sealing off a lagoon behind it. Example: Slapton Ley, Devon
  • Tombolo: A bar that connects the mainland to an offshore island. Example: Chesil Beach connecting Portland to the Dorset coast

Sand Dunes

Formed when sand is blown inland by onshore winds and trapped by vegetation or obstacles. Dunes develop in a succession (psammosere) from embryo dunes nearest the sea to mature fixed dunes inland, with increasing soil depth, humus, moisture, and biodiversity.

Concordant and Discordant Coastlines

  • Discordant coast: Rock bands run perpendicular to the coast → alternating headlands and bays (e.g., Swanage Bay area)
  • Concordant coast: Rock bands run parallel to the coast → if outer resistant rock is breached, the softer rock behind erodes into a cove (e.g., Lulworth Cove, Dorset)
Case Study: Lulworth Cove, Dorset
A concordant coastline where the outer band of resistant Portland limestone was breached by wave action. Behind it, the softer Wealden clay was rapidly eroded into a near-circular cove. The back wall of the cove is more resistant chalk, which slows further erosion. Stair Hole nearby shows an earlier stage in cove development with the limestone being breached in several places.

Coastal Management Strategies

Coastal management: Strategies used to protect coastlines from erosion and flooding, while considering economic, social, and environmental factors.

Shoreline Management Plans (SMPs)

SMP: A strategic plan for managing flood and erosion risk along a stretch of coastline. The coast is divided into management units and each is assigned one of four policies.
PolicyMeaning
Hold the lineMaintain current coastal defences; protect existing land
Advance the lineBuild new defences seaward of the current position
Managed realignmentAllow coastline to retreat in a controlled way
No active interventionLet natural processes take their course; no new defences
Case Study: Holderness Coast, East Yorkshire
Europe's fastest eroding coastline, retreating at an average of 1.8 m per year (some areas up to 10 m). The cliffs are made of soft, easily eroded boulder clay (glacial till). Key points:
  • Mappleton: Two rock groynes and rock armour were installed in 1991 at a cost of £2 million to protect the village. They were effective locally but starved beaches to the south (e.g., Cowden) of sediment, accelerating erosion there.
  • Withernsea: Sea wall and groynes protect the town. Tourism depends on the beach.
  • Skipsea: No defences — properties lost to the sea. Residents feel abandoned by the SMP "no active intervention" policy.
  • Spurn Point: The spit at the estuary mouth depends on material eroded from Holderness cliffs further north — protecting the cliffs would starve the spit of sediment.
  • The case illustrates conflicts between economic value, social justice, environmental sustainability, and the interconnectedness of the sediment cell.

Coastal Landscape as a System: Putting It All Together

Exam Tip: In 20-mark essays, use the systems framework to structure your answer. Show how inputs (wave energy, geology), throughputs (erosion, transport), and outputs (sediment loss) create landforms. Always include specific place examples and use correct terminology. Link management strategies back to the system — explain how human intervention disrupts dynamic equilibrium.

Key Connections

  • Cliff erosion provides sediment input → transported by longshore drift → deposited as spits/beaches
  • Geology determines cliff resistance → controls rate of erosion → shapes headlands and bays
  • Wave type (constructive/destructive) determines whether coastline is eroding or accreting
  • Human management interrupts natural processes → unintended consequences in other parts of the sediment cell
  • Climate change (rising sea levels, more storms) → increased erosion → positive feedback → management challenges
Exam Tip: For OCR A-Level, always use the terms "landscape system" and "dynamic equilibrium." Show you understand that landscapes are the result of interacting processes operating over time, and that human intervention creates new challenges within the system.
