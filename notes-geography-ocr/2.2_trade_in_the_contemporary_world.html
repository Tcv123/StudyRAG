2.2 Trade in the Contemporary World

This topic examines global trade patterns, the organisations and agreements that govern trade, the role of transnational corporations, and debates around free trade and fair trade. It considers how trade creates both opportunities and inequalities.

Global Trade Patterns

Trade: The exchange of goods and services between countries (international trade) or within countries (domestic trade). Trade allows countries to specialise in producing goods in which they have a comparative advantage, theoretically increasing global wealth.

Key Trade Terminology

TermDefinition
ExportsGoods and services sold to other countries
ImportsGoods and services bought from other countries
Balance of tradeDifference between value of exports and imports. A surplus means exports > imports; a deficit means imports > exports
Visible tradeTrade in physical goods (manufactured products, raw materials, food)
Invisible tradeTrade in services (finance, insurance, tourism, intellectual property)
Terms of tradeRatio of export prices to import prices. If export prices fall relative to import prices, terms of trade deteriorate
Comparative advantageA country can produce a good at a lower opportunity cost than another country, making specialisation and trade beneficial for both

Patterns of Global Trade

  • Dominance of HICs: The USA, EU, China, and Japan account for the majority of world trade. Trade is concentrated among wealthy nations and increasingly with China.
  • North-North trade: Most trade historically occurs between developed countries (manufactured goods and services)
  • North-South trade: Developing countries tend to export primary commodities (minerals, agricultural goods) to developed countries and import manufactured goods — this creates dependency
  • South-South trade: Growing rapidly, especially with China's investment in Africa and Asia
  • Rise of emerging economies: China is now the world's largest trading nation; India, Brazil, and other emerging economies are increasing their share
  • LICs' marginal share: Sub-Saharan Africa accounts for less than 3% of world trade despite having ~17% of world population

Why Trade Patterns Are Unequal

Primary Product Dependency

Primary product dependency: When a country relies heavily on exporting one or a few unprocessed raw materials (e.g., coffee, copper, cocoa, oil). This creates vulnerability because primary commodity prices are volatile and tend to decline relative to manufactured goods over time (deteriorating terms of trade).

Causes of Trade Inequality

  • Colonial legacy: Former colonies were structured to export raw materials to the colonial power; this dependency persists
  • Deteriorating terms of trade: Prices of primary commodities fall relative to manufactured goods, so LICs must export more to buy the same quantity of imports
  • Lack of infrastructure: Poor transport, storage, and processing facilities make it difficult to add value or access global markets
  • Protectionism: HICs use tariffs, quotas, and subsidies to protect their own industries, making it harder for LIC exports to compete
  • Subsidies: EU Common Agricultural Policy (CAP) and US farm subsidies allow HIC farmers to sell at below production cost, undercutting LIC farmers on the world market
  • Power imbalance: HICs dominate trade negotiations and institutions
  • Debt burden: Many LICs are trapped in debt cycles, diverting resources from investment to repayment
Exam Tip: Be able to explain the trade inequality cycle: LICs export cheap primary products → earn little → cannot invest in diversification/manufacturing → remain dependent on primary exports. This is linked to dependency theory (Andre Gunder Frank) — the idea that the "core" (HICs) develops at the expense of the "periphery" (LICs).

Trade Agreements and Organisations

World Trade Organization (WTO)

WTO: An international organisation (established 1995, successor to GATT) that regulates global trade, promotes free trade, and settles trade disputes between member states. Based in Geneva with 164 member states.

WTO Principles:

  • Most Favoured Nation (MFN): Trade advantages given to one country must be extended to all WTO members
  • Reciprocity: Trade concessions should be mutual
  • Transparency: Trade policies must be published and predictable
  • Special and differential treatment: LICs given extra time to comply with agreements

Criticisms of the WTO:

  • Dominated by HICs who set the agenda; LICs lack negotiating power
  • Free trade can damage LIC industries that cannot compete with subsidised HIC goods
  • Doha Development Round (2001) stalled because HICs refused to cut agricultural subsidies
  • Dispute resolution favours countries with resources to bring complex legal cases
  • Critics argue it prioritises economic growth over labour rights, environment, and development

Trade Blocs

Trade bloc: A group of countries that have agreed to reduce or eliminate trade barriers (tariffs, quotas) between members, while often maintaining common external tariffs against non-members.
TypeDescriptionExample
Free trade areaNo tariffs between members; each sets own external tariffsUSMCA (USA, Mexico, Canada); ASEAN FTA
Customs unionFree trade between members + common external tariffEU (also a single market)
Common marketCustoms union + free movement of labour and capitalEU Single Market
Economic unionCommon market + harmonised economic policies, possibly shared currencyEurozone (19 EU states sharing the euro)

Advantages and Disadvantages of Trade Blocs

AdvantagesDisadvantages
Increased trade between members (trade creation)Trade diversion: members may buy from less efficient bloc partners instead of cheaper non-members
Greater political cooperation and stabilityLoss of sovereignty: members must follow bloc rules
Larger market attracts foreign investmentExcludes non-members; can harm LICs
Economies of scale; lower consumer pricesMay benefit larger/richer members more
Greater negotiating power on world stageDecision-making can be slow and bureaucratic
Case Study: European Union (EU)
The EU is the world's largest single market (27 member states after Brexit). It demonstrates deep economic integration:
  • Single Market: Free movement of goods, services, capital, and people among members
  • Common Agricultural Policy (CAP): Subsidises EU farmers (~€50 billion/year); criticised for distorting global markets and harming LIC farmers who cannot compete with subsidised EU exports
  • Eurozone: 20 states share the euro currency, eliminating exchange rate risks but reducing monetary policy flexibility
  • Brexit (2016–2020): UK left the EU, arguing for sovereignty, immigration control, and ability to make independent trade deals. Resulted in new trade barriers, disrupted supply chains, and reduced UK-EU trade
  • Impact on LICs: EU "Everything But Arms" initiative gives 49 poorest countries duty-free, quota-free access to EU market, but CAP subsidies still undermine LIC agricultural exports

Transnational Corporations (TNCs)

Transnational Corporation (TNC): A company that operates in more than one country, with headquarters (HQ) typically in an HIC and production/operations in multiple countries. TNCs are key agents of globalisation and major players in global trade.

Why TNCs Locate Overseas

  • Lower labour costs: Wages in LICs/NEEs are a fraction of HIC wages
  • Access to raw materials: Mining, oil, and agricultural TNCs locate near resources
  • Access to new markets: Selling products in growing consumer economies
  • Reduced regulation: Weaker environmental and labour laws reduce costs
  • Tax incentives: Governments in developing countries offer tax breaks to attract FDI
  • Avoid trade barriers: Producing inside a trade bloc avoids external tariffs

Impacts of TNCs

PositiveNegative
EconomicCreate jobs; increase exports; transfer technology; tax revenue; infrastructure investmentProfit repatriation (leakage); exploit cheap labour; can leave if costs rise (footloose); tax avoidance
SocialTraining and skill development; improved infrastructure; access to global brandsPoor working conditions; long hours; child labour in supply chains; widening inequality
EnvironmentalMay bring higher standards; invest in green technologyPollution; deforestation; resource extraction; "race to the bottom" on regulations

Case Study: Apple Inc.
Apple is one of the world's most valuable companies, headquartered in Cupertino, California. It demonstrates the spatial division of labour:
  • Design & R&D: Cupertino, USA (high-skill, high-pay jobs)
  • Manufacturing: Outsourced to Foxconn in China (low-wage assembly; concerns about working conditions, long shifts, factory suicides). Also expanding to India and Vietnam.
  • Raw materials: Cobalt from DRC (artisanal mining, child labour allegations); lithium from Chile/Australia; rare earths from China
  • Sales: Global market — Apple Stores in major cities worldwide
  • Tax: Accused of routing profits through Ireland and other low-tax jurisdictions to minimise tax (the EU ordered Ireland to collect €13 billion in back taxes from Apple in 2016)
  • Value capture: Most of the retail price stays with Apple (design, marketing, profit); very little goes to assembly workers or raw material miners

Free Trade vs Protectionism

Arguments for Free Trade

  • Promotes specialisation and comparative advantage, increasing global efficiency
  • Lower prices for consumers through competition and economies of scale
  • Greater product variety and consumer choice
  • Encourages foreign direct investment (FDI) and technology transfer
  • Can stimulate economic growth in developing countries through export-led development

Arguments for Protectionism

  • Infant industry argument: New industries in LICs need protection from established HIC competitors until they are strong enough to compete
  • Job protection: Prevents "dumping" (selling goods below cost) and offshoring
  • National security: Protect strategic industries (food, energy, defence)
  • Environmental/labour standards: Prevent "race to the bottom" where countries lower standards to attract trade
  • Revenue: Tariffs generate government income (important for LICs with limited tax bases)

Methods of Protectionism

MethodDescription
TariffsTaxes on imported goods, making them more expensive and less competitive
QuotasLimits on the quantity of goods that can be imported
SubsidiesGovernment payments to domestic producers, reducing their costs and making them more competitive
Non-tariff barriersHealth/safety regulations, environmental standards, bureaucratic requirements that restrict imports
EmbargoesComplete ban on trade with a country (usually political)

Fair Trade

Fair trade: A trading partnership that seeks greater equity in international trade. It offers better trading conditions and rights to marginalised producers and workers, especially in the Global South. Fair trade-certified products guarantee a minimum price above production costs, plus a social premium for community investment.

How Fair Trade Works

  • Producers receive a guaranteed minimum price that covers the cost of sustainable production, regardless of market fluctuations
  • An additional Fairtrade Premium is paid for community development projects (schools, water, healthcare)
  • Requirements for democratic organisation (cooperatives), no child/forced labour, environmental standards
  • Long-term trading relationships provide stability and predictability for producers

Evaluating Fair Trade

StrengthsLimitations
Guaranteed minimum price provides income securityCovers only a tiny fraction of global trade (<1%)
Social premium funds community developmentHigher prices may reduce demand
Encourages sustainable farming practicesCertification costs can exclude the poorest farmers
Empowers farmers through cooperative structureDoesn't address structural inequalities in the global trading system
Raises consumer awareness of global trade issuesSome argue it creates dependency rather than competitiveness
Case Study: Fair Trade Coffee in Ethiopia
Ethiopia is the birthplace of coffee and Africa's largest coffee producer. Coffee accounts for ~30% of export earnings. Fair trade cooperatives (e.g., Oromia Coffee Farmers' Cooperative Union) have:
  • Provided farmers with a price floor above volatile market prices
  • Used the Fairtrade Premium to build schools, health clinics, clean water infrastructure
  • Improved farming techniques and quality, increasing export value
  • However, only a small proportion of Ethiopian coffee farmers are in fair trade cooperatives; most still sell through conventional middlemen at low prices
  • Broader issue: Ethiopia needs to develop its own coffee processing industry (roasting, branding) to capture more value — currently most value is added by European/US roasters

Trade and Development

Trade as a Development Strategy

  • Export-led growth: Countries like South Korea, China, and Vietnam have used trade (especially manufactured exports) as the engine of rapid development
  • Import substitution industrialisation (ISI): Some countries (e.g., Brazil, India in the mid-20th century) tried to develop by replacing imports with domestically produced goods, using tariffs to protect new industries
  • Special Economic Zones (SEZs): Designated areas with tax incentives and relaxed regulations to attract FDI and boost exports (e.g., Shenzhen, China)
Case Study: China's Trade-Led Development
China's transformation from an isolated, agricultural economy to the world's largest trading nation is the most significant trade story of the modern era:
  • After 1978 economic reforms (Deng Xiaoping), China opened SEZs to attract FDI
  • Joined the WTO in 2001, giving it access to global markets
  • Became the "factory of the world" for manufactured goods (cheap labour, massive workforce)
  • GDP grew from $150 billion (1978) to over $17 trillion (2023); 800 million lifted out of poverty
  • However: environmental degradation, labour exploitation, growing inequality, and geopolitical tensions (trade war with USA)
  • Belt and Road Initiative (BRI): China investing $1 trillion+ in infrastructure across Asia, Africa, and Europe to expand trade routes and geopolitical influence
Exam Tip: Trade questions often require you to evaluate whether trade benefits development. Structure your answer: (1) how trade can promote growth (theory); (2) how trade can be unequal and create dependency (reality); (3) role of TNCs, trade agreements, and fair trade; (4) case studies showing different outcomes. Always weigh both sides and reach a conclusion.
