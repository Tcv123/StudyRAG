1.2 Earth's Life Support Systems

This topic examines the water and carbon cycles as key Earth systems that sustain life. It explores how these cycles operate at different scales, how they are interconnected, and how human activity is disrupting them with consequences for climate change.

The Water Cycle (Hydrological Cycle)

Hydrological cycle: The continuous movement of water between the atmosphere, land surface, and subsurface. It is a closed system at the global scale (no water is added or lost), but open at the local/drainage basin scale (water enters and leaves).

Exam Tip: Only about 2.5% of all water is freshwater, and most of that is locked in ice. Only ~0.3% of freshwater is readily available in rivers, lakes, and accessible groundwater. This makes water a precious and unevenly distributed resource.

Key Flows (Transfers & Transformations)

  • Evaporation: Liquid water converts to water vapour using solar energy; greatest over warm tropical oceans
  • Transpiration: Water loss from plants through stomata; a major flux in vegetated areas
  • Evapotranspiration: Combined evaporation and transpiration from a land surface
  • Condensation: Water vapour cools and converts to liquid droplets, forming clouds
  • Precipitation: Water falling from the atmosphere as rain, snow, sleet, or hail
  • Infiltration: Water soaking into the soil from the surface
  • Percolation: Deeper movement of water through soil into underlying rock (groundwater)
  • Throughflow: Lateral movement of water through soil towards rivers
  • Groundwater flow: Slow movement of water through permeable rock (aquifer)
  • Surface runoff (overland flow): Water flowing over the ground surface into rivers; occurs when soil is saturated or impermeable
  • Interception: Precipitation caught by vegetation before reaching the ground
  • Stemflow: Water running down plant stems/trunks to the ground
  • Channel flow: Water flowing within a river channel towards the sea

Drainage Basin Hydrology

Drainage basin: The area of land drained by a river and its tributaries. It is an open system bounded by a watershed (ridge of high land). Water enters as precipitation and leaves via the river mouth (discharge) and evapotranspiration.

Water balance equation:

P = Q + E ± ΔS

Where P = precipitation, Q = discharge, E = evapotranspiration, ΔS = change in storage

Storm Hydrographs

Storm hydrograph: A graph showing how river discharge changes over time in response to a rainfall event. Key features: peak discharge, lag time, rising limb, falling (recession) limb, and baseflow.

The Carbon Cycle

Carbon cycle: The biogeochemical cycle by which carbon is exchanged between the atmosphere, oceans, biosphere, and lithosphere. Carbon is essential for all life and its movement between stores regulates Earth's climate through the greenhouse effect.

Carbon Fluxes (Transfers)

Biological fluxes:

  • Photosynthesis: Plants absorb CO2 from the atmosphere and convert it to organic carbon (glucose). Formula: 6CO2 + 6H2O → C6H12O6 + 6O2. This is the primary mechanism transferring carbon from atmosphere to biosphere.
  • Respiration: Living organisms break down glucose, releasing CO2 back to the atmosphere. The reverse of photosynthesis.
  • Decomposition: Dead organic matter broken down by bacteria/fungi, releasing CO2. Rate depends on temperature, moisture, and oxygen availability.

Geological/chemical fluxes:

  • Combustion: Burning of fossil fuels and biomass releases stored carbon as CO2. This is the largest human-caused flux.
  • Weathering: Chemical weathering of silicate rocks absorbs CO2 (carbonation). Very slow but significant over geological time.
  • Volcanic outgassing: CO2 released from Earth's interior during eruptions (small compared to human emissions).
  • Ocean absorption: CO2 dissolves in seawater (Henry's Law — cold water absorbs more). This creates carbonic acid (ocean acidification).
  • Sedimentation: Dead marine organisms (shells, skeletons) sink and accumulate on the ocean floor; over millions of years become limestone/chalk (lithification).

Interconnections Between Water and Carbon Cycles

The water and carbon cycles are deeply linked:

  • Vegetation: Plants need water for photosynthesis (which sequesters carbon). Transpiration links the two cycles — forests are both carbon sinks and drivers of local water cycling.
  • Oceans: The ocean is both the largest water store and a major carbon sink. CO2 dissolves more readily in cold water — thermohaline circulation transports carbon to the deep ocean.
  • Soil: Soil stores both water and carbon. Waterlogged conditions reduce decomposition, preserving soil carbon (e.g., peat bogs).
  • Climate: The carbon cycle regulates temperature via the greenhouse effect, which in turn drives evaporation rates and precipitation patterns in the water cycle.
  • Weathering: Chemical weathering requires water (carbonic acid) and removes atmospheric CO2.
Exam Tip: OCR examiners love questions on interconnections. Always link processes across the two cycles. For example: deforestation reduces photosynthesis (carbon cycle) AND reduces interception and transpiration (water cycle), increasing both atmospheric CO2 and flood risk.

Climate Change

The Greenhouse Effect

Natural greenhouse gases (CO2, CH4, N2O, water vapour) trap outgoing longwave radiation, keeping Earth's average temperature at about 15°C (without them it would be −18°C). The enhanced greenhouse effect is the additional warming caused by increased concentrations of these gases from human activity.

Evidence for Climate Change

  • Ice cores: Air bubbles trapped in Antarctic ice (Vostok, EPICA) show CO2 and temperature correlation over 800,000 years
  • Instrumental records: Global mean temperature has risen ~1.1°C since pre-industrial times
  • Sea level rise: ~20 cm rise since 1900 due to thermal expansion and ice melt
  • Glacial retreat: Most mountain glaciers are shrinking (e.g., Glacier National Park)
  • Ocean acidification: pH has dropped by ~0.1 units since pre-industrial era
  • Phenological changes: Earlier spring flowering, bird migration shifts

Feedback Mechanisms

Positive feedback: Amplifies the initial change. Negative feedback: Dampens/reverses the initial change.
FeedbackTypeMechanism
Ice-albedo feedbackPositiveWarming melts ice → darker surface exposed → absorbs more solar radiation → more warming
Permafrost thawPositiveWarming thaws permafrost → releases trapped CH4 and CO2 → enhanced greenhouse effect → more warming
Water vapour feedbackPositiveWarming increases evaporation → more water vapour (a greenhouse gas) → more warming
Increased cloud coverNegativeWarming increases evaporation → more clouds → increased albedo → reflect solar radiation → cooling (but complicated: low clouds cool, high clouds warm)
CO2 fertilisationNegativeHigher CO2 → increased photosynthesis → plants absorb more CO2 → slows warming (limited by water/nutrient availability)
Ocean solubility pumpPositiveWarming oceans absorb less CO2 (warmer water holds less gas) → more atmospheric CO2 → more warming

Human Impacts on the Carbon Cycle

Deforestation

  • Removes a major carbon sink (trees absorb CO2 via photosynthesis)
  • Burning or decomposition of trees releases stored carbon
  • Exposed soil oxidises, releasing more CO2
  • Reduces transpiration, affecting local water cycle and rainfall
Case Study: Amazon Rainforest
The Amazon stores ~150–200 billion tonnes of carbon in its biomass and soil. Deforestation (for cattle ranching, soy, logging) releases this carbon. Between 2001–2020, approximately 17% of the Amazon was lost. Studies suggest that parts of the eastern Amazon have become a net carbon source rather than a sink. Deforestation also reduces evapotranspiration, potentially reducing rainfall in the region (a positive feedback loop: less rain → more forest dieback → less rain). This is the "tipping point" risk.

Fossil Fuel Combustion

  • Releases carbon stored over millions of years in a very short time
  • CO2 levels have risen from ~280 ppm (pre-industrial) to over 420 ppm (2024)
  • Responsible for ~75% of anthropogenic greenhouse gas emissions
  • Accelerates the greenhouse effect far beyond natural rates

Human Impacts on the Water Cycle

  • Urbanisation: Impermeable surfaces increase surface runoff, reduce infiltration → flashier hydrographs, flood risk
  • Deforestation: Reduces interception and transpiration → more runoff, less evapotranspiration, reduced local rainfall
  • Agriculture: Irrigation depletes groundwater stores; drainage of wetlands removes water storage
  • Dam construction: Alters river regimes; creates new stores; reduces downstream sediment/water supply
  • Climate change: Increased evaporation, altered precipitation patterns, glacial melt
Case Study: Aral Sea
Once the world's 4th largest lake, the Aral Sea shrank dramatically from the 1960s when the Soviet Union diverted the Amu Darya and Syr Darya rivers for cotton irrigation. By 2014, the eastern basin had dried completely. This caused: collapse of the fishing industry (60,000 jobs lost), salinisation of remaining water, dust storms carrying pesticides from the exposed seabed, desertification of the surrounding area. The case study shows how human alteration of the water cycle can cause environmental catastrophe.

Management and Mitigation

Carbon Cycle Management

  • REDD+: Reducing Emissions from Deforestation and forest Degradation — financial incentives for developing countries to protect forests
  • Carbon capture and storage (CCS): Technology to capture CO2 from power stations and store it underground
  • Afforestation/reforestation: Planting trees to increase carbon sequestration
  • Renewable energy transition: Reducing fossil fuel dependence
  • International agreements: Paris Agreement (2015) — limit warming to 1.5–2°C above pre-industrial levels

Water Cycle Management

  • Sustainable drainage systems (SuDS): Permeable paving, green roofs, retention ponds to mimic natural drainage
  • Wetland restoration: Natural flood management and water storage
  • Water conservation: Reducing demand through efficient irrigation, metering
  • Catchment management: Integrated approach to managing water across entire drainage basins
Exam Tip: For 20-mark essays on this topic, structure your answer around: (1) the natural cycle, (2) human disruption of stores and flows, (3) consequences and feedbacks, (4) management strategies. Always include specific data and named examples. Show awareness that the water and carbon cycles are interconnected — disrupting one affects the other.
