2.1 Changing Spaces, Making Places

This topic explores the meaning and significance of places, how they are shaped by internal and external forces, and how places are represented, changed, and managed. It requires you to study your own local place and a contrasting place in depth.

What Is a Place?

Place: A location with meaning. Places are not just physical locations but are given identity and significance by human experiences, perceptions, and attachments. A space becomes a place when people give it meaning.
Space: A physical area without assigned meaning or emotional attachment. It is abstract and measurable.
Sense of place: The subjective, emotional attachment people feel towards a place, shaped by personal experience, memory, and identity.
Placelessness: Places that lack unique identity or character, often due to globalisation and standardisation (e.g., identical shopping centres, chain restaurants). Geographer Edward Relph introduced this concept.

Types of Place Identity

  • Locale: The physical setting of a place — its buildings, streets, landscape, layout
  • Location: The geographical position of a place in relation to other places (coordinates, accessibility)
  • Sense of place: The subjective meaning and emotional attachment people feel towards a place

Insider and Outsider Perspectives

Insider PerspectiveOutsider Perspective
Lived experience of people who belong to the placeViews of visitors, media, government
Deep knowledge and emotional connectionMay be superficial or based on stereotypes
Identity shaped by daily routines and relationshipsInfluenced by representations in media, art, tourism
May not notice gradual changesMay notice contrasts and differences more
Exam Tip: OCR expects you to understand that places are socially constructed — different people experience and perceive the same place differently depending on their age, gender, ethnicity, class, and length of residence. Use the terms "insider" and "outsider" in your answers.

Place Profiles: Demographic and Economic Characteristics

Quantitative Data for Place Profiles

Place profiles use statistical data to describe the characteristics of a place. Key indicators include:

  • Population size and density
  • Age structure: Young/ageing population; dependency ratios
  • Ethnicity and diversity: Ethnic composition; languages spoken
  • Employment structure: Primary/secondary/tertiary/quaternary sectors
  • Income levels: Average household income; IMD (Index of Multiple Deprivation)
  • Housing: Tenure (owner-occupied, private rented, social); house prices
  • Health: Life expectancy; infant mortality; access to healthcare
  • Education: GCSE/A-Level attainment; university participation rates
  • Crime rates

Qualitative Data

Understanding places also requires qualitative research: interviews, oral histories, photographs, mental maps, artistic representations, and lived experiences of residents.

How Places Change Over Time

Internal (Endogenous) Factors

Factors originating from within the place that shape its character:

  • Physical geography: Rivers, coastline, relief, climate, natural resources (e.g., coal deposits shaped mining towns)
  • Land use: Residential, commercial, industrial, agricultural zones
  • Built environment: Architecture, heritage buildings, infrastructure
  • Demographic characteristics: Age structure, ethnicity, population density
  • Local culture and community: Traditions, social networks, religious institutions

External (Exogenous) Factors

Forces acting on a place from outside:

  • Globalisation: Increased interconnectedness brings investment, migration, cultural exchange, but also homogenisation and job losses
  • Government policy: Planning decisions, enterprise zones, devolution, austerity measures
  • Transnational corporations (TNCs): Investment decisions by large companies can transform or devastate local economies
  • Migration: Inward migration changes demographics, culture, and services; outward migration can cause population decline
  • Technology: Digital connectivity, automation, online retail affect employment and accessibility
  • Economic restructuring: Shift from manufacturing to services (deindustrialisation) transforms places

Changing Economic Structures

Deindustrialisation

Deindustrialisation: The decline of manufacturing industry in a region or country, typically accompanied by job losses, population decline, and social deprivation. In the UK, manufacturing fell from 25% of GDP in 1970 to under 10% today.

The Shift to Service Economies

The UK has transitioned from a secondary (manufacturing) economy to a tertiary/quaternary (service/knowledge) economy. This has created winners and losers:

WinnersLosers
London and the South East (finance, tech, creative industries)Former industrial heartlands (North East, South Wales, Central Scotland)
Highly educated, skilled workersLow-skilled manual workers
University cities (Oxford, Cambridge, Bristol)Former mining/steel towns (Consett, Merthyr Tydfil)
Case Study: Detroit, USA — Deindustrialisation
Detroit was the centre of the American auto industry, home to Ford, GM, and Chrysler. In the 1950s, population peaked at 1.8 million. Deindustrialisation began in the 1970s as:
  • Automation replaced workers; foreign competition (Japanese cars) undercut US manufacturers
  • Suburbanisation drew wealthier residents (predominantly white) to the suburbs ("white flight")
  • Population fell to ~640,000 by 2020; huge areas of abandoned buildings
  • City filed for bankruptcy in 2013 ($18 billion in debt)
  • Poverty rate ~35%; crime rates among highest in USA
  • Racial inequality entrenched: 78% Black population left in the city, suburban areas predominantly white
  • Recent regeneration in the downtown core (tech startups, Dan Gilbert's investments), but benefits have not reached many residents

Globalisation and Place

Globalisation: The increasing interconnectedness of the world through trade, communication, migration, and cultural exchange, driven by advances in technology and transport, and facilitated by international agreements.

Impacts of Globalisation on Places

  • Economic: TNC investment creates jobs but also dependence; offshoring moves jobs abroad; global competition can destroy local industries
  • Cultural: Spread of Western culture (Americanisation); loss of local traditions; but also cultural diversity through migration
  • Demographic: International migration changes ethnic composition and age structures
  • Environmental: Increased consumption and transport; but also global environmental agreements
  • Placelessness: Globalisation creates identical "clone towns" with the same chain stores and fast food restaurants

Representations of Place

How Places Are Represented

The way a place is portrayed affects people's perceptions and can differ greatly from lived reality:

  • Media: News coverage often focuses on crime, deprivation, or spectacular events — creating stereotypes
  • Film and television: Can romanticise or stigmatise places (e.g., "gritty North" vs "affluent South" in UK dramas)
  • Marketing and place branding: Tourism boards, councils, and developers promote positive images to attract investment and visitors
  • Art and literature: Artists and writers shape perceptions (e.g., Constable's paintings of rural Suffolk, Dickens' London)
  • Social media: User-generated content creates new, often curated, representations
  • Statistics: Census data, deprivation indices provide quantitative representation
  • Maps: What is included/excluded from maps is a form of representation
Exam Tip: Always question whose perspective a representation reflects. Who created it? What was their purpose? What is included or excluded? Representations can be used to attract investment, justify regeneration, or reinforce stereotypes. Use the terms "formal" (official, statistical) and "informal" (lived experience, media) representations.

Place-Making and Regeneration

Regeneration: The investment in and renewal of areas that have experienced decline, aiming to improve economic, social, and environmental conditions. Can be government-led, private sector-led, or community-driven.

Types of Regeneration Strategy

StrategyDescriptionExample
Flagship developmentLarge-scale, iconic project to attract investment and change perceptionsLondon 2012 Olympics; Guggenheim Museum, Bilbao
Rebranding/reimagingChanging the identity and perception of a place through marketingGlasgow: "People Make Glasgow" campaign
Enterprise zonesDesignated areas with tax breaks and relaxed planning to attract businessesLondon Royal Docks EZ
Infrastructure investmentTransport improvements to increase accessibility and connectivityHS2; Crossrail (Elizabeth Line)
Housing renewalDemolition of poor housing and construction of new, mixed-tenure developmentsPark Hill, Sheffield
Cultural/creative regenerationUsing arts, culture, and heritage to transform an areaTate Modern, Bankside, London

Case Study: Stratford, East London — Olympic Regeneration
The 2012 Olympic Games was used as a catalyst for regeneration of one of London's most deprived areas:
  • Before: Stratford was characterised by brownfield land, industrial decline, contaminated soil, poor housing, high unemployment, low incomes, and poor connectivity
  • Investment: £9.3 billion spent on Olympic Park, transport, and infrastructure
  • Physical changes: Olympic Park converted to Queen Elizabeth Olympic Park; new Westfield Stratford City shopping centre; Athletes' Village became East Village (2,800 homes); new schools and health centres; Stratford International station (HS1)
  • Positive outcomes: 46,000 new jobs; 10,000+ new homes planned; improved transport (DLR, Jubilee Line, Overground); new green spaces; international profile raised
  • Criticisms: Gentrification displaced existing communities; house prices rose beyond locals' reach; affordable housing target missed (only 30% vs 50% planned); local businesses forced out by Westfield; benefits went to developers, not residents; social cleansing allegations
Case Study: Toxteth, Liverpool — Deprivation and Regeneration
  • Background: Deindustrialisation and port decline caused mass unemployment. Racial tensions led to the 1981 Toxteth riots. The area had severe deprivation: high unemployment, poor housing, low educational attainment, health inequalities
  • Regeneration attempts: Government investment in housing renewal; Liverpool European Capital of Culture 2008; Liverpool ONE shopping centre; waterfront regeneration (Albert Dock, Tate Liverpool)
  • Outcomes: City centre transformed; tourism increased; but Toxteth itself still among England's most deprived areas (IMD). Benefits concentrated in the city centre, not reaching surrounding communities. Gentrification in parts (Baltic Triangle) but persistent poverty in L8 postcode area.

Conflict and Inequality in Place Change

Gentrification

Gentrification: The process by which a previously low-income neighbourhood is transformed by the arrival of wealthier residents and businesses, leading to rising property prices, displacement of original residents, and changes in the character of the area.

Impacts of Gentrification

PositiveNegative
Physical improvement of buildings and streetsDisplacement of low-income residents who can no longer afford rent
Reduced crime and antisocial behaviourLoss of community identity and social networks
Increased local tax revenueLocal shops replaced by upmarket/chain businesses
Better services and amenitiesSocial tension between newcomers and original residents
Rising property values benefit ownersIncreased inequality; "social cleansing"

Stakeholder Conflicts

Place change creates tensions between different groups:

  • Developers want to maximise profit — prefer luxury housing and commercial space
  • Local residents want affordable housing, community facilities, and to preserve neighbourhood character
  • Local government balances economic growth with social needs; may favour investment over community wishes
  • National government sets policy frameworks (enterprise zones, planning regulations)
  • Environmental groups oppose development on green spaces or heritage sites
  • Businesses seek accessible locations with skilled labour pools

Your Local Place Study

Exam Tip: For the exam, you must be able to discuss TWO contrasting places in detail (your local place and one contrasting place). For each, you need:
  • Place profile data (demographics, economics, deprivation indices)
  • How the place has changed over time and the reasons for change
  • How different people perceive and experience the place (insider vs outsider)
  • How the place is represented in formal and informal ways
  • Evidence of connections to wider processes (globalisation, migration, government policy)
  • Qualitative and quantitative evidence to support your points
Use Census data, IMD data, local news, photographs, interviews, and fieldwork evidence. The examiner rewards specific, placed-based knowledge.

Exam Tip: Referencing Massey, Tuan, or Relph in your essays shows conceptual depth and will impress examiners. Use their ideas to frame your analysis of place change, identity, and conflict.
