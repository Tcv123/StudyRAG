Human Rights (OCR Component 2)

This topic explores the geographical dimensions of human rights, examining how rights are distributed unevenly across the world and the factors that influence their protection or violation. It covers development indicators, health and education inequalities, gender disparities, and the role of organisations in promoting and protecting human rights.

1. Geographies of Human Rights

Human rights: Fundamental rights and freedoms inherent to all human beings, regardless of race, sex, nationality, ethnicity, language, religion, or any other status. They include civil and political rights (freedom of speech, right to vote) and economic, social, and cultural rights (right to health, education, adequate standard of living).

The Universal Declaration of Human Rights (UDHR)

Adopted by the UN General Assembly on 10 December 1948 in Paris, the UDHR was drafted in response to the atrocities of World War II. It established for the first time a common standard of rights for all peoples and nations. Key articles include:

Article Right Geographical Significance
Article 3 Right to life, liberty, and security Violated in conflict zones (Syria, Yemen); threatened by environmental hazards
Article 13 Freedom of movement within and between states Restricted by border controls, walls, refugee policies; North Korea
Article 19 Freedom of opinion and expression Suppressed in authoritarian states; digital censorship (China's Great Firewall)
Article 25 Right to adequate standard of living (food, housing, healthcare) Not met for billions; linked to poverty, inequality, and development level
Article 26 Right to education 130+ million girls worldwide denied access; conflict disrupts schooling

The UDHR is not legally binding, but it has inspired legally binding treaties including the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights (ICCPR) and the International Covenant on Economic, Social, and Cultural Rights (ICESCR).

Exam Tip: When discussing human rights, distinguish between civil/political rights (freedoms FROM interference - speech, assembly, fair trial) and economic/social/cultural rights (entitlements TO resources - health, education, housing). Different states prioritise these differently, and this distinction is important for understanding debates about intervention.

2. Development Indicators and Inequality

Development: The process of improving the quality of life and economic well-being of a population. Development is multidimensional, encompassing economic growth, social progress, environmental sustainability, and political freedom.

Key Development Indicators

Indicator What It Measures Strengths Limitations
HDI Composite of health, education, and income (0-1) Multidimensional; widely comparable; annual data National averages hide internal inequalities; does not capture rights or freedoms
GNI per capita (PPP) Average income per person adjusted for purchasing power Allows international comparison; accounts for cost of living Hides income inequality; informal economy not captured
Gini coefficient Income inequality within a country (0 = perfect equality, 1 = perfect inequality) Direct measure of inequality; comparable Does not reveal absolute poverty levels
GDI (Gender Development Index) Gender gaps in HDI dimensions (health, education, income) Highlights gender-specific disparities Limited to three dimensions; binary gender framework
MPI (Multidimensional Poverty Index) Measures overlapping deprivations in health, education, and living standards Captures multiple dimensions of poverty simultaneously Complex methodology; data availability varies

Global Inequality Patterns

Key patterns of global inequality include:

  • North-South divide: The historical pattern (Brandt Line, 1980) of wealthier nations in the global North and poorer nations in the global South persists in broad terms but is increasingly challenged by the rise of MICs in Asia and Latin America
  • Intra-country inequality: Significant disparities exist within countries (e.g., coastal vs. inland China, northern vs. southern Nigeria, London vs. northern England)
  • Gender inequality: Women face systemic disadvantage in many regions, particularly in South Asia, Sub-Saharan Africa, and the Middle East
  • Ethnic and religious minorities: Often face discrimination in access to services, land, and political representation

3. Health Inequalities

Global Health Disparities

Health outcomes are among the most stark indicators of global inequality. Life expectancy varies by over 30 years between the highest and lowest performing countries.

Health Issue Global Pattern Contributing Factors
Maternal mortality Sub-Saharan Africa has 66% of global maternal deaths; 1 in 37 women face lifetime risk vs. 1 in 7,800 in Europe Lack of skilled birth attendants, poor infrastructure, distance to facilities, malnutrition
Child mortality Under-5 mortality is 14x higher in LICs than HICs; preventable diseases (pneumonia, diarrhoea, malaria) are leading causes Poverty, lack of clean water, limited vaccination, malnutrition
HIV/AIDS Sub-Saharan Africa has 65% of global cases; Southern and Eastern Africa most affected Poverty, gender inequality, stigma, limited healthcare access
Mental health Globally neglected; LICs spend less than 1% of health budgets on mental health; 75% of people with mental disorders in LMICs receive no treatment Stigma, lack of trained professionals, underfunding, conflict and displacement
Case Study - Health Inequalities in Sierra Leone: Sierra Leone has one of the world's lowest life expectancies (approximately 55 years) and highest maternal mortality rates (1,120 per 100,000 live births). Contributing factors include: the legacy of civil war (1991-2002) which destroyed 70% of health infrastructure; extreme poverty (GDP per capita ~$500); only 0.02 doctors per 1,000 people (compared to 2.8 in the UK); the 2014-15 Ebola epidemic which killed over 4,000 people and further weakened health systems. NGOs including MSF, Partners in Health, and the WHO provide essential healthcare services that the government cannot.

4. Education Access and Inequality

Education access: The ability of individuals to participate in formal education systems. Barriers include cost (direct fees and opportunity costs), distance, quality, cultural attitudes, conflict, disability, and gender discrimination.

Global Education Patterns

  • Primary education: Near-universal enrollment in most regions, but completion rates remain low in Sub-Saharan Africa (65%) and South Asia (82%). Quality varies enormously.
  • Secondary education: Significant gender and wealth gaps. In Sub-Saharan Africa, only 35% of girls complete lower secondary school. Rural-urban divides are stark.
  • Tertiary education: Highly concentrated in HICs and urban areas of MICs. Brain drain further disadvantages LICs as educated professionals emigrate.
  • Literacy: Global adult literacy is approximately 87%, but rates are below 40% in some LICs (Niger, Mali, South Sudan). Female literacy lags behind male literacy in many developing regions.
Case Study - Girls' Education in Afghanistan: Under Taliban rule (since August 2021), girls above age 12 have been banned from secondary education, and women from university. Before the Taliban takeover, female enrollment had grown significantly since 2001, reaching 3.5 million girls in school. The ban affects over 1 million girls and threatens to reverse two decades of progress. Consequences include increased rates of child marriage, reduced economic opportunity, poorer health outcomes, and inter-generational poverty. International condemnation has been widespread but ineffective, illustrating the limits of external pressure on sovereign states.

5. Gender Inequality

Gender inequality: The unequal treatment or perceptions of individuals based on their gender. It manifests in economic opportunities, political representation, education access, health outcomes, and legal protections.

Measuring Gender Inequality

Index What It Measures Key Findings
Gender Development Index (GDI) Gender gaps in HDI achievement (health, education, income) Most equal: Northern Europe, least equal: parts of Sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia
Gender Inequality Index (GII) Reproductive health, empowerment (education + parliamentary seats), and labour force participation Best performers: Switzerland, Denmark, Sweden; worst: Yemen, Chad, Papua New Guinea
Global Gender Gap Index (WEF) Economic opportunity, education, health, and political empowerment At current rates, global gender parity would take 130+ years to achieve

Forms of Gender Inequality

  • Economic: Globally, women earn approximately 77 cents for every dollar earned by men. Women perform 76% of unpaid care work. In many LICs, women cannot own land or access credit.
  • Education: 130 million girls are out of school globally. In South Sudan, only 1% of girls complete secondary school.
  • Health: 810 women die daily from preventable pregnancy/childbirth causes. FGM affects 200+ million women in 30+ countries.
  • Political: Women hold only 26% of parliamentary seats globally. Only 28 countries have ever had a female head of state.
  • Legal: In 49 countries, there are no laws against domestic violence. In some states, women cannot pass on nationality, travel, or work without male permission.
Case Study - Gender Inequality in India: India ranks 122 out of 162 countries on the GII. Despite economic growth, gender inequality persists due to deeply rooted cultural norms including preference for sons (leading to sex-selective abortions and a skewed sex ratio of 108 males per 100 females), dowry practices, and restrictions on female mobility. Female labour force participation has actually declined from 32% to 21% over recent decades. The government's Beti Bachao Beti Padhao (Save the Daughter, Educate the Daughter) scheme, alongside legal reforms such as criminalising dowry and child marriage, represent efforts to address these inequalities. However, enforcement remains weak and cultural change slow.

6. The Role of IGOs in Promoting Human Rights

Key IGOs and Their Roles

Organisation Human Rights Role Effectiveness
United Nations (UN) UN Human Rights Council monitors abuses; UNHCR protects refugees; UNICEF protects children's rights; Security Council can authorise intervention Global legitimacy but hampered by P5 veto power; under-resourced; states can ignore recommendations
International Criminal Court (ICC) Prosecutes individuals for genocide, war crimes, and crimes against humanity Symbolic importance but limited reach; major powers (USA, China, Russia) are not members; enforcement depends on state cooperation
European Court of Human Rights Enforces European Convention on Human Rights for 46 member states of the Council of Europe Binding judgments; strong compliance record; but limited to European context
African Union (AU) African Charter on Human and Peoples' Rights; peacekeeping missions (AMISOM in Somalia) Growing capacity but limited resources; sovereignty concerns among member states

7. Intervention and Its Consequences

The Sovereignty vs. Intervention Debate

One of the central tensions in human rights geography is between state sovereignty (the principle that states have exclusive authority within their borders) and the international community's responsibility to protect populations from mass atrocities.

Arguments for Intervention Arguments Against Intervention
Moral duty to prevent genocide and mass atrocities Violates state sovereignty and international law
Responsibility to Protect (R2P) doctrine Risk of mission creep and unintended consequences
Can stabilise regions and prevent refugee crises Intervening powers may have ulterior motives (resources, geopolitics)
Promotes universal human rights standards Western-centric; cultural imperialism; who decides what rights are universal?
Economic sanctions can pressure change without violence Sanctions often harm ordinary citizens more than regimes
Case Study - Rwanda (1994): The Rwandan genocide saw approximately 800,000 Tutsis and moderate Hutus killed in just 100 days. The international community's failure to intervene is widely regarded as a catastrophic moral failure. The UN peacekeeping force (UNAMIR) was under-resourced and had a restricted mandate. France, Belgium, and the USA all had prior knowledge of the genocide's planning but failed to act. The aftermath led to the development of R2P and strengthened the argument that sovereignty cannot be used as a shield for genocide. Rwanda's post-genocide recovery, including rapid economic growth, universal healthcare, and women making up 61% of parliament, provides a contrasting narrative of resilience and development.
Case Study - Sanctions on North Korea: North Korea faces some of the most comprehensive international sanctions in history, targeting its nuclear weapons programme and human rights violations. The UN Commission of Inquiry (2014) documented systematic human rights abuses including political prison camps holding an estimated 80,000-120,000 people. Despite decades of sanctions, the regime persists, and ordinary citizens suffer from poverty, malnutrition, and restricted freedoms. This case demonstrates the limitations of economic sanctions as a tool for promoting human rights when a regime prioritises its own survival over the welfare of its population.
Exam Tip: OCR questions frequently ask you to assess the effectiveness of different approaches to promoting human rights. Always use specific evidence from case studies, consider multiple scales (local, national, global), and weigh competing perspectives. The strongest answers acknowledge complexity and avoid simplistic conclusions.

8. Synoptic Links

Human rights connects to many other areas of the specification:

  • Global governance: How international institutions attempt to manage human rights, and why they often fail
  • Migration: Refugees fleeing human rights abuses; the rights of migrants and asylum seekers
  • Development: The relationship between poverty, inequality, and rights violations
  • Globalisation: How global interconnections can both spread and challenge human rights norms
  • Climate change: Environmental rights; climate refugees; disproportionate impact on vulnerable populations
  • Power and borders: How state power, sovereignty, and territorial control shape human rights outcomes
