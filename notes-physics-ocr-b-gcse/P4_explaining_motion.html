P4.1 Scalars and Vectors

A scalar quantity has magnitude (size) only — e.g. distance, speed, mass, energy, time.
A vector quantity has magnitude and direction — e.g. displacement, velocity, acceleration, force, momentum, weight.
ScalarVector
Distance — total path lengthDisplacement — straight-line distance and direction from start
Speed — how fastVelocity — speed in a stated direction

P4.2 Speed, Velocity and Acceleration

Speed:   v = distance / time   (m/s)
Acceleration:   a = Δv / t = (v − u) / t   (m/s²)
u = initial velocity, v = final velocity
SUVAT:   v² = u² + 2 a s
(m/s)² = (m/s)² + 2 (m/s²)(m)

A car accelerates from 10 m/s to 25 m/s in 5 s. Find its acceleration.
a = (v − u)/t = (25 − 10)/5 = 3 m/s²

P4.3 Distance–Time and Velocity–Time Graphs

  • On a distance–time graph, the gradient gives the speed.
  • On a velocity–time graph, the gradient gives the acceleration and the area under the line gives the distance travelled.

P4.4 Forces

A force is a push or a pull. Forces are vectors, measured in newtons (N). Forces can change an object's shape, speed or direction.

Contact and Non-Contact Forces

  • Contact: friction, tension, normal reaction, air resistance, drag, upthrust.
  • Non-contact: gravity, magnetic force, electrostatic force.

Weight and Mass

W = m × g    (N) = (kg) × (N/kg)

On Earth, g ≈ 9.8 N/kg. Mass (kg) is the amount of matter; weight (N) is the gravitational force acting on that mass.

P4.5 Newton's Laws of Motion

Newton's 1st Law: an object at rest stays at rest, and an object moving in a straight line continues at constant velocity, unless acted on by a resultant (net) force.
Newton's 2nd Law: the resultant force on an object equals its mass × its acceleration.
F = m × a    (N) = (kg) × (m/s²)
Newton's 3rd Law: when object A exerts a force on object B, object B exerts an equal and opposite force on A. These form a pair — same size, opposite direction, acting on two different bodies.
A 1200 kg car has a resultant force of 3000 N. What is its acceleration?
a = F / m = 3000 / 1200 = 2.5 m/s²

P4.6 Inertia and Inertial Mass

Inertia is the tendency of an object to keep doing what it is already doing (staying at rest, or continuing in a straight line at constant velocity). A more massive object has more inertia — it is harder to accelerate.

The ratio F / a in Newton's 2nd Law is called the inertial mass.

P4.7 Momentum

Momentum (p) = mass × velocity. Unit: kg m/s. Momentum is a vector.
p = m × v

Momentum is always conserved in a closed system (where no external forces act): total momentum before = total momentum after.

A 0.5 kg ball moving at 4 m/s hits a stationary 1.5 kg ball and they stick together. Find their common velocity.
Before: p = 0.5 × 4 + 1.5 × 0 = 2 kg m/s
After: p = (0.5 + 1.5) v = 2v. So v = 1 m/s.

Force as Rate of Change of Momentum

F = Δp / Δt = m(v − u) / t

A smaller force is needed if the change of momentum happens over a longer time. This is why airbags, crumple zones, cycle helmets, crash mats, and seat belts all increase stopping time — reducing the peak force on the body during a collision.

P4.8 Stopping Distance

Stopping distance = thinking distance + braking distance.
Thinking distance: distance travelled during the driver's reaction time.
Braking distance: distance travelled while the brakes decelerate the car to rest.

Energy Dissipated in Braking

During braking, the car's kinetic energy is transferred to thermal energy in the brake discs, pads and tyres (via friction). Because Ek = ½ m v², doubling the speed means four times as much energy to dissipate, so the braking distance also roughly quadruples.

P4.9 Hooke's Law (Forces and Elasticity)

The extension of an elastic object (spring, rubber band, wire) is directly proportional to the force applied, provided the limit of proportionality is not exceeded.
F = k × x    (N) = (N/m) × (m)
A spring of constant 40 N/m is stretched by 0.15 m. Find the elastic PE stored.
Ee = ½ k x² = ½ × 40 × 0.15² = ½ × 40 × 0.0225 = 0.45 J

P4.10 Moments (Turning Forces)

A moment is the turning effect of a force about a pivot.
Moment (N m) = Force (N) × perpendicular distance from pivot (m)
Principle of moments (for a body in equilibrium): total clockwise moments = total anticlockwise moments.
A see-saw is 4 m long, with the pivot in the middle. A 50 kg child sits 1.5 m left of the pivot. Where must a 30 kg child sit to balance it?
Left moment = 50 × 9.8 × 1.5 = 735 N m
Right moment = 30 × 9.8 × d = 735 → d = 735 / (30 × 9.8) = 2.5 m to the right.
