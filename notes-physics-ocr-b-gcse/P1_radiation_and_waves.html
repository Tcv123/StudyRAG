P1.1 What is a Wave?

A wave is a disturbance that transfers energy (and information) from one place to another without transferring matter. The particles of the medium oscillate about their fixed positions; they do not travel with the wave.

Waves come in two main types based on the direction of the oscillation relative to the direction the wave is travelling.

Key Properties of a Wave

  • Amplitude (A) — the maximum displacement of a particle from its rest position. Related to the energy the wave carries: bigger amplitude = more energy.
  • Wavelength (λ) — the distance from one point on a wave to the same point on the next wave (e.g. peak to peak). Measured in metres.
  • Frequency (f) — the number of complete waves passing a point per second. Measured in hertz (Hz).
  • Period (T) — the time for one complete wave cycle. T = 1 / f.
  • Wave speed (v) — how fast the energy is transferred through the medium, in m/s.
wave speed = frequency × wavelength
v = f × λ    (m/s) = (Hz) × (m)
A sound wave has a frequency of 440 Hz and travels at 340 m/s in air. Find its wavelength.
λ = v / f = 340 / 440 = 0.77 m (2 s.f.)

P1.2 The Electromagnetic Spectrum

All electromagnetic (EM) waves are transverse, travel at the same speed in a vacuum (3 × 10⁸ m/s, the speed of light), and transfer energy by oscillating electric and magnetic fields — no medium is needed.

The electromagnetic spectrum is the full continuous range of EM waves, ordered from long wavelength / low frequency / low energy to short wavelength / high frequency / high energy.

A very common 6-mark question is to compare two parts of the spectrum — e.g. microwaves vs infrared for cooking, or X-ray vs gamma in medicine. Always link the property of the wave (wavelength, penetration, absorption) to why it suits the use.

P1.3 Reflection, Refraction and Absorption

When a wave meets a boundary between two materials, one or more of the following can happen:

  • Reflection — the wave bounces off the surface.
  • Refraction — the wave changes direction as it crosses the boundary because its speed changes.
  • Absorption — the wave's energy is transferred into the material (often as heat).
  • Transmission — the wave passes through.

Reflection (Law of Reflection)

Refraction

When a wave passes from one medium into another its speed changes. Because the part of the wavefront in the new medium moves at a different speed to the part still in the old medium, the wave bends (unless it hits the boundary head-on along the normal).

  • Going into a denser (optically slower) medium: wave bends towards the normal.
  • Going into a less dense medium: wave bends away from the normal.
  • The frequency does not change during refraction — wavelength changes with speed.
Think of a toy car rolling off a smooth floor onto carpet: the wheel that hits the carpet first slows down, so the car turns towards the slower side. Same idea with light entering glass.

P1.4 Ionising vs Non-Ionising Radiation

Ionising radiation has enough energy to knock electrons off atoms, creating charged ions. In living tissue, ionisation can damage DNA and cause cancer.

In the EM spectrum, the ionising waves are ultraviolet, X-rays and gamma rays. The lower-energy waves (radio, microwave, IR, visible) are non-ionising.

Radiation Dose (sievert, Sv)

The equivalent dose measures the risk of harm from ionising radiation, in sieverts (Sv). 1 Sv is a very large dose; typical backgrounds are measured in millisieverts (mSv) or microsieverts (µSv).

ExposureTypical dose
Dental X-ray≈ 0.005 mSv
Chest X-ray≈ 0.02 mSv
Transatlantic flight (cosmic rays)≈ 0.08 mSv
UK annual background≈ 2.7 mSv
CT scan (whole body)≈ 10 mSv

Reducing Risk

  • Increase distance from the source — intensity falls with distance squared.
  • Reduce exposure time.
  • Use shielding — lead aprons for X-rays; thick concrete walls around radiotherapy rooms; radiographers leave the room.
  • Use the lowest dose that still gives a clear image / effective treatment.

P1.5 Waves in Different Media

The speed of a wave depends on the medium, not on its frequency.

WaveTypical speed
Sound in air (20 °C)≈ 340 m/s
Sound in water≈ 1500 m/s
Sound in steel≈ 5000 m/s
Light/EM in vacuum3.00 × 10⁸ m/s
Light in water≈ 2.25 × 10⁸ m/s
Light in glass≈ 2.00 × 10⁸ m/s

Sound travels faster through denser media because the particles are closer together and vibrations are passed on more quickly. Light, on the other hand, slows down in denser transparent materials.

P1.6 Sound and Ultrasound (overview)

Sound is a longitudinal wave. It needs a medium — it cannot travel through a vacuum. Humans typically hear 20 Hz to 20 000 Hz.

Ultrasound is sound with a frequency above the human hearing range (> 20 kHz). It is partially reflected at boundaries between different media, which allows it to be used for imaging.

Uses of ultrasound:

  • Medical imaging (pre-natal scans) — non-ionising, so safe for a foetus.
  • Industrial cleaning and detection of flaws in metal castings.
  • Sonar — measuring water depth; detecting shoals of fish or submarines.
A sonar pulse returns 0.4 s after leaving a ship. Speed of sound in seawater is 1500 m/s. How deep is the seabed?
Total distance = v × t = 1500 × 0.4 = 600 m. Depth = 600 / 2 = 300 m (pulse travels there and back).

P1.7 Summary & Key Equations

  • v = f λ
  • T = 1 / f
  • All EM waves travel at 3 × 10⁸ m/s in a vacuum
  • Energy increases with frequency: radio → gamma
  • UV, X-ray, gamma are ionising
Never write "waves transfer matter". They transfer energy. This single-mark mark scheme point catches students out every year.
