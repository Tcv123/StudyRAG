P5.1 The Atomic Model — A Historical Story

Ideas about the atom have changed over time as experimental evidence accumulated. You should know the sequence of models:

ModelScientist(s)Key idea
Tiny indivisible particles ("atomos")Democritus (≈ 400 BC)Matter made of indivisible units
Sphere of positive matter with negative electrons dotted through itJ.J. Thomson, 1897 — plum pudding modelDiscovery of the electron
Small, dense, positive nucleus with electrons around itRutherford, 1911 — gold foil / α-scattering experimentMost of the atom is empty space; nearly all its mass is in a tiny central nucleus
Electrons orbit in fixed shells (energy levels)Bohr, 1913Explains line spectra
Nucleus contains protons and neutronsChadwick, 1932Discovery of the neutron
Rutherford's conclusions from the results:
• Most α pass straight through → atoms are mostly empty space.
• A few deflected at large angles → small, dense, positively charged nucleus.
• 1 in 8000 bounced back → nucleus is very small but contains nearly all the mass.

Nuclear Notation

An atom is written as ᴬZX where:

  • Z = proton (atomic) number — number of protons; defines the element.
  • A = mass (nucleon) number — total protons + neutrons.
  • Number of neutrons = A − Z.

Isotopes are atoms of the same element with the same Z but different A (different number of neutrons). Example: hydrogen has three isotopes — 1H, 2H (deuterium), 3H (tritium).

P5.4 Nuclear Equations

α decay:   AZX  →  A−4Z−2Y  +  42He
(mass falls by 4, atomic number falls by 2)
β⁻ decay:   AZX  →  AZ+1Y  +  0−1e
(mass unchanged, atomic number rises by 1 — a neutron becomes a proton)
γ emission: no change in A or Z — the nucleus loses energy as a γ photon.
Uranium-238 decays by alpha emission. Write the nuclear equation.
23892U  →  23490Th  +  42He (the daughter is thorium-234).
Carbon-14 decays by β⁻ emission. Write the nuclear equation.
146C  →  147N  +  0−1e

P5.5 Activity and Half-Life

Activity (A) = number of decays per second. Unit: becquerel (Bq), 1 Bq = 1 decay/s.
Half-life (t½) = the average time for the number of unstable nuclei in a sample — or equivalently the activity — to halve.
A sample of iodine-131 has an initial activity of 8000 Bq. Its half-life is 8 days. Find the activity after 24 days.
24 ÷ 8 = 3 half-lives. Activity = 8000 × (½)³ = 8000 / 8 = 1000 Bq.

P5.8 Nuclear Fission and Fusion

Fission: a large unstable nucleus (e.g. U-235) splits into two smaller, more stable nuclei, releasing a few neutrons and energy. The neutrons can trigger further fissions — a chain reaction.
Fusion: two small nuclei (e.g. hydrogen isotopes) collide at very high speed and join to make a larger nucleus (helium), releasing energy. This is how the Sun and all stars shine.

Fusion releases much more energy per kg than fission but requires extremely high temperatures and pressures to overcome electrostatic repulsion between the nuclei. Sustainable Earth-based fusion power is still being developed (ITER, tokamak designs).

P5.9 Dangers of Ionising Radiation

  • Irradiation: being exposed to radiation from outside. The object does not become radioactive.
  • Contamination: radioactive material gets onto or into the body (skin, food, dust). Continues to irradiate from inside — much harder to stop.

Risk depends on the activity, the type of radiation, and the duration and location of exposure. Inside the body, alpha is the most dangerous because it is very strongly ionising; outside the body, gamma is the most dangerous because it penetrates the most.
