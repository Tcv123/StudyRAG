P3.1 Current, Charge and Potential Difference

Current (I) is the rate of flow of electric charge. Unit: ampere (A). A current of 1 A means 1 coulomb of charge passes a point each second.
Charge (Q) = current (I) × time (t)
(C) = (A) × (s)
Potential difference (p.d., V) is the energy transferred per coulomb of charge passing between two points. Unit: volt (V). 1 V = 1 J/C.
Energy transferred (E) = charge (Q) × potential difference (V)
(J) = (C) × (V)
A current of 0.5 A flows through a lamp for 2 minutes. How much charge flows?
Q = I × t = 0.5 × 120 = 60 C

P3.4 Resistance & Ohm's Law

Resistance (R) is a measure of how difficult it is for current to flow. Unit: ohm (Ω).
V = I × R    (Ohm's Law for ohmic conductors)
(V) = (A) × (Ω)
A 12 V battery pushes 0.4 A through a resistor. What is its resistance?
R = V / I = 12 / 0.4 = 30 Ω

Factors affecting resistance of a wire:

  • Longer wire → more resistance (R ∝ length).
  • Thicker wire → less resistance (larger cross-sectional area).
  • Different material (resistivity).
  • Higher temperature → usually more resistance in metals (ions vibrate more).

P3.5 I–V Characteristics

Thermistor and LDR

  • Thermistor: resistance decreases as temperature increases. Used in thermostats and fire alarms.
  • LDR (light-dependent resistor): resistance decreases as light intensity increases. Used in streetlight sensors and camera exposure.

P3.6 Electrical Power and Energy

Electrical power: P = V × I   or   P = I² R   or   P = V² / R
(W) = (V) × (A)
Energy transferred: E = P × t = V × I × t
(J) = (W) × (s)
A kettle is rated 2300 W at 230 V. Calculate the current and the fuse rating (3 A, 5 A or 13 A) it needs.
I = P / V = 2300 / 230 = 10 A. Nearest fuse above: 13 A.

P3.7 Mains Electricity

UK mains electricity is alternating current (a.c.) at 230 V, 50 Hz. Batteries supply direct current (d.c.) — the current flows one way.

Three-Pin Plug

  • Live (brown): at 230 V alternating. Dangerous.
  • Neutral (blue): at ≈ 0 V, completes the circuit.
  • Earth (green/yellow): safety wire. Normally carries no current; connects the metal casing of an appliance to the ground so that if the live wire touches the case, a large current flows to earth and blows the fuse.
  • Fuse: thin wire that melts and breaks the circuit if the current exceeds its rating.

Why a.c. for the Grid?

  • Transformers only work with a.c., allowing the grid to step the voltage up for transmission (minimising I²R heat losses in cables) and step it down again at the user.

P3.8 Charge and Static

Insulators can be charged by friction — electrons are rubbed off one surface onto the other. The object that gains electrons is negative; the one that loses them is positive.

  • Like charges repel; unlike charges attract.
  • Sparks (e.g. shocks from a door handle) occur when the p.d. is high enough to ionise the air so charge jumps across.
  • Static hazards: fuelling planes (grounding straps avoid sparks), volatile chemical factories.
  • Static uses: photocopiers, spray painting, inkjet printers.
Never say "positive charge flows". At GCSE, it is electrons (negative) that actually move; conventional current is drawn from + to − for historical reasons.
