P6 — Matter: Models and Explanations

OCR GCSE Physics B (J259) — 21st Century Science · Paper 2 (Depth in Physics)

P6.1 The Particle Model of Matter

All matter is made of tiny particles (atoms/molecules) that are in constant motion. The arrangement, spacing and motion of these particles differ between solids, liquids and gases and explain the bulk properties of each state.

P6.2 Density

Density (ρ) = mass per unit volume. Unit: kg/m³ (or g/cm³).
ρ = m / V    (kg/m³) = (kg) / (m³)
MaterialTypical density (kg/m³)
Air (at 20 °C)1.2
Cork240
Ice917
Water1000
Sea water1025
Aluminium2700
Iron7870
Gold19 300
A metal block of mass 1.35 kg has volume 1.5 × 10⁻⁴ m³. Find its density and suggest the material.
ρ = m / V = 1.35 / 1.5 × 10⁻⁴ = 9000 kg/m³ → close to copper (8960).

Measuring Density

  • Regular solid: measure mass with a balance; calculate volume from dimensions (l × w × h for a cuboid; πr²h for a cylinder).
  • Irregular solid: submerge in a eureka/displacement can; volume = volume of water displaced.
  • Liquid: measure mass of empty measuring cylinder; add liquid; re-measure mass and volume directly.

P6.3 Changes of State and Internal Energy

The internal energy of a substance is the total energy of its particles — kinetic (from motion) plus potential (from intermolecular forces).
  • When a substance is heated and not changing state: kinetic energy rises → temperature rises.
  • When a substance changes state: potential energy rises (bonds break) → temperature stays constant.
  • Mass is conserved in a change of state — the same particles rearrange, no matter is gained or lost.

Specific Heat Capacity

Specific heat capacity (c) = energy needed to raise 1 kg of a substance by 1 °C. Unit: J/kg °C.
ΔE = m × c × Δθ

Substances with high c (like water, c = 4200 J/kg °C) take a lot of energy to warm up — which is why water is used in central heating systems and as a coolant.

Specific Latent Heat

Specific latent heat (L) = energy needed to change the state of 1 kg of a substance with no temperature change. Unit: J/kg.
E = m × L
  • Fusion (solid ↔ liquid): Lf for water ≈ 334 000 J/kg
  • Vaporisation (liquid ↔ gas): Lv for water ≈ 2 260 000 J/kg
How much energy is needed to boil away 0.3 kg of water at 100 °C?
E = m Lv = 0.3 × 2 260 000 = 6.78 × 10⁵ J

P6.4 Pressure in Fluids

Pressure = force per unit area. Unit: pascal (Pa). 1 Pa = 1 N/m².
p = F / A

In a fluid, pressure acts in all directions at a given depth. Pressure due to a column of fluid depends on depth and density:

p = h × ρ × g    (Pa) = (m) × (kg/m³) × (N/kg)

Upthrust (Archimedes)

When an object is in a fluid, the pressure on its lower surface is greater than on its upper surface. This gives a net upward force called upthrust. Upthrust = weight of fluid displaced.

  • If upthrust > weight → object floats.
  • If upthrust < weight → object sinks.
  • Ships float because their shape displaces a large weight of water, even though their metal is denser than water.

P6.5 Atmospheric Pressure

The atmosphere is a fluid — a layer of air about 100 km thick. Air has weight. At sea level the pressure is about 1.0 × 10⁵ Pa (1 atmosphere, ~100 kPa). As altitude increases, there is less air above you, so atmospheric pressure falls.

This is why:

  • Ears pop in aircraft — the external pressure changes and the pressure inside the middle ear equalises through the Eustachian tube.
  • Boiling point of water falls at altitude — atmospheric pressure is lower, so it takes less molecular energy for water vapour to escape.

P6.6 The Gas Laws (qualitative)

At a fixed temperature, gas particles collide continuously with the container walls, exerting a pressure. This pressure changes if volume, temperature or amount of gas changes.

  • Temperature ↑ (constant V): particles move faster → more frequent/harder collisions → pressure rises.
  • Volume ↓ (constant T): same number of particles in smaller space → more frequent collisions → pressure rises. (Boyle's Law: pV = constant.)
  • Compressing a gas quickly (e.g. a bike pump) does work on it → temperature rises → pump gets hot.
Boyle's Law:   p₁ V₁ = p₂ V₂    (fixed temperature, fixed mass of gas)
A gas occupies 300 cm³ at 100 kPa. What is its pressure when compressed to 120 cm³ at constant T?
p₂ = (p₁ V₁) / V₂ = (100 × 300) / 120 = 250 kPa

P6.7 Forces and Materials

Hooke's Law Recap (from P4)

For a spring in its elastic region: F = k × x. The graph of force against extension is a straight line through the origin.

Elastic vs Plastic Deformation

  • Elastic deformation: object returns to its original shape when the force is removed (below the limit of proportionality).
  • Plastic deformation: object is permanently changed (above the elastic limit) — some of the work done is transferred to internal energy/heat.

P6.8 Summary & Key Equations

  • ρ = m / V
  • ΔE = m c Δθ (heating/cooling)
  • E = m L (change of state)
  • p = F / A
  • p = h ρ g (pressure in a fluid)
  • p₁ V₁ = p₂ V₂ (Boyle's Law)
  • F = k x (Hooke's Law)
Many students lose marks by confusing weight (a force, in N) and mass (an amount of matter, in kg), or pressure (N/m²) with force (N). Always state units.
