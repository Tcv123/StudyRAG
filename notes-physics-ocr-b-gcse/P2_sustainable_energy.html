P2.1 Energy Stores and Transfers

Energy cannot be created or destroyed — it is only transferred between stores (the Principle of Conservation of Energy). What we call "using energy" really means transferring energy from a useful store to less useful ones (often into the thermal store of the surroundings).

The Eight Energy Stores

StoreExample
KineticAnything moving — a running athlete, a flying cricket ball
Gravitational potentialAn object raised in a gravitational field — a book on a shelf
Elastic potentialA stretched spring, a drawn bow, a rubber band
Thermal (internal)A hot cup of tea, the Sun
ChemicalFood, fuels, batteries
MagneticTwo repelling magnets held apart
ElectrostaticCharged Van de Graaff, thunderclouds
NuclearStored in the nuclei of atoms (fission/fusion fuels)

The Four Pathways

Energy is moved between stores by four pathways:

  • Mechanical work — a force doing work (pushing, lifting, stretching).
  • Electrical work — charges being moved by a p.d.
  • Heating — energy transferred from hot to cold (particle collisions, radiation).
  • Radiation — energy carried by waves (light, sound, IR).

P2.2 Calculating Energy Changes

Kinetic energy:   Ek = ½ m v²
(J) = (kg) × (m/s)²
Gravitational potential energy:   Ep = m g h
(J) = (kg) × (N/kg) × (m)    (g ≈ 9.8 N/kg on Earth)
Elastic potential energy:   Ee = ½ k x²
(J) = (N/m) × (m)²
Heating (change in thermal store):   ΔE = m c Δθ
(J) = (kg) × (J/kg °C) × (°C)
A 70 kg cyclist is moving at 8 m/s. How much kinetic energy do they have?
Ek = ½ × 70 × 8² = ½ × 70 × 64 = 2240 J
A 2 kg book is lifted 1.5 m onto a shelf. How much GPE does it gain?
Ep = m g h = 2 × 9.8 × 1.5 = 29.4 J

P2.3 Efficiency and Sankey Diagrams

The efficiency of a device is the fraction (or %) of the input energy that is transferred usefully. The rest is wasted — usually to the thermal store of the surroundings.
Efficiency = useful energy output / total energy input
 = useful power output / total power input
A motor lifts a 5 kg load 2 m. If the motor uses 200 J of electricity, what is its efficiency?
Useful output = m g h = 5 × 9.8 × 2 = 98 J. Efficiency = 98/200 = 0.49 (49%)

P2.4 Power

Power is the rate at which energy is transferred (or the rate at which work is done). Unit: watts (W), where 1 W = 1 J/s.
P = E / t   or   P = W / t
(W) = (J) / (s)
A hairdryer transfers 36 000 J in 30 s. What is its power?
P = E / t = 36 000 / 30 = 1200 W (1.2 kW)

P2.5 Reducing Unwanted Energy Transfers

In our homes, most wasted energy leaves through the walls, roof, windows and floors as heat. We can reduce this with:

MethodHow it works
Loft insulation (fibreglass)Traps air — a poor conductor — reducing conduction through the ceiling
Cavity wall insulation (foam)Reduces conduction and convection currents inside the wall cavity
Double glazingThe gas/vacuum gap between the panes is a very poor conductor
Draught excludersPrevent heat loss by air exchange (convection)
Thicker walls / lower thermal conductivityReduce the rate of heat conduction
Rate of cooling depends on: thickness, thermal conductivity, surface area, and temperature difference between inside and outside.
Questions often ask about payback time: time = cost of insulation / annual saving. A £200 loft insulation that saves £50 a year pays back in 4 years.

P2.6 Energy Resources

An energy resource is a naturally occurring store from which useful energy can be extracted — usually to make electricity, heat buildings or move vehicles.

Renewable vs Non-Renewable

  • Non-renewable: will run out because they are used faster than they form. Fossil fuels (coal, oil, gas) and nuclear fuel (uranium).
  • Renewable: replenished naturally faster than they are used. Wind, solar, hydroelectric, tidal, wave, geothermal, biofuels.
ResourceHow it's usedAdvantagesDisadvantages
Coal / oil / gasBurnt to boil water → steam turns turbine → generatorReliable, high energy density, established infrastructureCO₂ → climate change; SO₂ → acid rain; non-renewable

P2.7 Trends in Electricity Generation

The UK electricity mix has shifted dramatically since 1990:

  • Coal: fell from > 70% of generation to almost zero by 2024.
  • Gas: rose through the 1990s; still the largest single source.
  • Renewables (wind + solar + biomass + hydro): rose from ~2% to ~40%+.
  • Nuclear: steady, around 15%.
A standard 6-mark question: "Compare the environmental and economic advantages/disadvantages of two named energy resources." Always name specific drawbacks (CO₂, acid rain, habitat loss, radioactive waste) — not generic "bad for environment".
