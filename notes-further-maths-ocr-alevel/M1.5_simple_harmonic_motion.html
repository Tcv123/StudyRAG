M1.5: Simple Harmonic Motion

1. Defining Equation

A particle undergoes simple harmonic motion (SHM) about the origin if its acceleration is proportional to displacement and directed towards the origin: \[\ddot{x} = -\omega^2 x.\] The positive constant \(\omega\) is the angular frequency.

The general solution is

\(x(t) = A\cos(\omega t + \phi) = C\cos\omega t + D\sin\omega t\)

with amplitude \(A\) (\(|x|\) max), phase \(\phi\), period \(T = 2\pi/\omega\), and frequency \(f = \omega/(2\pi)\).

2. Velocity and Energy Relations

\(v^2 = \omega^2(A^2 - x^2)\)

This follows by integration: max speed \(v_{\max} = A\omega\) at \(x = 0\), zero speed at \(x = \pm A\).

Energy

\(\text{KE} = \tfrac{1}{2}m v^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}m\omega^2(A^2 - x^2)\)
\(\text{PE} = \tfrac{1}{2}m\omega^2 x^2\)  (relative to equilibrium)
\(\text{Total} = \tfrac{1}{2}m\omega^2 A^2\)  (constant).

3. Springs (Hooke's Law)

A particle of mass \(m\) attached to a light spring of natural length \(\ell_0\) and stiffness \(k\) (force constant). At extension \(x\) from natural length, spring force = \(-kx\) (restoring).

If the system is displaced from equilibrium by \(y\), Newton's second law gives \(m\ddot{y} = -ky\), so SHM with

\(\omega = \sqrt{k/m}, \quad T = 2\pi\sqrt{m/k}.\)

Spring Hanging Vertically

Equilibrium extension \(e\) satisfies \(ke = mg\). Displacement from equilibrium \(y\) satisfies \(m\ddot{y} = -ky\) — same SHM as horizontal. Gravity shifts the equilibrium but does not affect the period.

Elastic Strings (Modulus of Elasticity \(\lambda\))

For a string of natural length \(\ell_0\) and modulus \(\lambda\), the tension at extension \(x\) is \(T = \lambda x/\ell_0\). The effective stiffness is \(k = \lambda/\ell_0\).

Caveat: strings cannot be compressed — tension is zero when "extension" would be negative. SHM applies only while the string is taut.

A particle of mass 2 kg hangs from a spring of natural length 0.5 m and modulus 20 N extended to 1 m equilibrium. Find the period of small oscillations.
Stiffness: \(k = \lambda/\ell_0 = 20/0.5 = 40\) N/m.
\(\omega = \sqrt{40/2} = \sqrt{20}\). \(T = 2\pi/\omega = 2\pi/\sqrt{20} \approx 1.40\) s.

4. Simple Pendulum

For small-angle oscillations of a simple pendulum of length \(\ell\):

\(\ddot{\theta} = -\dfrac{g}{\ell}\sin\theta \approx -\dfrac{g}{\ell}\theta\)  (for small \(\theta\))

SHM with \(\omega = \sqrt{g/\ell}\), \(T = 2\pi\sqrt{\ell/g}\). Period is independent of amplitude (for small swings) and mass.

5. Time, Position, Velocity

Given \(x = A\cos(\omega t)\) with \(x_0 = A\), \(\dot{x}_0 = 0\):

  • Time to first zero: \(\omega t = \pi/2\), so \(t = T/4\).
  • Time from \(x = A\) to \(x = A/2\): \(\cos\omega t = 1/2\), so \(\omega t = \pi/3\), \(t = T/6\).

If the particle starts at \(x = 0\) moving in the positive direction with speed \(v_0 = A\omega\), use \(x = A\sin(\omega t)\) instead.

6. Combining Conditions

Questions often give you two data points (e.g. \(v\) at a specific \(x\), or position at a specific \(t\)). Use:

  • \(v^2 = \omega^2(A^2 - x^2)\) to link \(v, x, \omega, A\).
  • Initial conditions to fix the phase \(\phi\) in \(x(t) = A\cos(\omega t + \phi)\).
Particle executes SHM with period 4 s and amplitude 5 cm. Find its speed when \(x = 3\) cm.
\(\omega = 2\pi/4 = \pi/2\) rad/s. \(v^2 = (\pi/2)^2 (5^2 - 3^2) = (\pi^2/4)\cdot 16 = 4\pi^2\).
\(|v| = 2\pi \approx 6.28\) cm/s.

7. Damped SHM (Contextual)

Real springs have damping. A linear damping force \(-c\dot{x}\) gives

\(m\ddot{x} + c\dot{x} + kx = 0.\)

This is a second-order DE (see §1.9) and exhibits under-, critical-, or over-damping depending on \(c^2\) vs. \(4mk\). Only the undamped case is classical SHM.

8. Exam Style Strategy

  • Always identify \(\omega\) first — from the differential equation or from \(T\).
  • Choose an appropriate parametrisation (\(\cos\) if starting at max displacement, \(\sin\) if starting at equilibrium moving).
  • Use energy / \(v^2 = \omega^2(A^2 - x^2)\) for "speed at this \(x\)" questions.
  • For elastic strings, check whether the string is taut throughout — SHM may break down if the particle overshoots.
