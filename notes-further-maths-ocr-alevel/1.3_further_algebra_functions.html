1.3: Further Algebra and Functions

1. Roots of Polynomials

For a polynomial with real (or complex) coefficients, we can relate the roots to the coefficients directly — useful when you know the roots satisfy a certain relation but the polynomial itself is messy.

Quadratic: \(ax^2 + bx + c = 0\) with roots \(\alpha, \beta\)

\(\alpha + \beta = -\dfrac{b}{a}, \qquad \alpha\beta = \dfrac{c}{a}\)

Cubic: \(ax^3 + bx^2 + cx + d = 0\) with roots \(\alpha, \beta, \gamma\)

\(\sum \alpha = -\dfrac{b}{a}, \quad \sum \alpha\beta = \dfrac{c}{a}, \quad \alpha\beta\gamma = -\dfrac{d}{a}\)

Quartic: \(ax^4 + bx^3 + cx^2 + dx + e = 0\) with roots \(\alpha, \beta, \gamma, \delta\)

\(\sum \alpha = -\dfrac{b}{a}, \;\; \sum \alpha\beta = \dfrac{c}{a}, \;\; \sum \alpha\beta\gamma = -\dfrac{d}{a}, \;\; \alpha\beta\gamma\delta = \dfrac{e}{a}\)

The sign pattern is \(-,+,-,+,\ldots\). Symbols \(\sum \alpha\), \(\sum \alpha\beta\) denote the elementary symmetric polynomials (sums over distinct pairs, triples, etc.).

Useful Symmetric Function Identities

For quadratic roots:

  • \(\alpha^2 + \beta^2 = (\alpha+\beta)^2 - 2\alpha\beta\)
  • \(\dfrac{1}{\alpha} + \dfrac{1}{\beta} = \dfrac{\alpha+\beta}{\alpha\beta}\)
  • \(\alpha^3 + \beta^3 = (\alpha+\beta)^3 - 3\alpha\beta(\alpha+\beta)\)
  • \((\alpha-\beta)^2 = (\alpha+\beta)^2 - 4\alpha\beta\)

For cubic roots:

  • \(\sum \alpha^2 = (\sum\alpha)^2 - 2\sum\alpha\beta\)
  • \(\sum \alpha^3 = (\sum\alpha)^3 - 3(\sum\alpha)(\sum\alpha\beta) + 3\alpha\beta\gamma\)

2. Forming a Polynomial with Transformed Roots

If \(\alpha, \beta, \gamma\) are roots of \(P(x) = 0\), we often need the polynomial whose roots are \(f(\alpha), f(\beta), f(\gamma)\).

Substitution Method

Let \(y = f(x)\) and solve for \(x\) in terms of \(y\). Substitute \(x = f^{-1}(y)\) into \(P(x) = 0\) and tidy up.

\(\alpha, \beta\) are the roots of \(x^2 - 5x + 6 = 0\). Find a quadratic whose roots are \(\alpha+2\) and \(\beta+2\).
Let \(y = x + 2\), so \(x = y - 2\). Substitute: \((y-2)^2 - 5(y-2) + 6 = 0\).
Expand: \(y^2 - 4y + 4 - 5y + 10 + 6 = y^2 - 9y + 20 = 0\).
\(\alpha, \beta\) are roots of \(x^2 - 3x + 2 = 0\). Find the quadratic with roots \(1/\alpha\) and \(1/\beta\).
Sub \(x = 1/y\): \(1/y^2 - 3/y + 2 = 0\). Multiply by \(y^2\): \(1 - 3y + 2y^2 = 0 \Rightarrow 2y^2 - 3y + 1 = 0\).
OCR Exam Tip: The substitution approach is fast and reliable. Alternative: compute the new sum and product of roots using the identities above, then write down the new polynomial.

3. Partial Fractions

A rational function \(\dfrac{P(x)}{Q(x)}\) with \(\deg P < \deg Q\) can be split into a sum of simpler fractions.

Standard Forms

Factor in denominatorPartial fraction
Linear \((ax+b)\)\(\dfrac{A}{ax+b}\)
Repeated linear \((ax+b)^2\)\(\dfrac{A}{ax+b} + \dfrac{B}{(ax+b)^2}\)
Repeated linear \((ax+b)^3\)\(\dfrac{A}{ax+b} + \dfrac{B}{(ax+b)^2} + \dfrac{C}{(ax+b)^3}\)
Irreducible quadratic \((ax^2+bx+c)\)\(\dfrac{Ax+B}{ax^2+bx+c}\)
If \(\deg P \ge \deg Q\), first do polynomial long division to reduce to a proper fraction plus a quotient.
Express \(\dfrac{3x + 5}{(x-1)(x+2)^2}\) in partial fractions.
Write \(\dfrac{A}{x-1} + \dfrac{B}{x+2} + \dfrac{C}{(x+2)^2}\). Multiply through:
\(3x + 5 = A(x+2)^2 + B(x-1)(x+2) + C(x-1)\).
\(x=1\): \(8 = 9A \Rightarrow A = 8/9\). \(x=-2\): \(-1 = -3C \Rightarrow C = 1/3\).
Compare \(x^2\): \(0 = A + B \Rightarrow B = -8/9\).
Answer: \(\dfrac{8/9}{x-1} - \dfrac{8/9}{x+2} + \dfrac{1/3}{(x+2)^2}\).

4. Rational Inequalities

Solving \(\dfrac{P(x)}{Q(x)} < k\) (or \(>, \le, \ge\)) requires care — you cannot simply multiply by \(Q(x)\) since its sign may vary.

Method 1: Multiply by \([Q(x)]^2\)

Always positive (except at roots of \(Q\)), so preserves the direction of the inequality:

\(\dfrac{P(x)}{Q(x)} < k \iff (P(x) - kQ(x))\,Q(x) < 0\)  (and \(Q(x) \ne 0\)).

Method 2: Rearrange to \(\dfrac{R(x)}{Q(x)} < 0\) and Use a Sign Diagram

Bring everything to one side over a common denominator, then analyse where each factor is positive/negative between its roots.

Solve \(\dfrac{x+1}{x-3} \le 2\).
Rearrange: \(\dfrac{x+1}{x-3} - 2 \le 0 \Rightarrow \dfrac{x+1 - 2(x-3)}{x-3} \le 0 \Rightarrow \dfrac{-x+7}{x-3} \le 0\).
Or: \(\dfrac{x - 7}{x - 3} \ge 0\) (flip sign when multiplying by \(-1\)).
Critical values: \(x = 3\) (excluded) and \(x = 7\). Sign diagram: positive for \(x < 3\), negative for \(3 < x < 7\), positive for \(x > 7\).
Solution: \(x < 3\) or \(x \ge 7\).

5. Modulus Function Equations and Inequalities

Solutions of \(|f(x)| = g(x)\) require \(g(x) \ge 0\). Then split into \(f(x) = g(x)\) or \(f(x) = -g(x)\) and check each solution satisfies the original equation.

For \(|f(x)| < k\) with \(k > 0\): \(-k < f(x) < k\). For \(|f(x)| > k\): \(f(x) > k\) or \(f(x) < -k\).

6. Graph Sketching and Rational Functions

Key features when sketching \(y = \dfrac{P(x)}{Q(x)}\):

  • Vertical asymptotes at zeros of \(Q(x)\) (that are not shared with \(P\)).
  • Horizontal asymptote: from \(\lim_{x\to\pm\infty}\). Compare degrees of \(P\) and \(Q\).
  • Oblique (slant) asymptote: if \(\deg P = \deg Q + 1\), do polynomial division; the quotient is the oblique asymptote.
  • \(x\)-intercepts: zeros of \(P\). \(y\)-intercept: value at \(x = 0\).
  • Sign behaviour between consecutive roots/asymptotes.
OCR Exam Tip: For "sketch" questions, always label: axis intercepts, asymptotes (with equations), and stationary points if asked. Use a dashed line for asymptotes.

7. Inverse Functions and Graph Transformations

A function has an inverse only if it is one-to-one (injective). You may need to restrict the domain to make \(f\) one-to-one.

The graph of \(y = f^{-1}(x)\) is the reflection of \(y = f(x)\) in the line \(y = x\).

Transformations Recap

TransformationReplaceEffect on graph
\(y = f(x) + a\)—Vertical translation by \(a\)
\(y = f(x - a)\)\(x \to x - a\)Horizontal translation by \(a\)
\(y = a f(x)\)—Vertical stretch, factor \(a\)
\(y = f(ax)\)\(x \to ax\)Horizontal stretch, factor \(1/a\)
\(y = |f(x)|\)—Reflect parts below \(x\)-axis upwards
\(y = f(|x|)\)—Reflect \(x \ge 0\) part in the \(y\)-axis

8. Summary

  • Roots-coefficient relations come from expanding \(a(x-\alpha)(x-\beta)\cdots\).
  • Transformed-root polynomials via substitution.
  • Partial fractions: linear, repeated, and irreducible quadratic factors each have their own form.
  • Rational inequalities: multiply by \([Q(x)]^2\) or use sign diagrams — never by \(Q(x)\) directly.
