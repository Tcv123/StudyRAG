1.5: Further Vectors

1. Scalar (Dot) Product — Recap

\(\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = a_1 b_1 + a_2 b_2 + a_3 b_3 = |\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|\cos\theta\)
  • Scalar result. Commutative.
  • \(\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = 0\) iff \(\mathbf{a}\) and \(\mathbf{b}\) are perpendicular (or one is zero).
  • Used to find angle between two vectors.

2. Vector (Cross) Product

\(\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b} = \begin{vmatrix} \mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \\ a_1 & a_2 & a_3 \\ b_1 & b_2 & b_3 \end{vmatrix} = (a_2 b_3 - a_3 b_2)\mathbf{i} - (a_1 b_3 - a_3 b_1)\mathbf{j} + (a_1 b_2 - a_2 b_1)\mathbf{k}\)

Key Properties

  • Vector result, perpendicular to both \(\mathbf{a}\) and \(\mathbf{b}\).
  • Magnitude \(|\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b}| = |\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|\sin\theta\) — area of the parallelogram spanned by \(\mathbf{a}\) and \(\mathbf{b}\).
  • Direction given by the right-hand rule: curl fingers from \(\mathbf{a}\) to \(\mathbf{b}\), thumb gives direction.
  • Anti-commutative: \(\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{a} = -(\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b})\).
  • \(\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b} = \mathbf{0}\) iff \(\mathbf{a}\) and \(\mathbf{b}\) are parallel (or one is zero).
  • Distributive over addition: \(\mathbf{a} \times (\mathbf{b} + \mathbf{c}) = \mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b} + \mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{c}\).
Area of triangle with vertices \(A(1,0,2)\), \(B(3,1,1)\), \(C(0,2,4)\).
\(\overrightarrow{AB} = (2, 1, -1)\), \(\overrightarrow{AC} = (-1, 2, 2)\).
\(\overrightarrow{AB} \times \overrightarrow{AC} = (1\cdot2 - (-1)\cdot2,\; -[2\cdot 2 - (-1)\cdot(-1)],\; 2\cdot 2 - 1\cdot(-1)) = (4, -3, 5)\).
Magnitude \(= \sqrt{16 + 9 + 25} = \sqrt{50} = 5\sqrt{2}\).
Area \(= \tfrac{1}{2}|\overrightarrow{AB} \times \overrightarrow{AC}| = \tfrac{5\sqrt{2}}{2}\).

3. Scalar Triple Product

\([\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b}, \mathbf{c}] := \mathbf{a} \cdot (\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{c}) = \det \begin{pmatrix} a_1 & a_2 & a_3 \\ b_1 & b_2 & b_3 \\ c_1 & c_2 & c_3 \end{pmatrix}\)
  • Scalar result.
  • \(|\mathbf{a} \cdot (\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{c})|\) = volume of the parallelepiped spanned by the three vectors.
  • \(\mathbf{a} \cdot (\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{c}) = \mathbf{b} \cdot (\mathbf{c} \times \mathbf{a}) = \mathbf{c} \cdot (\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b})\) (cyclic).
  • Zero iff the three vectors are coplanar.

4. Equation of a Line in 3D

Vector Form

\(\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{a} + \lambda \mathbf{d}, \;\; \lambda \in \mathbb{R}\)

where \(\mathbf{a}\) is the position vector of any point on the line and \(\mathbf{d}\) is a direction vector.

Cartesian Form

Eliminate the parameter from \(x = a_1 + \lambda d_1\), etc.:

\(\dfrac{x - a_1}{d_1} = \dfrac{y - a_2}{d_2} = \dfrac{z - a_3}{d_3}\)  (when all \(d_i \neq 0\))

5. Equation of a Plane

Vector (Normal) Form

\((\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{a}) \cdot \mathbf{n} = 0, \quad\text{equivalently}\quad \mathbf{r} \cdot \mathbf{n} = d\)

where \(\mathbf{n}\) is normal to the plane and \(\mathbf{a}\) lies on it.

Cartesian Form

\(n_1 x + n_2 y + n_3 z = d\)

Parametric Form

\(\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{a} + \lambda \mathbf{u} + \mu \mathbf{v}\)

where \(\mathbf{u}\), \(\mathbf{v}\) are two non-parallel direction vectors in the plane; \(\mathbf{n} = \mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v}\).

6. Intersections and Angles

Line and Plane

Substitute the line's parametric equations into \(\mathbf{r} \cdot \mathbf{n} = d\) and solve for \(\lambda\).

  • One solution ⇒ a single intersection point.
  • No solution ⇒ line is parallel to the plane (and not in it).
  • Identity ⇒ line lies in the plane.

Angle between Line and Plane

\(\sin\theta = \dfrac{|\mathbf{d} \cdot \mathbf{n}|}{|\mathbf{d}||\mathbf{n}|}\)

(\(\theta\) is the angle between the line and its projection onto the plane.)

Angle between Two Planes

\(\cos\theta = \dfrac{|\mathbf{n}_1 \cdot \mathbf{n}_2|}{|\mathbf{n}_1||\mathbf{n}_2|}\)

\(\mathbf{n} = (2, -1, 2)\), \(|\mathbf{n}| = 3\). \(\mathbf{p}\cdot\mathbf{n} - d = 8 - 1 + 4 - 5 = 6\). Distance \(= 6/3 = 2\).

7. Relationship Between Two Lines

Two lines in 3D may be:

  • Parallel — direction vectors proportional.
  • Intersecting — a single common point.
  • Skew — neither parallel nor intersecting.
  • Coincident — same line.

To test: solve the parametric equations component by component for \(\lambda, \mu\). If the \(x\)- and \(y\)-equations give consistent \((\lambda, \mu)\) that also satisfy the \(z\)-equation ⇒ lines meet; otherwise skew.

8. Exam Style Strategy

  • Always state the direction vector and a point when writing a line in vector form.
  • For cross products, use the determinant layout — faster and fewer errors.
  • Check whether three points are collinear by computing \((\mathbf{B} - \mathbf{A}) \times (\mathbf{C} - \mathbf{A})\) — zero iff collinear.
  • Check coplanarity of four points by taking a scalar triple product — zero iff coplanar.
