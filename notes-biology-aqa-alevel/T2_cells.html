T2 — Cells

2.1 Cell Structure

Cell: The basic structural and functional unit of all living organisms. Cells are classified as either prokaryotic (no membrane-bound nucleus; bacteria and archaea) or eukaryotic (membrane-bound nucleus and organelles; animals, plants, fungi, protists). All cells share certain features: a cell surface membrane, genetic material (DNA), ribosomes, and cytoplasm where metabolic reactions occur.

Prokaryotic Cells

Prokaryotic cells (bacteria and archaea) are significantly smaller (typically 0.5-5 micrometres diameter) and structurally simpler than eukaryotic cells (typically 10-100 micrometres):

  • No membrane-bound nucleus — DNA exists as a single, circular chromosome in the nucleoid region (not enclosed by a membrane). The DNA is not associated with histone proteins (though it is associated with other proteins for compaction).
  • Plasmids: Small, circular, self-replicating DNA molecules that may carry genes for antibiotic resistance, toxin production, or other survival advantages. Plasmids can be transferred between bacteria (horizontal gene transfer) and are widely used as vectors in genetic engineering.
  • 70S ribosomes: Smaller than eukaryotic 80S ribosomes but functionally equivalent. This size difference is medically important — antibiotics such as erythromycin and chloramphenicol specifically target 70S ribosomes, inhibiting bacterial protein synthesis without affecting human 80S ribosomes.
  • No membrane-bound organelles — no mitochondria, ER, Golgi, or lysosomes. All metabolic processes occur in the cytoplasm or on the cell membrane.
  • Cell wall: Made of peptidoglycan (murein) — a polymer of sugars cross-linked by short amino acid chains. This is chemically distinct from the cellulose walls of plants. Gram-positive bacteria have a thick peptidoglycan layer; Gram-negative bacteria have a thin layer plus an outer lipopolysaccharide membrane.
  • Capsule (slime layer): A polysaccharide layer outside the cell wall in some bacteria. Provides protection against phagocytosis by white blood cells, desiccation, and helps with adhesion to surfaces (biofilm formation).
  • Flagella: Long, whip-like protein structures used for locomotion (powered by a proton-motive motor). Structurally different from eukaryotic flagella.
  • Pili (fimbriae): Short, hair-like protein projections used for attachment to host cells and for conjugation (transfer of plasmid DNA between bacteria).
  • Mesosomes: Infoldings of the cell membrane (though some consider these preparation artefacts). If present, they increase membrane surface area for respiration (since there are no mitochondria, respiratory enzymes are located on the cell membrane).
Exam Tip: Both mitochondria and chloroplasts have their own circular DNA and 70S ribosomes — this is key evidence for the endosymbiotic theory, which proposes they were once free-living prokaryotes engulfed by an ancestral eukaryotic cell. Additional evidence: they have double membranes (the inner membrane from the original prokaryote, the outer from the host's vesicle), they replicate by binary fission independently of the cell cycle, and their DNA sequences are more similar to bacterial DNA than to the host cell's nuclear DNA.

2.2 Cell Fractionation and Ultracentrifugation

Cell fractionation: The process of separating cell components by breaking open cells (homogenisation) and spinning at increasing speeds (differential centrifugation) to isolate organelles by size and density. This allows the biochemical function of individual organelles to be studied in isolation.

Steps in cell fractionation:

  1. Homogenisation: Cells are broken open (by blender, Potter homogeniser, or ultrasound) in an ice-cold, isotonic, buffered solution.
    • Ice-cold: Reduces kinase and hydrolytic enzyme activity, preventing digestion of organelles and their contents.
    • Isotonic: Same water potential as cell contents — prevents organelles from swelling and bursting (in hypotonic solution) or shrinking (in hypertonic solution) due to osmosis.
    • Buffered: Maintains a constant pH, preventing denaturation of enzymes within organelles.
  2. Filtration: The homogenate is filtered through muslin or gauze to remove large cell debris (cell wall fragments, connective tissue, intact cells) that would interfere with separation.
  3. Differential centrifugation: The filtrate is centrifuged at progressively increasing speeds. At each speed, the heaviest remaining organelles sediment to the bottom as a pellet; the supernatant (liquid above) is carefully transferred to a new tube and spun at a higher speed.
    • Low speed (~1000g for 10 min): Nuclei sediment (largest and heaviest)
    • Medium speed (~3500g for 10 min): Mitochondria (and chloroplasts if from plant cells)
    • Higher speed (~15,000g for 20 min): Lysosomes and peroxisomes
    • Very high speed (~100,000g for 60 min): Ribosomes, endoplasmic reticulum fragments, and cell membrane vesicles (microsomes)

Each pellet is resuspended and can be further purified by density gradient centrifugation (centrifuging through a sucrose density gradient, where organelles settle at the level matching their own density).

Exam Tip: Examiners frequently ask why each condition (ice-cold, isotonic, buffered) is necessary. Learn the three reasons as a set. Also remember: the order of sedimentation reflects organelle size/density. Nuclei first (largest), then mitochondria, then smaller structures. The final supernatant contains soluble cytoplasmic proteins.

2.3 Transport Across Cell Membranes

The Fluid Mosaic Model

Fluid Mosaic Model (Singer and Nicolson, 1972): The cell surface membrane is a phospholipid bilayer with proteins embedded in or attached to it. It is called "fluid" because the individual phospholipids and proteins can move laterally within the plane of the membrane (lateral diffusion), and "mosaic" because the proteins are scattered across the membrane surface in a varied, patchwork pattern, like tiles in a mosaic.

Components of the membrane and their functions:

  • Phospholipid bilayer: Forms the fundamental barrier. The hydrophobic core prevents passage of polar molecules, ions, and large molecules. Small non-polar molecules (O2, CO2, steroid hormones) can pass through by dissolving in the hydrophobic core. The bilayer is selectively permeable.
  • Cholesterol: Sits between phospholipid tails, regulating membrane fluidity across a range of temperatures (see section 1.3). Makes the membrane less permeable to small water-soluble molecules and ions.
  • Integral (transmembrane) proteins: Span the entire bilayer. Include channel proteins (water-filled pores allowing specific ions or polar molecules to pass; some are gated — open/close in response to voltage or ligands) and carrier proteins (change shape to transport specific molecules across — used in facilitated diffusion and active transport).
  • Peripheral proteins: Attached to the inner or outer surface of the membrane (not embedded). Often have enzymatic or structural/signalling roles (e.g. components of the cytoskeleton, enzymes in metabolic pathways).
  • Glycoproteins: Proteins with carbohydrate chains attached, located on the extracellular surface. Function in cell-cell recognition (immune system identifies self vs non-self), cell signalling (hormone receptors), and cell adhesion.
  • Glycolipids: Lipids with carbohydrate chains attached, also on the extracellular surface. Contribute to cell recognition, stability of the membrane, and form part of the glycocalyx (sugar coat).

Diffusion

Diffusion: The net movement of molecules or ions from a region of higher concentration to a region of lower concentration, down a concentration gradient. It is a passive process — it requires no metabolic energy (ATP) because it is driven by the random kinetic energy of the molecules themselves.

Factors affecting rate of diffusion (described by Fick's Law):

Rate of diffusion ∝ (Surface area x Concentration difference) / Thickness of barrier
  • Concentration gradient: Steeper gradient = faster diffusion. Maintained by constantly removing the substance on one side (e.g. blood flow past exchange surfaces).
  • Temperature: Higher temperature = greater kinetic energy = faster molecular movement = faster diffusion.
  • Surface area: Larger area = more space for molecules to cross = faster overall diffusion.
  • Thickness of membrane/barrier: Thinner barrier = shorter diffusion distance = faster diffusion.
  • Size of molecule: Smaller molecules diffuse faster than larger ones.
  • Polarity: Non-polar molecules (O2, CO2) diffuse directly through the phospholipid bilayer. Polar molecules and ions cannot easily cross the hydrophobic core and require membrane proteins.

Facilitated diffusion: The passive movement of polar molecules or ions across a membrane through specific membrane proteins — either channel proteins (water-filled pores, often gated) or carrier proteins (which bind the molecule and change shape to release it on the other side). Facilitated diffusion is still passive (down the concentration gradient, no ATP required) but it depends on the number of available transport proteins, so it shows saturation kinetics (rate plateaus when all proteins are occupied).

Osmosis

Osmosis: The net movement of water molecules from a region of higher water potential (less negative / more dilute) to a region of lower water potential (more negative / more concentrated) across a partially permeable membrane. It is a special case of diffusion involving water only.
Water potential (Ψ) = Pressure potential (Ψp) + Solute potential (Ψs)

Water potential (Ψ) is measured in kilopascals (kPa). Pure water has Ψ = 0 (the maximum). Adding solute makes Ψs more negative, lowering overall Ψ. Water always moves from higher Ψ (less negative) to lower Ψ (more negative) across a partially permeable membrane. Pressure potential (Ψp) is the physical pressure exerted on water — in plant cells, the rigid cell wall creates a positive pressure potential when the cell is turgid.

Active Transport

Active transport: The movement of molecules or ions across a membrane against their concentration gradient (from low to high concentration), requiring metabolic energy from ATP hydrolysis and specific carrier proteins. The carrier protein binds the molecule, ATP provides energy for a conformational change, and the molecule is released on the other side.

Key examples of active transport:

  • Na+/K+ ATPase pump: Pumps 3 Na+ out and 2 K+ into the cell per ATP hydrolysed. Essential for maintaining resting potential in neurones, driving co-transport in the gut, and regulating cell volume.
  • Mineral ion uptake by root hair cells: Plants actively transport mineral ions (e.g. nitrate, magnesium) from the soil into root hair cells against steep concentration gradients.
  • H+ pumping in chemiosmosis: Protons are actively pumped across mitochondrial inner membranes and thylakoid membranes to create the gradient that drives ATP synthesis.

Co-transport (Secondary Active Transport)

Co-transport couples the movement of one substance down its concentration gradient with the transport of another substance against its gradient, using the same carrier protein. The gradient of the first substance was established by active transport (primary), so co-transport indirectly requires ATP.

Glucose absorption in the ileum — a key AQA example:

  1. Na+/K+ ATPase on the basal membrane pumps Na+ out of the epithelial cell into the blood, maintaining a low intracellular [Na+].
  2. Na+ diffuses back into the epithelial cell from the gut lumen via a sodium-glucose co-transporter (SGLT1) on the apical (brush border) membrane. The Na+ moves down its concentration gradient, and this provides the energy to simultaneously carry glucose into the cell against glucose's concentration gradient.
  3. Glucose accumulates inside the cell to a concentration higher than in the blood. It then exits the cell into the blood via facilitated diffusion through GLUT2 transporters on the basal membrane.
Exam Tip: Co-transport still requires ATP indirectly — the Na+/K+ ATPase pump creates the sodium gradient that drives glucose uptake. It is not purely passive. A common exam error is to describe co-transport as not requiring energy — always explain that the sodium gradient is maintained by active transport. Also: glucose absorption uses two different proteins — SGLT1 (apical, co-transport) and GLUT2 (basal, facilitated diffusion). Do not confuse them.

Endocytosis and Exocytosis

Endocytosis is the bulk uptake of materials into the cell by invagination of the cell membrane to form a vesicle. Phagocytosis (cell eating) takes in large particles (e.g. bacteria engulfed by macrophages); pinocytosis (cell drinking) takes in fluid and dissolved molecules. Both require ATP.

Exocytosis is the reverse — vesicles fuse with the cell membrane, releasing their contents outside the cell. Used for secretion of hormones (e.g. insulin from beta cells), neurotransmitters (e.g. ACh at synapses), digestive enzymes (e.g. from pancreatic acinar cells), and cell wall materials in plants.

2.4 Cell Division — Mitosis

Cell cycle: The ordered sequence of events in a cell's life from one division to the next. It consists of interphase (G1, S, G2 — the cell grows, replicates DNA, and prepares for division) and the mitotic phase (mitosis + cytokinesis).

Interphase is the longest phase (~90% of cell cycle time) and consists of:

  • G1 (Gap 1): Cell grows, synthesises proteins and organelles, carries out normal functions. Cell cycle checkpoints verify the cell is ready to proceed.
  • S phase (Synthesis): DNA is replicated — each chromosome is copied to form two identical sister chromatids joined at the centromere. Histones are also synthesised.
  • G2 (Gap 2): Cell continues to grow, synthesises proteins needed for mitosis (e.g. tubulin for spindle fibres), checks for DNA replication errors.

Importance of mitosis: Growth and development of multicellular organisms, repair of damaged tissues, asexual reproduction (e.g. binary fission in unicellular eukaryotes, vegetative propagation in plants). All daughter cells are genetically identical clones of the parent cell.

Mitotic index: The proportion of cells in a tissue that are undergoing mitosis at any given time. Mitotic index = number of cells in mitosis / total number of cells observed. A high mitotic index indicates rapid cell division and may suggest the presence of a tumour.

2.5 Cell Recognition and the Immune System

Self and Non-Self

Every nucleated cell displays MHC (Major Histocompatibility Complex) proteins on its surface — these are the "self" markers that identify the cell as belonging to the organism. The immune system is trained to tolerate self-antigens but to mount a response against non-self antigens (foreign molecules, usually proteins or glycoproteins on the surface of pathogens, toxins, or transplanted cells).

Antigen: Any molecule (usually a protein or glycoprotein) that can be recognised by the immune system and trigger an immune response. Pathogens carry non-self antigens on their surface. The immune system can also respond to abnormal self-antigens on cancer cells and to antigens on transplanted organs (causing rejection).

Phagocytosis — Non-Specific (Innate) Immune Response

Phagocytes (neutrophils and macrophages) provide a rapid, non-specific first line of defence:

  1. Chemotaxis: Phagocyte is attracted to the site of infection by chemical signals (cytokines, complement proteins, chemicals released by damaged cells).
  2. Recognition: Phagocyte recognises non-self antigens on the pathogen surface (may be enhanced by opsonisation — coating the pathogen with antibodies or complement proteins).
  3. Engulfment: Phagocyte extends pseudopodia (cytoplasmic extensions) around the pathogen, engulfing it by endocytosis into a membrane-bound vesicle called a phagosome.
  4. Digestion: Lysosomes fuse with the phagosome, forming a phagolysosome. Hydrolytic enzymes (lysozymes, proteases) and reactive oxygen species digest and destroy the pathogen.
  5. Antigen presentation: The phagocyte processes pathogen antigens and displays them on its surface bound to MHC II molecules. It becomes an antigen-presenting cell (APC), which is essential for activating the specific (adaptive) immune response.

T Lymphocytes (T Cells) — Specific (Adaptive) Immune Response

T cells mature in the thymus gland, where they are selected to respond to non-self antigens only (self-reactive T cells are eliminated — this prevents autoimmune disease). Each T cell has a unique T cell receptor (TCR) on its surface that is complementary to one specific antigen.

When an APC presents a matching antigen to a T cell, the T cell is activated and undergoes clonal expansion (rapid mitosis). T cells differentiate into several types:

  • Helper T cells (TH, CD4+): The coordinators of the immune response. They release cytokines (interleukins) that: stimulate B cells to divide and differentiate into plasma cells; activate cytotoxic T cells; attract more phagocytes to the infection site; stimulate macrophage activity. Without helper T cells, neither the humoral nor the cell-mediated immune response can function effectively.
  • Cytotoxic T cells (TC, CD8+): Kill infected body cells, cancer cells, and transplanted cells that display foreign antigens. They release perforin (a protein that creates pores in the target cell membrane, causing lysis) and granzymes (enzymes that trigger apoptosis — programmed cell death).
  • Memory T cells: Long-lived cells that remain in the body for years or decades. They provide a faster and stronger secondary immune response upon re-exposure to the same antigen.

B Lymphocytes (B Cells) and Antibodies — Humoral Immunity

B cells mature in the bone marrow. Each B cell produces one specific type of antibody, which is displayed on its surface as a B cell receptor. Activation requires two signals: the antigen binding to the B cell receptor, AND stimulation by cytokines from helper T cells (this dual requirement prevents inappropriate immune activation).

Once activated, B cells undergo clonal expansion (rapid mitosis) and differentiate into:

  • Plasma cells: Short-lived cells that act as antibody factories, secreting up to 2000 antibody molecules per second into the blood and lymph. They are full of rough ER for protein synthesis.
  • Memory B cells: Long-lived cells that persist in the body and can rapidly differentiate into plasma cells upon re-exposure to the same antigen, providing a much faster and larger secondary response.
Antibody (Immunoglobulin): A Y-shaped glycoprotein with quaternary structure, produced by plasma cells. It consists of four polypeptide chains (two identical heavy chains and two identical light chains) linked by disulfide bonds. Each antibody has two identical antigen-binding sites in the variable regions at the tips of the Y — these are complementary in shape to one specific antigen (like an enzyme's active site). The constant region (stem of the Y) determines the antibody class (IgG, IgA, IgM, etc.) and how it interacts with the rest of the immune system.

How antibodies help destroy pathogens:

  • Agglutination: Antibodies (with two binding sites each) cross-link multiple pathogens together, forming clumps. This prevents pathogen movement and makes phagocytosis more efficient (one phagocyte can engulf the whole clump).
  • Neutralisation: Antibodies bind to toxins or viral surface proteins, blocking the active/binding sites so the toxin cannot bind to host cell receptors and the virus cannot enter cells.
  • Opsonisation: Antibodies coat the pathogen surface. Phagocytes have receptors for the constant region of antibodies, so opsonised pathogens are recognised and engulfed much more efficiently.
  • Complement activation: Antibodies bound to pathogen surfaces activate the complement cascade, leading to formation of membrane attack complexes (MACs) that punch holes in pathogen membranes, causing lysis.

Vaccination

Vaccination (Immunisation): The deliberate introduction of antigenic material (a vaccine) to stimulate a primary immune response and production of memory cells, providing immunity against future infection without causing the disease. The memory cells enable a rapid secondary response if the real pathogen is encountered later.

Types of vaccine:

  • Live attenuated: Weakened form of the pathogen that replicates but does not cause disease (e.g. MMR, BCG). Strong, long-lasting immunity but unsuitable for immunocompromised individuals.
  • Inactivated (killed): Pathogen killed by heat or chemicals (e.g. influenza injection, hepatitis A). Safe but may require booster doses.
  • Subunit/recombinant: Purified antigen proteins only, no whole pathogen (e.g. hepatitis B — recombinant HBsAg protein produced in yeast). Very safe but may need adjuvants to boost immune response.
  • Toxoid: Inactivated toxin (e.g. tetanus, diphtheria). Produces antibodies against the toxin rather than the pathogen itself.
  • mRNA vaccines: Contain mRNA encoding a pathogen antigen (e.g. spike protein of SARS-CoV-2). Host cells translate the mRNA, produce the antigen, and the immune system responds. mRNA is rapidly degraded and does not enter the nucleus or alter DNA.

HIV and the Immune System

HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus) is a retrovirus that specifically targets and destroys helper T cells (CD4+ cells):

  1. HIV glycoprotein gp120 binds to the CD4 receptor on helper T cells (and a co-receptor, CCR5 or CXCR4).
  2. The viral envelope fuses with the host cell membrane; viral RNA and enzymes enter the cell.
  3. Reverse transcriptase converts viral RNA into complementary DNA (cDNA), which is then made double-stranded.
  4. Integrase inserts the viral DNA into the host cell's genome (it becomes a provirus).
  5. The host cell's machinery transcribes the viral DNA and translates it, producing new viral proteins and RNA.
  6. New viral particles assemble and bud from the host cell membrane, destroying the helper T cell in the process.

As the helper T cell count progressively falls (from a normal ~1000 per mm3 to below 200), the immune system becomes severely compromised. The patient becomes vulnerable to opportunistic infections (e.g. Pneumocystis pneumonia, tuberculosis, Candida infections, Kaposi's sarcoma) — this stage is defined as AIDS (Acquired Immunodeficiency Syndrome).

Why HIV is difficult to control with vaccines:

  • HIV has a very high mutation rate (reverse transcriptase is error-prone), producing many antigenic variants
  • HIV integrates into the host genome, hiding from the immune system (latent provirus)
  • HIV directly attacks the immune cells (helper T cells) needed to mount a response against it
  • The virus can spread through cell-to-cell contact without being exposed to antibodies
Exam Tip: HIV does not directly kill the person — it destroys helper T cells, meaning the body cannot coordinate effective immune responses against other infections. Death results from opportunistic infections, not from HIV itself. Always make this distinction clear. Also: HIV is a retrovirus because it uses reverse transcriptase to convert RNA to DNA (the reverse of the normal flow of genetic information). Antiretroviral therapy (ART) targets multiple stages of the viral life cycle (e.g. reverse transcriptase inhibitors, protease inhibitors, integrase inhibitors) — combination therapy reduces the chance of resistance evolving.

Monoclonal Antibodies

Monoclonal antibodies: Identical antibodies produced by a single clone of B cells (hybridoma cells). They are specific to one epitope (binding site) on one antigen, making them highly targeted tools for medicine and research.

Production of monoclonal antibodies:

  1. Inject a mouse with the target antigen to stimulate an immune response.
  2. Extract B cells from the mouse's spleen (these produce the desired antibody but have a limited lifespan).
  3. Fuse the B cells with immortal myeloma (cancer) cells using polyethylene glycol, creating hybridoma cells that both produce antibodies and divide indefinitely.
  4. Screen hybridomas to select those producing the correct antibody.
  5. Clone the selected hybridoma and grow in culture to produce large quantities of identical antibodies.

Medical applications:

  • Pregnancy tests: Detect hCG (human chorionic gonadotropin) in urine using monoclonal antibodies bound to coloured particles.
  • Cancer treatment: Monoclonal antibodies targeted to tumour-specific antigens (e.g. Herceptin / trastuzumab targets HER2 on breast cancer cells). Can be conjugated to cytotoxic drugs, radioactive isotopes, or immune-stimulating molecules.
  • Diagnostic testing: ELISA tests for infections (e.g. HIV antibody test), blood typing, measuring hormone levels.
  • Transplant rejection prevention: Anti-CD3 antibodies suppress T cell activation.
Exam Tip: Ethical concerns with monoclonal antibody production include animal use (mice) in the initial stages and side effects in patients (e.g. allergic reactions to mouse-derived proteins — humanised antibodies have been developed to reduce this). Be prepared to discuss both benefits and limitations in extended response questions.
