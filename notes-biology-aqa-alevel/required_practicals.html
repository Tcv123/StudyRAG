RP1: Effect of a Variable on Enzyme-Controlled Reaction Rate

Aim: Investigate the effect of temperature (or pH, or substrate/enzyme concentration) on the rate of an enzyme-controlled reaction.

Biology link: Enzymes are biological catalysts (Topic 1). Their rate of reaction is affected by temperature (kinetic energy and denaturation), pH (ionisation of R groups in active site), and substrate concentration (collision frequency and saturation).

Apparatus

  • Test tubes and rack, boiling tubes
  • Catalase source (potato extract or liver homogenate) and hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) solution (typically 3-10 volume)
  • Gas syringe (most accurate) or inverted measuring cylinder over water
  • Delivery tube with bung (tight-fitting to prevent gas escape)
  • Water baths at range of temperatures (e.g. 20, 30, 40, 50, 60°C) or buffer solutions for pH investigation
  • Thermometer, stopwatch, graduated syringes or pipettes for measuring volumes

Method

  1. Set up water bath at the required temperature. Place both enzyme and substrate solutions in separate test tubes in the water bath for 5 minutes to equilibrate to the target temperature (this is a key step often missed).
  2. Measure a fixed volume of enzyme and a fixed volume of H2O2 using graduated syringes.
  3. Add the enzyme to the H2O2, immediately fit the bung and delivery tube connected to the gas syringe (or inverted measuring cylinder).
  4. Record the volume of O2 gas collected every 30 seconds for 5 minutes.
  5. Repeat at each temperature (or pH/concentration). Perform at least 3 repeats per condition and calculate mean values.
  6. Calculate the initial rate of reaction from the steepest (linear) part of the volume-time curve (typically the first 30-60 seconds).

Alternative method (for amylase): Amylase breaks down starch. Mix amylase with starch solution at different temperatures. At regular intervals, take a sample and add to iodine solution. Time how long until iodine no longer turns blue-black (starch fully digested). Rate = 1/time.

Variables

IndependentDependentControl Variables
Temperature (or pH, or concentration)Volume of O2 produced per unit time (rate of reaction)Volume and concentration of enzyme, volume and concentration of substrate, same enzyme batch, same total volume, equilibration time

Safety

  • H2O2 is an irritant at higher concentrations — wear eye protection and gloves.
  • Care with hot water baths — do not exceed 60°C; handle tubes with tongs.
  • O2 is released — ensure good ventilation. Do not approach with naked flames.
  • If using buffer solutions, some are irritant — check MSDS.

Analysis

  • Plot volume of O2 (y-axis) against time (x-axis) for each condition — gives a product-time curve.
  • Calculate initial rate = gradient of the tangent at time = 0 (or from the linear portion). Rate = Δvolume / Δtime (cm3 s-1 or cm3 min-1).
  • Plot initial rate (y-axis) against temperature or pH (x-axis) to find the optimum.
  • For temperature: rate increases with temperature (more kinetic energy, more enzyme-substrate collisions) up to an optimum, then decreases sharply as the enzyme denatures (tertiary structure disrupted, active site shape changed permanently).
  • For pH: bell-shaped curve with a peak at the optimum pH. Extreme pH values disrupt ionic bonds and hydrogen bonds in the enzyme, altering the active site shape.

Exam Tip: Always state that you would calculate the initial rate of reaction (not just "rate"). The rate at time = 0 avoids issues with substrate depletion, product inhibition, and enzyme inactivation. To calculate it: draw a tangent to the curve at t = 0 (or use the first linear portion) and measure its gradient. Also: when discussing temperature, distinguish between increased kinetic energy (reversible) and denaturation (irreversible — bonds broken, active site permanently changed).

RP2: Stained Squashes of Root Tip Cells (Mitosis)

Aim: Observe mitosis in root tip cells and calculate the mitotic index.

Biology link: The root tip meristem contains actively dividing cells (Topic 2). The mitotic index can indicate how rapidly a tissue is growing and has clinical relevance — a high mitotic index in tumour tissue indicates aggressive cancer.

Apparatus

  • Garlic or onion root tips (grown in water for 3-5 days; garlic is often preferred as it has large chromosomes and a fast growth rate)
  • Acetic orcein stain (or toluidine blue / Feulgen stain) — stains chromosomes dark red/purple
  • 1 M hydrochloric acid, watch glass, water bath (or Bunsen burner with care)
  • Microscope slides, coverslips, filter paper
  • Optical microscope (x100 and x400 magnification), mounted needle, pencil eraser or thumb

Method

  1. Cut approximately 2 mm of the growing root tip (the meristematic region where cells are actively dividing) and place on a watch glass.
  2. Add a few drops of 1 M HCl and warm gently at ~60°C for 5 minutes in a water bath. This hydrolyses the middle lamella (breaks down pectin between cell walls), softening the tissue and separating cells so they form a single layer when squashed.
  3. Carefully blot dry with filter paper, then add a few drops of acetic orcein stain. Leave for 10-15 minutes. The stain binds to DNA, making chromosomes visible (darkly stained).
  4. Transfer to a clean microscope slide. Add a coverslip. Place filter paper over the coverslip and squash firmly with a pencil eraser (press vertically, do not twist — this would smear cells). This spreads cells into a single layer.
  5. View under microscope: start at low power (x100) to locate a good region of well-spread cells, then switch to high power (x400) to identify individual cells.
  6. Identify and count cells in each stage of mitosis (prophase, metaphase, anaphase, telophase) and those in interphase. Count at least 200 cells from multiple fields of view for reliability.

Safety

  • HCl is corrosive — wear gloves and eye protection. Handle hot HCl with extra care.
  • Acetic orcein stains skin and clothing permanently — handle carefully, wear gloves and lab coat.
  • Care with hot water or Bunsen burner. Water bath is safer than direct heating.
  • Sharp instruments (scalpels, needles) — cut away from body on a tile.

Analysis

Mitotic index = number of cells undergoing mitosis / total number of cells observed
  • Record the number of cells at each stage: interphase, prophase, metaphase, anaphase, telophase.
  • The proportion of cells at each stage reflects the relative duration of that phase. Prophase is the longest phase of mitosis, so most dividing cells will be in prophase. Anaphase is the shortest, so fewest cells will be seen in this stage.
  • A higher mitotic index indicates faster cell division (more cells in mitosis at any one time).
  • Clinically, mitotic index is used to assess tumour aggressiveness — higher values indicate faster growth.

Exam Tip: Prophase typically has the most cells (longest phase), anaphase the fewest (shortest). Always state the formula: mitotic index = cells in mitosis / total cells observed. If asked to identify stages from a photograph: prophase = chromosomes visible but scattered; metaphase = chromosomes aligned at equator; anaphase = chromosomes being pulled apart to poles (V-shaped); telophase = two groups of chromosomes at poles, cell beginning to divide. Interphase cells show a dark nucleus with no visible individual chromosomes.

RP3: Dilution Series & Calibration Curve (Colorimetry)

Aim: Produce a calibration curve using a serial dilution and colorimetry, then use it to determine the concentration of an unknown solution.

Biology link: Quantitative analysis of biological molecules (Topic 1). Colorimetry is used to measure concentrations of coloured solutions — applicable to enzyme assays, glucose testing (Benedict's), protein quantification (biuret), and many medical diagnostic tests.

Apparatus

  • Stock solution of known concentration (e.g. glucose solution, food dye, or bovine serum albumin)
  • Distilled water, graduated pipettes or syringes (accurate to 0.1 cm3)
  • Test tubes and rack (at least 6 — for serial dilution + blank + unknown)
  • Colorimeter (or spectrophotometer) and clean cuvettes
  • Appropriate colour filter for the colorimeter (use the complementary colour to the solution — e.g. blue/green filter for a red/orange solution like Benedict's)
  • Reagent for colour development if needed (e.g. Benedict's reagent for reducing sugars, biuret reagent for protein)

Method

  1. Prepare a serial dilution: Transfer a fixed volume (e.g. 5 cm3) of stock solution to the first tube. Add an equal volume (5 cm3) of distilled water and mix thoroughly. Transfer 5 cm3 from this to the next tube. Add 5 cm3 distilled water. Repeat for 5-6 tubes total. This gives a doubling dilution series (100%, 50%, 25%, 12.5%, 6.25%, 3.125%).
  2. If a colour-development reagent is needed, add the same volume to each tube (including a blank of distilled water + reagent). Allow colour to develop fully (e.g. heat Benedict's in a water bath at 80°C for 5 minutes).
  3. Zero/calibrate the colorimeter using the blank (distilled water + reagent, no analyte).
  4. Measure the absorbance (or % transmission) of each known concentration solution in the colorimeter. Use clean, fingerprint-free cuvettes oriented consistently.
  5. Plot absorbance (y-axis) against concentration (x-axis) to produce a calibration curve.
  6. Measure the absorbance of the unknown solution (treated identically) and read off its concentration from the calibration curve.

Safety

  • Benedict's reagent contains copper sulfate — irritant. Wear eye protection.
  • Biuret reagent contains NaOH — corrosive. Handle with care, wear gloves.
  • Hot water baths — use tongs; do not overfill tubes.

Analysis

  • Draw a line of best fit through the calibration points (should be approximately linear at lower concentrations — Beer-Lambert law region).
  • Read the unknown concentration: draw a horizontal line from its absorbance value to the calibration curve, then a vertical line down to the x-axis. Read the concentration.
  • If the unknown's absorbance falls outside the calibration range, it must be diluted and re-measured (then multiply by the dilution factor).
  • At high concentrations, the calibration curve may plateau (deviates from linearity) — only interpolate within the linear range.

Sources of Error and Improvements

ErrorEffectImprovement
Air bubbles in cuvetteScatter light; give incorrectly high absorbance readingsTap cuvette gently to dislodge bubbles; wipe with lens tissue
Inaccurate pipetting during dilutionDilution concentrations wrong; calibration curve unreliableUse calibrated graduated pipettes; pipette at eye level; practise technique

Exam Tip: A serial dilution gives a wide range of concentrations in equal ratio steps. Each 1-in-2 dilution halves the concentration. Always use the same volume transferred at each step and the same volume of diluent. The colorimeter measures absorbance (how much light is absorbed by the solution) — higher concentration = darker colour = higher absorbance. Make sure you understand when to use absorbance vs % transmission (absorbance is proportional to concentration; % transmission is inversely related).

RP4: Effect of a Variable on Membrane Permeability (Beetroot)

Aim: Investigate the effect of temperature (or alcohol concentration, or pH) on cell membrane permeability, using beetroot as a model system.

Biology link: Cell membrane structure (Topic 2). Beetroot cells contain the red-purple pigment anthocyanin in their vacuoles. The tonoplast (vacuolar membrane) and cell surface membrane normally retain this pigment inside the cell. When membranes are disrupted, anthocyanin leaks out into the surrounding solution. The amount of leakage (measured by colorimetry) indicates the degree of membrane damage.

Apparatus

  • Fresh beetroot, cork borer (standardised size), white tile, sharp knife/scalpel
  • Test tubes and rack, distilled water (measured volumes, e.g. 5 cm3)
  • Water baths at range of temperatures (e.g. 20, 30, 40, 50, 60, 70°C)
  • Colorimeter and cuvettes (using green or blue filter — complementary to red pigment)
  • Thermometer, stopwatch, paper towels

Method

  1. Use a cork borer to cut equal-sized beetroot cylinders (or use a ruler and knife to cut uniform discs of equal diameter and thickness). This standardises the surface area exposed.
  2. Rinse thoroughly in cold distilled water until the water runs clear. This removes any anthocyanin released during cutting from damaged surface cells. This is critical — without adequate rinsing, all samples will appear to have high leakage.
  3. Place one disc in each labelled test tube containing 5 cm3 distilled water (measured precisely).
  4. Place each tube in the appropriate water bath for a standardised time (e.g. exactly 20 minutes).
  5. After exactly 20 minutes, remove the beetroot discs. Gently agitate each solution to distribute the pigment evenly.
  6. Measure the absorbance of each solution using the colorimeter with a green/blue filter (zeroed with distilled water blank).
  7. Repeat 3 times at each temperature and calculate the mean absorbance.

Safety

  • Sharp instruments (scalpel, cork borer) — cut on a white tile, away from the body.
  • Hot water baths — handle tubes with tongs; take care with boiling water.
  • Beetroot stains clothing and skin — wear lab coat and gloves if concerned.

Expected Results and Explanation

  • At low temperatures (20-30°C): low absorbance — membranes are intact, little pigment leaks out.
  • At moderate temperatures (30-40°C): slight increase — phospholipid bilayer becomes slightly more fluid, minor increase in permeability.
  • At high temperatures (above ~40°C): sharp increase in absorbance — membrane proteins denature (tertiary structure disrupted, proteins unfold and leave gaps in the membrane) AND phospholipids become much more fluid, increasing membrane permeability dramatically. Anthocyanin leaks freely.
  • At very high temperatures (60-70°C): maximum leakage — membranes essentially destroyed.

Sources of Error and Improvements

ErrorEffectImprovement
Unequal disc sizesVariable surface area affects amount of pigment releasedUse same cork borer; cut all discs to uniform thickness with a ruler
Insufficient rinsing of cut surfacesSurface pigment from damaged cells inflates all readingsRinse until water runs completely clear before starting
Different time in water bathLonger exposure = more leakage regardless of temperatureUse a stopwatch; stagger start times at 1-minute intervals; time precisely to 20 minutes each
Cells damaged during cuttingExtra pigment released from damaged cells (not due to temperature)Discard end pieces; use only middle sections; handle gently; use a sharp borer
Pigment settling in tubeUneven colour distribution; inconsistent readingsAgitate solution gently before transferring to cuvette
Exam Tip: Link results directly to membrane structure. At high temperatures: (1) phospholipids gain kinetic energy and move more, increasing membrane fluidity and creating gaps; (2) membrane proteins denature — hydrogen bonds and ionic bonds within the protein's tertiary structure are broken, the protein unfolds and can no longer maintain the membrane's barrier function. Mention both protein denaturation AND phospholipid fluidity for full marks. If asked about alcohol: ethanol dissolves phospholipids (it is a solvent for lipids), disrupting the bilayer structure and increasing permeability.

RP5: Dissection of Gas Exchange / Mass Transport System

Aim: Dissect an organism or organ to examine adaptations for gas exchange or mass transport (e.g. fish head/gills, heart, lungs, leaf).

Biology link: Gas exchange surfaces (Topic 3). Organisms have evolved specialised surfaces with features that maximise the rate of diffusion: large surface area, thin barrier (short diffusion distance), maintained concentration gradient (ventilation, blood supply).

Apparatus

  • Fresh fish head (from fishmonger — not preserved), OR heart (lamb/pig), OR lungs
  • Dissecting board, pins, scissors, forceps, scalpel, mounted needle
  • Magnifying glass or dissecting microscope
  • Paper towels, gloves, eye protection, lab coat

Method (Fish Gills)

  1. Place the fish head on the dissecting board. Lift the operculum (gill cover) to expose the gills beneath.
  2. Use scissors to carefully cut away the operculum, exposing the gill arches.
  3. Identify the 4 gill arches on each side, the gill filaments (primary lamellae — feathery pink structures), and the gill rakers (bony projections that filter food particles from water).
  4. Remove one gill arch and place it in a dish of water on a white tile. Use a magnifying glass or dissecting microscope to observe the secondary lamellae (thin plate-like projections from each filament — the actual gas exchange surfaces).
  5. Produce a labelled biological drawing: use clear, continuous lines; no shading; labels with ruled lines; include a title, magnification, and scale.

Safety

  • Wear gloves when handling biological specimens (bacterial contamination risk).
  • Sharp instruments — always cut away from the body on a dissecting board.
  • Wash hands thoroughly with antibacterial soap after the practical.
  • Dispose of biological material in designated biological waste bins (not regular bins).

Analysis — Structure-Function Relationships

AdaptationHow It Maximises Gas Exchange
Many gill filaments and lamellaeProvides a very large surface area for diffusion of O2 and CO2
Lamellae are very thin (one cell thick epithelium)Short diffusion distance — gases cross quickly by diffusion
Dense network of capillaries in each lamellaRich blood supply maintains a steep concentration gradient; carries O2 away and brings CO2

Exam Tip: In dissection questions, always link structure to function. For any gas exchange surface, mention: (1) large surface area, (2) thin barrier/short diffusion distance, (3) mechanism to maintain concentration gradient (ventilation + blood supply). For fish specifically, explain countercurrent flow: because blood flows in the opposite direction to water, there is always a diffusion gradient at every point along the lamella — this is far more efficient than if they flowed in the same direction (parallel flow), where equilibrium would be reached partway along.

RP6: Aseptic Technique & Antimicrobial Substances

Aim: Use aseptic technique to investigate the effect of antimicrobial substances (antibiotics or plant extracts) on bacterial growth.

Biology link: Infectious disease and antibiotics (Topic 3/6). Antibiotic resistance is a major global health concern. This practical demonstrates how antibiotics inhibit bacterial growth and how their effectiveness can be compared quantitatively.

Apparatus

  • Nutrient agar plates (pre-poured and sterilised by autoclaving)
  • Broth culture of bacteria (e.g. non-pathogenic Escherichia coli or Micrococcus luteus)
  • Antibiotic discs (multidiscs or individual, e.g. penicillin, streptomycin, tetracycline) and/or plant extract soaked filter paper discs (e.g. garlic, mint, tea tree oil)
  • Sterile inoculating loop or sterile spreader, Bunsen burner
  • Forceps (to be flamed), marker pen (for labelling base of plate), adhesive tape, ruler, calculator

Aseptic Technique — Key Steps

  • Work near a lit Bunsen burner — the updraft creates a convection current that carries airborne microorganisms upward, away from the work area (creating an aseptic zone).
  • Flame the inoculating loop until red hot before and after use — this sterilises it by killing all microorganisms.
  • Flame the neck of culture bottles briefly after opening and before closing — kills microorganisms at the rim.
  • Do not breathe on or cough over open plates or cultures.
  • Keep lids on plates as much as possible; only open when necessary.
  • Wipe down the bench with disinfectant before and after the practical.

Method

  1. Flame the inoculating loop, allow to cool briefly, dip into bacterial broth culture, and spread evenly across the agar plate surface using a zigzag pattern (streak plate method) or use a sterile glass spreader.
  2. Using flamed forceps, place antibiotic discs (or plant extract discs) at equal distances on the agar surface. Press gently to ensure contact with the agar.
  3. Seal the plate with two strips of adhesive tape in a cross pattern (do NOT seal completely around the edge — this could create anaerobic conditions inside the plate, promoting the growth of dangerous anaerobic pathogens).
  4. Label the base of the plate (not the lid — lids can be accidentally swapped) with name, date, and disc identities.
  5. Incubate the plate upside down (prevents condensation dripping onto colonies, which could spread bacteria and blur zones) at a maximum of 25°C for 24-48 hours.
  6. After incubation, measure the diameter of each clear zone of inhibition (the area around each disc where bacteria have not grown) using a ruler. Do NOT open the plate.

Safety

  • Incubate at ≤25°C in school laboratories (not at 37°C — human body temperature would encourage growth of human pathogens that may have contaminated the plate).
  • Do not open plates after incubation — unknown organisms may have grown.
  • Autoclave all plates (121°C, 15 min) before disposal to kill all microorganisms.
  • Maintain strict aseptic technique throughout to prevent contamination of the plate AND to prevent release of microorganisms into the laboratory.
  • Wash hands with antibacterial soap before and after the practical.

Analysis

  • Measure the diameter of each zone of inhibition (in mm). Take two measurements at right angles and average.
  • Calculate the area of each zone: A = πr2 (where r = diameter / 2). Always use area, not diameter, for comparison — area is proportional to the amount of antibiotic that has diffused, while diameter is not (area increases with the square of the radius).
  • Larger zone of inhibition = more effective antimicrobial substance (inhibits bacterial growth over a wider area).
  • Subtract the area of the disc itself from the total zone area for a more accurate comparison.

Exam Tip: Two key points examiners look for: (1) Always calculate the area of the zone of inhibition (πr2), not just the diameter — marks are specifically allocated for this. (2) Know why plates are incubated at ≤25°C in schools — to reduce the risk of growing human pathogens (which thrive at 37°C). In industrial/hospital labs, plates ARE incubated at 37°C because facilities are appropriate for handling pathogens safely.

RP7: Chromatography of Leaf Pigments

Aim: Separate and identify photosynthetic pigments from a leaf extract using thin-layer or paper chromatography.

Biology link: Photosynthesis (Topic 5). Chloroplasts contain multiple pigments that absorb different wavelengths of light — this broadens the range of light energy that can be captured. Separating them demonstrates that leaves contain a mixture of pigments, not just chlorophyll.

Apparatus

  • Fresh green leaves (spinach, nettle, or grass), pestle and mortar, clean sand (abrasive), small volume of propanone (acetone)
  • Chromatography paper (or TLC plate with silica gel), pencil (NOT pen), ruler
  • Boiling tube or chromatography jar with tight-fitting lid, capillary tube or micropipette
  • Running solvent: propanone (acetone) or petroleum ether : acetone (9:1) mix

Method

  1. Grind leaves with a small amount of clean sand (to break open cells and chloroplasts) and a few cm3 of propanone in a mortar. The sand acts as an abrasive. Filter the deep green extract through muslin or filter paper.
  2. Draw a pencil line (origin line) 2 cm from the bottom of the chromatography paper. Use pencil because ink would dissolve in the solvent and interfere with results.
  3. Apply leaf extract to the origin line using a capillary tube — build up a concentrated spot by applying small amounts repeatedly, allowing to dry between applications (5-10 applications).
  4. Place the paper in the jar with the solvent level below the origin line (so the spot is above the solvent — otherwise pigments would dissolve directly into the solvent pool rather than being carried up by capillary action). Seal the jar with a lid to prevent solvent evaporation.
  5. Allow the solvent to rise by capillary action until it is approximately 1-2 cm from the top. Mark the solvent front immediately with pencil before it evaporates.
  6. Remove the paper and allow it to dry. Measure the distance from the origin line to the centre of each pigment spot, and the distance from the origin to the solvent front.

Safety

  • Propanone (acetone) is highly flammable and produces irritating fumes — NO naked flames near the experiment; work in a well-ventilated area or fume cupboard.
  • Petroleum ether is extremely flammable — same precautions.
  • Avoid prolonged skin contact with solvents — wear nitrile gloves.

Analysis

Rf = distance moved by pigment from origin / distance moved by solvent front from origin
  • Calculate Rf value for each separated pigment. Compare to reference/published Rf values to identify each pigment.
  • Typical order of separation (highest Rf = most soluble in the non-polar solvent, travels furthest):
PigmentColourApproximate RfSolubility

XanthophyllYellow~0.7Moderately soluble

Exam Tip: Carotene has the highest Rf because it is the most non-polar pigment and therefore most soluble in the non-polar organic solvent. Chlorophyll b has the lowest Rf because it is the most polar (it has more polar -CHO groups compared to chlorophyll a's -CH3 group) and is more attracted to the polar stationary phase (paper/silica). Rf values are specific to a given solvent system and temperature — always state the solvent used.

RP8: Effect of a Variable on Respiration Rate (Respirometer)

Aim: Investigate the effect of a variable (temperature, type of substrate, or organism) on the rate of aerobic respiration, using a respirometer to measure O2 consumption.

Biology link: Respiration (Topic 5). All living organisms respire continuously. The rate of O2 consumption is a direct measure of aerobic respiration rate. Temperature affects enzyme activity (respiratory enzymes in glycolysis, Krebs cycle, and ETC).

Apparatus

  • Simple respirometer (two tubes connected to a capillary tube/manometer with a scale)
  • Organisms: germinating seeds (e.g. peas, mung beans), small invertebrates (e.g. woodlice, blowfly larvae), or yeast suspension
  • Soda lime or potassium hydroxide pellets (to absorb CO2 produced by respiration)
  • Capillary tube with graduated scale, coloured oil or fluid drop (indicator of gas volume change)
  • Water bath (for temperature control), thermometer, stopwatch
  • Glass beads (for the control tube — same volume as organisms, non-living)

How the Respirometer Works

The key principle: soda lime (or KOH) absorbs all CO2 produced by the organisms. Therefore, the only gas change is the consumption of O2. As O2 is used up, the gas volume in the sealed tube decreases, and the coloured fluid drop moves towards the organisms. The distance moved per unit time = rate of O2 uptake = rate of aerobic respiration.

The control tube (containing glass beads + soda lime, but no living organisms) accounts for any changes in gas volume due to temperature or atmospheric pressure changes that are NOT due to respiration.

Method

  1. Set up the respirometer: place organisms (e.g. germinating seeds) in one tube with a layer of soda lime at the base (separated from organisms by cotton wool to prevent direct contact). Set up a control tube with glass beads of equal volume and soda lime.
  2. Seal both tubes and connect them to the manometer/capillary tube via airtight connections (use Vaseline on joints to prevent leaks).
  3. Place in a water bath at the desired temperature. Allow 5 minutes to equilibrate (temperature stabilisation and organisms to settle).
  4. Introduce a coloured fluid drop (or oil droplet) into the capillary tube using a syringe.
  5. Record the distance the fluid drop moves towards the organisms every minute for 10-15 minutes.
  6. Repeat at different temperatures (e.g. 15, 20, 25, 30, 35, 40°C). Calculate rate of O2 uptake (mm min-1 or mm3 min-1 if capillary tube cross-section is known).

Safety

  • Soda lime and KOH are corrosive — wear gloves and eye protection. Avoid direct contact with organisms.
  • Handle organisms ethically — return woodlice to their habitat after the experiment; do not expose to extreme temperatures.
  • Do not heat organisms above 40°C or below 5°C.

Analysis

  • Rate of O2 consumption = distance moved by fluid drop / time (mm min-1).
  • Plot rate of O2 uptake (y-axis) against temperature (x-axis). Expect a general increase with temperature (Q10 effect on enzyme activity) up to an optimum, then a decrease at high temperatures (enzyme denaturation).
  • The control tube corrects for any volume changes due to atmospheric pressure and temperature fluctuations. If the control drop moves, subtract this from the experimental reading.

Soda lime exhausted or insufficientCO2 not fully absorbed; gas volume change is less than expected (CO2 partially replaces O2)Use fresh, excess soda lime for each experiment

Exam Tip: The soda lime absorbs CO2, so only O2 consumption causes the gas volume change. Without soda lime, the CO2 produced would partially replace the O2 consumed, and the volume change would be much smaller (depending on the RQ of the substrate). The control tube is essential — always explain its purpose: it accounts for any volume changes due to factors other than respiration (atmospheric pressure changes, temperature fluctuations). To measure anaerobic respiration, you would remove the soda lime and measure CO2 production instead.

RP9: Choice Chamber (Animal Responses to Stimuli)

Aim: Investigate the effect of an environmental factor (light/dark, humidity) on the distribution and movement of small invertebrates (e.g. woodlice, maggots).

Biology link: Organisms respond to stimuli (Topic 6). Woodlice show kinesis (non-directional response — they change their speed or rate of turning in response to stimulus intensity) rather than taxis (directional movement towards or away from a stimulus). They tend to move slower and turn less in favourable conditions (dark, humid), accumulating there over time.

Apparatus

  • Choice chamber (circular or rectangular, divided into 2-4 compartments with a perforated divider and clear lid)
  • 10-20 woodlice (collected humanely from under logs, stones, or leaf litter)
  • Damp cotton wool (for humid conditions) and dry silica gel / anhydrous calcium chloride (desiccant, for dry conditions)
  • Black card or aluminium foil (for dark conditions), lamp (for light conditions)
  • Stopwatch, data recording sheet

Method

  1. Set up two contrasting environments in the choice chamber. Test only one variable at a time:
    • For humidity: one side has damp cotton wool (humid), the other has dry silica gel (dry) — both kept at the same light level
    • For light: one side is covered with black card (dark), the other is exposed to a lamp (light) — both kept at the same humidity
  2. Place 10 woodlice in the centre of the chamber (on the divider), ensuring they have equal access to both sides.
  3. Replace the lid and start the timer immediately.
  4. Count the number of woodlice on each side every 2 minutes for 20-30 minutes. Record in a table.
  5. Repeat the entire experiment at least 3 times with different woodlice. Calculate mean number on each side at each time interval.

Safety and Ethics

  • Handle woodlice gently — they are living organisms deserving ethical treatment.
  • Return all woodlice to their original habitat after the experiment.
  • Do not expose to extreme conditions (very hot, direct strong light) for extended periods.
  • Wash hands after handling. Silica gel can be irritating — avoid direct skin contact.

Analysis

  • Calculate the mean number of woodlice on each side at each time interval across all repeats.
  • Woodlice show kinesis: they move more slowly and turn less in preferred conditions (dark, humid), so they accumulate in the dark/humid side over time — not because they are attracted to it, but because they spend more time there once they arrive.
  • Use a chi-squared test to determine if the final distribution is significantly different from the 50:50 distribution expected if woodlice showed no preference (null hypothesis: no significant difference from a 1:1 distribution).

Sources of Error and Improvements

ErrorEffectImprovement
Woodlice on the divider/boundaryDifficult to assign to one sideDecide a consistent rule beforehand (e.g. assign by head position); exclude borderline individuals from count
Two variables changed at once (e.g. dark AND humid on same side)Cannot determine which factor causes the preferenceOnly vary ONE factor per experiment; keep all other conditions identical
Small sample size / few repeatsResults may not be statistically significant or representativeUse at least 10 woodlice per trial; repeat at least 3 times; pool data from class
Temperature differences between sidesConfounding variable — woodlice may respond to temperature, not the intended variableEnsure both sides are at the same temperature (no lamp heat on one side only — use a distant lamp or LED)
Organisms stressed by handlingErratic initial behaviour unrelated to the variableAllow a 2-minute settling period before starting to count
Exam Tip: Woodlice show kinesis, not taxis. Kinesis is a non-directional response where the organism changes its speed of movement or rate of turning depending on stimulus intensity. In unfavourable conditions (light, dry), they move faster and turn more frequently (increasing the chance of finding better conditions). In favourable conditions (dark, humid), they slow down and turn less — so they accumulate there. This is different from taxis, which is a directional movement towards or away from a stimulus (e.g. positive phototaxis in Euglena).

RP10: Distribution of Organisms (Quadrats & Transects)

Aim: Investigate the distribution of organisms in a habitat and the effect of an abiotic factor on their distribution, using appropriate sampling techniques.

Biology link: Ecology and biodiversity (Topics 4 and 7). The distribution of organisms is influenced by both abiotic factors (light, temperature, soil pH, moisture, exposure) and biotic factors (competition, predation). Sampling must be done appropriately to give a representative picture of the community.

Apparatus

  • Quadrats (e.g. 0.5 m x 0.5 m frame, possibly subdivided into 25 smaller squares for % cover estimation)
  • Tape measure (30 m or longer) for transect line
  • Random number generator or random number table (for random quadrat placement)
  • Species identification keys, recording sheets, pencil
  • Abiotic measurement tools: light meter, soil pH probe/meter or BDH soil test kit, soil moisture meter, thermometer, anemometer (wind speed)

Two Sampling Approaches

MethodWhen to UseHow
Random quadrat samplingEstimating the abundance/density of species in a uniform habitat (e.g. a field)Use random number pairs as x,y coordinates to position each quadrat. This avoids sampling bias. Count organisms or estimate % cover in each quadrat. Repeat many times (at least 10-20).
Systematic sampling along a transectStudying changes in species distribution along an environmental gradient (e.g. from seashore to cliff top, from woodland edge to interior, from path to field centre)Lay a tape measure along the gradient. Place quadrats at regular intervals (e.g. every 2 metres). Record species and abundance + abiotic factors at each position.

Method (Belt Transect Example)

  1. Lay a tape measure in a straight line across the habitat, along the environmental gradient of interest (e.g. from the high water mark up a rocky shore).
  2. Place a quadrat at regular intervals along the transect (e.g. every 2 metres, or every 5 metres for longer transects).
  3. At each quadrat position:
    • Identify and record all species present (use identification keys)
    • Estimate % cover for each plant species (or count individuals for animals)
    • Measure relevant abiotic factors at the same point (light intensity, soil pH, soil moisture, temperature, wind speed)
  4. Record all data in a prepared table. Repeat the transect in parallel (if possible) for reliability.

Safety

  • Conduct a risk assessment for fieldwork: assess terrain (uneven ground, cliffs, slippery rocks), weather conditions, tidal risks (on a seashore), and lone working policy.
  • Work in pairs or groups. Carry a mobile phone. Inform someone of your location and expected return time.
  • Wash hands after handling soil (may contain tetanus bacteria, Weil's disease from rat urine).
  • Be aware of hazardous plants (stinging nettles, thorns, giant hogweed) and animals (ticks, adders).
  • Wear appropriate clothing and footwear for the habitat.

Analysis

  • Calculate species density = mean count per quadrat / quadrat area (number per m2).
  • Calculate % frequency = (number of quadrats containing the species / total number of quadrats) x 100.
  • Use a kite diagram or bar chart to display species distribution along a transect.
  • Calculate Simpson's Diversity Index: D = 1 - Σ(n/N)2.
  • Use Spearman's rank correlation to test for a significant correlation between species abundance and an abiotic factor (e.g. "Is there a significant correlation between % cover of a plant species and light intensity?").
  • Spearman's rs ranges from -1 (perfect negative correlation) through 0 (no correlation) to +1 (perfect positive correlation). Compare rs to the critical value for your sample size at p = 0.05.

Sources of Error and Improvements

ErrorEffectImprovement
Non-random placement of quadrats (e.g. choosing "interesting" spots)Sampling bias — results not representative of the whole habitatUse a random number table/generator for coordinates; place quadrats without looking
Too few quadratsUnrepresentative data; poor statistical powerUse at least 10-20 quadrats per area; pool class data
Misidentification of speciesInaccurate species count and diversity calculationUse reliable identification keys; take photographs for verification; have an expert check
Seasonal/time-of-day variationDifferent results at different timesSample at the same time of day and same season for valid comparisons
Trampling or disturbing the habitatAlters the community being studiedApproach carefully; use a random walk pattern; minimise time in each area
Exam Tip: Random sampling avoids bias — always explain how you would achieve randomness (random number coordinates from a baseline and side-line). For transects, explain why systematic sampling is appropriate: you want to detect changes in distribution along a gradient, which random sampling might miss. Know when to use each statistical test: chi-squared for comparing observed vs expected frequencies (categories); Spearman's rank correlation for testing the relationship between two continuous variables (e.g. species abundance vs distance along transect); Student's t-test for comparing means of two groups.

RP11: Effect of a Variable on Organism Development

Aim: Investigate the effect of an environmental variable (light intensity, temperature, water availability, mineral ions) on the growth and development of plants (seed germination and seedling growth).

Biology link: Plant responses and growth (Topics 5/6). Light, temperature, water, and mineral ions all affect plant growth. This practical also tests understanding of experimental design, control of variables, and statistical analysis.

Apparatus

  • Seeds (e.g. cress, mustard, or mung bean — fast-growing species are best)
  • Petri dishes or seed trays, cotton wool or vermiculite, distilled water
  • Light source (lamp at different distances or different wattages), or dark cupboard, or coloured filters
  • Ruler (mm), labels, marker pen, thermometer
  • Incubator or windowsill locations at different temperatures (if investigating temperature)

Method

  1. Place equal numbers of seeds (e.g. 10-20) on damp cotton wool in each labelled Petri dish.
  2. Set up different conditions for the independent variable being tested (e.g. different light intensities by varying distance from lamp, or different temperatures, or different concentrations of mineral solution).
  3. Keep all other variables constant: same number of seeds per dish, same volume of water added, same temperature (if not the variable), same seed batch, same type of cotton wool.
  4. Record observations daily for 10-14 days:
    • Germination percentage = (number germinated / total seeds) x 100
    • Shoot height (mm) — measure from base to tip of tallest shoot
    • Number of leaves, root length, fresh/dry mass (if appropriate)
  5. Set up at least 3 replicates for each condition and calculate mean values.

Safety

  • Some seeds may be treated with fungicide — wash hands after handling.
  • Some seeds are toxic if ingested — do not eat any experimental material.
  • Avoid inhalation of vermiculite dust if used as a growing medium.
  • Mould may grow on damp cotton wool — dispose of mouldy dishes appropriately.

Analysis

  • Calculate germination percentage at each time point for each condition.
  • Plot mean shoot height (y-axis) against time (x-axis) for each condition on the same axes for comparison.
  • Calculate mean and standard deviation for each condition — standard deviation indicates the spread/variability of the data. Plot error bars on graphs.
  • Use a Student's t-test (for two conditions) or one-way ANOVA (for multiple conditions) to determine if differences in mean shoot height between conditions are statistically significant.
  • Alternatively, use the Mann-Whitney U test if data are not normally distributed.

Sources of Error and Improvements

ErrorEffectImprovement
Variable seed viabilitySome seeds may not germinate regardless of conditionsUse seeds from the same packet/batch; pre-test a sample for viability
Unequal wateringConfounding variable — water availability affects growth independentlyMeasure exact volume of water added to each dish using a graduated syringe
Temperature differences between locationsConfounding variable if temperature is not the independent variableKeep all dishes at the same temperature (e.g. same incubator or room); monitor with thermometer
Fungal contaminationSeeds or seedlings may die; reduces sample sizeUse clean equipment; sterilise cotton wool if possible; replace contaminated dishes
Uneven light exposure within a dishSeedlings on the edge may receive different light from those in the centreRotate dishes daily; use diffused light source
Exam Tip: Always clearly identify the independent variable (what you are changing), dependent variable (what you are measuring), and control variables (what you keep constant). Use a large sample size (many seeds per dish, many replicates) to improve reliability. State a specific statistical test you could use to analyse the results and explain what it tests (e.g. "I would use a Student's t-test to determine if there is a significant difference in mean shoot height between the two light conditions").

RP12: Immobilised Enzyme Investigation

Aim: Investigate the use of immobilised enzymes (e.g. lactase immobilised in alginate beads) and compare their effectiveness to free enzymes.

Biology link: Enzyme technology and biotechnology (Topic 8). Immobilised enzymes are widely used in industry because they can be reused, are more stable, and do not contaminate the product. This practical demonstrates enzyme immobilisation by entrapment in alginate beads and its application in producing lactose-free milk.

Apparatus

  • Sodium alginate solution (2-3%), calcium chloride solution (1-2%)
  • Enzyme: lactase solution (or other enzyme e.g. catalase, amylase)
  • Syringe (10 cm3, without needle) or pipette for forming beads
  • Beakers, sieve or tea strainer, glass wool
  • Syringe barrel (10-20 cm3) as a column, clamp stand
  • Substrate: full-fat or semi-skimmed milk (contains lactose)
  • Glucose test strips (clinistix) or glucose oxidase strips for quantitative measurement; or Benedict's reagent + colorimeter

Method

  1. Making the beads: Mix equal volumes of enzyme solution and sodium alginate solution thoroughly (the enzyme is trapped within the alginate matrix).
  2. Draw the mixture into a syringe. Slowly drip the mixture from the syringe into a beaker of calcium chloride solution. The alginate reacts with Ca2+ ions to form insoluble calcium alginate — creating firm, spherical beads with enzyme trapped inside.
  3. Leave the beads in CaCl2 for 5-10 minutes to harden fully. Rinse with distilled water.
  4. Setting up the column: Place a small piece of glass wool at the bottom of a syringe barrel (to retain the beads). Pack the beads into the column. Clamp the column vertically.
  5. Running the experiment: Pour milk (substrate) slowly through the column. Collect the liquid emerging from the bottom (the product).
  6. Test the collected product for glucose using glucose test strips or quantitative Benedict's test (glucose is a product of lactose hydrolysis: lactose → glucose + galactose).
  7. Compare the product from immobilised enzyme with a control using free (dissolved) lactase mixed directly with milk for the same duration.
  8. Investigate reusability: pass fresh milk through the column multiple times and test the product each time. Compare glucose concentration across successive runs.

Safety

  • Calcium chloride is an irritant — wear eye protection and avoid skin contact.
  • Wash hands after handling enzymes and chemicals.
  • Do not consume any products — they are not food-safe laboratory preparations.

Analysis

  • Compare glucose concentration in the product from immobilised vs free enzyme — both should convert lactose to glucose, but the immobilised enzyme may give a lower initial conversion rate (substrate must diffuse through the alginate to reach the enzyme).
  • Test reusability: pass substrate through the column 5+ times. The immobilised enzyme should still be active after multiple uses, while a new batch of free enzyme would be needed each time.
  • Investigate the effect of temperature on immobilised vs free enzyme: immobilised enzymes are often more thermally stable because the alginate matrix restricts molecular movement, reducing the chance of denaturation.

Exam Tip: Be able to state the advantages of immobilised enzymes over free enzymes: (1) they can be reused, reducing costs; (2) the product is not contaminated with enzyme; (3) they are often more thermally stable; (4) they allow continuous-flow processing. The most common industrial example is immobilised lactase used to produce lactose-free dairy products for people with lactose intolerance. Other examples: immobilised glucose isomerase (converts glucose to fructose for "high-fructose corn syrup"), immobilised penicillin acylase (modifies penicillin to produce semi-synthetic antibiotics).
