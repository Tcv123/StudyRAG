T8 — Control of Gene Expression

8.1 Gene Expression and Regulation

Gene expression: The process by which the genetic information encoded in DNA is used to produce a functional product (usually a protein via transcription and translation). Not all genes are expressed in all cells at all times — differential gene expression determines cell specialisation, development, and responses to environmental signals. Every cell in an organism contains the same genome, but different cells express different subsets of genes, producing different proteomes.

Levels of Gene Expression Control

Gene expression can be regulated at multiple points in the pathway from DNA to functional protein:

LevelMechanismExamples
Transcriptional (most common and important control point)Whether or not a gene is transcribed into mRNA — controlled by transcription factors, epigenetic modificationsTranscription factors, promoter methylation, histone modification, the lac operon
Post-transcriptionalmRNA processing, stability, and transport — controls which mRNAs survive and reach ribosomesAlternative splicing (different exon combinations), mRNA degradation rate, RNA interference (RNAi)
TranslationalWhether mRNA is translated into protein — controls rate and timing of translationRNA interference (siRNA/miRNA blocking ribosome binding), initiation factor availability
Post-translationalProtein modification, activation, targeting, or degradation after synthesisPhosphorylation, glycosylation, proteolytic cleavage (e.g. proinsulin to insulin), ubiquitin-mediated degradation

Transcription Factors

Transcription factor: A protein that binds to specific DNA sequences (regulatory regions) and controls the rate of transcription of a target gene. Activators increase transcription; repressors decrease or prevent transcription. Transcription factors are essential for differential gene expression — different cell types express different transcription factors, which switch on cell-type-specific genes.

How transcription factors work:

  • Activators bind to enhancer regions (which may be thousands of base pairs from the promoter). They interact with mediator proteins to help RNA polymerase bind to the promoter, increasing transcription rate. DNA looping brings enhancer-bound activators close to the promoter.
  • Repressors bind to silencer regions or directly to the promoter/operator, physically blocking RNA polymerase binding and preventing transcription.
  • Some transcription factors are activated by signalling molecules:
    • Steroid hormones (e.g. oestrogen, testosterone) are lipid-soluble, so they cross the cell membrane and bind to intracellular receptors in the cytoplasm or nucleus. The hormone-receptor complex acts directly as a transcription factor, entering the nucleus and binding to specific DNA response elements to activate target genes. Example: oestrogen activates genes involved in female sexual development and the menstrual cycle.
    • Non-steroid hormones (e.g. insulin, glucagon) are water-soluble and cannot cross the membrane, so they use second messenger systems (e.g. cAMP) that ultimately activate transcription factors inside the cell.

The Lac Operon — Gene Regulation in Prokaryotes

The lac operon in E. coli is a classic example of an inducible gene system — the structural genes are normally switched OFF and only switched ON when their product is needed (i.e. when lactose is present as a food source).

Components of the lac operon:

  • Regulatory gene (lacI): Constitutively expressed; codes for the repressor protein
  • Promoter: Where RNA polymerase binds to begin transcription
  • Operator: A short DNA sequence between the promoter and structural genes. The repressor protein binds here to block transcription.
  • Structural genes (lacZ, lacY, lacA): Code for enzymes needed to metabolise lactose:
    • lacZ = beta-galactosidase (hydrolyses lactose into glucose + galactose)
    • lacY = permease (transports lactose into the cell)
    • lacA = transacetylase

Mechanism:

  • No lactose: The repressor protein (from lacI gene) has a shape complementary to the operator. It binds tightly to the operator, physically blocking RNA polymerase from transcribing the structural genes. Genes are OFF.
  • Lactose present: Lactose (the inducer) binds to an allosteric site on the repressor protein, changing its shape (allosteric inhibition). The repressor can no longer bind to the operator. RNA polymerase can now access the structural genes and transcription proceeds. Genes are ON. The enzymes are produced, lactose is metabolised, and when lactose runs out, the repressor returns to its original shape and re-binds the operator — genes switch OFF again.
Exam Tip: The lac operon is a negative control system — the default is OFF (repressor blocks transcription) and the inducer (lactose) removes the repression. This is efficient because the cell only produces lactose-metabolising enzymes when lactose is available — saving energy and resources. Be able to explain each component's role and what happens when each component is mutated (e.g. a mutant repressor that cannot bind lactose = genes permanently OFF even when lactose is present).

Epigenetics

Epigenetics: Heritable changes in gene expression that do not involve changes to the DNA base sequence itself. These modifications affect the accessibility of DNA to transcription machinery, effectively switching genes "on" or "off." Epigenetic modifications can be influenced by environmental factors and may be passed to daughter cells during mitosis and sometimes to offspring across generations.

1. DNA Methylation

  • Methyl groups (-CH3) are added to cytosine bases, usually in CpG islands (regions rich in CG dinucleotides, often near gene promoters). Catalysed by DNA methyltransferases.
  • Methylation typically silences (switches off) gene expression by: (1) directly preventing transcription factor binding; (2) attracting methyl-CpG-binding proteins that recruit histone deacetylases, leading to chromatin condensation.
  • Hypermethylation of tumour suppressor gene promoters can silence them, contributing to cancer development (cell division is no longer properly controlled).
  • Hypomethylation of oncogene promoters can activate them, also contributing to cancer.
  • Methylation patterns are copied during DNA replication (maintenance methyltransferases), so they are heritable through cell division.
  • Patterns can sometimes be inherited across generations (transgenerational epigenetic inheritance), though this is more clearly demonstrated in plants than in mammals.

2. Histone Modification

  • Histones can be chemically modified by the addition or removal of acetyl, methyl, or phosphate groups to their "tails" (N-terminal amino acid chains).
  • Histone acetylation (adding acetyl groups, catalysed by histone acetyltransferases, HATs):
    • Reduces the positive charge on histone tails (neutralises lysine residues)
    • Weakens the electrostatic attraction between positively charged histones and negatively charged DNA (phosphate backbone)
    • DNA wraps less tightly around histones → chromatin is more open (euchromatin)
    • Transcription factors and RNA polymerase can access DNA → transcription is promoted
  • Histone deacetylation (removing acetyl groups, catalysed by histone deacetylases, HDACs):
    • Restores positive charge → tighter DNA-histone binding
    • Condensed chromatin (heterochromatin) → genes are silenced
  • Histone methylation can either activate or silence genes depending on which amino acid is methylated and how many methyl groups are added.

Environmental factors that can cause epigenetic changes include: diet (e.g. folate, methyl donors), stress, smoking, toxins (e.g. BPA), exercise, and ageing. These changes may explain why identical twins (monozygotic — same DNA) can develop different phenotypes, disease susceptibilities, and even different appearances as they age, despite having the same base sequence.

RNA Interference (RNAi)

RNA interference: A mechanism of post-transcriptional gene silencing in which small double-stranded RNA molecules (siRNA or miRNA) cause degradation or translational inhibition of complementary mRNA, preventing protein synthesis from that mRNA.

Mechanism of RNAi:

  1. Double-stranded RNA (dsRNA) — either from a virus, transposon, or artificially introduced — is processed by the enzyme Dicer into short fragments of ~21-23 nucleotides called small interfering RNA (siRNA).
  2. The siRNA double strand is separated; one strand (the guide strand) is incorporated into the RISC (RNA-Induced Silencing Complex) protein complex.
  3. The guide strand in RISC base-pairs with complementary mRNA by complementary base pairing.
  4. RISC either: (a) cleaves (cuts) the mRNA using an Argonaute protein (leading to mRNA degradation by cellular nucleases); or (b) blocks translation by preventing ribosome binding/progression.
  5. The target gene is effectively silenced — its mRNA is destroyed or blocked, so no protein is produced from it.

Significance of RNAi:

  • Natural defence against viruses: Many viruses produce dsRNA during replication; RNAi destroys it
  • Regulation of development: miRNAs (microRNAs, encoded by the organism's own genome) regulate gene expression during normal development and cell differentiation
  • Transposon silencing: Prevents "jumping genes" from inserting into essential genes
  • Therapeutic potential: siRNA can be designed to silence specific disease-causing genes (e.g. oncogenes in cancer, viral genes in infection, mutant huntingtin in Huntington disease). FDA-approved RNAi drugs include patisiran (for hereditary transthyretin amyloidosis).
  • Research tool: Gene knockdown experiments — scientists synthesise siRNA complementary to a gene of interest, introduce it into cells, and observe the effect of silencing that gene to determine its function.
Exam Tip: Epigenetics is a common topic for extended response questions. Always emphasise these key points: (1) epigenetic changes are heritable but reversible; (2) they do not alter the DNA base sequence itself; (3) they can be influenced by environmental factors; (4) they involve DNA methylation and histone modification; (5) they explain how genetically identical cells become different cell types, and why identical twins can develop different phenotypes. Link to real examples like cancer (tumour suppressor gene silencing) and twin studies.

8.2 Gene Technology

Restriction Enzymes (Restriction Endonucleases)

Restriction enzyme: An enzyme (originally from bacteria, where they defend against bacteriophage DNA) that cuts DNA at specific palindromic recognition sequences (restriction sites), typically 4-8 base pairs long. Some produce sticky ends (short single-stranded overhangs) and others produce blunt ends.

Sticky ends are preferable in genetic engineering because:

  • They have short, unpaired base sequences that can form complementary base pairs with other sticky ends produced by the same restriction enzyme
  • This allows DNA fragments from different sources to anneal (join) by hydrogen bonding
  • DNA ligase then seals the sugar-phosphate backbone, forming phosphodiester bonds to create a stable recombinant DNA molecule

Example: EcoRI recognises GAATTC and cuts between G and A on each strand, producing sticky ends with 4-base overhangs (AATT).

PCR (Polymerase Chain Reaction)

PCR: A technique for rapidly amplifying (making millions of copies of) a specific DNA sequence in vitro. It uses thermal cycling with a heat-stable DNA polymerase (Taq polymerase from Thermus aquaticus, a thermophilic bacterium found in hot springs).

Requirements: Template DNA, Taq polymerase, two specific DNA primers (short single-stranded sequences complementary to regions flanking the target), free deoxyribonucleotide triphosphates (dNTPs: dATP, dTTP, dCTP, dGTP), buffer solution with Mg2+ ions (cofactor for Taq).

Steps in Each PCR Cycle (repeated 25-30 times)

  1. Denaturation (94-96°C, ~1 min): High temperature breaks hydrogen bonds between complementary strands, separating double-stranded DNA into two single strands. (Taq polymerase is not denatured at this temperature because it evolved in a thermophilic organism.)
  2. Annealing (50-65°C, ~1 min): Temperature is lowered to allow short DNA primers to bind (anneal) to their complementary sequences flanking the target region on each strand. The lower temperature favours primer-template binding. Two different primers are used — one for each strand.
  3. Extension/Elongation (72°C, ~1-2 min): Taq polymerase extends the primers by adding free nucleotides complementary to the template strand, working in the 5' to 3' direction. 72°C is the optimum temperature for Taq polymerase activity.
Number of DNA copies after n cycles = 2n (exponential amplification)
After 30 cycles: 230 ≈ 1 billion copies from a single template molecule

Applications of PCR: forensic DNA profiling (amplifying tiny samples from crime scenes), genetic testing for hereditary diseases, prenatal diagnosis, paternity testing, ancient DNA analysis (e.g. Neanderthal genome), detection of pathogens (diagnostic PCR for HIV, COVID-19), research cloning of genes.

Gel Electrophoresis

Gel electrophoresis: A technique for separating DNA (or protein) fragments by size. Fragments are loaded into wells in an agarose or polyacrylamide gel, and an electric field is applied. DNA fragments are negatively charged (due to phosphate groups) and migrate towards the positive electrode (anode).

Key principles:

  • Smaller fragments travel faster and further through the gel matrix (less resistance)
  • Larger fragments travel shorter distances
  • Fragments are visualised using UV fluorescence after staining (ethidium bromide or SYBR Safe) or by autoradiography with radioactive/fluorescent probes
  • A DNA ladder (known fragment sizes) is run alongside samples to determine fragment sizes

Applications: DNA fingerprinting/profiling (comparing STR or VNTR patterns), checking PCR products, restriction mapping, diagnosing genetic diseases, forensic identification, paternity testing.

Genetic Engineering (Recombinant DNA Technology)

Genetic engineering involves isolating a gene from one organism and inserting it into another organism's DNA so that the gene is expressed in the host organism, producing the desired protein.

Steps in Genetic Engineering

  1. Isolation of the desired gene: Three main methods:
    • Restriction enzymes: Cut the gene from genomic DNA (includes introns)
    • Reverse transcriptase: Uses mature mRNA as a template to produce complementary DNA (cDNA) — this contains only exons (no introns), making it suitable for expression in prokaryotes (which cannot splice introns). DNA polymerase then makes the cDNA double-stranded.
    • Chemical synthesis (gene machine): Automated synthesis based on known amino acid sequence — the base sequence is worked out from the genetic code
  2. Insertion into a vector: The gene is ligated (using DNA ligase) into a vector (a carrier of DNA into a host cell). Common vectors: bacterial plasmids, bacteriophages, yeast artificial chromosomes (YACs). The vector is cut with the same restriction enzyme as the gene, producing complementary sticky ends. A promoter sequence (recognised by the host's RNA polymerase) must be included upstream of the gene to ensure expression.
  3. Transformation: The recombinant vector is introduced into the host cell. Methods:
    • Heat shock (42°C for 30-90 seconds makes E. coli membranes temporarily permeable)
    • Electroporation (brief high-voltage pulse creates temporary pores)
    • CaCl2 treatment (Ca2+ neutralises charge repulsion between DNA and membrane)
    • Viral vectors (for eukaryotic cells)
    • Gene gun / biolistics (for plant cells — DNA-coated gold particles shot into cells)
  4. Identification of transformed cells: Not all host cells take up the vector, and not all vectors contain the inserted gene. Selection methods:
    • Antibiotic resistance genes: The vector contains one or two antibiotic resistance genes. Cells grown on antibiotic-containing medium — only transformed cells survive.
    • Fluorescent markers: GFP (green fluorescent protein) gene on the vector — transformed cells glow under UV
    • Blue-white screening: If the gene is inserted into the lacZ gene on the plasmid, lacZ is disrupted. On X-gal medium: cells with intact lacZ produce blue colonies (no insert); cells with disrupted lacZ produce white colonies (gene successfully inserted).
  5. Growth and expression: Confirmed transformed cells are cultured on a large scale (in fermenters); the inserted gene is transcribed and translated, producing the desired protein, which is then extracted and purified.

Gene Therapy

Gene therapy: The introduction of a normal/functional copy of a gene into cells to treat a genetic disorder caused by a faulty or absent gene. It provides a working copy — it does not correct the defective gene itself (though gene editing with CRISPR is changing this).
FeatureSomatic Cell Gene TherapyGerm Line Gene Therapy
Target cellsBody (somatic) cells — e.g. lung epithelial cells in CF, bone marrow stem cellsGametes (eggs/sperm) or early embryo cells
Inherited?NOT inherited by offspring — only affects the treated individualIS inherited by all future generations
Ethical statusGenerally accepted — similar to conventional medicineHighly controversial and currently illegal in most countries (UK, EU)
ChallengesGene may not be expressed long-term; immune response to vector; may need repeated treatmentRisk of unintended changes passed to future generations; "designer baby" concerns; unpredictable effects

Vectors for gene therapy:

  • Viral vectors (adenoviruses, retroviruses, lentiviruses, AAV): Pathogenic genes removed and replaced with therapeutic gene. Efficient at entering cells and delivering DNA. Risks: immune response to viral proteins, random insertion into genome (may disrupt tumour suppressor genes or activate oncogenes — this occurred in early X-SCID gene therapy trials where some patients developed leukaemia).
  • Liposomes: Artificial lipid vesicles encapsulating DNA. Fuse with cell membranes to deliver DNA. Safer (no immune response) but much less efficient — DNA does not integrate into chromosomes well, so expression is often transient.

Example — Cystic Fibrosis: Attempts to deliver a functional CFTR gene (which codes for a chloride ion channel protein) to lung epithelial cells via adenoviral vectors or liposomes inhaled as an aerosol. Challenges: immune response destroys viral vectors on repeat administration; thick mucus in CF lungs reduces vector access to cells; expression is temporary (lung epithelial cells are replaced every few weeks); only a small proportion of cells are successfully transfected.

Exam Tip: Gene therapy currently works best for monogenic recessive disorders (caused by a single gene, need to add one working copy — e.g. CF, SCID, haemophilia). It is much harder for dominant conditions (need to silence the faulty allele, not just add a normal one) or polygenic conditions (multiple genes involved — e.g. heart disease, diabetes). The recent development of CRISPR-Cas9 gene editing offers the possibility of actually correcting mutations rather than just adding extra copies, which could address dominant conditions too.

8.3 Biotechnology

Using Microorganisms in Industry

Microorganisms are widely used in industrial biotechnology because they offer many advantages:

  • Rapid growth with short generation times (E. coli divides every ~20 minutes)
  • Can be genetically modified to produce specific human proteins
  • Can use cheap substrates (agricultural waste, molasses, whey)
  • Can be grown in fermenters under controlled conditions year-round, independent of climate
  • Relatively simple biochemistry; proteins can be secreted into the medium for easy extraction
  • Produce no ethical concerns compared to animal farming

Industrial fermenters are large vessels (up to hundreds of thousands of litres) in which microorganisms are cultured under carefully controlled conditions:

Immobilised Enzymes

Enzymes can be immobilised (attached to or trapped within an insoluble support) for industrial use:

  • Methods: Adsorption onto a surface (e.g. glass beads), covalent bonding to a support, entrapment in a gel matrix (e.g. alginate beads), encapsulation in a membrane
  • Advantages: Enzymes can be reused (reducing cost); product is not contaminated with enzyme; enzyme is more stable (less susceptible to temperature/pH changes due to restricted movement); continuous-flow processing is possible (substrate flows over immobilised enzyme column)
  • Disadvantages: Immobilisation may reduce enzyme activity (active site may be partially blocked); diffusion of substrate to active site is slower
  • Example: Immobilised lactase (used to produce lactose-free milk by passing milk over a column of lactase trapped in alginate beads)

DNA Sequencing and Bioinformatics

DNA sequencing: Determining the precise order of nucleotide bases (A, T, C, G) in a DNA molecule. Modern next-generation sequencing (NGS) technologies can sequence entire genomes rapidly and cheaply (a human genome can now be sequenced in hours for ~$200).

Applications of genome sequencing:

  • Identifying genes associated with genetic diseases (e.g. BRCA1/BRCA2 and breast/ovarian cancer risk)
  • Pharmacogenomics — personalised medicine tailored to individual genetic variation (e.g. choosing cancer drugs based on tumour mutations)
  • Comparative genomics — understanding evolutionary relationships; constructing phylogenetic trees
  • Identifying antibiotic resistance genes in pathogenic bacteria (guiding treatment choices)
  • Forensic identification (DNA profiling using STR analysis)
  • Ancient DNA analysis (Neanderthal and Denisovan genomes)
Bioinformatics: The use of computers, databases, and statistical algorithms to store, retrieve, and analyse large biological datasets — particularly DNA, RNA, and protein sequences. It is essential for making sense of the vast amounts of data generated by genome sequencing.

Key bioinformatics tools:

  • BLAST (Basic Local Alignment Search Tool) — compares a query DNA or protein sequence against databases to find similar sequences in other organisms
  • GenBank / EMBL / UniProt — public repositories of sequence data, freely accessible to researchers worldwide
  • Phylogenetic tree construction from multiple sequence alignment data
  • Protein structure prediction from amino acid sequences (e.g. AlphaFold)
  • Identifying conserved sequences (regions unchanged across species — indicating critical function preserved by natural selection)
