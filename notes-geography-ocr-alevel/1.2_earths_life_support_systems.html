1.2 Earth's Life Support Systems

This topic examines the water and carbon cycles as fundamental Earth systems that sustain life. It explores how these cycles operate at scales from local to global, how they are interconnected, and how human activity is disrupting their natural balance with profound consequences for climate, ecosystems, and human societies.

The Water Cycle (Hydrological Cycle)

Hydrological cycle: The continuous movement of water between the atmosphere, land surface, oceans, and subsurface stores, driven primarily by solar energy (evaporation) and gravity (precipitation, runoff, percolation). At the global scale, it is a closed system — no water is created or destroyed; the total amount of water on Earth remains constant (~1.4 billion km3). At the local/drainage basin scale, it operates as an open system with inputs (precipitation) and outputs (river discharge, evapotranspiration) crossing the system boundary (the watershed).

Use these statistics to support arguments about water security and resource conflicts.

Key Flows (Transfers and Transformations)

  • Evaporation: Liquid water converts to water vapour using solar energy (latent heat of vaporisation). Greatest over warm tropical oceans. Rate increases with temperature, wind speed, and low humidity. Global evaporation: ~505,000 km3/year
  • Transpiration: Water loss from plants through open stomata on leaf surfaces. A single large tree can transpire 100+ litres per day. Accounts for ~10% of atmospheric moisture. A major flux in forested areas
  • Evapotranspiration: The combined water loss from evaporation (from surfaces) and transpiration (from plants). Measured using lysimeters. Potential evapotranspiration (PET) is the amount that would occur if water were unlimited; actual evapotranspiration is often lower in arid areas
  • Condensation: Water vapour cools (reaches dew point) and converts to liquid droplets around condensation nuclei (dust, salt, pollen). Forms clouds, fog, and dew
  • Precipitation: Water falling from the atmosphere as rain, snow, sleet, or hail when cloud droplets grow heavy enough to fall. Global precipitation: ~505,000 km3/year (balanced with evaporation in the long term)
  • Interception: Precipitation caught by vegetation canopy or built surfaces before reaching the ground. Can account for 10–40% of rainfall in forested areas. Intercepted water evaporates directly back to the atmosphere or reaches the ground via stemflow or throughfall
  • Infiltration: Water soaking into the soil from the surface, driven by gravity and capillary action. Rate depends on soil porosity, permeability, antecedent moisture, vegetation cover, and land use. Infiltration capacity is the maximum rate at which soil can absorb water
  • Percolation: Deeper, slower movement of water through soil and rock into the groundwater store (aquifer). Occurs through permeable rocks like chalk, sandstone, and limestone
  • Throughflow: Lateral (sideways) movement of water through the soil layer towards rivers and streams. Slower than surface runoff but faster than groundwater flow. Often follows natural pipes and macropores in the soil
  • Groundwater flow: Very slow movement of water through saturated permeable rock (aquifer) towards rivers, lakes, or the sea. The water table marks the upper surface of the saturated zone. Groundwater feeds rivers as baseflow, maintaining river levels during dry periods
  • Surface runoff (overland flow): Water flowing over the ground surface directly into rivers. Occurs when rainfall intensity exceeds infiltration capacity (Hortonian overland flow) or when the soil is fully saturated (saturation overland flow). Fastest transfer — responds quickly to rainfall, contributing to flood peaks
  • Stemflow: Water running down plant stems and trunks to the ground after interception
  • Channel flow: Water flowing within a river channel towards the sea. Velocity depends on gradient, channel shape, roughness, and discharge. Measured in cumecs (m3/s)

Drainage Basin Hydrology

Drainage basin: The area of land drained by a river and all its tributaries. It is an open system bounded by a watershed (ridge of high land forming the boundary). Water enters as precipitation and leaves via the river mouth (discharge) and evapotranspiration. The drainage basin is the fundamental unit for studying the water cycle at a local/regional scale.

Water balance equation:

P = Q + E ± ΔS

Where P = precipitation, Q = discharge (river output), E = evapotranspiration, ΔS = change in storage (soil moisture, groundwater, lakes). If ΔS is positive, the basin is gaining water (wet season); if negative, it is losing stored water (dry season).

Storm Hydrographs

Storm hydrograph: A graph showing how river discharge changes over time in response to a rainfall event. Key features: peak discharge (maximum flow), lag time (time between peak rainfall and peak discharge), rising limb (increasing discharge as rainfall reaches the river), falling/recession limb (declining discharge as water drains from stores), and baseflow (the steady contribution from groundwater seeping into the river).

Factors Affecting Storm Hydrograph Shape

A flashy hydrograph (short lag time, high peak discharge, steep rising limb) indicates rapid water transfer to the river, increasing flood risk. A flat hydrograph (long lag time, low peak, gentle rising limb) indicates water is being stored and slowly released.

Exam Tip: When analysing a hydrograph, always explain the link between the physical characteristic and the hydrological process. For example: "Urbanisation increases the peak discharge and reduces lag time because impermeable surfaces (concrete, tarmac) prevent infiltration, so precipitation rapidly becomes surface runoff, entering storm drains and reaching the river channel very quickly." Do not just state the factor — explain the mechanism.

The Carbon Cycle

Carbon cycle: The biogeochemical cycle by which carbon is exchanged between the atmosphere, oceans, biosphere (living organisms), pedosphere (soils), and lithosphere (rocks and fossil fuels). Carbon is the fundamental element of all organic life and its movement between these stores — through processes like photosynthesis, respiration, combustion, weathering, and ocean absorption — regulates Earth's climate through the greenhouse effect. The carbon cycle operates on timescales from seconds (respiration) to millions of years (rock formation).

Carbon Fluxes (Transfers)

Biological fluxes:

  • Photosynthesis: Plants, algae, and cyanobacteria absorb CO2 from the atmosphere and convert it to organic carbon compounds (glucose) using solar energy. 6CO2 + 6H2O → C6H12O6 + 6O2. This is the primary mechanism transferring carbon from the atmosphere to the biosphere. Global terrestrial photosynthesis fixes ~120 GtC/year
  • Respiration: All living organisms (plants, animals, microbes) break down organic carbon compounds to release energy, producing CO2 as a waste product. The reverse of photosynthesis. Returns ~119 GtC/year to the atmosphere from terrestrial sources (approximately balanced with photosynthesis before human interference)
  • Decomposition: Dead organic matter (detritus) is broken down by bacteria and fungi (decomposers), releasing CO2 (aerobic decomposition) or CH4 (anaerobic decomposition in waterlogged conditions like wetlands and rice paddies). Rate depends on temperature (faster when warm), moisture, oxygen availability, and the chemical composition of the material

Geological and chemical fluxes:

  • Combustion of fossil fuels: Burning coal, oil, and natural gas releases carbon stored over millions of years. This is the largest human-caused carbon flux (~9.5 GtC/year and rising). It has increased atmospheric CO2 by ~50% since the Industrial Revolution
  • Chemical weathering: Carbonic acid (formed from CO2 dissolving in rainwater) reacts with silicate and carbite rocks, drawing down atmospheric CO2. Very slow but significant over geological timescales (millions of years). Acts as a natural negative feedback on atmospheric CO2
  • Volcanic outgassing: CO2 and other gases released from Earth's interior during volcanic eruptions and through hydrothermal vents. Approximately 0.15–0.26 GtC/year — small compared to human emissions but important over geological time
  • Ocean absorption and outgassing: CO2 dissolves in surface seawater (governed by Henry's Law — cold water absorbs more than warm water). CO2 reacts with water to form carbonic acid (H2CO3), contributing to ocean acidification (pH has fallen by ~0.1 units since pre-industrial, a 26% increase in acidity). The ocean is currently absorbing ~2.5 GtC/year (a critical carbon sink), but its capacity may decrease as waters warm
  • Sedimentation and lithification: Dead marine organisms with calcium carbonate shells (foraminifera, corals, molluscs) sink to the ocean floor. Over millions of years, these sediments are compressed and lithified into limestone and chalk, locking carbon away in the lithosphere for geological timescales

Interconnections Between Water and Carbon Cycles

The water and carbon cycles are deeply and fundamentally linked — disrupting one inevitably affects the other. Understanding these interconnections is essential for OCR examiners:

  • Vegetation links both cycles: Plants need water for photosynthesis (which sequesters atmospheric carbon). Transpiration drives the local water cycle by returning water vapour to the atmosphere. Forests are simultaneously carbon sinks (absorbing CO2) and drivers of local water cycling (generating atmospheric moisture that falls as "recycled" precipitation). Deforestation disrupts both cycles simultaneously
  • Oceans link both cycles: The ocean is both the largest water store (96.5%) and a major carbon sink (~2.5 GtC/year). CO2 dissolves more readily in cold water (Henry's Law), so cold polar waters are important CO2 sinks. The thermohaline circulation transports dissolved carbon to the deep ocean (the "biological pump" and "solubility pump")
  • Soil links both cycles: Soil stores both water (soil moisture) and carbon (humus, organic matter). Waterlogged conditions (anaerobic) dramatically slow decomposition, preserving soil carbon — this is how peat bogs form (accumulating carbon over thousands of years). Draining peatlands exposes the organic matter to oxygen, rapidly releasing stored carbon as CO2
  • Climate links both cycles: The carbon cycle regulates temperature via the greenhouse effect, which in turn drives evaporation rates and precipitation patterns in the water cycle. A warmer atmosphere holds more water vapour (~7% more per degree C — Clausius-Clapeyron relation), intensifying the hydrological cycle (more intense rainfall events, greater flood risk)
  • Chemical weathering links both cycles: Requires water (as carbonic acid: CO2 + H2O → H2CO3) to dissolve silicate and carbonate rocks, drawing down atmospheric CO2. More rainfall → more weathering → more CO2 removal (a slow negative feedback on warming)
Exam Tip: OCR examiners frequently ask about interconnections between the two cycles. Always link processes across both systems. For example: "Deforestation reduces photosynthesis (carbon cycle: less CO2 sequestered) AND reduces interception and transpiration (water cycle: less evapotranspiration, more surface runoff, increased flood risk, reduced local rainfall through loss of moisture recycling). This demonstrates how a single human action disrupts both cycles simultaneously, with cascading consequences." This kind of synoptic thinking scores very highly.

Climate Change

The Greenhouse Effect

The natural greenhouse effect is essential for life: greenhouse gases (CO2, CH4, N2O, water vapour, ozone) in the atmosphere absorb and re-emit outgoing longwave (infrared) radiation from Earth's surface, trapping heat and keeping the global mean temperature at about 15 degrees C (without them it would be approximately −18 degrees C). The enhanced greenhouse effect is the additional warming caused by human-induced increases in greenhouse gas concentrations, particularly CO2 from fossil fuel combustion and CH4 from agriculture.

Human Impacts on the Carbon Cycle

Deforestation

  • Removes a major carbon sink (trees absorb CO2 via photosynthesis and store it in biomass for decades to centuries)
  • Burning or decomposition of felled trees releases stored carbon back to the atmosphere as CO2
  • Exposed soil dries out and oxidises, releasing additional CO2. Erosion by rain removes carbon-rich topsoil
  • Reduces transpiration, decreasing local atmospheric moisture and potentially reducing rainfall (positive feedback: less rain → forest stress → more dieback → less rain)
  • Deforestation is responsible for approximately 10% of global CO2 emissions
Case Study: Amazon Rainforest
The Amazon basin stores approximately 150–200 billion tonnes of carbon in its biomass and soil, making it one of the most important carbon stores on Earth. Deforestation — primarily for cattle ranching (~80% of cleared land), soy cultivation, and logging — is releasing this carbon at an alarming rate. Between 2001 and 2022, approximately 17–20% of the original Amazon forest was lost or severely degraded. Recent studies (Gatti et al., 2021) found that parts of the eastern Amazon have already become a net carbon source rather than a sink, emitting more CO2 than they absorb due to fires and deforestation. Deforestation also reduces evapotranspiration — the Amazon generates approximately 50% of its own rainfall through moisture recycling (the "flying rivers" phenomenon). Continued deforestation risks pushing the Amazon past a tipping point (~20–25% deforestation) where reduced rainfall triggers irreversible large-scale dieback, converting forest to savanna and releasing catastrophic amounts of stored carbon. This would have global climate consequences.

Fossil Fuel Combustion

  • Transfers carbon from the long-term geological store (where it was locked away over millions of years) to the atmosphere in a geological instant (decades)
  • Atmospheric CO2 has risen from ~280 ppm (pre-industrial, 1750) to over 420 ppm (2024) — a 50% increase and the highest level in at least 3 million years (Pliocene epoch)
  • Fossil fuel combustion is responsible for approximately 75% of all anthropogenic greenhouse gas emissions (~36 GtCO2/year or ~9.5 GtC/year)
  • Fundamentally disrupts the balance between natural carbon fluxes that maintained stable atmospheric CO2 for millennia

Human Impacts on the Water Cycle

  • Urbanisation: Impermeable surfaces (concrete, tarmac, roofs) dramatically increase surface runoff and reduce infiltration. Storm drains channel water rapidly to rivers. Result: flashier hydrographs, increased flood frequency and magnitude, reduced groundwater recharge. Example: the area of urban land in England increased by 16% between 1990 and 2018
  • Deforestation: Reduces interception (no canopy to catch rain) and transpiration (no trees to return moisture to atmosphere). Result: more surface runoff, increased flood risk, reduced local rainfall through loss of moisture recycling, soil erosion. The Amazon generates ~50% of its own rainfall through evapotranspiration
  • Agriculture: Irrigation depletes groundwater stores and rivers (the Ogallala Aquifer in the US Great Plains is declining by ~1 m/year in some areas). Drainage of wetlands removes natural water storage. Ploughing increases runoff. Fertiliser runoff causes eutrophication of water bodies
  • Dam construction: Creates new storage (reservoir), alters downstream river regime (flow pattern), traps sediment (downstream erosion), enables irrigation and hydroelectric power, can displace communities. Example: Three Gorges Dam (China) displaced 1.3 million people
  • Climate change: Intensifies the hydrological cycle (warmer atmosphere holds ~7% more moisture per degree C). Leads to: more intense rainfall events (increased flood risk), longer dry periods (drought risk), glacial melt (temporary increase then loss of meltwater supply), rising sea levels, altered monsoon patterns
Case Study: Aral Sea, Central Asia
Once the world's 4th largest lake (68,000 km2 in 1960), the Aral Sea has shrunk catastrophically since the 1960s when the Soviet Union diverted the Amu Darya and Syr Darya rivers to irrigate cotton fields in Uzbekistan and Kazakhstan. By 2014, the eastern basin had completely dried up for the first time in 600 years. The lake has lost over 90% of its volume. Consequences: (1) Collapse of the fishing industry — 60,000 jobs lost; the port of Moynaq is now 150 km from the water, (2) Salinisation — remaining water became hypersaline (100+ g/L), killing aquatic life, (3) Dust storms — toxic dust containing pesticides and salt from the exposed seabed blows over surrounding populations, causing respiratory disease, kidney disease, and cancer, (4) Climate change — loss of the moderating influence of the lake has made local climate more extreme (hotter summers, colder winters), (5) Desertification of the former lake bed. The Aral Sea is one of the most dramatic examples of how human alteration of the water cycle can cause environmental and humanitarian catastrophe. Partial recovery of the northern Aral Sea has been achieved through the Kok-Aral Dam (funded by the World Bank), but the southern basin is considered irreversibly lost.
