2.4 Human Rights (OCR Component 2)

This topic explores the geographical dimensions of human rights: how rights are distributed unevenly across the world, the factors that influence their protection or violation, and the role of different actors in promoting them. It covers development indicators, health and education inequalities, gender disparities, and debates about sovereignty versus intervention. The geographical lens focuses on spatial patterns, scale, and the connections between rights, development, and power.

1. Geographies of Human Rights

Human rights: Fundamental rights and freedoms inherent to all human beings, regardless of race, sex, nationality, ethnicity, language, religion, or any other status. They include: (1) civil and political rights (freedoms FROM state interference — freedom of speech, assembly, religion, right to a fair trial, right to vote); and (2) economic, social, and cultural rights (entitlements TO resources — right to health, education, adequate standard of living, work, cultural participation).

The Universal Declaration of Human Rights (UDHR)

Adopted by the UN General Assembly on 10 December 1948 in Paris, the UDHR was drafted in response to the atrocities of World War II and the Holocaust. It established, for the first time, a common standard of fundamental rights for all peoples and nations. Eleanor Roosevelt chaired the drafting committee. The UDHR contains 30 articles.

The UDHR is not legally binding, but it has inspired over 80 legally binding international treaties and conventions, including:

  • International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights (ICCPR, 1966): 173 states parties; covers freedom of speech, religion, assembly, right to a fair trial
  • International Covenant on Economic, Social, and Cultural Rights (ICESCR, 1966): 171 states parties; covers right to work, health, education, adequate living standard
  • Convention on the Rights of the Child (CRC, 1989): Most widely ratified human rights treaty (196 states; only non-ratifier is the USA)
  • Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination Against Women (CEDAW, 1979): 189 states parties; addresses gender-based discrimination
Exam Tip: Distinguish between civil/political rights (freedoms FROM interference — speech, assembly, fair trial) and economic/social/cultural rights (entitlements TO resources — health, education, housing). Western democracies traditionally prioritise civil/political rights; socialist/developing states often prioritise economic/social rights. This tension is central to debates about universalism vs cultural relativism. Some governments argue that economic development must come before political freedoms; critics counter that both sets of rights are indivisible and interdependent.

2. Development Indicators and Inequality

Development: The process of improving the quality of life and economic well-being of a population. Development is multidimensional: it encompasses economic growth (rising GDP), social progress (improved health, education, equality), environmental sustainability, and political freedom. Amartya Sen defined development as the expansion of human capabilities and freedoms — not merely income growth.

Global Inequality Patterns

Key patterns of global inequality:

  • North-South divide: The Brandt Line (1980) identified a broad division between wealthier nations in the global North and poorer nations in the South. This persists in broad terms but is increasingly complicated by: the rise of China, India, and other Asian economies; oil-wealthy Gulf states; persistent poverty in parts of the "North" (rural Appalachia, post-industrial northern England); and growing middle classes in "Southern" countries
  • Intra-country inequality: Often greater than inter-country inequality. Examples: coastal vs inland China (Shanghai's GDP per capita is 6x that of Gansu province); northern vs southern Nigeria (the north has HDI levels comparable to the poorest African countries while Lagos competes with middle-income nations); London vs northern England (life expectancy gap of 10+ years between Kensington and Blackpool)
  • Gender inequality: Women face systemic disadvantage in many regions, particularly South Asia, Sub-Saharan Africa, and the Middle East/North Africa. Gender gaps in education, income, political representation, and legal rights persist globally
  • Ethnic and indigenous minorities: Indigenous peoples and ethnic minorities face discrimination in access to land, services, education, and political representation in virtually every country. Australia's indigenous population has life expectancy 8 years lower than non-indigenous Australians; Brazil's indigenous peoples face ongoing land dispossession in the Amazon

3. Health Inequalities

Global Health Disparities

Health outcomes are among the starkest indicators of global inequality. Life expectancy varies by over 30 years between the highest-performing countries (Japan: 84.8 years; Switzerland: 83.4) and the lowest (Central African Republic: 53.3; Chad: 52.5; South Sudan: 55.0).

Case Study: Health Inequalities in Sierra Leone
Sierra Leone consistently ranks among the world's poorest performers on health indicators, illustrating the intersection of poverty, conflict, governance, and health:
  • Key statistics: Life expectancy ~55 years (vs UK ~81); maternal mortality 1,120/100,000 live births (115x the UK rate); under-5 mortality 109/1,000 (22x UK); 0.02 doctors per 1,000 people (vs UK: 2.8 — 140x fewer); GDP per capita ~$500
  • Historical context: The civil war (1991–2002) destroyed 70% of health infrastructure; killed 50,000 people and displaced 2.6 million. Health worker flight (brain drain) during and after the war decimated the medical workforce
  • Ebola crisis (2014–2016): 14,124 cases, 3,956 deaths in Sierra Leone. The epidemic overwhelmed the health system (which had only ~120 doctors for 7 million people), collapsed routine healthcare (vaccination, maternal care, malaria treatment), and caused an estimated 10,600 additional deaths from other causes due to health system failure. International response was slow; MSF called for military-level deployment months before it arrived
  • Ongoing challenges: Chronic underfunding (government health spending ~$9 per person/year vs WHO recommendation of $86); 80% of healthcare provided by NGOs (MSF, Partners in Health, WHO); shortage of equipment, drugs, and trained staff; rural populations often 25+ km from nearest health facility; cultural practices (traditional birth attendants vs skilled midwives)
  • Progress: Free Healthcare Initiative (2010) removed user fees for pregnant women, lactating mothers, and children under 5, increasing utilisation. Polio eradicated. But structural poverty remains the fundamental barrier

4. Education Access and Inequality

Education access: The ability of individuals to participate in formal education systems. Barriers include: direct costs (school fees, uniforms, books), opportunity costs (lost income from child labour), distance to school, quality of teaching, cultural attitudes (especially towards girls' education), conflict and insecurity, disability, and language (instruction in non-native languages).

Global Education Patterns

  • Primary education: Near-universal enrolment in most regions (global net enrolment ~90%), but completion rates remain low in Sub-Saharan Africa (~65%) and South Asia (~82%). Quality varies enormously: in many LICs, children attend school but learn very little (the "learning crisis" — World Bank estimates 53% of children in LMICs cannot read a simple story by age 10)
  • Secondary education: Significant gender and wealth gaps persist. In Sub-Saharan Africa, only 35% of girls complete lower secondary school. In Chad and Niger, fewer than 5% of the poorest girls complete secondary education. Rural-urban divides are stark: urban secondary completion rates are often 3–5x higher than rural rates
  • Tertiary education: Highly concentrated in HICs and urban areas of MICs. Gross enrolment in tertiary education: 80%+ in North America and Europe vs ~9% in Sub-Saharan Africa. Brain drain further disadvantages LICs as their best-educated graduates emigrate
  • Literacy: Global adult literacy ~87%, but below 40% in some LICs (Niger: 35%, Mali: 35%, South Sudan: 35%). Female literacy lags behind male literacy in many regions: in South Asia, the female literacy rate is 10+ percentage points below the male rate; in some West African countries, the gap exceeds 20 points
  • Impact of COVID-19: School closures affected 1.6 billion learners at their peak (2020). Learning losses were greatest in LICs where online alternatives were unavailable (only 6% of households in Sub-Saharan Africa have internet access). UNICEF estimates 10 million additional girls may never return to school
Case Study: Girls' Education in Afghanistan
Afghanistan represents the most extreme contemporary example of state-imposed educational exclusion:
  • Taliban ban: Since August 2021, the Taliban have banned girls above age 12 from secondary education and women from university. This is the only country in the world where girls are barred from secondary school by government policy
  • Scale: Over 1.1 million girls directly affected. Before the Taliban takeover, significant progress had been made since 2001: female enrolment grew from near-zero to 3.5 million girls in school; female literacy rose from 17% to 30%
  • Consequences: Increased rates of child marriage (girls married off because schooling is no longer an option — UNICEF estimates 28% of Afghan girls married before 18); reduced economic opportunity (women excluded from most employment); poorer health outcomes (educated mothers have lower child mortality rates); intergenerational poverty; psychological harm (depression, anxiety among excluded girls)
  • International response: Universal condemnation from UN, Western governments, and Muslim-majority countries alike. The Organisation of Islamic Cooperation declared the ban un-Islamic. But: diplomatic pressure has been ineffective. No country has recognised the Taliban government. Foreign aid (which funded 75% of government spending pre-2021) has been largely frozen, but this harms the population more than the regime
  • Broader significance: Illustrates the limits of international pressure on sovereign states; demonstrates that rights gains can be reversed rapidly; highlights the political instrumentalisation of education and gender

5. Gender Inequality

Gender inequality: The unequal treatment, status, or opportunities available to individuals based on their gender. It manifests across economic opportunities, political representation, education access, health outcomes, legal protections, and personal freedoms. Gender inequality is not confined to LICs — it persists in all societies, though its forms and severity vary greatly.

Forms of Gender Inequality

  • Economic: Globally, women earn approximately 77 cents for every dollar earned by men (unadjusted pay gap). Women perform 76% of unpaid care and domestic work (worth an estimated $10.8 trillion/year if valued at minimum wage). In many LICs, women cannot own land, inherit property, or access credit. Only 55% of women globally participate in the labour force vs 80% of men
  • Education: 130 million girls are out of school globally. In South Sudan, only 1% of girls complete secondary school. But: in many HICs and MICs, girls now outperform boys educationally (in the UK, girls outperform boys at every level from GCSE to university)
  • Health: 287,000 women die annually from preventable pregnancy and childbirth causes (800/day). Female genital mutilation (FGM) affects 200+ million women in 30+ countries. Sex-selective abortion has produced 140+ million "missing women" in Asia (China, India). Women live longer than men in most countries but face higher rates of disability and morbidity
  • Political: Women hold only 26.5% of parliamentary seats globally (2023). Only 28 countries have ever had a female head of state/government. Women are excluded from politics entirely in some contexts (Saudi Arabia only granted women the right to vote in 2015; Taliban-ruled Afghanistan bars women from public life)
  • Legal: In 49 countries, there are no laws specifically addressing domestic violence. In some jurisdictions, women cannot travel freely, work, or open a bank account without male permission. Marital rape remains legal in 36 countries. In many countries, inheritance and divorce laws heavily favour men
  • Violence: 1 in 3 women globally experiences physical or sexual violence in her lifetime. Conflict-related sexual violence is used systematically as a weapon of war (documented in DRC, Bosnia, Myanmar, Iraq)
Case Study: Gender Inequality in India
India ranks 122 out of 162 countries on the GII, despite being the world's 5th largest economy:
  • Son preference: Deep-rooted cultural preference for sons has led to sex-selective abortions (despite being illegal since 1994) and produced a skewed sex ratio: 108 males per 100 females nationally, reaching 120:100 in some northern states (Haryana, Punjab). An estimated 63 million women are "missing" from India's population
  • Economic participation: Female labour force participation has declined from 32% to 21% over recent decades (one of the world's lowest rates). Women own only 12.8% of land. The gender pay gap is 27% even in formal sector employment
  • Education: Female literacy (65%) lags behind male literacy (82%). In rural Rajasthan and Bihar, female literacy is below 50%. However, at university level, female enrolment has risen dramatically and now exceeds male enrolment in many states
  • Violence: A woman is raped every 15 minutes in India (official statistics, widely considered underestimates). Dowry-related deaths: ~7,000 per year. Acid attacks, honour killings, and trafficking remain widespread. The 2012 Delhi gang rape case prompted national outrage and some legal reform
  • Government responses: Beti Bachao Beti Padhao (Save the Daughter, Educate the Daughter) scheme targets son preference through awareness campaigns and financial incentives. Reservation of 33% of local government seats for women (73rd Constitutional Amendment). Criminalisation of dowry, child marriage, and FGM. Maternity Benefit Act (2017) extended paid maternity leave to 26 weeks
  • Limitations: Laws exist but enforcement is weak. Police frequently refuse to register complaints of violence against women. Judicial processes are slow (average rape case takes 5+ years). Deep-rooted patriarchal cultural norms change slowly. Rural-urban divide remains vast

7. Intervention and Its Consequences

The Sovereignty vs. Intervention Debate

The central tension in human rights geography: state sovereignty (the principle that states have exclusive authority within their borders and cannot be interfered with by external actors) vs. the international community's moral and legal responsibility to protect populations from mass atrocities.

Responsibility to Protect (R2P): A political commitment adopted unanimously by UN member states at the 2005 World Summit. It states that: (1) every state has the responsibility to protect its population from genocide, war crimes, ethnic cleansing, and crimes against humanity; (2) the international community should assist states in meeting this obligation; (3) if a state manifestly fails to protect its population, the international community should take collective action through the UN Security Council, including (as a last resort) military intervention.
Arguments for InterventionArguments Against Intervention
Moral duty to prevent genocide and mass atrocities — the lessons of Rwanda (1994) and the Holocaust demand actionViolates state sovereignty and the principle of non-interference in internal affairs (UN Charter Article 2(7))
R2P doctrine creates a framework for legitimate, multilateral intervention when states fail to protect their peopleRisk of mission creep, unintended consequences, and prolonging conflict (Libya 2011 intervention led to state collapse and civil war)
Can stabilise regions, prevent refugee crises, and protect vulnerable populationsIntervening powers may have ulterior motives (resources, geopolitics, regime change) disguised as humanitarianism
Promotes universal human rights standards and holds perpetrators accountableWestern-centric: who decides which crises merit intervention? Why intervene in Libya but not Syria, Yemen, or Myanmar? Selective application undermines legitimacy
Economic sanctions can pressure change without military violenceSanctions often harm ordinary citizens more than the regime they target (Iraq sanctions in the 1990s contributed to 500,000 excess child deaths according to UNICEF estimates)
ICC prosecution deters future atrocities by ending impunityMajor powers are not ICC members and refuse to submit to its jurisdiction; enforcement is impossible without state cooperation
Case Study: Rwanda Genocide (1994) — Failure to Intervene
The Rwandan genocide is the defining case of international failure to protect human rights:
  • Events: Between April and July 1994, approximately 800,000 Tutsis and moderate Hutus were killed in just 100 days — a rate of 8,000 deaths per day. Perpetrators included the Hutu-dominated government, army, militias (Interahamwe), and ordinary citizens
  • International failure: The UN peacekeeping force (UNAMIR, commanded by General Romeo Dallaire) was under-resourced (2,500 troops, later reduced to 270) and had a restricted mandate that prevented it from using force. Dallaire sent a fax to UN headquarters warning of genocide planning 3 months before it began — it was ignored. France, Belgium, and the USA all had intelligence but chose not to act. The Clinton administration deliberately avoided using the word "genocide" to evade the obligation to intervene under the Genocide Convention. Belgium withdrew its troops after 10 peacekeepers were killed, sending a signal that the international community would not resist
  • Aftermath: The genocide ended when the Rwandan Patriotic Front (RPF) under Paul Kagame took control. 2 million Hutus fled to neighbouring countries (primarily eastern DRC), destabilising the region and contributing to the First and Second Congo Wars (which killed an estimated 5.4 million people). The International Criminal Tribunal for Rwanda (ICTR) was established, convicting 93 individuals including the former prime minister
  • Legacy: Rwanda's experience directly led to the development of R2P. It demonstrated that sovereignty cannot be a shield for genocide and that the international community bears responsibility when it fails to act. Rwanda's post-genocide recovery has been remarkable: rapid economic growth (7%+ GDP growth/year); universal healthcare; women hold 61% of parliamentary seats (highest in the world); poverty fell from 60% to 38%. But: Kagame's government is authoritarian, with severe restrictions on press freedom and political opposition
Case Study: Sanctions on North Korea — Limits of Economic Pressure
North Korea (DPRK) illustrates the limitations of sanctions as a tool for promoting human rights:
  • Human rights situation: The UN Commission of Inquiry (2014) documented systematic violations including: political prison camps (kwanliso) holding an estimated 80,000–120,000 people; torture, execution, and forced labour; denial of freedom of movement, expression, and religion; state-controlled information (no internet access, no independent media); severe food insecurity (40% of the population chronically malnourished)
  • Sanctions regime: Among the most comprehensive in history, targeting North Korea's nuclear weapons programme. UN Security Council resolutions ban arms trade, restrict luxury goods imports, limit oil supply, freeze assets of officials, and impose travel bans. Additional unilateral sanctions from USA, EU, Japan, and South Korea
  • Effectiveness: Sanctions have not changed the regime's behaviour on nuclear weapons or human rights. The Kim regime prioritises its own survival over population welfare ("byungjin" policy of parallel nuclear and economic development). Sanctions are circumvented through China (which provides 90% of North Korea's trade and is reluctant to enforce sanctions fully for fear of regime collapse). Ordinary citizens bear the cost: humanitarian organisations report that sanctions restrictions impede delivery of food, medicine, and humanitarian supplies
  • Key lesson: Sanctions can impose economic costs but cannot force regime change when the ruling elite is willing to let its population suffer. Authoritarian regimes with tight control over information and minimal civil society are particularly resistant to external pressure
Exam Tip: OCR questions frequently ask you to assess the effectiveness of different approaches to promoting human rights. Always use specific evidence from case studies, consider multiple scales (local, national, global), and weigh competing perspectives. The strongest answers: (1) evaluate multiple approaches (military intervention, sanctions, diplomacy, NGO advocacy, grassroots empowerment); (2) acknowledge that effectiveness varies by context; (3) discuss the sovereignty vs intervention tension; (4) consider whose human rights are being promoted and by whom (the Western-centric critique); (5) recognise that progress is often incremental and contested, not linear. Avoid simplistic conclusions — show awareness of complexity and trade-offs.

8. Synoptic Links

Human rights connects to every other area of the OCR specification. The strongest answers draw these links explicitly:

  • Global governance (2.5 Power and Borders): How international institutions (UN, ICC, EU) attempt to manage human rights, and why they often fail due to sovereignty concerns and power imbalances
  • Migration (2.3): Refugees fleeing human rights abuses; the rights of migrants and asylum seekers; the tension between border control and human rights obligations; climate migration and the governance gap
  • Development and trade (2.2): The relationship between poverty, inequality, and rights violations; how trade structures can perpetuate or alleviate inequality; TNCs and labour rights in global supply chains
  • Globalisation: How global interconnections can both spread and challenge human rights norms; the role of social media in exposing abuses and mobilising campaigns; cultural globalisation and the universalism vs relativism debate
  • Climate change and hazards (1.3): Environmental rights; climate justice (those least responsible suffer most); climate refugees and the legal protection gap; the right to a safe environment
  • Place and identity (2.1): How state power, sovereignty, and territorial control shape which rights are protected and for whom; how place-based identity intersects with rights claims (indigenous land rights, minority rights)
