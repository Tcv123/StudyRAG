1.1 Landscape Systems — Coastal Landscapes

Systems Approach to Landscapes

System: A set of interrelated components connected by flows of energy and materials, working together as a complex whole. Coastal landscapes operate as open systems with inputs of energy and matter from outside the system, internal throughputs (transfers and transformations), and outputs that leave the system. The systems approach allows geographers to understand how changes to one component ripple through the entire landscape.

Systems Terminology

  • Inputs: Energy from waves (derived from wind), tidal energy (gravitational pull of moon/sun), wind energy, solar radiation (drives weathering and evaporation); sediment from rivers, cliff erosion, offshore seabed, and longshore drift from adjacent cells; precipitation
  • Throughputs (transfers and processes): Erosion (hydraulic action, abrasion, attrition, corrosion), weathering (freeze-thaw, carbonation, biological), mass movement (rockfall, slumping, mudflow), sediment transport (longshore drift, traction, saltation, suspension, solution), deposition
  • Outputs: Sediment removed by longshore drift to adjacent cells or offshore, dissolved minerals lost in solution, energy dissipated as heat and sound
  • Stores: Beaches (temporary sediment stores), sand dunes, spits, bars, mudflats, cliff faces, wave-cut platforms
Dynamic equilibrium: A balanced state in a system where inputs and outputs are roughly equal over time. The system appears stable but is constantly adjusting to small changes. Any significant change in one component (e.g. increased storm frequency, human intervention, sea-level rise) causes adjustments elsewhere to restore balance. For example, increased cliff erosion adds more sediment to the beach → wider beach absorbs more wave energy → erosion rate decreases (negative feedback). However, if the disturbance is too great, the system may shift to a new equilibrium state.

Feedback Loops

  • Negative feedback (self-regulating): A change triggers a response that counteracts the original change, stabilising the system. Example: cliff collapse adds sediment to the beach → wider beach absorbs wave energy before it reaches the cliff → erosion rate decreases → less cliff collapse. This is the dominant feedback mechanism that maintains dynamic equilibrium
  • Positive feedback (self-reinforcing): A change triggers a response that amplifies the original change, destabilising the system and pushing it towards a new equilibrium. Example: sea-level rise causes increased wave erosion → narrower beach → less wave energy absorption → even more erosion. Also: removal of coastal vegetation → increased erosion → less vegetation can establish → more erosion

Waves

Wave: A transfer of energy through water caused by wind blowing across the sea surface. Importantly, in open water the water itself does not travel forward — individual water particles move in circular orbits (orbital motion), returning approximately to their starting position. Only the energy moves forward. When waves approach shallow water, the circular orbits become elliptical, friction with the seabed slows the wave base, the wavelength decreases, the wave height increases, and eventually the wave breaks.

Wave Formation and Size

Three factors determine wave size and energy:

  • Wind speed: Stronger winds transfer more energy to the water surface, generating larger, more powerful waves
  • Wind duration: The longer the wind blows in a consistent direction, the more energy is transferred and the bigger the waves become
  • Fetch: The distance of open water over which the wind blows without obstruction. A longer fetch allows waves to accumulate more energy. The prevailing south-westerly winds in the UK have a fetch across the entire Atlantic Ocean (~5,000 km), which is why the western coastline receives much more powerful waves than the eastern coast (short fetch across the North Sea, ~500 km)

Wave Behaviour at the Coast

In deep water, water particles move in circular orbits that decrease in diameter with depth. When the water depth is less than half the wavelength, the seabed interferes with these orbits (wave base touches the bottom). This causes friction, which slows the wave, steepens it, and eventually causes it to break. The swash is the rush of water up the beach after a wave breaks. The backwash is the return of water back down the beach under gravity.

Wave Refraction

When waves approach an irregular coastline with headlands and bays, the parts of the wave in shallower water near the headland slow down first, while the parts in deeper water in the bay continue at their original speed. This causes the wave fronts to bend (refract) and converge on the headlands, concentrating wave energy there and causing erosion. In the bays, wave energy is dispersed, favouring deposition. This is why headlands erode faster and bays tend to have beaches.

Exam Tip: When explaining wave type, always explicitly link the swash/backwash balance to the resulting beach profile and landform. Constructive waves have a strong swash that deposits material faster than the weak backwash removes it → net deposition → wide beach. Destructive waves have a strong backwash that removes material faster than the weak swash deposits it → net erosion → narrow beach. Examiners want to see the process-landform connection clearly articulated.

Sub-aerial Processes (Weathering and Mass Movement)

These operate on the cliff face from above the waterline, weakening rock and making it more vulnerable to marine erosion from below. The combination of sub-aerial weathering (top-down) and marine erosion (base) is what drives cliff retreat.

Weathering types:

  • Freeze-thaw (frost shattering): Water enters cracks and joints during the day. At night, it freezes and expands by approximately 9%, exerting pressure of up to 2,100 kPa on the rock. When it thaws, the water penetrates deeper into the widened crack. Repeated freeze-thaw cycles (above and below 0 degrees C) progressively shatter the rock. Most effective in environments with frequent temperature oscillations around 0 degrees C (e.g. northern UK coasts in winter)
  • Salt crystallisation: Seawater spray enters rock pores and cracks. As water evaporates, salt crystals grow, exerting pressure on the rock and causing granular disintegration. Particularly effective on porous rocks like sandstone in warm, exposed coastal environments
  • Carbonation (chemical weathering): Rainwater absorbs CO2 from the atmosphere to form weak carbonic acid (H2CO3). This reacts with calcium carbonate in limestone and chalk, dissolving it into soluble calcium bicarbonate. Accelerated by increased atmospheric CO2
  • Oxidation: Iron-rich minerals in rocks react with oxygen and water, weakening the rock structure (forming rust-coloured iron oxides). Particularly visible in rocks with high iron content
  • Biological weathering: Plant roots grow into cracks and exert pressure as they enlarge (root wedging). Burrowing organisms (piddocks, sea urchins) bore into rock, weakening it. Algae and lichens produce organic acids that chemically dissolve rock surfaces

Mass Movement

The downslope movement of rock, soil, and debris under the influence of gravity. Mass movement is triggered when the force of gravity exceeds the resisting forces (friction, cohesion) holding material in place. This balance is affected by: undercutting by wave erosion (removes support), saturation by rainfall (adds weight, reduces friction, increases pore water pressure), weathering (weakens rock), vegetation removal, and steep slope angles.

Exam Tip: In exam answers about cliff retreat, always combine marine erosion at the base with sub-aerial weathering and mass movement above. The process is cyclical: (1) waves erode a notch at the base (hydraulic action + abrasion), (2) the cliff above becomes unsupported and weakened by weathering, (3) mass movement (rockfall, slump) causes the cliff to collapse, (4) debris is removed by waves, exposing a fresh cliff face, and the cycle repeats. This demonstrates the interaction of different process groups within the landscape system.

Sediment Transport

Methods of Sediment Transport

  • Traction: Large boulders and cobbles rolled along the seabed by strong wave action. Requires the most energy
  • Saltation: Smaller pebbles and gravel bounced along the seabed in a hopping motion by moderate wave energy
  • Suspension: Fine sand, silt, and clay particles carried within the water column. Gives the sea a brown/turbid colour during storms. Can transport material long distances
  • Solution: Dissolved minerals (e.g. calcium bicarbonate from limestone dissolution) carried invisibly in the water. Cannot be deposited mechanically — only through chemical precipitation or biological processes

Longshore Drift (Littoral Drift)

Longshore drift: The systematic movement of sediment along the coast by wave action. Waves approach the beach at an angle determined by the prevailing wind direction. The swash carries sediment up the beach at this oblique angle. The backwash, under the influence of gravity, drags sediment straight back down the beach perpendicular to the shoreline. The net result is a zigzag movement of sediment along the coast in the direction of the prevailing wind. On England's south coast, longshore drift generally moves sediment from west to east (prevailing south-westerly winds). On the east coast, it moves southward.

Sediment Cells (Littoral Cells)

Sediment cell: A self-contained stretch of coastline within which sediment movement (input, transport, and output) is largely independent of adjacent cells. The coast of England and Wales is divided into 11 major sediment cells. Each cell has sources (cliff erosion, river input), transfers (longshore drift), and sinks (deposition as beaches, spits, or loss offshore). Understanding sediment cells is crucial for coastal management because interventions in one part of a cell (e.g. building groynes) can have unintended consequences in other parts of the same cell (e.g. sediment starvation of downdrift beaches).

Coastal Deposition and Depositional Landforms

Deposition occurs when the sea loses energy and can no longer carry its sediment load. This happens when:

  • Waves enter sheltered areas (bays, behind headlands, estuaries) — wave energy is reduced
  • Wind speed decreases or changes direction
  • There is a large sediment supply (e.g. from river mouths or rapidly eroding cliffs)
  • Constructive waves dominate (strong swash deposits sediment high on the beach)
  • Longshore drift reaches a change in coastline direction (material can no longer be transported forward)

Beaches

The most common depositional landform, found at the interface between land and sea. Beaches vary in composition, gradient, and morphology depending on sediment type and wave conditions:

  • Sandy beaches: Wide, gently sloping (2–5 degrees). Fine sand is easily transported by constructive waves and deposited uniformly. Common in sheltered bays
  • Shingle/pebble beaches: Steep (10–20 degrees). Coarse material is too heavy for the backwash to move far, so it piles up steeply. Water percolates quickly through pebbles, reducing backwash energy further (positive feedback for steepening)
  • Beach features: Berms (ridges of material deposited at successive high tide marks), cusps (semicircular depressions formed by swash/backwash interaction), ripples (small-scale features on sandy surfaces), runnels (channels formed by drainage)

Spits

A spit is a long, narrow ridge of sand or shingle extending from the mainland into the sea (or across an estuary) at a point where the coastline changes direction. It forms because longshore drift continues to carry material in its original direction even though the coast turns. The end of the spit may curve landward (forming a recurved tip) due to wave refraction or secondary currents. A sheltered, low-energy area behind the spit often develops into a salt marsh through ecological succession (halophyte colonisation).

Case Study: Spurn Point, Humber Estuary, East Yorkshire
Spurn Point is a 5.5 km long sand and shingle spit extending across the mouth of the Humber Estuary. It is formed by longshore drift carrying material southward from the rapidly eroding Holderness cliffs (the source of sediment). The spit has a pronounced recurved tip and encloses extensive mudflats and salt marshes within the estuary. Spurn is extremely dynamic and migrates westward over time — it has been breached and reformed at least twice in recorded history (1849 and 2013). The December 2013 storm surge breached the spit in several places, and it is now classified as a tidal island (only accessible at low tide). It is managed by the Yorkshire Wildlife Trust as a nature reserve and illustrates key concepts: the link between cliff erosion (source) and spit formation (store/output) within a sediment cell; the dynamic, ever-changing nature of coastal landforms; and the tension between "holding the line" and allowing natural processes.

Bars and Tombolos

  • Bar: A spit that grows all the way across a bay, sealing off a lagoon of trapped water behind it. Bars form in sheltered conditions where wave energy is low enough to allow deposition across the full bay width. Example: Slapton Ley, Devon — a freshwater lagoon trapped behind a shingle bar
  • Tombolo: A bar of deposited sediment that connects the mainland to an offshore island. Forms when waves refract around an island, creating a zone of constructive interference and deposition in the sheltered area between the island and the shore. Example: Chesil Beach connecting the Isle of Portland to the Dorset coast (28 km long, graded from pebbles at the Portland end to fine sand at the Bridport end — evidence of longshore sorting)

Sand Dunes

Formed when sand is blown inland by onshore winds and trapped by obstacles (driftwood, vegetation). Dunes develop through ecological succession (psammosere):

  1. Embryo dunes — nearest the strand line; small mounds around obstacles; colonised by pioneer species (sea rocket, sea sandwort) that tolerate salt spray, drought, and burial
  2. Foredunes (yellow dunes) — taller (5–10 m); dominated by marram grass (Ammophila arenaria), which has adaptations for sand dune life: deep roots stabilise sand, rolled leaves reduce water loss, buried stems survive burial. Still very little humus in soil; alkaline pH; harsh conditions
  3. Grey dunes — further inland; more humus develops; greater species diversity (mosses, lichens, low shrubs); more sheltered; slightly acidic soil
  4. Dune slacks — low-lying wet areas between dune ridges where the water table is close to the surface; support wetland species (creeping willow, orchids)
  5. Mature/fixed dunes — furthest inland; thick soil with abundant humus; climax vegetation may include shrubs, heather, and woodland; pH acidic; well-established ecosystem

Erosional Landforms

Headlands and Bays

Form where the coast is made of alternating bands of hard and soft rock perpendicular to the shore (a discordant coastline). The sea erodes the weaker rock (e.g. clay, shale) faster, forming bays. The more resistant rock (e.g. granite, limestone) protrudes as headlands. Over time, wave refraction concentrates energy on the headlands (accelerating their erosion) and disperses it in the bays (encouraging deposition of beaches). Eventually, this differential erosion will straighten the coastline — a demonstration of the system seeking dynamic equilibrium.

Cliff Profiles and Wave-Cut Platforms

  1. Waves attack the base of the cliff, concentrated between high and low tide marks (the inter-tidal zone)
  2. Hydraulic action and abrasion create a wave-cut notch (an indent at the cliff base)
  3. As the notch deepens, the rock above becomes unsupported and collapses (mass movement)
  4. The cliff retreats inland, leaving behind a gently sloping (<1–3 degrees) rocky platform — the wave-cut platform
  5. As the platform extends seaward, waves lose energy crossing its shallow, rough surface, which gradually slows further erosion (negative feedback). Maximum platform width is typically ~500 m before erosion effectively ceases

Caves, Arches, Stacks, and Stumps

A sequence of erosional landforms that develops on headlands, demonstrating the progressive erosion of resistant rock through geological weaknesses:

  1. Cave: Hydraulic action and abrasion exploit a line of weakness (joint, fault, bedding plane) in the headland, eroding it into a tunnel-like cave. The back of the cave is continually eroded deeper
  2. Arch: When caves on both sides of a narrow headland erode through and meet, they form an arch — a bridge of rock spanning the gap with sea on both sides
  3. Stack: The roof of the arch is weakened by weathering (freeze-thaw, biological) and wave action from below. Eventually it collapses, leaving an isolated column of rock separated from the headland
  4. Stump: Continued erosion at the base of the stack (abrasion, hydraulic action) undermines it. Eventually the stack collapses, leaving a low stump that is only visible at low tide. The stump may eventually be completely eroded away
Case Study: Old Harry Rocks, Purbeck Coast, Dorset
Located at the eastern end of the Jurassic Coast World Heritage Site, Old Harry Rocks are chalk stacks and stumps on the Foreland headland. Old Harry is a prominent stack, and Old Harry's Wife was a stack that collapsed to become a stump in 1896 — demonstrating the erosion sequence in action within recent history. The Foreland headland has caves, arches, and stacks in various stages of development, making it an ideal location for studying the complete erosion sequence. The chalk is relatively resistant but is heavily jointed, and these joints are exploited by hydraulic action, freeze-thaw weathering, and biological weathering (plant roots, boring organisms). The site illustrates how geological structure (concordant/discordant coastline, rock resistance, joint patterns) controls the rate and pattern of landform development. The Jurassic Coast demonstrates coastal landforms from 185 million years of geological history in just 155 km.

Geo and Blow Hole

  • Geo: A narrow, steep-sided coastal inlet formed when a cave roof collapses or a major joint/fault is eroded by wave action. Common on well-jointed rock coastlines
  • Blow hole: A vertical shaft formed when wave pressure exploits a weakness in the roof of a sea cave, eventually breaking through to the surface. During storms, air and spray are forced up through the blow hole with considerable force. Example: Blow Hole, Flamborough Head, East Yorkshire

Concordant and Discordant Coastlines

  • Discordant coastline: Rock bands run perpendicular to the coast. Differential erosion of hard and soft rock creates alternating headlands and bays. Example: the Purbeck coast around Swanage Bay — resistant chalk forms Ballard Point headland while softer clay/sands erode to form Swanage Bay
  • Concordant coastline: Rock bands run parallel to the coast. If the outer resistant rock band is breached by wave action, the softer rock behind erodes rapidly to form a cove (rounded bay). Example: Lulworth Cove, Dorset
Case Study: Lulworth Cove, Dorset
A textbook example of a concordant coastline landform. The outer band of very resistant Portland limestone (hard, resistant to erosion) runs parallel to the coast. At Lulworth, this limestone band was breached by wave action at a point of weakness. Behind it, the much softer Wealden clay was rapidly eroded into a near-circular cove (~500 m wide). The back wall of the cove is formed by more resistant chalk, which slows further inland erosion. Nearby Stair Hole shows an earlier stage in cove development: the limestone barrier is being breached in several places, and small coves are forming in the clay behind. Together, Lulworth and Stair Hole demonstrate the progressive sequence of concordant coastline evolution. The distinctive scallop shape of the cove results from wave refraction within the breached bay, which distributes erosive energy evenly around the circumference.

Coastal Management Strategies

Coastal management: Strategies used to protect coastlines from erosion and flooding, while balancing economic, social, environmental, and political considerations. The fundamental choice is between fighting natural processes (hard engineering), working with them (soft engineering), or allowing retreat (managed realignment / no active intervention).

Shoreline Management Plans (SMPs)

SMP: A long-term strategic plan for managing flood and erosion risk along a stretch of coastline (typically one sediment cell). Developed by the Environment Agency and local authorities, they assess the economic, social, and environmental value of each section of coast and assign one of four policies for three time periods (0–20, 20–50, and 50–100 years into the future).
PolicyMeaningWhere Applied
Hold the lineMaintain or improve current coastal defences to keep the coastline in its present positionHigh-value settlements, infrastructure, industry (e.g. major towns, ports)
Advance the lineBuild new defences seaward of the current coastline (rare and expensive)Very high-value areas requiring new land reclamation (e.g. some ports)
Managed realignment (retreat)Allow the coastline to retreat in a controlled, planned way; may involve breaching existing defencesLow-value agricultural land where habitat creation is beneficial; areas where defending is not cost-effective
No active interventionAllow natural processes to take their course without building or maintaining any defencesRemote, low-value, sparsely populated areas where the cost of defence exceeds the value of what is protected
Case Study: Holderness Coast, East Yorkshire
Europe's fastest-eroding coastline, retreating at an average of 1.8 metres per year (some sections up to 10 m in a single storm event). Over 30 villages listed in the Domesday Book (1086) have been lost to the sea. The cliffs are made of soft, easily eroded boulder clay (glacial till) deposited during the last Ice Age — it is weakly consolidated, impermeable (so easily saturated by rain), and offers almost no resistance to wave erosion. The coast faces the North Sea with a long fetch from the north-east. Key management issues:
  • Mappleton: Two rock groynes and granite rock armour were installed in 1991 at a cost of £2 million to protect the B1242 road and the village of 50 properties. They were effective locally (stabilised the cliffs). However, they starved beaches to the south (particularly at Cowden and Great Cowden) of sediment by intercepting longshore drift, accelerating erosion there from 1.8 m/yr to 3–4 m/yr. This is the terminal groyne effect — a textbook example of how protecting one section of coast can damage another within the same sediment cell.
  • Withernsea: A sea wall and groynes protect the town centre and promenade. Tourism depends on maintaining the beach. "Hold the line" policy.
  • Skipsea: No defences — classified as "no active intervention" in the SMP because the cost of protection exceeds the economic value of the properties at risk. Multiple homes have been lost; residents feel abandoned and argue the policy is socially unjust.
  • Spurn Point spit: Depends on sediment eroded from Holderness cliffs being transported southward by longshore drift. Protecting the entire Holderness coast would starve Spurn of its sediment supply, potentially causing the spit to disappear — demonstrating the interconnectedness of the sediment cell system.
  • Broader lessons: The Holderness case illustrates the conflicts between economic value vs social justice (who decides which communities are "worth" protecting?), local vs strategic management (protecting one place can harm another), and short-term fixes vs long-term sustainability (hard engineering buys time but transfers problems; managed retreat is unpopular but may be the only sustainable option for much of the coast under rising sea levels).

Climate Change and Future Challenges

Climate change poses significant challenges for coastal landscape systems:

  • Sea-level rise: Global mean sea level is rising at ~3.6 mm per year (accelerating) due to thermal expansion of ocean water and melting of ice sheets. Higher sea levels mean waves reach further up the cliff, increasing erosion rates and flooding frequency. The IPCC projects 0.3–1.1 m rise by 2100 depending on emissions scenario
  • Increased storminess: Climate models suggest more frequent and intense storms in the North Atlantic, increasing destructive wave energy and the frequency of storm surge events. The 2013/14 winter storms caused severe coastal damage across southern England
  • Changes to sediment supply: Altered wave patterns and storm tracks may change longshore drift patterns, disrupting established sediment cells and the landforms they sustain
  • Implications for management: Many current defences were designed for historical conditions and may be inadequate for future sea levels and storm intensities. The economic cost of "holding the line" everywhere is unsustainable. Managed retreat may become the default policy for many stretches of coast, raising difficult social and political questions about which communities to protect and which to abandon

Putting It All Together: The Coastal System

Exam Tip: In 20-mark essays, use the systems framework to structure your answer. Show how inputs (wave energy, geology, sediment supply), throughputs (erosion, transport, deposition), and outputs (sediment loss, landform creation) interact to create the landscape. Always include specific place examples with data (e.g. Holderness erodes at 1.8 m/yr; Mappleton groynes cost £2 million). Use correct terminology (dynamic equilibrium, negative/positive feedback, sediment cell, concordant/discordant). Link management strategies back to the system — explain how human intervention disrupts dynamic equilibrium and creates unintended consequences elsewhere in the system. For the highest marks, discuss how landscape systems are being altered by climate change and evaluate whether current management approaches are sustainable in the long term.

Key System Connections

  • Cliff erosion (throughput) provides sediment input → transported by longshore drift → deposited as spits, beaches, bars (stores)
  • Geology (input) determines cliff resistance → controls rate and pattern of erosion → shapes headlands, bays, and cliff profiles
  • Wave type (constructive/destructive) determines whether the coastline is net eroding or net accreting
  • Human management interrupts natural processes → unintended consequences in other parts of the sediment cell (e.g. terminal groyne effect)
  • Climate change alters inputs (more energy, higher sea level) → positive feedback → accelerated change → management challenges
  • The coastal landscape is always evolving — it is a dynamic system, not a static one
