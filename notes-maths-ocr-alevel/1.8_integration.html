1.8 Integration

Mathematics — OCR A-Level (H240) — Paper 1: Pure Mathematics

Integration is the reverse process of differentiation. Where differentiation tells you how a quantity is changing, integration lets you recover the quantity from its rate of change — or, geometrically, compute the area under a curve. The two operations are bound together by the fundamental theorem of calculus, one of the most important results in mathematics. This chapter develops the techniques of indefinite and definite integration — power rule, standard integrals, substitution, integration by parts, partial fractions, and trigonometric identities — and uses them to find areas, volumes of revolution, and solutions to first-order separable differential equations. Every applied problem involving accumulation, total change, work, or volume ultimately rests on integration.

1. Integration as the Reverse of Differentiation

If differentiation takes a function \(F(x)\) and produces its derivative \(f(x) = F'(x)\), then integration runs the process backwards: given \(f(x)\), find a function whose derivative is \(f(x)\). Such a function is called an antiderivative of \(f\). The essential subtlety is that antiderivatives are not unique — adding any constant to an antiderivative still yields an antiderivative, because the derivative of a constant is zero.

Indefinite integral (antiderivative). An antiderivative of \(f(x)\) is any function \(F(x)\) with \(F'(x) = f(x)\). The set of all antiderivatives is denoted \[ \int f(x)\, dx = F(x) + C, \] where \(C\) is an arbitrary constant — the constant of integration. The symbol \(\int\) is an elongated "S" (from "sum"); \(dx\) indicates the variable of integration.

Why the constant? If \(F'(x) = f(x)\) and \(G(x) = F(x) + C\) for any constant \(C\), then \(G'(x) = F'(x) + 0 = f(x)\) as well. So \(F\) and \(G\) are equally valid antiderivatives. Conversely, any two antiderivatives of \(f\) differ by a constant (this is a consequence of the mean value theorem). The "\(+C\)" captures this ambiguity.

Worked Example 1 — Why the constant matters

1.  Consider f(x) = 2x.
2.  F(x) = x²       has derivative 2x ✓
3.  G(x) = x² + 7   has derivative 2x ✓
4.  H(x) = x² − 3.4 has derivative 2x ✓
5.  All three are antiderivatives of 2x — they differ only by a constant.
6.  We capture all of them at once by writing:
7.     ∫ 2x dx = x² + C.

// The +C is mandatory in every indefinite integral. Losing it loses a mark.
Exam tip — always write +C for indefinite integrals. OCR mark schemes list the constant of integration as a standalone awarded mark on almost every indefinite-integral question. Forgetting it is the single most common error at A-level. The only place you don't need \(+C\) is in a definite integral, because the constant cancels when you subtract \(F(a)\) from \(F(b)\).

2. The Power Rule for Integration

Reversing the power rule \(\frac{d}{dx}(x^{n+1}) = (n+1)x^n\) gives the integration power rule. Divide by \(n+1\) to isolate \(x^n\):

Power rule for integration. For any real \(n \neq -1\), \[ \int x^n\, dx = \frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} + C. \] In words: add one to the power, divide by the new power. The exclusion \(n = -1\) is needed because division by zero would occur. The case \(n = -1\) is handled by the logarithm: \[ \int \frac{1}{x}\, dx = \ln|x| + C. \] The absolute value is essential — \(\ln\) is only defined for positive arguments, but \(1/x\) is defined for all \(x \neq 0\).

The power rule extends term-by-term via linearity: for constants \(a, b\) and integrable functions \(f, g\),

\( \displaystyle\int \big[a f(x) + b g(x)\big]\, dx = a\!\int f(x)\, dx + b\!\int g(x)\, dx. \)

Worked Example 2 — Polynomial with mixed powers

Find \(\displaystyle\int \big(3x^2 - 4x + 1\big)\, dx\).

1.  Integrate term by term using the power rule:
2.     ∫ 3x² dx = 3 · (x³/3) = x³.
3.     ∫ −4x dx = −4 · (x²/2) = −2x².
4.     ∫ 1 dx  = x           (since 1 = x⁰, add 1 to get x¹/1.)
5.  Combine and add the constant:
6.     ∫ (3x² − 4x + 1) dx = x³ − 2x² + x + C.
7.  Check by differentiating: d/dx (x³ − 2x² + x + C) = 3x² − 4x + 1 ✓.

// Always verify by differentiating — it catches arithmetic errors in seconds.

A five-second check prevents a lost mark.

4. Definite Integrals and the Fundamental Theorem

An indefinite integral produces a family of antiderivatives. A definite integral produces a number — the net change of an antiderivative between two specified limits, equivalently the (signed) area between the curve and the x-axis.

Definite integral. If \(F\) is an antiderivative of \(f\) on \([a, b]\), then the definite integral of \(f\) from \(a\) to \(b\) is \[ \int_a^b f(x)\, dx = \Big[F(x)\Big]_a^b = F(b) - F(a). \] The numbers \(a\) and \(b\) are the lower and upper limits of integration. No constant of integration is written — it would cancel in \(F(b) + C - (F(a) + C)\).
Fundamental theorem of calculus. If \(f\) is continuous on \([a, b]\), then \(F(x) = \int_a^x f(t)\, dt\) is differentiable on \((a, b)\) and \(F'(x) = f(x)\). Consequently, if \(F\) is any antiderivative of \(f\) on \([a, b]\), then \[ \int_a^b f(x)\, dx = F(b) - F(a). \] This theorem ties the two branches of calculus together: the integral is the inverse of the derivative.

Useful properties of definite integrals:

  • Swap limits, change sign: \(\int_b^a f(x)\, dx = -\int_a^b f(x)\, dx\).
  • Adjacent interval additivity: \(\int_a^b f\, dx + \int_b^c f\, dx = \int_a^c f\, dx\).
  • Zero interval: \(\int_a^a f(x)\, dx = 0\).
  • Linearity: \(\int_a^b [\alpha f + \beta g]\, dx = \alpha \int_a^b f\, dx + \beta \int_a^b g\, dx\).

Worked Example 3 — Evaluate \(\int_1^3 (2x + 1)\, dx\)

1.  Find an antiderivative:
2.     ∫ (2x + 1) dx = x² + x    (+C, but we may drop C for definite integrals).
3.  Apply the limits:
4.     [x² + x]₁³ = (3² + 3) − (1² + 1)
5.                = (9 + 3)   − (1 + 1)
6.                = 12 − 2
7.                = 10.
8.  ∫₁³ (2x + 1) dx = 10.

// Geometric check: this integral is the area of a trapezium under y = 2x + 1
// from x = 1 to x = 3 — heights 3 and 7, width 2, area = ½(3+7)(2) = 10 ✓.

5. Area Under a Curve

If \(f(x) \geq 0\) throughout \([a, b]\), the definite integral \(\int_a^b f(x)\, dx\) equals the area enclosed between the curve \(y = f(x)\), the x-axis, and the vertical lines \(x = a\) and \(x = b\). If \(f(x) < 0\) somewhere on the interval, the integral measures a signed area — portions below the axis contribute negative amounts.

Exam tip — "area" versus "integral" can differ. If the curve dips below the x-axis on the interval of interest, the integral gives signed area (parts below count as negative) while the geometric area is always positive. For geometric area you must (i) find where \(f\) crosses the axis inside \([a, b]\), (ii) split the interval there, and (iii) take the absolute value of each piece before summing. Always sketch the curve first.

Worked Example 4 — Signed vs unsigned area

Compute \(\displaystyle\int_0^3 (x^2 - 2x)\, dx\) and also find the total geometric area between \(y = x^2 - 2x\) and the x-axis on \([0, 3]\).

1.  Antiderivative: ∫ (x² − 2x) dx = x³/3 − x².
2.  Signed integral:
3.     [x³/3 − x²]₀³ = (9 − 9) − (0 − 0) = 0.
4.  The signed integral is ZERO — the positive and negative parts cancel.
5.
6.  Find the zero of the integrand inside [0, 3]:
7.     x² − 2x = x(x − 2) = 0  ⇒  x = 0 or x = 2.
8.  On (0, 2) the curve is BELOW the axis (f(1) = −1 < 0).
9.  On (2, 3) the curve is ABOVE the axis (f(2.5) = 1.25 > 0).
10.
11. Split and take absolute values:
12.    ∫₀² (x² − 2x) dx = (8/3 − 4) − 0 = −4/3   ⇒  |−4/3| = 4/3.
13.    ∫₂³ (x² − 2x) dx = (9 − 9) − (8/3 − 4) = 0 − (−4/3) = 4/3.
14. Total geometric area = 4/3 + 4/3 = 8/3.

// The integral is 0 but the area is 8/3 — two different numbers from the same question.

6. Area Between Curves

To find the area enclosed between two curves \(y = f(x)\) (top) and \(y = g(x)\) (bottom) on \([a, b]\), integrate the difference:

\( \displaystyle\text{Area} = \int_a^b \big[f(x) - g(x)\big]\, dx, \quad \text{where } f \geq g \text{ on } [a, b]. \)

The limits \(a, b\) are usually the x-coordinates of the intersection points, found by solving \(f(x) = g(x)\). If the roles of "top" and "bottom" swap within the interval, split the integral at the crossover.

Worked Example 5 — Area enclosed by \(y = x^2\) and \(y = 4\)

1.  Find intersection points: x² = 4 ⇒ x = ±2.
2.  On (−2, 2) the line y = 4 is ABOVE the parabola y = x².
3.     (top − bottom) = 4 − x².
4.  Area = ∫₋₂² (4 − x²) dx
5.       = [4x − x³/3]₋₂²
6.       = (8 − 8/3) − (−8 + 8/3)
7.       = (24/3 − 8/3) − (−24/3 + 8/3)
8.       = 16/3 − (−16/3)
9.       = 32/3.
10. Area enclosed = 32/3 square units.

// By symmetry of the region in the y-axis, 2 · ∫₀² (4 − x²) dx = 2 · 16/3 = 32/3 ✓.

7. Integration by Substitution

Substitution is the integration analogue of the chain rule. If a composite function \(f(g(x))\) has been multiplied by the derivative \(g'(x)\) of its inner function, the substitution \(u = g(x)\) collapses the integrand into a pure function of \(u\).

Integration by substitution. If \(u = g(x)\) is differentiable with \(du = g'(x)\, dx\), then \[ \int f(g(x))\, g'(x)\, dx = \int f(u)\, du. \] For a definite integral, the limits must also be transformed: when \(x = a\), set \(u = g(a)\); when \(x = b\), set \(u = g(b)\). Equivalently, you may substitute back to \(x\) before applying the original limits.

Recognising when substitution helps: the integrand should contain a function of \(g(x)\) alongside (a constant multiple of) the derivative \(g'(x)\). If you spot \(g'(x)\) multiplying \(f(g(x))\), substitute \(u = g(x)\).

Worked Example 6 — Substitution with \(u = x^2 + 1\)

Find \(\displaystyle\int \frac{2x}{x^2 + 1}\, dx\).

1.  Let u = x² + 1. Then du/dx = 2x, so du = 2x dx.
2.  The integrand contains 2x dx in the numerator — perfect.
3.  Rewrite:
4.     ∫ (2x / (x² + 1)) dx = ∫ (1/u) du.
5.  Integrate:
6.     ∫ (1/u) du = ln|u| + C.
7.  Substitute back:
8.     = ln|x² + 1| + C = ln(x² + 1) + C   (since x² + 1 > 0 always).

// Template: ∫ f'(x) / f(x) dx = ln|f(x)| + C — a very common pattern.

Worked Example 7 — Substitution with \(u = x^2\)

Find \(\displaystyle\int 2x\, e^{x^2}\, dx\).

1.  Let u = x². Then du = 2x dx.
2.  Rewrite:
3.     ∫ 2x e^(x²) dx = ∫ e^u du.
4.  Integrate:
5.     ∫ e^u du = e^u + C.
6.  Back-substitute:
7.     = e^(x²) + C.
8.  Check by differentiation:
9.     d/dx [e^(x²)] = e^(x²) · 2x ✓.

Worked Example 8 — Linear substitution

Find \(\displaystyle\int \cos(3x + 1)\, dx\).

1.  Let u = 3x + 1. Then du = 3 dx, so dx = du/3.
2.  Rewrite:
3.     ∫ cos(3x + 1) dx = ∫ cos u · (1/3) du = (1/3) ∫ cos u du.
4.  Integrate:
5.     (1/3) sin u + C.
6.  Back-substitute:
7.     = (1/3) sin(3x + 1) + C.

// For any LINEAR inner function ax + b, the substitution just adds a factor of 1/a.
Exam tip — for definite integrals, change the limits too. When you substitute \(u = g(x)\) in \(\int_a^b f(g(x)) g'(x)\, dx\), the limits \(a, b\) are values of \(x\). Under the new variable they become \(g(a), g(b)\). Either (i) change the limits and never return to \(x\), or (ii) keep the original limits and substitute back to \(x\) before evaluating. Mixing the two — evaluating \(u\)-limits on an \(x\)-expression — is a frequent and costly error.

8. Integration by Parts

Parts is the integration analogue of the product rule. Starting from \((uv)' = u'v + uv'\) and integrating both sides:

Integration by parts. If \(u, v\) are differentiable functions of \(x\), then \[ \int u\, \frac{dv}{dx}\, dx = uv - \int v\, \frac{du}{dx}\, dx, \] often written compactly as \(\int u\, dv = uv - \int v\, du\). The technique trades one integral for another; for it to help, the new integral \(\int v\, du\) must be simpler than the original.

Choosing \(u\) and \(dv\): you want \(u\) to simplify on differentiation, and \(dv\) to remain integrable. A standard mnemonic is LIATE:

\( \text{L}\text{ogarithmic} \prec \text{I}\text{nverse trig} \prec \text{A}\text{lgebraic} \prec \text{T}\text{rig} \prec \text{E}\text{xponential} \)

Whichever type appears earlier in this list, set it as \(u\). The remaining factor becomes \(dv\).

Integrand typeLet \(u =\)Let \(dv =\)Reason
\(x^n \ln x\)\(\ln x\) (L)\(x^n\, dx\)\(\ln x\) simplifies on differentiation.
\(x^n e^{kx}\)\(x^n\) (A)\(e^{kx}\, dx\)\(x^n\) becomes a polynomial of lower degree.
\(x^n \sin x\) or \(x^n \cos x\)\(x^n\) (A)trig \(dx\)Polynomial degree decreases.
\(\ln x\) (alone)\(\ln x\) (L)\(dx\)Differentiating \(\ln x\) gives \(1/x\).

Worked Example 9 — \(\int x\, e^x\, dx\)

1.  LIATE: algebraic x before exponential e^x, so let u = x, dv = e^x dx.
2.  Then du = dx and v = ∫ e^x dx = e^x.
3.  Apply parts:
4.     ∫ x e^x dx = uv − ∫ v du
5.                = x e^x − ∫ e^x dx
6.                = x e^x − e^x + C
7.                = e^x (x − 1) + C.
8.  Check: d/dx [e^x (x − 1)] = e^x (x − 1) + e^x = x e^x ✓.

Worked Example 10 — \(\int x \cos x\, dx\)

1.  LIATE: algebraic before trig. u = x, dv = cos x dx.
2.  du = dx;  v = sin x.
3.  Parts:
4.     ∫ x cos x dx = x sin x − ∫ sin x dx
5.                   = x sin x − (−cos x) + C
6.                   = x sin x + cos x + C.
7.  Check: d/dx [x sin x + cos x] = sin x + x cos x − sin x = x cos x ✓.

Worked Example 11 — \(\int \ln x\, dx\) (the "trick")

1.  No obvious product — but write ln x = (ln x) · 1.
2.  Let u = ln x, dv = 1 dx.
3.  du = (1/x) dx;  v = x.
4.  Parts:
5.     ∫ ln x dx = x ln x − ∫ x · (1/x) dx
6.               = x ln x − ∫ 1 dx
7.               = x ln x − x + C
8.               = x (ln x − 1) + C.
9.  Check: d/dx [x ln x − x] = ln x + 1 − 1 = ln x ✓.

// The "dv = dx" trick works for any single logarithm or inverse trig.

Worked Example 12 — \(\int x^2 e^x\, dx\) (parts twice)

1.  First pass. u = x², dv = e^x dx  ⇒  du = 2x dx, v = e^x.
2.     ∫ x² e^x dx = x² e^x − ∫ 2x e^x dx
3.                 = x² e^x − 2 ∫ x e^x dx.
4.  Use Worked Example 9: ∫ x e^x dx = e^x (x − 1) + C₁.
5.  Substitute:
6.     ∫ x² e^x dx = x² e^x − 2 [ e^x (x − 1) ] + C
7.                 = x² e^x − 2x e^x + 2 e^x + C
8.                 = e^x (x² − 2x + 2) + C.
9.  Check: d/dx [e^x (x² − 2x + 2)] = e^x (x² − 2x + 2) + e^x (2x − 2)
10.    = e^x [x² − 2x + 2 + 2x − 2] = x² e^x ✓.

// Rule of thumb: ∫ xⁿ e^(kx) dx needs parts applied n times.
Exam tip — LIATE is a guideline, not a law. It works in the vast majority of A-level integrals, but if LIATE gives an integrand that does not simplify, try the other way. The real test is whether \(\int v\, du\) is simpler than \(\int u\, dv\). Also, for integrands like \(\int x \ln x\, dx\), LIATE sends you to \(u = \ln x\) (correct), but always double-check by trying to integrate the alternative to see if it terminates.

9. Integration Using Partial Fractions

A rational function \(P(x)/Q(x)\) (with \(\deg P < \deg Q\)) can often be decomposed into a sum of simpler fractions. Each piece integrates to a logarithm or power. For distinct linear factors in the denominator:

\( \dfrac{f(x)}{(x - a)(x - b)} = \dfrac{A}{x - a} + \dfrac{B}{x - b}, \quad \int \dfrac{1}{x - a}\, dx = \ln|x - a| + C. \)

Worked Example 13 — Partial fractions

Find \(\displaystyle\int \frac{3x + 5}{(x - 1)(x + 2)}\, dx\).

1.  Write: (3x + 5) / [(x − 1)(x + 2)] = A/(x − 1) + B/(x + 2).
2.  Multiply through:
3.     3x + 5 = A(x + 2) + B(x − 1).
4.  Cover-up at x = 1:   3(1) + 5 = A(3)   ⇒  8 = 3A  ⇒  A = 8/3.
5.  Cover-up at x = −2:  3(−2) + 5 = B(−3) ⇒ −1 = −3B ⇒  B = 1/3.
6.  Rewrite the integrand:
7.     (3x + 5) / [(x − 1)(x + 2)] = (8/3)/(x − 1) + (1/3)/(x + 2).
8.  Integrate term by term:
9.     ∫ ... dx = (8/3) ln|x − 1| + (1/3) ln|x + 2| + C.

// Always write the cover-up values at the "bad" points of the denominator.
// Combine the two logs if asked: = (1/3) ln|(x − 1)⁸ (x + 2)| + C.

10. Integration Using Trigonometric Identities

Many trigonometric integrands don't match the standard forms directly, but they can be rewritten using identities. The most useful identities for integration are:

IdentityUseful for
\(\cos^2 x = \tfrac{1}{2}(1 + \cos 2x)\)\(\int \cos^2 x\, dx\)
\(\sin^2 x = \tfrac{1}{2}(1 - \cos 2x)\)\(\int \sin^2 x\, dx\)
\(\sin 2x = 2\sin x \cos x\)\(\int \sin x \cos x\, dx\)
\(\sec^2 x = 1 + \tan^2 x\)\(\int \tan^2 x\, dx\)
Product-to-sum, e.g. \(\sin A \cos B = \tfrac{1}{2}[\sin(A + B) + \sin(A - B)]\)\(\int \sin(mx)\cos(nx)\, dx\)

Worked Example 14 — \(\int \cos^2 x\, dx\)

1.  Apply the double-angle identity cos²x = (1 + cos 2x)/2.
2.  Rewrite:
3.     ∫ cos²x dx = ∫ ½(1 + cos 2x) dx
4.               = ½ ∫ 1 dx + ½ ∫ cos 2x dx
5.               = ½ x + ½ · ½ sin 2x + C
6.               = x/2 + (sin 2x)/4 + C.
7.  Check: d/dx [x/2 + (sin 2x)/4] = 1/2 + (cos 2x)/2 = (1 + cos 2x)/2 = cos²x ✓.

// The same trick with sin²x = (1 − cos 2x)/2 gives ∫ sin²x dx = x/2 − (sin 2x)/4 + C.

Worked Example 15 — Product-to-sum

Find \(\displaystyle\int \sin(2x)\cos(3x)\, dx\).

1.  Apply product-to-sum:
2.     sin A cos B = ½ [sin(A + B) + sin(A − B)].
3.     sin(2x) cos(3x) = ½ [sin(5x) + sin(−x)]
4.                     = ½ sin(5x) − ½ sin x.        (sin(−x) = −sin x)
5.  Integrate:
6.     ∫ sin(2x) cos(3x) dx = ∫ [½ sin(5x) − ½ sin x] dx
7.                         = ½ · (−1/5) cos(5x) − ½ · (−cos x) + C
8.                         = −(1/10) cos(5x) + ½ cos x + C.
Exam tip — cos²x and sin²x cannot be integrated directly. Students often try \(\int \cos^2 x\, dx = \frac{\cos^3 x}{3}\) (applying the power rule as if \(\cos x\) were \(x\)) — this is wrong, because differentiating \(\cos^3 x / 3\) gives \(-\cos^2 x \sin x\), not \(\cos^2 x\). The only A-level-safe method is to linearise with the double-angle identity first.

11. Volumes of Revolution

Rotating the region between a curve \(y = f(x)\), the x-axis, and the lines \(x = a\), \(x = b\) through a full turn about the x-axis sweeps out a solid whose volume can be computed by integration. The thin-disk approximation: each thin strip of width \(dx\) at position \(x\) becomes a disk of radius \(y = f(x)\) and thickness \(dx\), contributing volume \(\pi y^2\, dx\). Integrating gives:

Solid of revolution — volume formula. The solid obtained by rotating the curve \(y = f(x)\) around the x-axis from \(x = a\) to \(x = b\) has volume \[ V = \pi \int_a^b y^2\, dx = \pi \int_a^b \big[f(x)\big]^2\, dx. \] For rotation about the y-axis of the curve \(x = g(y)\) from \(y = c\) to \(y = d\), \[ V = \pi \int_c^d x^2\, dy = \pi \int_c^d \big[g(y)\big]^2\, dy. \]
Axis of revolutionVariable of integrationVolume formula
x-axis\(x\)\(V = \pi \int_a^b y^2\, dx\)
y-axis\(y\)\(V = \pi \int_c^d x^2\, dy\)

Worked Example 16 — Volume of revolution of \(y = \sin x\) about the x-axis, \(0 \le x \le \pi\)

1.  Apply the disk formula:
2.     V = π ∫₀^π y² dx = π ∫₀^π sin²x dx.
3.  Use sin²x = (1 − cos 2x)/2:
4.     V = π ∫₀^π ½(1 − cos 2x) dx
5.       = (π/2) [x − (sin 2x)/2]₀^π.
6.  Evaluate:
7.     at x = π:  π − (sin 2π)/2 = π − 0 = π.
8.     at x = 0:  0 − (sin 0)/2  = 0.
9.  V = (π/2) · π = π²/2.

// Volume of the "lemon" obtained by rotating one arch of a sine about the x-axis.
// Numerical: π²/2 ≈ 4.9348 cubic units.

12. First-Order Separable Differential Equations

A differential equation relates a function to its derivative(s). The simplest useful case is a first-order separable equation in which the derivative factors into a function of \(x\) times a function of \(y\):

First-order separable differential equation. An equation of the form \[ \frac{dy}{dx} = f(x)\, g(y) \] is called separable. Separate the variables by treating \(dy/dx\) as a quotient of differentials: \[ \frac{1}{g(y)}\, dy = f(x)\, dx \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \int \frac{1}{g(y)}\, dy = \int f(x)\, dx + C. \] The resulting equation (after integrating both sides) is called the general solution. An initial condition \(y(x_0) = y_0\) pins \(C\) down to give the particular solution.

Worked Example 17 — Solve \(dy/dx = 2y\) with \(y(0) = 3\)

1.  Separate variables (valid while y ≠ 0):
2.     (1/y) dy = 2 dx.
3.  Integrate both sides:
4.     ∫ (1/y) dy = ∫ 2 dx
5.     ln|y| = 2x + C.
6.  Exponentiate:
7.     |y| = e^(2x + C) = e^C · e^(2x).
8.     y = A · e^(2x)        (A = ±e^C, any nonzero constant.)
9.  Apply the initial condition y(0) = 3:
10.    3 = A · e^0 = A  ⇒  A = 3.
11. Particular solution: y = 3 e^(2x).
12. Check: dy/dx = 6 e^(2x) = 2 · 3 e^(2x) = 2y ✓. y(0) = 3 ✓.

// dy/dx proportional to y gives exponential growth — the classic population /
// radioactive-decay model.

Worked Example 18 — Solve \(dy/dx = xy\) with \(y(0) = 1\)

1.  Separate:
2.     (1/y) dy = x dx.
3.  Integrate:
4.     ∫ (1/y) dy = ∫ x dx
5.     ln|y| = x²/2 + C.
6.  Exponentiate:
7.     y = A · e^(x²/2).
8.  Initial condition y(0) = 1:
9.     1 = A · e^0  ⇒  A = 1.
10. Particular solution:  y = e^(x²/2).
11. Check: dy/dx = e^(x²/2) · x = x y ✓.

13. Applications in Mechanics (SUVAT Links)

Integration provides the link between acceleration, velocity, and displacement for a particle in motion along a line. If \(a(t)\) is the acceleration as a function of time:

\( v(t) = \displaystyle\int a(t)\, dt + C_1, \qquad s(t) = \int v(t)\, dt + C_2. \)

The constants \(C_1, C_2\) are fixed by initial conditions (usually \(v(0) = u\) the initial velocity, and \(s(0) = 0\) the starting position). For constant acceleration \(a\), integrating gives the familiar SUVAT equations:

\( v = u + a t, \qquad s = u t + \tfrac{1}{2} a t^2. \)

The power of integration is that it handles variable acceleration too. If \(a(t) = 6 - 2t\) and a particle starts from rest, then \(v(t) = 6t - t^2\) and \(s(t) = 3t^2 - t^3/3\) — no SUVAT formula applies, but integration still gives the answer immediately.

Worked Example 19 — \(\int x \ln x\, dx\) (parts with LIATE)

1.  LIATE: logarithmic before algebraic, so u = ln x, dv = x dx.
2.  du = (1/x) dx;  v = x²/2.
3.  Parts:
4.     ∫ x ln x dx = (x²/2) ln x − ∫ (x²/2)(1/x) dx
5.                 = (x²/2) ln x − ∫ (x/2) dx
6.                 = (x²/2) ln x − x²/4 + C
7.                 = (x²/4)(2 ln x − 1) + C.
8.  Check: d/dx [(x²/2) ln x − x²/4] = x ln x + x²/2 · (1/x) − x/2
9.                                  = x ln x + x/2 − x/2 = x ln x ✓.

Chapter-wide checklist for Integration.
  • Always write \(+C\) on every indefinite integral.
  • For definite integrals, no \(C\) — but always show \([F(x)]_a^b\) and the subtraction \(F(b) - F(a)\).
  • Substitution: change the limits if it's a definite integral, or substitute back if indefinite.
  • Parts: use LIATE to pick \(u\). Check whether \(\int v\, du\) is actually simpler.
  • Sign of area: if the curve dips below the axis, split the integral at the zeros.
  • For area between curves: find intersection points first, then (top − bottom).
  • \(\cos^2 x\) and \(\sin^2 x\): always linearise with the double-angle formula before integrating.
  • Volumes of revolution: it is \(y^2\), not \(y\) — remember to square \(f(x)\).
  • Differential equations: after integrating, always apply the initial condition to fix \(C\).
  • Check by differentiation whenever time permits — a 10-second sanity check.
Typical OCR examiner complaints (from mark reports).
  • Forgetting \(+C\) in indefinite integrals — a reliable way to lose an easy mark.
  • Changing the variable via substitution but forgetting to change the limits, or evaluating \(u\)-limits on an \(x\)-expression.
  • Choosing \(u\) and \(dv\) the wrong way round in integration by parts and then getting stuck in a harder integral.
  • Integrating \(\cos^2 x\) as \(\cos^3 x / 3\) (wrong — not a power of \(x\)).
  • In volumes of revolution, integrating \(y\) instead of \(y^2\), or forgetting the factor of \(\pi\).
  • For "area between a curve and the x-axis", failing to check whether the curve crosses the axis — taking the integral alone when the answer required the geometric area.
  • In partial fractions, cover-up arithmetic errors, or decomposing a non-proper rational function (where \(\deg P \ge \deg Q\)) without first doing polynomial division.
  • Solving a differential equation but forgetting to apply the initial condition — leaving \(C\) unspecified when the question demanded the particular solution.
Formula snapshot (memorise).
  • Power rule: \(\int x^n\, dx = x^{n+1}/(n+1) + C\) (for \(n \neq -1\)). \(\int 1/x\, dx = \ln|x| + C\).
  • Trig: \(\int \sin x\, dx = -\cos x + C\); \(\int \cos x\, dx = \sin x + C\); \(\int \sec^2 x\, dx = \tan x + C\).
  • Exp: \(\int e^x\, dx = e^x + C\); \(\int e^{kx}\, dx = (1/k) e^{kx} + C\).
  • Linear inside: \(\int f(ax + b)\, dx = (1/a) F(ax + b) + C\).
  • Definite: \(\int_a^b f(x)\, dx = F(b) - F(a)\).
  • Substitution: \(u = g(x)\), \(du = g'(x)\, dx\). Change limits for definite.
  • Parts: \(\int u\, dv = uv - \int v\, du\). Choose \(u\) by LIATE.
  • Partial fractions: decompose, then each piece integrates to \(\ln\) or a power.
  • \(\cos^2 x = \tfrac{1}{2}(1 + \cos 2x)\); \(\sin^2 x = \tfrac{1}{2}(1 - \cos 2x)\).
  • Volume of revolution: \(V = \pi \int_a^b y^2\, dx\) (about x-axis); \(V = \pi \int_c^d x^2\, dy\) (about y-axis).
  • Separable ODE: \(\int dy/g(y) = \int f(x)\, dx + C\); apply initial condition.
