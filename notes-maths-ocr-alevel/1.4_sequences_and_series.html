1.4 Sequences & Series

Mathematics — OCR A-Level (H240) — Paper 1: Pure Mathematics

A sequence is an ordered list of numbers, each one called a term. A series is the sum produced by adding those terms together. The study of sequences and series lies at the centre of pure mathematics: arithmetic and geometric progressions appear in compound interest, population models, and physics problems; sigma notation compresses large sums into a single symbol; the binomial theorem extends polynomial expansion from exponents like \((1+x)^5\) into the infinite world of \((1+x)^{1/2}\) or \((1+x)^{-3}\). This topic also introduces the idea of a limit — the value a sequence approaches as more and more terms are produced — which foreshadows calculus.

1. Sequences — Notation and Definition

A sequence is a function whose domain is the positive integers (or sometimes \(\{0, 1, 2, \dots\}\)). The \(n\)th term is denoted \(a_n\) or \(u_n\); the whole sequence is written \((a_n)_{n \geq 1}\) or simply \(a_1, a_2, a_3, \dots\).

Sequence. An ordered list of numbers \(a_1, a_2, a_3, \dots\) indexed by a positive integer \(n\). Each number \(a_n\) is a term; \(n\) is the position or index of that term. A sequence may be finite (with a last term) or infinite (continuing indefinitely).

Two Ways to Specify a Sequence

There are two standard ways to describe a sequence:

  1. Position-to-term formula — an explicit expression \(a_n = f(n)\) giving the \(n\)th term directly. Example: \(a_n = 3n + 2\) gives \(5, 8, 11, 14, \dots\).
  2. Term-to-term (recurrence) formula — a rule \(a_{n+1} = g(a_n)\) expressing each term in terms of the preceding one(s), together with an initial term. Example: \(a_{n+1} = a_n + 3\) with \(a_1 = 5\) gives the same sequence \(5, 8, 11, 14, \dots\).
Recurrence relation. An equation \(a_{n+1} = g(a_n)\) (or involving more earlier terms) that expresses each term of a sequence as a function of one or more preceding terms. Together with initial condition(s) this uniquely determines the sequence.
Exam tip — deducing a formula from the first few terms. If a sequence begins \(2, 5, 8, 11, 14, \dots\) and you spot a common difference \(d = 3\), the explicit formula is \(a_n = a_1 + (n-1)d = 2 + 3(n-1) = 3n - 1\). Always verify your guess on at least two terms (e.g. \(n = 1\) gives \(2\) ✓; \(n = 4\) gives \(11\) ✓). Do not just check the first term — many different formulae agree at \(n = 1\).

Worked Example 1 — Converting between position-to-term and term-to-term

A sequence is defined by \(a_{n+1} = 2a_n - 1\) with \(a_1 = 2\). Compute \(a_1, \dots, a_5\) and find a position-to-term formula.

1.  Compute terms:
2.     a₁ = 2
3.     a₂ = 2·2 − 1 = 3
4.     a₃ = 2·3 − 1 = 5
5.     a₄ = 2·5 − 1 = 9
6.     a₅ = 2·9 − 1 = 17.
7.  Differences 1, 2, 4, 8 double → suggests 2ⁿ involvement.
8.  Try aₙ = 2^(n−1) + 1:
9.     n=1: 1 + 1 = 2 ✓
10.    n=2: 2 + 1 = 3 ✓
11.    n=3: 4 + 1 = 5 ✓
12.    n=4: 8 + 1 = 9 ✓
13. ∴ aₙ = 2^(n−1) + 1.      // verified explicit formula

2. Arithmetic Progressions (AP)

An arithmetic progression (AP) is a sequence in which each term is obtained from the previous one by adding a fixed constant, called the common difference.

Arithmetic progression. A sequence \(a_1, a_2, a_3, \dots\) such that \(a_{n+1} - a_n = d\) for all \(n\), where \(d\) is a fixed real constant (the common difference). Equivalently, the \(n\)th term is \[ a_n = a + (n-1)d, \] where \(a = a_1\) is the first term.

Sum of an AP

Let \(S_n = a_1 + a_2 + \dots + a_n\) denote the sum of the first \(n\) terms. Writing the sum forwards and backwards and adding term-by-term gives Gauss's formula:

\( S_n = \dfrac{n}{2}\bigl[2a + (n-1)d\bigr] = \dfrac{n}{2}(a + l), \)

where \(l = a_n = a + (n-1)d\) is the last (or \(n\)th) term. The second form is especially useful when both endpoints of the sum are known.

Exam tip — which AP sum formula to use. Use \(S_n = \tfrac{n}{2}(a + l)\) when you already know the first and last term of the sum (e.g. "sum all integers from 1 to 100"). Use \(S_n = \tfrac{n}{2}[2a + (n-1)d]\) when you know the first term and the common difference but not the last term. Both give the same answer — pick whichever avoids extra arithmetic.

Worked Example 2 — Sum of the first 100 positive integers

Compute \(1 + 2 + 3 + \dots + 100\).

1.  This is an AP:  a = 1,  d = 1,  n = 100,  l = 100.
2.  Using S_n = n/2 · (a + l):
3.     S₁₀₀ = 100/2 · (1 + 100) = 50 · 101 = 5050.
4.  Check via other formula:
5.     S₁₀₀ = 100/2 · [2·1 + 99·1] = 50 · 101 = 5050 ✓.

// Gauss famously produced this answer as a schoolboy by pairing
// 1+100, 2+99, ..., 50+51 — fifty pairs each summing to 101.

Worked Example 3 — AP with known first and last terms

An AP has 20 terms, first term \(5\) and last term \(100\). Find the common difference and the sum.

1.  Last term:  a_n = a + (n − 1)d.
2.     100 = 5 + 19d   ⇒   19d = 95   ⇒   d = 5.
3.  Sum:  S_n = n/2 · (a + l)
4.     S₂₀ = 20/2 · (5 + 100) = 10 · 105 = 1050.
5.  Quick sanity check — average term × number of terms:
6.     mean = (5 + 100)/2 = 52.5;   52.5 × 20 = 1050 ✓.

Applications of APs

  • Salary with fixed annual raise. A worker earns £25 000 in year 1 with a £1 000 annual increase. Lifetime earnings over 30 years form an AP.
  • Pile of logs / cans. A triangular stack with \(1\) log on top, \(2\) in the next row, \(\dots\), \(n\) at the base has total \(\tfrac{n}{2}(1 + n)\).
  • Walking / driving with constant change. First day walk \(5\) km, each day walk \(0.5\) km more — total distance after \(n\) days is an AP sum.

3. Geometric Progressions (GP)

A geometric progression (GP) is a sequence in which each term is obtained by multiplying the previous one by a fixed non-zero constant called the common ratio.

Geometric progression. A sequence \(a_1, a_2, a_3, \dots\) such that \(a_{n+1}/a_n = r\) for all \(n\), where \(r \neq 0\) is a fixed real constant (the common ratio). The \(n\)th term is \[ a_n = a \cdot r^{n-1}, \] where \(a = a_1\).

Sum of a GP

Let \(S_n = a + ar + ar^2 + \dots + ar^{n-1}\). Multiplying by \(r\) gives \(rS_n = ar + ar^2 + \dots + ar^n\). Subtracting:

\( S_n = \dfrac{a(r^n - 1)}{r - 1} = \dfrac{a(1 - r^n)}{1 - r}, \qquad r \neq 1. \)

The two forms are algebraically identical; the first is neater when \(|r| > 1\), the second when \(|r| < 1\).

Sum to Infinity — Convergence

If \(|r| < 1\) then \(r^n \to 0\) as \(n \to \infty\), and the infinite sum has a finite value:

\( S_\infty = \dfrac{a}{1 - r}, \qquad \text{valid only if } |r| < 1. \)

If \(|r| \geq 1\) the partial sums do not settle to any single number — we say the series diverges and \(S_\infty\) is undefined.

Convergence of a GP. An infinite geometric series \(a + ar + ar^2 + \dots\) converges if and only if \(|r| < 1\). When it converges, its sum is \(S_\infty = a/(1 - r)\). When \(|r| \geq 1\) (and \(a \neq 0\)) it diverges — the partial sums either grow without bound or oscillate.
Limit of a sequence. The sequence \((a_n)\) has limit \(L\) (written \(a_n \to L\) or \(\lim_{n \to \infty} a_n = L\)) if, informally, \(a_n\) can be made arbitrarily close to \(L\) by taking \(n\) sufficiently large. If no such \(L\) exists, the sequence diverges.
Exam tip — check convergence before summing. Every year OCR scripts lose marks by blindly applying \(S_\infty = a/(1-r)\) without verifying \(|r| < 1\). If the ratio is \(r = 2\) or \(r = -3\), the infinite sum is undefined — writing down \(a/(1-r)\) anyway will score zero on that part. Always state the validity condition explicitly in your answer.

Worked Example 4 — GP with known sum

A GP has 5 terms with first term \(2\) and sum \(62\). Find the common ratio (\(r > 0\)).

1.  S_n = a(r^n − 1)/(r − 1):
2.     62 = 2(r⁵ − 1)/(r − 1).
3.  ⇒ 31 = (r⁵ − 1)/(r − 1) = 1 + r + r² + r³ + r⁴.
4.  Try r = 2:  1 + 2 + 4 + 8 + 16 = 31 ✓.
5.  ∴ r = 2.     // common ratio
6.  Verify series: 2, 4, 8, 16, 32. Sum = 62 ✓.

Worked Example 5 — Sum to infinity

A convergent GP has sum to infinity \(8\) and first term \(6\). Find the common ratio, and write down the first four terms.

1.  S∞ = a/(1 − r):
2.     8 = 6/(1 − r)  ⇒  1 − r = 6/8 = 3/4  ⇒  r = 1/4.
3.  Check convergence:  |r| = 1/4 < 1 ✓ — valid.
4.  First four terms:  6, 6·(1/4) = 3/2, 3/2·(1/4) = 3/8, 3/8·(1/4) = 3/32.
5.  Sum check:  6 + 1.5 + 0.375 + 0.09375 + ... ≈ 8 ✓.

Applications of GPs

  • Compound interest. £1000 invested at 5% per year grows as \(1000, 1000(1.05), 1000(1.05)^2, \dots\) — a GP with \(r = 1.05\).
  • Radioactive decay. Mass remaining after \(n\) half-lives is \(M_0, M_0/2, M_0/4, \dots\) — a GP with \(r = 1/2\).
  • Repeating decimals as fractions. \(0.\overline{3} = 0.3 + 0.03 + 0.003 + \dots = \tfrac{0.3}{1 - 0.1} = \tfrac{1}{3}\).
  • Bouncing ball. A ball dropped from \(h\) metres rebounds to a fraction \(r\) of its previous height, so total distance travelled is a converging GP sum.

4. Sigma Notation

Sigma notation compresses long sums into a compact symbol. The Greek capital sigma \(\Sigma\) means "add up".

\( \displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{n} f(k) \;=\; f(1) + f(2) + f(3) + \dots + f(n). \)

Here \(k\) is the dummy index (any letter will do — \(i, j, r\) are common alternatives). The lower limit is the starting value; the upper limit is the last value (inclusive). Changing the letter does not change the sum.

Standard Sums

SumClosed formUsed for
\(\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{n} 1\)\(n\)Counting
\(\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{n} k\)\(\dfrac{n(n+1)}{2}\)AP sum (natural numbers)
\(\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{n} k^2\)\(\dfrac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6}\)Sum of squares — used in Pearson correlation, moment of inertia
\(\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{n} k^3\)\(\left[\dfrac{n(n+1)}{2}\right]^2\)Sum of cubes — equals \((\sum k)^2\), a surprising identity

Linearity of Sigma

Sums distribute over addition and scalar multiplication:

\( \displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{n} [\,\alpha f(k) + \beta g(k)\,] = \alpha \sum_{k=1}^{n} f(k) + \beta \sum_{k=1}^{n} g(k). \)

This is used to split a complicated sum into pieces you can evaluate using standard results.

Changing Limits

To compute \(\sum_{k=m}^{n}\) where \(m > 1\), subtract:

\( \displaystyle\sum_{k=m}^{n} f(k) = \sum_{k=1}^{n} f(k) - \sum_{k=1}^{m-1} f(k). \)

Worked Example 6 — Using standard sum results

Evaluate \(\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{20} (3k^2 - 2k + 1).\)

1.  Split using linearity:
2.     Σ (3k² − 2k + 1) = 3 Σ k² − 2 Σ k + Σ 1.
3.  Apply standard results with n = 20:
4.     Σ k²  = 20·21·41/6 = 2870.
5.     Σ k   = 20·21/2    = 210.
6.     Σ 1   = 20.
7.  Combine:
8.     3(2870) − 2(210) + 20
9.     = 8610 − 420 + 20
10.    = 8210.
11. ∴ Σ_{k=1..20} (3k² − 2k + 1) = 8210.
Exam tip — watch the index start. \(\sum_{k=0}^{n}\) has \(n + 1\) terms, not \(n\). \(\sum_{k=1}^{n}\) has \(n\) terms. A frequent slip is reading a GP problem as "five terms starting with \(k = 0\)" and using \(n = 5\) in the formula when the correct value is \(n = 5\) with \(a = a_0\), but still five terms. Count the terms explicitly before plugging in.

5. Binomial Expansion — Positive Integer Index

The binomial theorem gives an expansion of \((1 + x)^n\) when \(n\) is a non-negative integer. The coefficients are the binomial coefficients, appearing in Pascal's triangle.

Binomial coefficient. For non-negative integers \(n\) and \(k\) with \(0 \leq k \leq n\), \[ \binom{n}{k} = \;^nC_k = \dfrac{n!}{k!\,(n-k)!}, \] read "\(n\) choose \(k\)". It counts the number of ways to choose \(k\) objects from \(n\) distinguishable objects and is always a positive integer.

Binomial Theorem (Positive Integer \(n\))

\( (1 + x)^n = \displaystyle\sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} x^k = 1 + \binom{n}{1} x + \binom{n}{2} x^2 + \dots + \binom{n}{n} x^n. \)

For a general binomial \((a + b)^n\):

\( (a + b)^n = \displaystyle\sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} a^{n-k} b^k. \)

For a positive integer \(n\) the expansion has exactly \(n + 1\) terms and terminates. No convergence condition is needed: the expansion is a finite polynomial identity, true for all real \(x\).

Exam tip — finding a specific term. In \((a + b)^n\) the term containing \(b^k\) is \(\binom{n}{k} a^{n-k} b^k\). For example, the \(x^3\) term in \((1 + 2x)^6\) comes from \(k = 3\): coefficient \(= \binom{6}{3} \cdot 1^{3} \cdot 2^3 = 20 \cdot 8 = 160\), so the term is \(160 x^3\). You rarely need the whole expansion — just isolate the term you want.

Worked Example 8 — Specific term in (3 − x)⁸

Find the coefficient of \(x^4\) in the expansion of \((3 - x)^8\).

1.  General term:  C(8, k) · 3^(8−k) · (−x)^k  =  C(8, k) · 3^(8−k) · (−1)^k · x^k.

6.  ∴ coefficient of x⁴ is 5670.

6. Binomial Expansion — Rational / Negative Index

For a real (not necessarily integer) exponent \(n\), the binomial series still has a meaning, but as an infinite series that only converges for \(|x| < 1\).

\( (1 + x)^n = 1 + nx + \dfrac{n(n-1)}{2!} x^2 + \dfrac{n(n-1)(n-2)}{3!} x^3 + \dots, \qquad |x| < 1. \)

Notice two differences from the integer case:

  • The expansion is infinite — it does not terminate because \(n(n-1)(n-2)\cdots(n-k+1)\) is never zero when \(n\) is not a non-negative integer.
  • It is only valid for \(|x| < 1\). Outside this range the series diverges and gives nonsense.
Generalised binomial coefficient. For any real \(n\) and non-negative integer \(k\), \[ \binom{n}{k} = \dfrac{n(n-1)(n-2)\cdots(n-k+1)}{k!}. \] When \(n\) is a non-negative integer and \(k > n\) one of the factors in the numerator is zero, so \(\binom{n}{k} = 0\), recovering the terminating case.

Expanding \((a + bx)^n\)

When the constant inside the brackets is not \(1\), factor it out first:

\( (a + bx)^n = a^n \left(1 + \dfrac{bx}{a}\right)^n, \qquad \text{valid for } \left|\dfrac{bx}{a}\right| < 1, \text{ i.e. } |x| < |a/b|. \)

Then expand \((1 + u)^n\) with \(u = bx/a\) and multiply through by \(a^n\).

Worked Example 9 — First four terms of (1 + 3x)⁻²

Find the first four terms of the expansion of \((1 + 3x)^{-2}\) in ascending powers of \(x\), and state the range of \(x\) for which it is valid.

1.  General expansion:
2.     (1 + u)^n = 1 + nu + n(n−1)/2! · u² + n(n−1)(n−2)/3! · u³ + ...
3.  Here n = −2, u = 3x.
4.  Compute coefficients:
5.     n           =  −2
6.     n(n−1)/2!   =  (−2)(−3)/2    =  3
7.     n(n−1)(n−2)/3! = (−2)(−3)(−4)/6 = −4.
8.  Terms:
9.     1
10.    (−2)(3x)           = −6x
11.    3 · (3x)²          = 3 · 9x²     = 27x²
12.    (−4) · (3x)³       = −4 · 27x³   = −108x³.
13. ∴ (1 + 3x)⁻² = 1 − 6x + 27x² − 108x³ + ...

14. Validity:  |3x| < 1   ⇒   |x| < 1/3.

7. Approximation Using the Binomial

The binomial series lets us approximate powers of numbers close to \(1\). If \(x\) is small the first few terms of \((1 + x)^n\) give a good estimate.

Worked Example 10 — Approximating √1.02

Use a binomial expansion up to the \(x^2\) term to approximate \(\sqrt{1.02}\), and estimate the error.

1.  Write √1.02 = (1 + 0.02)^(1/2).
2.  Expansion with n = 1/2, u = 0.02:
3.     (1 + u)^(1/2) = 1 + (1/2)u + (1/2)(−1/2)/2! · u² + ...
4.                   = 1 + u/2 − u²/8 + ...
5.  Substitute u = 0.02:
6.     ≈ 1 + 0.01 − (0.0004)/8
7.     = 1 + 0.01 − 0.00005
8.     = 1.00995.

9.  Calculator value:  √1.02 = 1.00995049...
10. Error ≈ 5 × 10⁻⁸ — extremely accurate because u is small.

// The next term (u³ term) has coefficient (1/2)(−1/2)(−3/2)/6 = 1/16,
// giving u³/16 ≈ (8 × 10⁻⁶)/16 = 5 × 10⁻⁷, consistent with observed error.
Exam tip — how many terms for a target accuracy? Each successive term in the binomial series shrinks roughly by a factor of \(x\) (when \(|x|\) is small). So if you want four-decimal-place accuracy and \(|x| = 0.02\), two terms past the constant usually suffice. Rule of thumb: the first omitted term is the dominant error — estimate it and check it is below your tolerance.

8. Recurrence Relations — Convergence to a Limit

Some recurrence relations \(a_{n+1} = g(a_n)\) produce sequences that settle down to a limit \(L\). If \(a_n \to L\) and \(g\) is continuous, then taking limits of both sides of \(a_{n+1} = g(a_n)\) gives \(L = g(L)\) — the limit must be a fixed point of \(g\).

\( \text{If } a_n \to L \text{ as } n \to \infty, \text{ then } a_{n+1} \to L \text{ too, so } L = g(L). \)

Solving \(L = g(L)\) locates the candidate limits. Not every fixed point is attracting; whether the sequence actually converges depends on the starting value and on \(|g'(L)|\) (a detail beyond the specification), but candidate limits are found by this fixed-point equation.

Worked Example 11 — Convergent recurrence

Let \(a_{n+1} = \tfrac{1}{2}(a_n + 4)\) with \(a_1 = 0\). Compute \(a_1, \dots, a_5\), and find the limit \(L\).

1.  Compute terms:
2.     a₁ = 0
3.     a₂ = (0 + 4)/2    = 2
4.     a₃ = (2 + 4)/2    = 3
5.     a₄ = (3 + 4)/2    = 3.5
6.     a₅ = (3.5 + 4)/2  = 3.75
7.     a₆ = (3.75 + 4)/2 = 3.875
8.  Terms approach 4 from below.

// Fixed point equation: assume limit L exists and let n → ∞.
9.  L = (L + 4)/2
10. 2L = L + 4
11. L = 4.         ∴ aₙ → 4 as n → ∞.

// Distance to limit halves each step:  |aₙ − 4| → 4, 2, 1, 0.5, 0.25, ...
// geometric decrease with ratio 1/2, confirming convergence.

Worked Example 12 — Recurrence giving an AP

A sequence satisfies \(u_{n+1} = u_n + 5\) with \(u_1 = 3\). Find a closed-form expression for \(u_n\) and hence \(u_{20}\) and \(\sum_{k=1}^{20} u_k\).

1.  Recurrence u_{n+1} − u_n = 5 identifies an AP with
        first term a = 3 and common difference d = 5.
2.  Closed form:  u_n = a + (n − 1)d = 3 + 5(n − 1) = 5n − 2.
3.  Check:  u₁ = 3 ✓, u₂ = 8 ✓, u₃ = 13 ✓.
4.  u₂₀ = 5·20 − 2 = 98.
5.  Sum of first 20 terms:
6.     S₂₀ = 20/2 · (u₁ + u₂₀) = 10 · (3 + 98) = 10 · 101 = 1010.
7.  Check with other formula:
8.     S₂₀ = 20/2 · [2·3 + 19·5] = 10 · [6 + 95] = 10 · 101 = 1010 ✓.

9. Worked Exam-Style Problems — Combined Techniques

Worked Example 13 — Repeating decimal as a fraction

Express the recurring decimal \(0.\overline{27} = 0.272727\dots\) as a fraction in lowest terms using an infinite GP.

1.  Write as a sum:
2.     0.272727... = 0.27 + 0.0027 + 0.000027 + ...
3.  This is a GP with:
4.     first term a = 0.27 = 27/100
5.     common ratio r = 0.01 = 1/100 (each term is 1/100 of the previous).
6.  |r| = 0.01 < 1, so the series converges.
7.  Sum to infinity:
8.     S∞ = a/(1 − r) = (27/100) / (99/100) = 27/99.
9.  Simplify: gcd(27, 99) = 9, so 27/99 = 3/11.
10. ∴ 0.272727... = 3/11.
11. Check:  3 ÷ 11 = 0.272727... ✓.

Worked Example 14 — Compound interest

£2000 is deposited at 4% compound interest per year. Find the value after 10 years and the total interest earned.

1.  After n years balance = 2000 · (1.04)^n — a GP with a = 2000, r = 1.04.
2.  After 10 years:
3.     V = 2000 · (1.04)^10.
4.  (1.04)^10 — expand as (1 + 0.04)^10 using binomial (n = 10, positive integer):
5.     = 1 + 10(0.04) + 45(0.04)² + 120(0.04)³ + ... ≈ 1.48024.
6.     (Calculator value: 1.48024428...)
7.  V ≈ 2000 × 1.48024 ≈ £2960.49.
8.  Interest earned ≈ 2960.49 − 2000 = £960.49.

Worked Example 15 — Wage calculation (AP application)

An employee earns £22 000 in year 1 and receives a £900 annual pay rise. Find total earnings over a 25-year career.

1.  AP with a = 22000, d = 900, n = 25.
2.  Salary in year n:  a_n = 22000 + 900(n − 1).
3.  Year 25 salary:  22000 + 900·24 = 22000 + 21600 = £43600.
4.  Sum:
5.     S₂₅ = 25/2 · [2·22000 + 24·900]
6.          = 25/2 · [44000 + 21600]
7.          = 25/2 · 65600
8.          = 25 · 32800
9.          = £820 000.

// Alternatively:  S₂₅ = 25/2 · (22000 + 43600) = 12.5 · 65600 = 820000. ✓

10. Consolidated Summary and Exam Strategy

Chapter-wide checklist for Sequences & Series.
  • Identify the sequence type first. Common difference ⇒ AP; common ratio ⇒ GP; neither ⇒ use recurrence or general formulae.
  • AP: \(a_n = a + (n-1)d\), \(S_n = \tfrac{n}{2}(a + l) = \tfrac{n}{2}[2a + (n-1)d]\).
  • GP: \(a_n = a r^{n-1}\), \(S_n = a(r^n - 1)/(r - 1)\). Sum to infinity requires \(|r| < 1\).
  • Sigma notation: memorise \(\Sigma k, \Sigma k^2, \Sigma k^3\); use linearity to split complicated sums.
  • Binomial (positive integer \(n\)): finite expansion with \(n+1\) terms, valid for all real \(x\); coefficients from Pascal's triangle.
  • Binomial (rational/negative \(n\)): infinite series, valid for \(|x| < 1\); for \((a + bx)^n\) factor out \(a^n\) first and validity becomes \(|x| < |a/b|\).
  • Recurrence limits: assume limit \(L\) exists, substitute \(a_{n+1} = a_n = L\), solve \(L = g(L)\).
  • State validity conditions explicitly for infinite GP and non-integer binomial — OCR awards a mark for this.
Typical OCR examiner complaints (from mark reports).
  • Using \(S_\infty = a/(1-r)\) without checking or stating \(|r| < 1\).
  • Omitting the validity range \(|x| < |a/b|\) for the expansion of \((a + bx)^n\).
  • Arithmetic slips in \(n(n-1)(n-2)/3!\) when \(n\) is negative — sign errors abound.
  • Wrong number of terms in a sigma sum (index starts at 0 vs 1).
  • Forgetting to factor \(a^n\) out of \((a + bx)^n\) before applying the series formula.
  • Writing \((1+x)^n = 1 + nx + \tfrac{n(n-1)}{2} x^2 + \dots\) while missing the \(2!\) in the denominator of subsequent terms' factorials.
  • Failing to verify a guessed explicit formula on multiple terms, not just the first.
  • Mixing up AP and GP formulae under exam pressure — confirm the structure before reaching for a formula.
Formula snapshot (memorise).
  • AP \(n\)th term: \(a_n = a + (n-1)d\).
  • AP sum: \(S_n = \tfrac{n}{2}[2a + (n-1)d] = \tfrac{n}{2}(a + l)\).
  • GP \(n\)th term: \(a_n = a\, r^{n-1}\).
  • GP sum: \(S_n = a(r^n - 1)/(r - 1),\ r \neq 1\).
  • GP sum to infinity: \(S_\infty = a/(1 - r),\ |r| < 1\).
  • Standard sigma sums: \(\Sigma k = n(n+1)/2,\ \Sigma k^2 = n(n+1)(2n+1)/6,\ \Sigma k^3 = [n(n+1)/2]^2\).
  • Binomial (integer \(n\)): \((a+b)^n = \sum_{k=0}^n \binom{n}{k} a^{n-k} b^k\).
  • Binomial (general \(n\)): \((1+x)^n = 1 + nx + \tfrac{n(n-1)}{2!}x^2 + \tfrac{n(n-1)(n-2)}{3!}x^3 + \dots,\ |x| < 1\).
  • Fixed point of recurrence: \(L = g(L)\) if the limit exists.
