1.3 Coordinate Geometry

Mathematics — OCR A-Level (H240) — Paper 1: Pure Mathematics

Coordinate geometry translates geometric objects — lines, circles, and parametric curves — into algebraic equations in the Cartesian \((x, y)\) plane. The philosophy is Descartes': every geometric statement becomes algebraic, every algebraic statement becomes a picture. At A-level this topic blends synthetic-geometry reasoning (angles, perpendicularity, radii) with algebraic techniques (completing the square, discriminants, substitution) and sets the stage for calculus applications later in the course.

1. Straight Lines

A line in the plane is uniquely determined by either two distinct points or one point together with a direction (gradient). The gradient measures rise over run: the vertical change per unit horizontal change.

Gradient. For two distinct points \(A(x_1, y_1)\) and \(B(x_2, y_2)\) with \(x_1 \neq x_2\), the gradient of the line \(AB\) is \[ m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}. \] If \(x_1 = x_2\) the line is vertical and its gradient is undefined; if \(y_1 = y_2\) the line is horizontal with gradient \(0\).

Three Standard Forms of a Line

FormEquationBest used when...
Slope–intercept\(y = mx + c\)Gradient \(m\) and \(y\)-intercept \(c\) are known; also best for sketching.
Point–slope\(y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)\)One point \((x_1, y_1)\) and the gradient \(m\) are known.
General (implicit)\(ax + by + c = 0\)Required final form in some OCR questions; handles vertical lines (\(b = 0\)).
Two-point\(\dfrac{y - y_1}{y_2 - y_1} = \dfrac{x - x_1}{x_2 - x_1}\)Two points are given; equivalent to point–slope after computing \(m\).
Exam tip — which form to use. OCR mark schemes usually accept any correct form unless a specific form is demanded. However, a question ending "...giving your answer in the form \(ax + by + c = 0\) where \(a, b, c\) are integers" means the implicit form with integer coefficients — not \(y = 2.5x - 1.5\). Multiply through to clear fractions and move everything to the left-hand side.

Parallel and Perpendicular Lines

Two non-vertical lines are parallel iff they have equal gradients: \(m_1 = m_2\). They are perpendicular iff the product of gradients is \(-1\).

Perpendicularity condition. Two non-vertical, non-horizontal lines with gradients \(m_1\) and \(m_2\) are perpendicular if and only if \[ m_1 \cdot m_2 = -1, \qquad \text{equivalently} \qquad m_2 = -\frac{1}{m_1}. \] A horizontal line (\(m = 0\)) is perpendicular to a vertical line (gradient undefined); this is the limiting case not captured by the product rule.

Distance Between Two Points and Midpoint

Given \(A(x_1, y_1)\) and \(B(x_2, y_2)\):

\( |AB| = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2}, \qquad M_{AB} = \left(\dfrac{x_1 + x_2}{2}, \dfrac{y_1 + y_2}{2}\right). \)

The distance formula is Pythagoras applied to the right triangle with legs \(|x_2 - x_1|\) and \(|y_2 - y_1|\). The midpoint is simply the coordinate-wise average.

Worked Example 1 — Line through two points

Find the equation of the line through \(A(-1, 4)\) and \(B(3, -2)\) in the form \(ax + by + c = 0\) with integer \(a, b, c\). State the midpoint and the distance \(|AB|\).

 1.  Gradient  m = (−2 − 4) / (3 − (−1))  =  −6 / 4  =  −3/2.
 2.  Point–slope using A(−1, 4):
 3.     y − 4 = −3/2 · (x − (−1))
 4.     y − 4 = −3/2 · (x + 1)
 5.     2(y − 4) = −3(x + 1)
 6.     2y − 8  = −3x − 3
 7.     3x + 2y − 5 = 0.           // final form

 8.  Midpoint  M = ((−1 + 3)/2, (4 + (−2))/2)  =  (1, 1).
 9.  Distance  |AB| = √[(3 − (−1))² + (−2 − 4)²]
10.                 = √[16 + 36]  =  √52  =  2√13.
11.  Check M lies on the line:  3(1) + 2(1) − 5 = 0 ✓.

Intersection of Two Lines

Two lines in the plane either (i) intersect at one point — non-parallel; (ii) are identical — infinitely many intersection points; (iii) are parallel and distinct — no intersection. To find the intersection, solve the system simultaneously (substitution or elimination).

Worked Example 2 — Perpendicular through a point

Find the equation of the line through \((1, 2)\) perpendicular to \(y = 3x - 1\).

1.  Gradient of given line:  m₁ = 3.
2.  Perpendicular gradient:  m₂ = −1/m₁ = −1/3.
3.  Point–slope at (1, 2):
4.     y − 2 = −1/3 · (x − 1)
5.     3(y − 2) = −(x − 1)
6.     3y − 6   = −x + 1
7.     x + 3y − 7 = 0.         // or y = −x/3 + 7/3
8.  Check perpendicularity:  3 · (−1/3) = −1 ✓.
9.  Check point:  1 + 3·2 − 7 = 0 ✓.

2. Circles — Standard and General Forms

A circle is the locus of points equidistant from a fixed centre. Translating this definition using the distance formula gives the equation of a circle.

Circle — standard (centre–radius) form. The circle with centre \((a, b)\) and radius \(r > 0\) has equation \[ (x - a)^2 + (y - b)^2 = r^2. \] A point \((x_0, y_0)\) lies on the circle iff the equation is satisfied; inside iff LHS \(< r^2\); outside iff LHS \(> r^2\).
Circle — general form. Expanding the standard form gives \[ x^2 + y^2 + 2gx + 2fy + c = 0, \] with centre \((-g, -f)\) and radius \(r = \sqrt{g^2 + f^2 - c}\) (a real circle requires \(g^2 + f^2 - c > 0\); equality gives a point, and a negative value gives no real locus).

Comparison of Forms

FeatureStandard form \((x - a)^2 + (y - b)^2 = r^2\)General form \(x^2 + y^2 + 2gx + 2fy + c = 0\)

Radius\(r\) — square root of RHS\(r = \sqrt{g^2 + f^2 - c}\)
Convenient for...Sketching, geometric reasoningSubstituting a line equation; line–circle intersection
How to convertExpand the bracketsComplete the square in \(x\) and \(y\) separately

Exam tip — completing the square for a circle. OCR questions frequently hand you a circle in general form (e.g. \(x^2 + y^2 - 4x + 6y - 3 = 0\)) and ask for centre and radius. Complete the square in \(x\) and \(y\) separately: group \(x^2 - 4x\) and add/subtract \(4\); group \(y^2 + 6y\) and add/subtract \(9\); move the constants. Many students forget to subtract what they added and end up with the wrong \(r^2\).

Worked Example 3 — General to standard form

Find the centre and radius of the circle \(x^2 + y^2 - 4x + 6y - 3 = 0\).

1.  Group:        (x² − 4x) + (y² + 6y) − 3 = 0.
2.  Complete the square in x:   x² − 4x = (x − 2)² − 4.
3.  Complete the square in y:   y² + 6y = (y + 3)² − 9.
4.  Substitute:   (x − 2)² − 4 + (y + 3)² − 9 − 3 = 0
5.                (x − 2)² + (y + 3)² = 16.
6.  ∴ centre (2, −3), radius r = 4.

// Sanity check using g, f, c directly:
7.  Compare with x² + y² + 2gx + 2fy + c = 0:  2g = −4, 2f = 6, c = −3.
8.  g = −2, f = 3, so centre = (−g, −f) = (2, −3).  ✓
9.  r² = g² + f² − c = 4 + 9 − (−3) = 16, so r = 4.  ✓

Point Lying on / Inside / Outside a Circle

Given a circle with centre \(C(a, b)\) and radius \(r\), and a point \(P(x_0, y_0)\), compute the distance \(d = |CP|\):

  • \(d = r\) — \(P\) lies on the circle;
  • \(d < r\) — \(P\) lies inside the circle;
  • \(d > r\) — \(P\) lies outside the circle.

In practice you compare \(d^2\) with \(r^2\) to avoid square roots.

3. Circle Theorems Relevant to Coordinate Geometry

Three classical Euclidean theorems translate cleanly into coordinate-geometry tools. They are not proved here in detail (see GCSE circle theorems) but stated in the forms you will deploy.

Theorem A — angle in a semicircle. If \(AB\) is a diameter of a circle and \(P\) is any other point on the circle, then \(\angle APB = 90°\). Conversely, if \(\angle APB = 90°\) for points \(A, B, P\), then \(P\) lies on the circle with diameter \(AB\).
Theorem B — radius perpendicular to chord. The perpendicular from the centre of a circle to a chord bisects the chord. Conversely, the perpendicular bisector of any chord passes through the centre.
Theorem C — tangent perpendicular to radius. The tangent to a circle at a point \(T\) is perpendicular to the radius drawn to \(T\). Thus if the radius \(CT\) has gradient \(m\), the tangent at \(T\) has gradient \(-1/m\) (when neither is vertical or horizontal).

Applications

  • Equation of tangent at a given point: gradient of tangent = \(-1/\)(gradient of radius from centre to point); use point–slope form.
  • Centre via perpendicular bisectors: for three points on a circle, the centre is the intersection of any two perpendicular bisectors.
  • Right-angle detection: if three points satisfy Pythagoras' theorem, the right-angle vertex lies on the circle whose diameter is the hypotenuse.
Exam tip — tangent at a point. If a question asks for the equation of the tangent to a circle at a specific point \(T\) on the circle, do not substitute \(y = mx + c\) into the circle equation and set the discriminant to zero. Use the radius-perpendicular-to-tangent theorem: compute the gradient of \(CT\), take the negative reciprocal, and apply point–slope at \(T\). This is faster and less error-prone than the discriminant approach, which is reserved for tangents from an external point.

Worked Example 4 — Tangent at a point on a circle

Find the equation of the tangent at \(T(5, 6)\) to the circle \((x - 2)^2 + (y - 2)^2 = 25\).

1.  Verify T lies on the circle:  (5−2)² + (6−2)² = 9 + 16 = 25 ✓.
2.  Centre C = (2, 2). Gradient of radius CT:
3.     m(CT) = (6 − 2)/(5 − 2) = 4/3.
4.  Gradient of tangent at T = −3/4 (negative reciprocal).
5.  Tangent equation, point–slope at (5, 6):
6.     y − 6 = −3/4 · (x − 5)
7.     4(y − 6) = −3(x − 5)
8.     4y − 24  = −3x + 15
9.     3x + 4y − 39 = 0.

4. Intersection of a Line and a Circle

To find the intersection(s) of a line \(L\) and a circle \(C\): solve the line for \(y\) (or \(x\)) in terms of the other variable and substitute into the circle equation. The result is a quadratic in one variable, whose discriminant \(\Delta\) classifies the geometry.

Sign of \(\Delta\)Number of intersectionsGeometric meaning
\(\Delta > 0\)Two distinct pointsLine is a secant (cuts the circle)
\(\Delta = 0\)One (repeated) pointLine is a tangent to the circle
\(\Delta < 0\)NoneLine misses the circle entirely
Exam tip — tangent from an external point. When the line passes through an external point \((p, q)\) and is tangent to the circle, parametrise it as \(y - q = m(x - p)\), substitute into the circle equation, and set the discriminant equal to zero. You'll get a quadratic in \(m\) — two real solutions giving the two tangent gradients from the external point. Do not forget that the vertical line \(x = p\) may also be a tangent; check separately if the algebra hides this case.

Worked Example 5 — Tangents from an external point

Find the equations of both tangents from the external point \(P(7, 1)\) to the circle \(x^2 + y^2 = 25\).

 1.  Circle: x² + y² = 25, centre (0,0), radius 5.
 2.  |OP|² = 49 + 1 = 50 > 25, so P is external. ✓
 3.  Line through P with gradient m:   y − 1 = m(x − 7), i.e. y = mx − 7m + 1.
 4.  Substitute into x² + y² = 25:
 5.     x² + (mx − 7m + 1)² = 25.
 6.  Let k = 1 − 7m.  Then y = mx + k.
 7.     x² + m²x² + 2mkx + k² = 25
 8.     (1 + m²)x² + 2mk x + (k² − 25) = 0.
 9.  Tangent ⇒ discriminant = 0:
10.     (2mk)² − 4(1 + m²)(k² − 25) = 0
11.     4m²k² − 4(k² − 25 + m²k² − 25m²) = 0
12.     4m²k² − 4k² + 100 − 4m²k² + 100m² = 0
13.     −4k² + 100 + 100m² = 0
14.     k² = 25(1 + m²).
15.  Substitute k = 1 − 7m:
16.     (1 − 7m)² = 25(1 + m²)
17.     1 − 14m + 49m² = 25 + 25m²
18.     24m² − 14m − 24 = 0
19.     12m² − 7m − 12 = 0.
20.  Solve: m = [7 ± √(49 + 576)] / 24  =  [7 ± 25]/24.
21.  ⇒ m = 32/24 = 4/3   or   m = −18/24 = −3/4.
22.  Tangent equations:
23.    y − 1 =  4/3 (x − 7)   ⇒   4x − 3y − 25 = 0.
24.    y − 1 = −3/4 (x − 7)   ⇒   3x + 4y − 25 = 0.
25.  Check product of gradients: (4/3)·(−3/4) = −1, so the two tangents are perpendicular.

Worked Example 6 — Circle through three points

Find the equation of the circle through \(A(1, 1)\), \(B(5, 1)\), and \(C(1, 5)\).

 1.  Strategy A — general form.  Substitute each point into
         x² + y² + 2gx + 2fy + c = 0.
 2.     A(1,1):  1 + 1 + 2g + 2f + c = 0   ⇒   2g + 2f + c = −2.
 3.     B(5,1): 25 + 1 + 10g + 2f + c = 0  ⇒  10g + 2f + c = −26.
 4.     C(1,5):  1 + 25 + 2g + 10f + c = 0 ⇒   2g + 10f + c = −26.
 5.  Subtract (2) from (3):  8g = −24  ⇒  g = −3.
 6.  Subtract (2) from (4):  8f = −24  ⇒  f = −3.
 7.  From (2):  −6 − 6 + c = −2  ⇒  c = 10.
 8.  ∴ x² + y² − 6x − 6y + 10 = 0,
      centre (3, 3), r² = 9 + 9 − 10 = 8, r = 2√2.

// Strategy B — perpendicular bisectors (for geometric insight).
 9.  AB is horizontal, midpoint (3, 1), so its perpendicular bisector is x = 3.
10.  AC is vertical, midpoint (1, 3),  so its perpendicular bisector is y = 3.
11.  Centre = (3, 3), agreeing with Strategy A.
12.  Radius = distance from (3,3) to A(1,1) = √(4 + 4) = 2√2.  ✓

5. Parametric Equations

Not every curve in the plane is conveniently described by a single equation \(y = f(x)\) or \(F(x, y) = 0\). Parametric equations describe a curve by letting both \(x\) and \(y\) depend on a third variable, the parameter, often called \(t\) or \(\theta\).

Parametric equations. A curve given by \[ x = f(t), \quad y = g(t), \qquad t \in I, \] for some interval (or union of intervals) \(I \subseteq \mathbb{R}\). As \(t\) varies over \(I\), the point \((x, y)\) traces out the curve.
Parameter. The independent variable \(t\) (or \(\theta, s, \lambda\), etc.) indexing the points of a parametric curve. The parameter often has a physical or geometric meaning — time in mechanics; angle for circles and ellipses; a non-geometric algebraic label otherwise.

Standard Parametrisations

CurveParametric formCartesian equationElimination technique
Parabola\(x = t^2,\ y = 2t\)\(y^2 = 4x\)From \(y = 2t\), \(t = y/2\); substitute
Circle, radius \(a\)\(x = a\cos t,\ y = a\sin t\)\(x^2 + y^2 = a^2\)Pythagorean identity \(\sin^2 t + \cos^2 t = 1\)
Ellipse\(x = a\cos t,\ y = b\sin t\)\(\dfrac{x^2}{a^2} + \dfrac{y^2}{b^2} = 1\)\((x/a)^2 + (y/b)^2 = 1\)
Rectangular hyperbola\(x = t,\ y = c^2/t\)\(xy = c^2\)Multiply \(x\) and \(y\)
Line\(x = p + \lambda u,\ y = q + \lambda v\)\(v(x - p) - u(y - q) = 0\)Solve one for \(\lambda\), substitute

Conversion Techniques — Parametric to Cartesian

Parametric featureElimination method
Polynomial in \(t\), one equation easily invertibleSolve for \(t\) in one equation, substitute into the other
Both \(x\) and \(y\) are trigonometric (e.g. \(\cos t, \sin t\))Use \(\sin^2 t + \cos^2 t = 1\), or \(1 + \tan^2 t = \sec^2 t\)
Exponential parameters (e.g. \(e^t, e^{-t}\))Multiply, or use \(\cosh^2 - \sinh^2 = 1\)
Involves reciprocals \(t\) and \(1/t\)Add or multiply to produce a simple symmetric function
Exam tip — range of the curve. Eliminating the parameter gives an equation \(F(x, y) = 0\) that the points of the curve must satisfy — but the Cartesian equation often describes a larger set than the original parametric curve. For example, \(x = t^2,\ y = t\) with \(t \in \mathbb{R}\) yields \(x = y^2\) — the full sideways parabola. But \(x = t^2,\ y = t,\ t \geq 0\) traces only the upper half (\(y \geq 0\)). Always state any restrictions on \(x\) or \(y\) inherited from the parameter's range.

Worked Example 7 — Trigonometric parametrisation

Show that \(x = 4\cos\theta,\ y = 4\sin\theta\) traces a circle; give its Cartesian equation and state its centre and radius.

1.   x² = 16 cos²θ,     y² = 16 sin²θ.
2.   x² + y² = 16(cos²θ + sin²θ) = 16 · 1 = 16.
3.   ∴ x² + y² = 16  —  circle, centre (0, 0), radius 4.

// As θ runs over [0, 2π), (x, y) traces the whole circle exactly once.
// θ = 0 → (4, 0); θ = π/2 → (0, 4); θ = π → (−4, 0); θ = 3π/2 → (0, −4).

Worked Example 8 — Parabolic parametrisation

A curve is given by \(x = t^2,\ y = 2t\). (a) Find its Cartesian equation. (b) Find the points where it meets the line \(y = x - 3\).

// (a) Eliminate t.
1.  From y = 2t:  t = y/2.
2.  Substitute into x = t²:  x = (y/2)² = y²/4.
3.  ∴ y² = 4x  —  a parabola, vertex at origin, axis along the x-axis.

// (b) Intersection with y = x − 3.
4.  Substitute x = y + 3 into y² = 4x:
5.     y² = 4(y + 3)
6.     y² − 4y − 12 = 0
7.     (y − 6)(y + 2) = 0
8.  ⇒ y = 6, x = 9    or    y = −2, x = 1.
9.  Intersection points:  (9, 6) and (1, −2).

6. Differentiation of Parametric Curves

When \(x\) and \(y\) are both functions of \(t\), the gradient \(\mathrm{d}y/\mathrm{d}x\) of the curve can be computed without first eliminating the parameter.

\( \dfrac{\mathrm{d}y}{\mathrm{d}x} = \dfrac{\mathrm{d}y/\mathrm{d}t}{\mathrm{d}x/\mathrm{d}t}, \qquad \text{provided } \dfrac{\mathrm{d}x}{\mathrm{d}t} \neq 0. \)

This is the chain rule in disguise: \(\dfrac{\mathrm{d}y}{\mathrm{d}x} = \dfrac{\mathrm{d}y}{\mathrm{d}t} \cdot \dfrac{\mathrm{d}t}{\mathrm{d}x}\). When \(\mathrm{d}x/\mathrm{d}t = 0\) the tangent is vertical — the formula breaks down, which it should, because the gradient is undefined.

Exam tip — parametric \(\mathrm{d}y/\mathrm{d}x\). Always write out both \(\mathrm{d}y/\mathrm{d}t\) and \(\mathrm{d}x/\mathrm{d}t\) separately on the page, then divide. OCR marks the derivatives and the division as separate steps. Also: leave your answer in terms of \(t\) (not \(x\) or \(y\)) unless asked to convert — converting midway adds unnecessary algebra and is a common source of errors.

Worked Derivatives

Curve\(\mathrm{d}x/\mathrm{d}t\)\(\mathrm{d}y/\mathrm{d}t\)\(\mathrm{d}y/\mathrm{d}x\)
Parabola: \(x = t^2,\ y = 2t\)\(2t\)\(2\)\(1/t\) (equivalently \(2/y\))
Circle: \(x = a\cos t,\ y = a\sin t\)\(-a\sin t\)\(a\cos t\)\(-\cot t = -x/y\)
Ellipse: \(x = a\cos t,\ y = b\sin t\)\(-a\sin t\)\(b\cos t\)\(-\dfrac{b}{a}\cot t\)

Worked Example 9 — Tangent to a parametric curve

Find the equation of the tangent to the curve \(x = t^2,\ y = 2t\) at the point where \(t = 3\).

1.  Point at t = 3:  (x, y) = (9, 6).
2.  dx/dt = 2t,   dy/dt = 2.
3.  dy/dx = 2/(2t) = 1/t.
4.  At t = 3:   dy/dx = 1/3.
5.  Tangent:   y − 6 = (1/3)(x − 9)
6.             3y − 18 = x − 9
7.             x − 3y + 9 = 0.
8.  Check on Cartesian form y² = 4x: implicit differentiation
        2y·y' = 4 ⇒ y' = 2/y = 2/6 = 1/3 ✓.

7. Applications — Worked Exam Problems

Worked Example 10 — Area enclosed by a line and a parabola

Find the area enclosed between the parabola \(y = x^2 - 2x - 3\) and the line \(y = x - 3\).

 1.  Find intersections:
 2.     x² − 2x − 3 = x − 3  ⇒  x² − 3x = 0  ⇒  x = 0 or x = 3.
 3.  On [0, 3] the line lies above the parabola (check at x = 1:
        line = −2, parabola = 1 − 2 − 3 = −4, so line > parabola). ✓
 4.  Area = ∫₀³ [(x − 3) − (x² − 2x − 3)] dx
 5.         = ∫₀³ [−x² + 3x] dx
 6.         = [−x³/3 + 3x²/2]₀³
 7.         = (−9 + 27/2) − 0
 8.         = 9/2.

// The enclosed region is the "lens" between the secant chord and the parabola.

Worked Example 11 — Semicircle theorem in coordinates

Show that the triangle with vertices \(A(-3, 0), B(5, 0), C(1, 4)\) has a right angle at \(C\), and deduce the equation of the circle through \(A, B, C\).

1.  Vectors:  CA = A − C = (−4, −4);  CB = B − C = (4, −4).
2.  Dot product:  CA · CB = (−4)(4) + (−4)(−4) = −16 + 16 = 0.
3.  ∴ CA ⊥ CB, i.e. ∠ACB = 90°.
4.  By the converse of "angle in a semicircle", C lies on the
        circle with diameter AB.
5.  Centre = midpoint of AB = ((−3+5)/2, 0) = (1, 0).
6.  Radius = |AB|/2 = 8/2 = 4.
7.  Circle:  (x − 1)² + y² = 16.
8.  Check C(1,4):  0 + 16 = 16 ✓.

Worked Example 12 — Chord bisected by a perpendicular from centre

The circle \((x - 3)^2 + (y - 1)^2 = 25\) is cut by the line \(y = 2x - 3\). Find the length of the chord and verify that the perpendicular from the centre to the chord bisects it.

 1.  Substitute y = 2x − 3 into the circle equation:
 2.     (x − 3)² + (2x − 4)² = 25
 3.     (x² − 6x + 9) + (4x² − 16x + 16) = 25
 4.     5x² − 22x + 0 = 0
 5.     x(5x − 22) = 0  ⇒  x = 0 or x = 22/5.
 6.  Corresponding y:  y = −3  and  y = 29/5.
 7.  Chord endpoints:  P(0, −3), Q(22/5, 29/5).
 8.  Chord length:
 9.     |PQ|² = (22/5)² + (29/5 + 3)²  = (22/5)² + (44/5)²
10.           = (484 + 1936)/25  =  2420/25  =  96.8.
11.     |PQ| = √96.8 = (22/5)√5.    // exact form
12.  Midpoint M of PQ = (11/5, 7/5).
13.  Vector from centre C(3, 1) to M:  (11/5 − 3, 7/5 − 1) = (−4/5, 2/5).
14.  Direction of chord:  Q − P = (22/5, 44/5), parallel to (1, 2).
15.  Dot product CM · (1, 2) = −4/5 + 4/5 = 0   ⇒   CM ⊥ chord ✓.
16.  This confirms Theorem B: the perpendicular from the centre bisects the chord.

8. Locus Problems

A locus is the set of points satisfying a geometric condition. Translating the condition into coordinates and simplifying usually produces a line or circle equation.

Worked Example 13 — Locus equidistant from two points

Find the locus of points \(P(x, y)\) equidistant from \(A(-2, 3)\) and \(B(4, 1)\).

1.  |PA|² = |PB|² translates the condition:
2.     (x + 2)² + (y − 3)² = (x − 4)² + (y − 1)²
3.     x² + 4x + 4 + y² − 6y + 9 = x² − 8x + 16 + y² − 2y + 1
4.     4x − 6y + 13 = −8x − 2y + 17
5.     12x − 4y − 4 = 0
6.     3x − y − 1 = 0.
7.  ∴ the locus is the straight line 3x − y − 1 = 0
        — this is the perpendicular bisector of AB.
8.  Check: midpoint of AB = (1, 2) lies on 3(1) − 2 − 1 = 0 ✓.
        gradient of AB = (1 − 3)/(4 + 2) = −1/3;
        perpendicular gradient = 3 — matches line gradient ✓.

9. Consolidated Summary and Exam Strategy

Chapter-wide checklist for Coordinate Geometry.
  • Lines: know all three forms; convert to \(ax + by + c = 0\) with integer coefficients when asked.
  • Gradient of perpendicular: negative reciprocal. Check \(m_1 m_2 = -1\) at the end.
  • Circle from general form: complete the square in \(x\) and \(y\) separately; do not forget to subtract what you added.
  • Point inside/outside circle: compare \((x_0 - a)^2 + (y_0 - b)^2\) with \(r^2\).
  • Tangent at a point on a circle: gradient is negative reciprocal of radius gradient; use point–slope.
  • Tangent from an external point: set discriminant of line-into-circle quadratic to zero.
  • Line–circle intersection: substitute \(y\) from the line into the circle, solve the resulting quadratic; sign of discriminant tells the geometry.
  • Parametric to Cartesian: eliminate \(t\) by substitution or trig identity; state any range restriction on \(x, y\).
  • Parametric \(\mathrm{d}y/\mathrm{d}x\): \((\mathrm{d}y/\mathrm{d}t)/(\mathrm{d}x/\mathrm{d}t)\); leave in terms of \(t\).
  • Three points on a circle: substitute into general form and solve for \(g, f, c\), or use two perpendicular bisectors to find the centre.
Typical OCR examiner complaints (from mark reports).
  • Writing a line equation in non-integer form when integer form was demanded.
  • Arithmetic slips when completing the square for a circle — especially sign errors on the constant term.
  • Using the "discriminant = 0" method for a tangent at a given point on the circle (over-complicated — use the perpendicular-radius method).
  • Failing to state the parameter range, so the Cartesian equation describes more of the plane than the actual parametric curve.
  • Dividing \(\mathrm{d}y/\mathrm{d}t\) by \(\mathrm{d}x/\mathrm{d}t\) in the wrong order, giving \(\mathrm{d}x/\mathrm{d}y\) instead.
  • Forgetting to verify that a point claimed to lie on a circle actually satisfies the equation before computing the tangent there.
  • Mishandling the external-tangent problem by assuming the tangent has a finite gradient, missing a vertical tangent case.
Formula snapshot (memorise).
  • Gradient: \(m = (y_2 - y_1)/(x_2 - x_1)\).
  • Distance: \(|AB| = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2}\).
  • Midpoint: \(M = ((x_1 + x_2)/2,\ (y_1 + y_2)/2)\).
  • Perpendicular: \(m_1 m_2 = -1\).
  • Circle standard: \((x - a)^2 + (y - b)^2 = r^2\).
  • Circle general: \(x^2 + y^2 + 2gx + 2fy + c = 0\); centre \((-g, -f)\), \(r^2 = g^2 + f^2 - c\).
  • Parametric derivative: \(\mathrm{d}y/\mathrm{d}x = (\mathrm{d}y/\mathrm{d}t)/(\mathrm{d}x/\mathrm{d}t)\).
