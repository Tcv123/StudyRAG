1.6 Exponentials & Logarithms

Mathematics — OCR A-Level (H240) — Paper 1: Pure Mathematics

Exponential and logarithmic functions describe the mathematics of proportional change: a quantity whose instantaneous rate of change is proportional to its current size. This is the natural model for population growth, compound interest, radioactive decay, Newton's law of cooling, charge on a capacitor and countless other physical processes. The chapter builds the pair of inverse functions \(y = e^x\) and \(y = \ln x\) — together with their general-base relatives \(y = a^x\) and \(y = \log_a x\) — develops the algebra of logarithm laws, and uses them to solve exponential equations, fit power and exponential laws to experimental data, and model real-world growth and decay. A central idea is that the number \(e \approx 2.71828\) is uniquely "natural" because \(\tfrac{d}{dx} e^x = e^x\); every other exponential function carries an awkward factor of \(\ln a\).

1. The Exponential Function y = a^x

An exponential function has the variable in the exponent rather than the base. This is fundamentally different from a power function like \(x^2\): in \(y = 2^x\) the base is fixed and the exponent varies, giving explosive growth; in \(y = x^2\) the exponent is fixed and the growth is merely polynomial. By \(x = 10\), \(x^2 = 100\) but \(2^x = 1024\) — already ten times larger, and the gap widens without bound.

Exponential function. For a constant \(a > 0\), \(a \neq 1\), the function \[ f(x) = a^x, \qquad x \in \mathbb{R} \] is called the exponential function with base \(a\). Its principal properties are:
  • Domain: all real \(x\).
  • Range: \((0, \infty)\) — the function is strictly positive for every real \(x\).
  • Intercept: passes through \((0, 1)\), since \(a^0 = 1\) for any \(a \neq 0\).
  • Monotonicity: strictly increasing if \(a > 1\); strictly decreasing if \(0 < a < 1\).
  • Horizontal asymptote: \(y = 0\) (the \(x\)-axis) — as \(x \to -\infty\) if \(a > 1\), or \(x \to +\infty\) if \(0 < a < 1\).
  • One-to-one: so it has an inverse, the logarithm \(\log_a\).

Graphs of y = a^x for Various Bases

All exponential curves pass through \((0, 1)\) and approach the \(x\)-axis asymptotically. The base controls how steeply they rise or fall. For \(a > 1\) they grow faster as \(a\) increases; for \(a < 1\) they decay faster as \(a\) decreases.

Three features are worth noting: (i) all four curves share the point \((0, 1)\); (ii) the curves for \(a = 2, e, 10\) are all increasing, with \(y = 10^x\) rising fastest and \(y = 2^x\) rising slowest; (iii) the curve \(y = (1/2)^x = 2^{-x}\) is the reflection of \(y = 2^x\) in the \(y\)-axis — it decays from \(+\infty\) (as \(x \to -\infty\)) through \((0,1)\) down towards \(0\).

Exam tip — exponentials are always positive. Because \(a^x > 0\) for every real \(x\) and every positive base \(a\), an equation like \(e^x = -2\) or \(3^x + 1 = 0\) has no real solution. Spotting this saves wasted algebra. If after manipulation you end up trying to take \(\log\) of a negative number, either there is no solution or there is an error earlier.

2. The Number e

Among all exponential functions \(y = a^x\), exactly one has the property that its gradient at every point equals its height — that base is Euler's constant \(e \approx 2.71828\). This single fact is the reason \(e\) is called the natural base: calculus with \(e^x\) avoids extra multiplicative factors.

Euler's number \(e\). The irrational constant \[ e = 2.71828182845904523536\ldots \] can be defined in several equivalent ways, of which two are standard at A-level: \[ e = \lim_{n \to \infty} \left(1 + \frac{1}{n}\right)^n, \qquad e = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n!} = 1 + 1 + \tfrac{1}{2} + \tfrac{1}{6} + \tfrac{1}{24} + \cdots \] The first (limit) definition arises naturally from continuously compounded interest; the second (series) definition is the Taylor expansion of \(e^x\) evaluated at \(x = 1\).

Why e is Natural

Differentiating \(y = a^x\) from first principles gives \[ \frac{dy}{dx} = a^x \cdot \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{a^h - 1}{h}. \] The limit on the right depends only on the base \(a\), not on \(x\). For \(a = 2\) it equals \(\ln 2 \approx 0.693\); for \(a = 10\) it equals \(\ln 10 \approx 2.303\); for \(a = e\) it equals exactly \(1\). So \(e\) is the unique base for which \(\tfrac{d}{dx} a^x = a^x\) — no awkward factor.

\( \dfrac{d}{dx}(e^x) = e^x. \)

Compound Interest and the Limit Definition

If £1 is invested at 100% interest compounded \(n\) times per year, the year-end value is \((1 + 1/n)^n\). As compounding becomes continuous (\(n \to \infty\)) this value approaches \(e\). Numerically: with \(n = 1\) it is 2; with \(n = 12\), 2.613; with \(n = 365\), 2.7146; in the limit, \(e\).

3. The Natural Logarithm ln(x)

The natural logarithm is the inverse of the exponential function with base \(e\). It answers the question: "to what power must I raise \(e\) to get \(x\)?"

Natural logarithm. For \(x > 0\), \[ \ln x = \log_e x \iff e^{\ln x} = x. \] Equivalently, \(y = \ln x\) means \(e^y = x\). Properties:
  • Domain: \((0, \infty)\) — cannot take log of zero or a negative number.
  • Range: all real numbers.
  • Intercept: passes through \((1, 0)\), since \(\ln 1 = 0\).
  • Monotonicity: strictly increasing.
  • Vertical asymptote: \(x = 0\). As \(x \to 0^+\), \(\ln x \to -\infty\).
  • Inverse identities: \(\ln(e^x) = x\) for all real \(x\); \(\,e^{\ln x} = x\) for \(x > 0\).

Graphs of y = e^x and y = ln x

Because \(\ln\) is the inverse of the exponential, their graphs are reflections of each other in the line \(y = x\). The exponential rises steeply from the horizontal asymptote; the logarithm rises slowly from the vertical asymptote.

General-Base Logarithm

For any base \(a > 0\), \(a \neq 1\), the logarithm \(\log_a\) is defined by \[ y = \log_a x \iff a^y = x. \] Special cases: \(\log_{10}\) (written just \(\log\) on calculators, the common logarithm), \(\log_2\) (common in computer science) and \(\log_e = \ln\).

Common logarithm. \(\log_{10} x\), often written simply \(\log x\) on calculators and in engineering, is the logarithm with base 10. It tells you the number's order of magnitude: \(\log 1000 = 3\), \(\log 10{,}000 = 4\). The decibel and Richter scales are defined in terms of \(\log_{10}\).

4. Logarithm Laws

Three laws summarise how logarithms interact with multiplication, division and powers. They turn exponential relationships into linear ones — which is why they were invented in 1614 by John Napier, long before calculus, for the purpose of simplifying astronomical calculation.

Laws of logarithms. For a fixed base \(b > 0\), \(b \neq 1\), and positive reals \(x\), \(y\): \[ \log_b(xy) = \log_b x + \log_b y \qquad \text{(product rule)} \] \[ \log_b\!\left(\tfrac{x}{y}\right) = \log_b x - \log_b y \qquad \text{(quotient rule)} \] \[ \log_b(x^n) = n \log_b x \qquad \text{(power rule, any real } n) \] Special values: \(\log_b b = 1\); \(\log_b 1 = 0\); \(\log_b(b^x) = x\); \(b^{\log_b x} = x\).

Proof of the Product Rule

Let \(\log_b x = p\) and \(\log_b y = q\), so by definition \(x = b^p\) and \(y = b^q\). Then \[ xy = b^p \cdot b^q = b^{p + q}, \] using the index law \(b^p b^q = b^{p+q}\). Taking \(\log_b\) of both sides, \[ \log_b(xy) = p + q = \log_b x + \log_b y. \qquad\square \] The quotient rule follows identically using \(b^p / b^q = b^{p-q}\); the power rule from \((b^p)^n = b^{np}\).

Change of Base

Change of base. For positive \(a, b, x\) with \(a, b \neq 1\), \[ \log_a x = \frac{\log_b x}{\log_b a} \quad\text{and in particular}\quad \log_a x = \frac{\ln x}{\ln a}. \] Proof. Let \(y = \log_a x\), so \(a^y = x\). Taking \(\log_b\) of both sides, \(y \log_b a = \log_b x\), which rearranges to the stated formula.

The change-of-base formula means a calculator with only \(\ln\) and \(\log_{10}\) buttons can compute any logarithm: e.g. \(\log_2 5 = \ln 5 / \ln 2 \approx 1.6094 / 0.6931 \approx 2.322\).

Exam tip — log laws require positivity. Every log law is stated for positive arguments. Writing \(\log(x^2) = 2\log x\) is wrong if \(x\) could be negative — the left side is defined (\(x^2 > 0\)) but the right side is not. The safe version is \(\log(x^2) = 2\log|x|\). When solving log equations, always check at the end that every argument of every log was positive.

Worked Example 1 — Combining logs

Write \(2\ln 3 + \ln 5 - \ln 2\) as a single logarithm.

1.  Use power rule on the first term:
2.     2 ln 3 = ln(3²) = ln 9.
3.  Use product rule to combine ln 9 + ln 5:
4.     ln 9 + ln 5 = ln(9·5) = ln 45.
5.  Use quotient rule to subtract ln 2:
6.     ln 45 − ln 2 = ln(45/2) = ln 22.5.
7.  ∴ 2 ln 3 + ln 5 − ln 2 = ln(45/2).

// Numerical check: 2(1.0986) + 1.6094 − 0.6931 = 3.1135 ≈ ln(22.5). ✓

5. Solving Exponential Equations

An equation of the form \(a^x = b\) or \(e^{f(x)} = c\) is solved by taking logs of both sides. Which log? In principle any base works, but in practice use \(\ln\) (for \(e^{\cdots}\)) or \(\log_{10}\) (for \(10^{\cdots}\)), because every calculator has these. The power rule \(\log(a^x) = x \log a\) is what makes this work — it moves the unknown out of the exponent.

Worked Example 2 — Simple exponential

Solve \(2^x = 10\).

1.  Take ln of both sides (any base works; ln is convenient):
2.     ln(2^x) = ln 10
3.     x · ln 2 = ln 10                 (power rule)
4.     x = ln 10 / ln 2.
5.  Numerical value:
6.     x = 2.302585 / 0.693147
7.     x ≈ 3.3219 (4 d.p.)
8.  Check: 2^3.3219 = e^(3.3219 · 0.693) = e^2.303 ≈ 10. ✓

// The exact answer is x = log₂ 10 = ln 10/ln 2 — both are acceptable forms.

Worked Example 3 — Different bases on two sides

Solve \(3^{2x+1} = 5^x\).

1.  Take ln of both sides:
2.     ln(3^(2x+1)) = ln(5^x)
3.     (2x + 1) ln 3 = x ln 5             (power rule)
4.  Expand and gather x-terms:
5.     2x ln 3 + ln 3 = x ln 5
6.     2x ln 3 − x ln 5 = − ln 3
7.     x (2 ln 3 − ln 5) = − ln 3
8.     x = − ln 3 / (2 ln 3 − ln 5) = ln 3 / (ln 5 − 2 ln 3).
9.  Numerically:
10.    x = 1.0986 / (1.6094 − 2.1972) = 1.0986 / (−0.5878) ≈ −1.869.

// Using the quotient rule: denominator = ln 5 − ln 9 = ln(5/9), so x = ln 3 / ln(5/9).

Worked Example 4 — Exponential plus constant

Solve \(e^x + 3 = 5\).

1.  Isolate the exponential:
2.     e^x = 5 − 3 = 2.
3.  Take ln:
4.     x = ln 2 ≈ 0.6931.

// Reminder: e^x > 0 always. If we had e^x + 5 = 3, the equation would read
// e^x = −2, which has NO real solution.

6. Solving Logarithmic Equations

Log equations are solved by combining terms using the log laws until one side is a single log, then removing the log by exponentiating. Every candidate solution must then be checked: it must make every log argument in the original equation positive.

Worked Example 5 — Combined-log equation with domain check

Solve \(\log_2 x + \log_2(x - 2) = 3\).

1.  Domain: need x > 0 AND x − 2 > 0, so x > 2.
2.  Combine using product rule:
3.     log₂(x(x − 2)) = 3
4.     x(x − 2) = 2³ = 8.
5.  Expand:
6.     x² − 2x − 8 = 0
7.     (x − 4)(x + 2) = 0
8.     x = 4   or   x = −2.
9.  Check domain: x > 2.
10.    x = 4 ✓     x = −2 ✗ (rejected — would give log of negative).
11. ∴ x = 4.

// Forgetting to reject x = −2 is a classic lost mark.

Worked Example 6 — Natural-log equation

Solve \(\ln x = 2\).

1.  By definition of ln, e^(ln x) = x, so exponentiate both sides:
2.     e^(ln x) = e²
3.     x = e² ≈ 7.389.
4.  Domain: x > 0 ✓.

7. Modelling with Exponentials

Any quantity whose rate of change is proportional to its current size obeys an exponential law. If \(\tfrac{dP}{dt} = kP\) then \(P = P_0 e^{kt}\); positive \(k\) gives growth, negative \(k\) gives decay. This is the backbone of population models, radioactive decay, compound interest, discharge of a capacitor and heat loss (Newton's law of cooling).

Exponential growth model. \[ P(t) = P_0 \, e^{kt}, \qquad k > 0. \] \(P_0 = P(0)\) is the initial value; \(k\) is the continuous growth rate. The doubling time \(T_2\) satisfies \(P(T_2) = 2P_0\), giving \[ T_2 = \frac{\ln 2}{k}. \]
Exponential decay model. \[ N(t) = N_0 \, e^{-\lambda t}, \qquad \lambda > 0. \] \(\lambda\) is the decay constant. The half-life \(T_{1/2}\) is the time for the quantity to fall to half its value. From \(N_0 e^{-\lambda T_{1/2}} = \tfrac{1}{2} N_0\): \[ T_{1/2} = \frac{\ln 2}{\lambda}. \]
QuantityFormulaWhen the variable appears
Doubling time\(T_2 = (\ln 2)/k\)Population, investment growing at rate \(k\).
Half-life\(T_{1/2} = (\ln 2)/\lambda\)Radioactive decay, drug elimination.
Time-to-multiple \(m\)\(t = (\ln m)/k\)Population reaches \(m\) times its initial size.
Fraction remaining at \(t\)\(N/N_0 = e^{-\lambda t} = (1/2)^{t/T_{1/2}}\)Radiocarbon dating, drug levels.

Newton's Law of Cooling

An object at temperature \(T\) placed in surroundings at temperature \(T_s\) cools at a rate proportional to the excess temperature: \[ \frac{dT}{dt} = -k(T - T_s). \] Setting \(u = T - T_s\) gives \(du/dt = -ku\), so \(u = u_0 e^{-kt}\) and \[ T(t) = T_s + (T_0 - T_s)\, e^{-kt}. \] At \(t = 0\) this gives \(T = T_0\); as \(t \to \infty\), \(T \to T_s\). The graph is a decaying exponential asymptoting to ambient temperature.

Exam tip — read off the model carefully. In \(P = P_0 e^{kt}\): \(P_0\) is the initial value (plug in \(t = 0\)); \(k\) is the continuous rate, not the percentage rate. If population grows by 5% per year, then after 1 year \(P = 1.05 P_0\), so \(e^k = 1.05\), giving \(k = \ln 1.05 \approx 0.04879\) — not 0.05. Always convert between percentage-per-period and continuous rate \(k\) with care.

Worked Example 7 — Population doubling

A town of 10,000 people grows at 5% per year. After how many whole years will the population first exceed 20,000?

1.  Annual multiplier = 1.05, so P(t) = 10000 · (1.05)^t.
2.  Set P(t) = 20000:
3.     10000 · (1.05)^t = 20000
4.     (1.05)^t = 2.
5.  Take ln of both sides:
6.     t · ln 1.05 = ln 2
7.     t = ln 2 / ln 1.05 = 0.6931 / 0.04879 ≈ 14.207.
8.  t must be a whole year, so first whole year exceeding is t = 15.
9.  Check:   P(14) = 10000 · 1.05^14 ≈ 19799    (under 20000)
10.           P(15) = 10000 · 1.05^15 ≈ 20789    (over 20000) ✓

// Continuous-rate version: k = ln 1.05 ≈ 0.04879; T₂ = ln 2 / k ≈ 14.21 yr.

Worked Example 8 — Radiocarbon dating

Carbon-14 has half-life \(T_{1/2} = 5730\) years. What fraction of the original carbon-14 remains after 10,000 years?

1.  Decay constant from half-life:
2.     λ = ln 2 / T½ = 0.6931 / 5730 ≈ 1.2097 × 10⁻⁴  yr⁻¹.
3.  Fraction remaining after t = 10000 years:
4.     N/N₀ = e^(−λt) = e^(−1.2097e-4 · 10000) = e^(−1.2097).
5.     N/N₀ ≈ 0.2982.
6.  Equivalent form:
7.     N/N₀ = (1/2)^(t/T½) = (1/2)^(10000/5730) = (1/2)^1.7452 ≈ 0.2982. ✓
8.  ∴ about 29.8% remains.

// Radiocarbon dating is reliable up to ~50,000 yr — about 8 half-lives,
// after which the remaining fraction (~0.4%) becomes too small to measure.

Worked Example 9 — Newton's law of cooling

A cup of coffee follows \(T = 20 + 60\,e^{-0.1 t}\), with \(t\) in minutes and \(T\) in °C. When is \(T = 40\)°C?

1.  Set up the equation:
2.     20 + 60 e^(−0.1 t) = 40
3.     60 e^(−0.1 t) = 20
4.     e^(−0.1 t) = 1/3.
5.  Take ln:
6.     −0.1 t = ln(1/3) = −ln 3
7.     t = (ln 3)/0.1 = 10 ln 3 ≈ 10.986 min.
8.  Initial check: T(0) = 20 + 60 = 80°C ✓; ambient T₍s₎ = 20°C ✓.

// As t → ∞, T → 20°C (the room temperature). The graph is a decaying
// exponential shifted up by 20.

8. Logarithmic Scales — Linearising Data

A key experimental technique is to use logs to convert a non-linear relationship into a linear one. Plotting logged quantities and looking for a straight line lets you read off unknown constants from the intercept and gradient.

Exponential Law y = a·b^x

Take logs of both sides (any base — pick \(\log_{10}\) or \(\ln\)): \[ \log y = \log a + x \log b. \] This is linear in \(x\): plotting \(\log y\) (vertical) against \(x\) (horizontal) gives a straight line with intercept \(\log a\) and gradient \(\log b\). So an exponential relationship in raw variables becomes a straight line after taking the log of only one axis (semi-log plot).

Power Law y = a·x^n

Take logs: \(\log y = \log a + n \log x\). Now plot \(\log y\) against \(\log x\) — a log-log plot. A straight line with gradient \(n\) and intercept \(\log a\) confirms a power law and reveals both constants.

Exam tip — plot and check. In OCR modelling questions, if the examiner tells you data "fits a law \(y = a b^x\)", expect to plot \(\log y\) against \(x\) and show — using a table of values — that the points are (approximately) collinear. Then fit the line, read off gradient and intercept, and recover \(a, b\) by exponentiating. The straight line is the evidence that the model is correct; without it, you've only assumed the law.

Worked Example 10 — Fitting an exponential to data

Experimental data: \((x, y) = (1, 3.0), (2, 4.5), (3, 6.75)\). Find \(a\) and \(b\) so that \(y = a \cdot b^x\).

1.  Linearise: log y = log a + x log b.
2.  Compute log₁₀ of each y:
3.     x = 1:  log 3.0   = 0.4771
4.     x = 2:  log 4.5   = 0.6532
5.     x = 3:  log 6.75  = 0.8293
6.  Check constant first differences (confirming linearity):
7.     0.6532 − 0.4771 = 0.1761
8.     0.8293 − 0.6532 = 0.1761    ✓ equal ⇒ exactly linear in x.
9.  Gradient = log b = 0.1761   ⇒   b = 10^0.1761 = 1.5.
10. Intercept at x = 1:  log a + 0.1761 = 0.4771  ⇒  log a = 0.3010  ⇒  a = 2.
11. ∴ y = 2 · 1.5^x.
12. Verify:  x = 1:  2 · 1.5 = 3.0 ✓;  x = 2:  2 · 2.25 = 4.5 ✓;  x = 3:  2 · 3.375 = 6.75 ✓.

// Each y is obtained from the previous by multiplying by 1.5 — a geometric
// progression with common ratio b. Spotting that shortcuts the whole calculation.

9. Differentiation and Integration of Exponentials and Logarithms

The calculus rules for these functions are short, but ubiquitous. Only \(e^x\) and \(\ln x\) give clean formulas; general-base versions carry an extra factor of \(\ln a\).

\( \dfrac{d}{dx}(e^x) = e^x, \qquad \dfrac{d}{dx}(e^{kx}) = k\,e^{kx}, \qquad \dfrac{d}{dx}(a^x) = a^x \ln a. \)
\( \dfrac{d}{dx}(\ln x) = \dfrac{1}{x} \quad (x > 0), \qquad \dfrac{d}{dx}(\log_a x) = \dfrac{1}{x \ln a}. \)
\( \int e^{kx}\, dx = \dfrac{1}{k} e^{kx} + C, \qquad \int \dfrac{1}{x}\, dx = \ln|x| + C. \)

The absolute-value sign in \(\ln|x|\) is important: \(1/x\) is defined for \(x < 0\) as well, and integrating gives \(\ln(-x) + C\) there; \(\ln|x|\) covers both cases in one formula.

FunctionDerivativeAntiderivative
\(e^x\)\(e^x\)\(e^x + C\)
\(e^{kx}\)\(k e^{kx}\)\((1/k) e^{kx} + C\)
\(a^x\)\(a^x \ln a\)\(a^x / \ln a + C\)
\(\ln x\)\(1/x\)\(x \ln x - x + C\) (integration by parts)
\(1/x\)\(-1/x^2\)\(\ln|x| + C\)
\(\log_a x\)\(1/(x \ln a)\)\((x \log_a x - x/\ln a) + C\)

(The antiderivative of \(\ln x\), while listed, is a topic of chapter 1.8 — integration by parts — rather than strict 1.6 content.)

Exam tip — when to use logs to differentiate. For \(y = a^x\) or \(y = x^x\) or anything with a variable in the exponent, take \(\ln\) of both sides first, then differentiate implicitly. For \(y = a^x\): \(\ln y = x \ln a\) ⇒ \(y'/y = \ln a\) ⇒ \(y' = a^x \ln a\). This "logarithmic differentiation" trick converts an exponent-position variable into a multiplier and then a derivative.

Two handy identities used throughout: \(\ln 2 \approx 0.693\), \(\ln 10 \approx 2.303\). And because \(\log_{10} x = \ln x / \ln 10\), every \(\ln\)-to-\(\log_{10}\) conversion is a division by \(2.303\).

Exam tip — the range of a logarithm is all of \(\mathbb{R}\). Unlike sine or cosine, the output of \(\ln\) or \(\log_a\) is not bounded. If an equation reads \(\ln x = -15\), the solution \(x = e^{-15} \approx 3.06 \times 10^{-7}\) is perfectly valid — not "no solution". The only forbidden input is \(x \leq 0\); any real value can come out.

11. Consolidated Summary and Exam Strategy

Chapter-wide checklist for Exponentials and Logarithms.
  • \(a^x\) is always positive. Rule out solutions where you'd need \(a^x \leq 0\).
  • \(e^x\) and \(\ln x\) are inverses. \(e^{\ln x} = x\) for \(x > 0\); \(\ln(e^x) = x\) for all \(x\).
  • Three log laws + change of base. Product, quotient, power — plus \(\log_a x = \ln x / \ln a\). These collapse every log expression.
  • Solving \(a^x = b\): take logs, use power rule, divide. Any base works but \(\ln\) is default.
  • Solving log equations: combine to a single log, exponentiate, then check domain of every argument.
  • Growth/decay: \(P = P_0 e^{kt}\); doubling time \(T_2 = \ln 2 / k\); half-life \(T_{1/2} = \ln 2 / \lambda\).
  • Linearising data: \(y = a b^x\) → plot \(\log y\) vs \(x\); \(y = a x^n\) → plot \(\log y\) vs \(\log x\). Gradient gives the power, intercept gives the constant.
  • Calculus: \((e^{kx})' = k e^{kx}\); \((\ln x)' = 1/x\); \(\int e^{kx} dx = e^{kx}/k + C\); \(\int dx/x = \ln|x| + C\).
  • State units and check realism in modelling answers. A population of \(-200\), or a half-life of \(-3\) years, is a sign of arithmetic error.
Typical OCR examiner complaints (from mark reports).
  • Writing \(\log(x + y) = \log x + \log y\) — wrong: it's the product, not the sum, that splits.
  • Failing to reject a log-equation root that makes a log argument negative (e.g. \(x = -2\) in Worked Example 5).
  • Confusing the percentage growth rate \(r\) (e.g. 5%) with the continuous rate \(k\): \(k = \ln(1 + r)\), not \(r\).
  • Misapplying the power rule as \(\log(x^n) = (\log x)^n\) — the exponent multiplies, it does not raise.
  • Giving \(\ln(-1)\) or \(\ln 0\) as a finite number — both are undefined in the reals.
  • Dropping the \(\ln a\) factor when differentiating \(a^x\) for \(a \neq e\).
  • Integrating \(1/x\) as \(\ln x + C\) instead of \(\ln|x| + C\) and losing marks when the region includes \(x < 0\).
  • Plotting \(y\) against \(\log x\) instead of \(\log y\) against \(x\) for an exponential model, giving a curved graph and no law.
Formula snapshot (memorise).
  • Exponential: \(y = a^x\), domain \(\mathbb{R}\), range \((0, \infty)\), through \((0, 1)\), asymptote \(y = 0\).
  • Natural exponential: \(y = e^x\), with \((e^x)' = e^x\); Euler's constant \(e = \lim_{n\to\infty}(1 + 1/n)^n \approx 2.71828\).
  • Natural log: \(y = \ln x\), inverse of \(e^x\); domain \((0, \infty)\), range \(\mathbb{R}\), through \((1, 0)\), asymptote \(x = 0\).
  • Log laws: \(\log(xy) = \log x + \log y\); \(\log(x/y) = \log x - \log y\); \(\log(x^n) = n\log x\); \(\log_a x = \ln x / \ln a\).
  • Growth / decay: \(P = P_0 e^{kt}\); doubling time \(T_2 = \ln 2 / k\); half-life \(T_{1/2} = \ln 2 / \lambda\).
  • Linearising: \(y = a b^x\) ⇒ \(\log y = \log a + x\log b\); \(y = a x^n\) ⇒ \(\log y = \log a + n \log x\).
  • Calculus: \((e^{kx})' = k e^{kx}\); \((a^x)' = a^x \ln a\); \((\ln x)' = 1/x\); \(\int e^{kx} dx = e^{kx}/k + C\); \(\int dx/x = \ln|x| + C\).
