1.10 Vectors

Mathematics — OCR A-Level (H240) — Paper 1: Pure Mathematics

Vectors are the mathematical language of direction. Where an ordinary number — a scalar — answers the question "how much?", a vector additionally answers "which way?". Every displacement, every velocity, every force, every magnetic field is a vector; modern physics, computer graphics, machine learning and engineering are unthinkable without them. At A-level the scope is deliberately concrete: two- and three-dimensional vectors expressed in Cartesian components, the scalar (dot) product, and the vector equation of a straight line. Once these ideas are secure, geometric problems that would otherwise require lengthy coordinate algebra — "are these three points collinear?", "what is the angle between these two lines?", "do these lines intersect, and if so where?" — collapse into a few lines of vector arithmetic.

1. Introduction: Scalars vs Vectors

A scalar quantity is described by a single real number: temperature (20°C), mass (3 kg), speed (60 mph), time (5 s). A vector quantity requires both a magnitude (a positive real number) and a direction: displacement (5 m north), velocity (60 mph due east), force (10 N downwards).

Vector. A vector is a quantity that has both a magnitude (a non-negative size) and a direction. Two vectors are equal if and only if they have the same magnitude and the same direction — their position in space is irrelevant. Vectors that differ only by a translation are considered the same vector.

Three common ways of writing a vector in print and by hand:

  • Bold face: \(\mathbf{a}\), \(\mathbf{v}\), \(\mathbf{F}\) — standard in textbooks.
  • Arrow (handwritten): \(\vec{a}\), or \(\overrightarrow{AB}\) for the directed line segment from \(A\) to \(B\).
  • Underline (handwritten): \(\underline{a}\) — examiner-friendly and unmistakeable.

A vector in 2D or 3D is most easily specified by its components relative to a chosen set of perpendicular axes. Two notations are in constant use:

\( \mathbf{a} = \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix} = x\,\mathbf{i} + y\,\mathbf{j} \qquad \text{(2D)} \)
\( \mathbf{a} = \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{pmatrix} = x\,\mathbf{i} + y\,\mathbf{j} + z\,\mathbf{k} \qquad \text{(3D)} \)

Here \(\mathbf{i}, \mathbf{j}, \mathbf{k}\) are the base unit vectors of unit length along the \(x\)-, \(y\)- and \(z\)-axes respectively. The column form and the \(\mathbf{i}/\mathbf{j}/\mathbf{k}\) form are interchangeable; you should be fluent in both because examiners switch between them without warning.

Position vector. Fix an origin \(O\). The position vector of a point \(P\) is the directed line segment from \(O\) to \(P\), written \(\overrightarrow{OP}\) or simply \(\mathbf{p}\). If \(P\) has Cartesian coordinates \((x, y, z)\) then \[ \mathbf{p} = \overrightarrow{OP} = \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{pmatrix} = x\mathbf{i} + y\mathbf{j} + z\mathbf{k}. \] Position vectors live on a fixed tail (the origin); arbitrary "free" vectors do not.
Exam tip — mark vectors clearly. In handwritten work, always indicate that a symbol stands for a vector: underline it (\(\underline{a}\)) or draw an arrow (\(\vec{a}\)). A naked letter \(a\) reads as a scalar and may cost method marks. For named segments write the arrow explicitly: \(\overrightarrow{AB}\), not just \(AB\) (which is the length of the segment — a scalar).

2. Magnitude and Unit Vectors

The magnitude (or modulus, or length) of a vector is the length of the directed segment that represents it. In components it is given directly by Pythagoras:

Magnitude of a vector. For \(\mathbf{a} = x\mathbf{i} + y\mathbf{j}\) in 2D, \[ |\mathbf{a}| = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}. \] For \(\mathbf{a} = x\mathbf{i} + y\mathbf{j} + z\mathbf{k}\) in 3D, \[ |\mathbf{a}| = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2 + z^2}. \] The magnitude is a non-negative scalar; \(|\mathbf{a}| = 0\) if and only if \(\mathbf{a}\) is the zero vector \(\mathbf{0}\).
Unit vector. A unit vector is any vector of magnitude 1. For a non-zero vector \(\mathbf{a}\), the unit vector in the same direction as \(\mathbf{a}\) is \[ \hat{\mathbf{a}} = \frac{\mathbf{a}}{|\mathbf{a}|}. \] Every non-zero vector can be written as \(\mathbf{a} = |\mathbf{a}|\,\hat{\mathbf{a}}\) — the product of its magnitude and a pure direction. The three base vectors \(\mathbf{i}, \mathbf{j}, \mathbf{k}\) are unit vectors along the coordinate axes, with \(|\mathbf{i}| = |\mathbf{j}| = |\mathbf{k}| = 1\).

Worked Example 1 — Magnitude and unit vector

Find the magnitude of \(\mathbf{a} = 2\mathbf{i} + 3\mathbf{j} - \mathbf{k}\) and the unit vector in the same direction.

1.  Components: (x, y, z) = (2, 3, −1).
2.  |a| = √(2² + 3² + (−1)²) = √(4 + 9 + 1) = √14.
3.  â = a / |a| = (1/√14)(2i + 3j − k)
4.      = (2/√14) i + (3/√14) j − (1/√14) k.
5.  Check: |â|² = (4 + 9 + 1)/14 = 14/14 = 1  ✓
6.
7.  Numerically: |a| ≈ 3.742, so â ≈ 0.535 i + 0.802 j − 0.267 k.

// Rationalising the denominator is optional — examiners accept (2/√14) i etc.
Exam tip — leave magnitudes in surd form. Unless the question specifically asks for a decimal, leave \(|\mathbf{a}| = \sqrt{14}\) rather than \(3.742\ldots\). Surd form is exact, shorter to write, and is what the mark scheme usually shows. Rounding early compounds error.

3. Addition, Subtraction and Scalar Multiplication

Vectors behave algebraically much like ordinary numbers — except that we always work component-wise.

\( \begin{pmatrix} a_1 \\ a_2 \\ a_3 \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} b_1 \\ b_2 \\ b_3 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} a_1 + b_1 \\ a_2 + b_2 \\ a_3 + b_3 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad \lambda \begin{pmatrix} a_1 \\ a_2 \\ a_3 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \lambda a_1 \\ \lambda a_2 \\ \lambda a_3 \end{pmatrix}. \)

Geometrically these operations are visualised by the classic diagrams:

  • Triangle law of addition. To add \(\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b}\), place the tail of \(\mathbf{b}\) at the head of \(\mathbf{a}\); the sum is the vector from the start of \(\mathbf{a}\) to the end of \(\mathbf{b}\).
  • Parallelogram law. Placing \(\mathbf{a}\) and \(\mathbf{b}\) tail-to-tail, \(\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b}\) is the diagonal of the parallelogram with sides \(\mathbf{a}\) and \(\mathbf{b}\). The other diagonal represents \(\mathbf{b} - \mathbf{a}\).
  • Scalar multiplication. \(\lambda \mathbf{a}\) has magnitude \(|\lambda|\cdot|\mathbf{a}|\). If \(\lambda > 0\) it points the same way as \(\mathbf{a}\); if \(\lambda < 0\) it points in the opposite direction; if \(\lambda = 0\) it is the zero vector.

Worked Example 2 — Adding and scaling vectors

Given \(\mathbf{a} = 2\mathbf{i} + 3\mathbf{j} - \mathbf{k}\) and \(\mathbf{b} = \mathbf{i} - \mathbf{j} + 4\mathbf{k}\), find \(\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b}\), \(\mathbf{a} - \mathbf{b}\), \(3\mathbf{a} - 2\mathbf{b}\) and \(|\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b}|\).

1.  a + b = (2 + 1)i + (3 + (−1))j + (−1 + 4)k
2.        = 3 i + 2 j + 3 k.
3.  a − b = (2 − 1)i + (3 − (−1))j + (−1 − 4)k
4.        = i + 4 j − 5 k.
5.  3a    = 6 i + 9 j − 3 k.
6.  2b    = 2 i − 2 j + 8 k.
7.  3a − 2b = (6 − 2)i + (9 − (−2))j + (−3 − 8)k
8.          = 4 i + 11 j − 11 k.
9.
10. |a + b| = √(3² + 2² + 3²) = √(9 + 4 + 9) = √22  ≈ 4.690.

4. The Vector Between Two Points

Once every point has a position vector, the directed segment between any two points is just the difference:

Vector between two points. Let \(A\) and \(B\) have position vectors \(\mathbf{a}\) and \(\mathbf{b}\) respectively. Then the displacement from \(A\) to \(B\) is \[ \overrightarrow{AB} = \mathbf{b} - \mathbf{a}. \] (Think "finish minus start", or "head minus tail".) In particular \(\overrightarrow{BA} = \mathbf{a} - \mathbf{b} = -\overrightarrow{AB}\).

This single identity is the backbone of almost every A-level vector problem. From it follow:

  • Distance between points: \(AB = |\overrightarrow{AB}| = |\mathbf{b} - \mathbf{a}|\).
  • Midpoint. The midpoint \(M\) of \(AB\) has position vector \(\displaystyle \mathbf{m} = \tfrac{1}{2}(\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b}).\)
  • Point dividing \(AB\) in ratio \(\lambda : \mu\): position vector \(\dfrac{\mu \mathbf{a} + \lambda \mathbf{b}}{\lambda + \mu}.\)

Worked Example 3 — Distance, midpoint and displacement

Points \(A(1, -2, 4)\) and \(B(4, 2, 0)\). Find \(\overrightarrow{AB}\), the distance \(AB\) and the midpoint of \(AB\).

1.  a = i − 2j + 4k,   b = 4i + 2j + 0k.
2.  AB = b − a = (4 − 1)i + (2 − (−2))j + (0 − 4)k
3.             = 3 i + 4 j − 4 k.
4.  |AB| = √(3² + 4² + (−4)²) = √(9 + 16 + 16) = √41  ≈ 6.403.
5.  Midpoint m = (a + b)/2
6.             = ( (1+4)/2 ) i + ( (−2+2)/2 ) j + ( (4+0)/2 ) k
7.             = 2.5 i + 0 j + 2 k.
8.  So M = (2.5, 0, 2).

5. Parallel, Equal and Collinear

Parallel vectors. Two non-zero vectors \(\mathbf{a}\) and \(\mathbf{b}\) are parallel if and only if there exists a scalar \(k \neq 0\) with \[ \mathbf{b} = k\,\mathbf{a}. \] If \(k > 0\) they point the same way; if \(k < 0\) they point in opposite directions — anti-parallel. The zero vector \(\mathbf{0}\) is conventionally considered parallel to every vector.

To test whether \(\mathbf{a}\) and \(\mathbf{b}\) are parallel in practice, check whether the component ratios are equal: in 3D, \(a_1/b_1 = a_2/b_2 = a_3/b_3\) (with the usual care if any \(b_i = 0\)).

This test extends to collinearity of three points. \(A\), \(B\), \(C\) are collinear (they lie on one straight line) exactly when \(\overrightarrow{AB}\) and \(\overrightarrow{AC}\) are parallel:

\( \overrightarrow{AC} = k\,\overrightarrow{AB} \quad\text{for some scalar } k. \)

Worked Example 4 — Showing three points are collinear

Show that \(A(1, 2, 3)\), \(B(3, 5, 7)\) and \(C(7, 11, 15)\) are collinear.

1.  AB = b − a = (3−1, 5−2, 7−3)  = (2, 3, 4).
2.  AC = c − a = (7−1, 11−2, 15−3) = (6, 9, 12).
3.  Notice AC = 3·(2, 3, 4) = 3·AB.
4.  So AC is a (positive) scalar multiple of AB — the two vectors are parallel,
5.  and they share the point A. Hence A, B, C lie on one straight line.   ∎
6.  (Since k = 3 > 1, C lies beyond B on the ray from A through B.)
Exam tip — reduce direction vectors to simplest form. A vector \((4, 6, 8)\) and \((2, 3, 4)\) describe the same direction — one is exactly twice the other. When writing a line or comparing directions, factor out any common integer factor. Cleaner numbers make the comparison "are these parallel?" obvious and make arithmetic less error-prone.

6. The Scalar (Dot) Product

Addition and scalar multiplication tell us about vectors alongside each other; the scalar product is how we quantify the angle between them. It is the single most useful tool in vector geometry at A-level.

Scalar (dot) product — algebraic definition. For \(\mathbf{a} = a_1\mathbf{i} + a_2\mathbf{j} + a_3\mathbf{k}\) and \(\mathbf{b} = b_1\mathbf{i} + b_2\mathbf{j} + b_3\mathbf{k}\), \[ \mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = a_1 b_1 + a_2 b_2 + a_3 b_3. \] In 2D the third term is simply absent. The result is a scalar (a real number), not a vector.
Scalar product — geometric definition. If \(\theta\) is the angle between \(\mathbf{a}\) and \(\mathbf{b}\) when they are placed tail-to-tail (with \(0 \le \theta \le \pi\)), then \[ \mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = |\mathbf{a}|\,|\mathbf{b}|\,\cos\theta. \] The two definitions are equivalent — they always give the same number — and together they let us compute angles directly from components.

From the geometric form we can read off three immediate and very useful facts:

  • \(\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{a} = |\mathbf{a}|^2\) (setting \(\mathbf{b} = \mathbf{a}\), so \(\theta = 0\) and \(\cos\theta = 1\)).
  • \(\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = 0\) exactly when \(\mathbf{a}\) and \(\mathbf{b}\) are perpendicular (or one is \(\mathbf{0}\)), because \(\cos(\pi/2) = 0\).
  • \(\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} > 0\) when \(\theta\) is acute, \(\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} < 0\) when \(\theta\) is obtuse.
Algebraic properties of the dot product.
  • Commutative: \(\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = \mathbf{b} \cdot \mathbf{a}\).
  • Distributive over addition: \(\mathbf{a} \cdot (\mathbf{b} + \mathbf{c}) = \mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} + \mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{c}\).
  • Compatible with scalar multiplication: \((\lambda \mathbf{a}) \cdot \mathbf{b} = \lambda(\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}) = \mathbf{a} \cdot (\lambda \mathbf{b})\).
  • Self-product: \(\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{a} = |\mathbf{a}|^2 \ge 0\), with equality iff \(\mathbf{a} = \mathbf{0}\).
  • Base vectors: \(\mathbf{i}\cdot\mathbf{i} = \mathbf{j}\cdot\mathbf{j} = \mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{k} = 1\); \(\mathbf{i}\cdot\mathbf{j} = \mathbf{j}\cdot\mathbf{k} = \mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{i} = 0\).
PropertyStatementIn words
Symmetry\(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b} = \mathbf{b}\cdot\mathbf{a}\)Order does not matter.
Linearity\(\mathbf{a}\cdot(\lambda\mathbf{b} + \mu\mathbf{c}) = \lambda(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}) + \mu(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{c})\)Dot product distributes like multiplication.
Self-product\(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{a} = |\mathbf{a}|^2\)Dotting a vector with itself gives its squared length.
Zero product\(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b} = 0 \Leftrightarrow \mathbf{a} \perp \mathbf{b}\) (for non-zero vectors)Orthogonality criterion.

Worked Example 5 — Computing a scalar product

For \(\mathbf{a} = 2\mathbf{i} + 3\mathbf{j} - \mathbf{k}\) and \(\mathbf{b} = \mathbf{i} - \mathbf{j} + 4\mathbf{k}\), evaluate \(\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}\) and determine whether the angle between them is acute, right, or obtuse.

1.  a · b = (2)(1) + (3)(−1) + (−1)(4)
2.        = 2 − 3 − 4
3.        = −5.
4.  Since a · b < 0, the angle between a and b is OBTUSE.
5.  (Had it been 0, the vectors would be perpendicular.
6.   Had it been positive, the angle would be acute.)
Exam tip — dot product is a scalar, not a vector. Watch your units: \(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}\) is a single number, written without bold face or underline. A frequent slip is to write "\(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b} = 5\mathbf{i}\)" or to leave it in bold — both cost marks. Similarly, \(|\mathbf{a}|\) is a scalar.

7. Angle Between Vectors

Rearranging the geometric definition of the scalar product gives a direct formula for the angle between two non-zero vectors:

Angle between two vectors. For non-zero vectors \(\mathbf{a}\) and \(\mathbf{b}\), the angle \(\theta \in [0, \pi]\) between them satisfies \[ \cos\theta = \frac{\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}}{|\mathbf{a}|\,|\mathbf{b}|}. \] Perpendicularity is the case \(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b} = 0\); parallel (same direction) is \(\cos\theta = 1\); anti-parallel is \(\cos\theta = -1\).

Worked Example 6 — Angle between two 3D vectors

Find, to the nearest degree, the angle between \(\mathbf{a} = 2\mathbf{i} + 3\mathbf{j} - \mathbf{k}\) and \(\mathbf{b} = \mathbf{i} - \mathbf{j} + 4\mathbf{k}\).

1.  a · b = 2(1) + 3(−1) + (−1)(4) = 2 − 3 − 4 = −5   (from WE 5).
2.  |a|   = √(4 + 9 + 1)  = √14.
3.  |b|   = √(1 + 1 + 16) = √18 = 3√2.
4.  cos θ = (a · b)/(|a| |b|) = −5 / (√14 · 3√2) = −5 / (3√28).
5.  Simplify:  3√28 = 3·2√7 = 6√7.
6.             cos θ = −5 / (6√7) ≈ −0.31497.
7.  θ = arccos(−0.31497) ≈ 108.36°.
8.
9.  Answer:  θ ≈ 108° (to the nearest degree).   Obtuse, as predicted.

Worked Example 7 — Angle in 2D

Find the angle between \(\mathbf{d}_1 = \mathbf{i} + 2\mathbf{j}\) and \(\mathbf{d}_2 = 3\mathbf{i} - \mathbf{j}\).

1.  d₁ · d₂ = (1)(3) + (2)(−1) = 3 − 2 = 1.
2.  |d₁|    = √(1 + 4) = √5.
3.  |d₂|    = √(9 + 1) = √10.
4.  cos θ   = 1 / (√5 · √10) = 1/√50 = 1/(5√2) ≈ 0.1414.
5.  θ       = arccos(0.1414) ≈ 81.87°.
6.
7.  Answer:  θ ≈ 81.9°  (acute, as expected since d₁·d₂ > 0).
Exam tip — use |cos θ| for the acute angle between lines. For two vectors the angle \(\theta \in [0, \pi]\) is uniquely determined by \(\cos\theta\); a negative cosine gives an obtuse angle. But for two lines (which are not oriented), the convention is to quote the acute angle. Take the absolute value: \(\cos\theta = |\mathbf{d}_1 \cdot \mathbf{d}_2| / (|\mathbf{d}_1|\,|\mathbf{d}_2|)\), so that \(\theta \in [0, \pi/2]\). Always read the question carefully — "angle between vectors" vs "angle between lines".

8. Vector Equation of a Line

A straight line in space is completely determined by (i) one point on it, and (ii) a direction.

Vector equation of a line. The line passing through the point with position vector \(\mathbf{a}\), in the direction of the non-zero vector \(\mathbf{d}\), is the set of points \(P\) with position vector \[ \mathbf{r} = \mathbf{a} + t\,\mathbf{d}, \qquad t \in \mathbb{R}. \] The parameter \(t\) sweeps out the whole line: at \(t = 0\) we are at \(\mathbf{a}\); positive \(t\) moves in the direction of \(\mathbf{d}\), negative \(t\) moves the other way.

The direction vector \(\mathbf{d}\) is not unique — any non-zero scalar multiple \(\lambda \mathbf{d}\) describes the same line (one simply reparameterises the \(t\)). Similarly, any point on the line can serve as \(\mathbf{a}\).

Equating components gives the parametric Cartesian form:

\( x = a_1 + t\,d_1, \qquad y = a_2 + t\,d_2, \qquad z = a_3 + t\,d_3. \)

Eliminating \(t\) (where all \(d_i \neq 0\)) yields the symmetric Cartesian form of the line:

\( \dfrac{x - a_1}{d_1} = \dfrac{y - a_2}{d_2} = \dfrac{z - a_3}{d_3}. \)

Worked Example 8 — Vector equation through a given point

Find the vector equation of the straight line through \(A(1, 2, 3)\) parallel to the direction \(2\mathbf{i} - \mathbf{j} + \mathbf{k}\).

1.  Take a = i + 2j + 3k   and  d = 2i − j + k.
2.  Vector equation:
3.      r = (1)i + (2)j + (3)k + t[(2)i + (−1)j + (1)k]
4.        = (1 + 2t) i + (2 − t) j + (3 + t) k,       t ∈ ℝ.
5.  Parametric form:  x = 1 + 2t,  y = 2 − t,  z = 3 + t.
6.  Cartesian form:   (x − 1)/2 = (y − 2)/(−1) = (z − 3)/1.
7.
8.  Check: t = 0 gives (1, 2, 3) = A.   ✓
9.         t = 1 gives (3, 1, 4) — another point on the line.

Worked Example 9 — Does a point lie on a given line?

Show that the point \(C(4, 5, 7)\) lies on the line \(\mathbf{r} = (1, 2, 1) + t(1, 1, 2)\).

1.  If C lies on the line, there is a single value of t for which
2.     1 + t = 4,   2 + t = 5,   1 + 2t = 7.
3.  From the x-equation: t = 3.
4.  Check y:  2 + 3 = 5.  ✓
5.  Check z:  1 + 2(3) = 1 + 6 = 7.  ✓
6.  All three equations are satisfied with t = 3, so C is on the line.   ∎
7.
8.  (If any one component had disagreed, C would not have been on the line.)
Exam tip — check all three components. To verify a point lies on a 3D line, solving one component gives a candidate \(t\); you must substitute it into the remaining two components to confirm. Stopping after matching one or two is a classic error — and invites the examiner to set a question where exactly two components agree but the third does not.

9. Intersection of Two Lines

In 2D any two lines are either parallel (no intersection, unless they coincide) or meet in a single point. In 3D there is a richer set of possibilities.

Skew lines. Two lines in 3D are skew if they are neither parallel nor intersecting — that is, they do not meet and are not going in the same direction. Skew lines are unique to three (or more) dimensions; they cannot exist in a plane.
CaseDirectionsIntersect?Description
Identical / coincidentParallelInfinitely many common pointsSame line; every point of one is a point of the other.
Parallel (not coincident)ParallelNoSame direction, different lines; never meet.
IntersectingNon-parallelExactly one common pointThe lines cross at a single point.
SkewNon-parallelNoLines miss each other in 3D; no common point.

To classify two lines in 3D, first compare their direction vectors:

  • If the directions are parallel (\(\mathbf{d}_2 = \lambda \mathbf{d}_1\)), either the lines are the same (any point on one is on the other) or they are parallel-but-not-equal.
  • If the directions are not parallel, the lines are either intersecting or skew. Set the two vector equations equal and try to solve.
Algorithm for testing intersection of two lines in 3D. Given lines \(\mathbf{r}_1 = \mathbf{a}_1 + s\,\mathbf{d}_1\) and \(\mathbf{r}_2 = \mathbf{a}_2 + t\,\mathbf{d}_2\) with non-parallel directions:
  1. Write \(\mathbf{a}_1 + s\,\mathbf{d}_1 = \mathbf{a}_2 + t\,\mathbf{d}_2\); this gives three scalar equations in the two unknowns \(s\) and \(t\).
  2. Solve any two of the equations (usually the \(x\) and \(y\) components) for \(s\) and \(t\).
  3. Substitute these values into the third equation. If it is satisfied, the lines intersect; substitute \(s\) back into \(\mathbf{r}_1\) to find the common point. If not, the lines are skew.
Exam tip — a skew test requires two conclusions. To prove two lines are skew you must state both (a) the directions are not parallel, and (b) there is no simultaneous solution for the parameters. Missing either half (for example, concluding "skew" just because the parameters don't match) is an incomplete answer. Parallel-but-non-intersecting is not the same as skew.

Worked Example 10 — Do two lines intersect?

Determine whether the lines \(\mathbf{r}_1 = (1, 0, 2) + s(2, 1, -1)\) and \(\mathbf{r}_2 = (3, 1, 1) + t(1, -1, 2)\) intersect. If they do, find the point.

1.  Directions:  d₁ = (2, 1, −1),  d₂ = (1, −1, 2).
2.  These are NOT parallel — no scalar λ gives d₂ = λ d₁
3.      (would need 1 = 2λ ⇒ λ = 1/2, but then −1 ≠ 1/2).
4.  So the lines are either intersecting or skew.
5.
6.  Set r₁ = r₂ component-wise:
7.     x:   1 + 2s = 3 + t          ... (i)
8.     y:   0 + s  = 1 − t          ... (ii)
9.     z:   2 − s  = 1 + 2t         ... (iii)
10.
11. Solve (i) and (ii):
12.    (i):   2s − t = 2.
13.    (ii):  s + t  = 1.
14.    Add:   3s = 3     ⇒  s = 1,  then t = 0.
15.
16. Check with (iii):  LHS = 2 − 1 = 1,  RHS = 1 + 2(0) = 1.   ✓
17. All three satisfied, so the lines DO intersect.
18.
19. Point of intersection:  sub s = 1 into r₁:
20.    r₁(1) = (1, 0, 2) + 1·(2, 1, −1) = (3, 1, 1).
21. (Consistent: same point as r₂(0) = (3, 1, 1).)
22.
23. Answer: the lines meet at P(3, 1, 1).

Worked Example 11 — A pair of skew lines

Show that \(\mathbf{r}_1 = (0, 0, 0) + s(1, 1, 0)\) and \(\mathbf{r}_2 = (0, 1, 1) + t(1, 0, 1)\) are skew.

1.  Directions d₁ = (1, 1, 0),  d₂ = (1, 0, 1).
2.  Not parallel: d₂ is not a scalar multiple of d₁ (components disagree).
3.
4.  Set r₁ = r₂:
5.     x:  s      = 0 + t          ⇒  s = t      ... (i)
6.     y:  s      = 1 + 0           ⇒  s = 1      ... (ii)
7.     z:  0      = 1 + t           ⇒  t = −1     ... (iii)
8.
9.  From (ii) and (iii):  s = 1, t = −1. But (i) requires s = t.
10.    1 ≠ −1, so the system is INCONSISTENT — no common (s, t).
11.
12. Conclusion: directions not parallel AND no intersection ⇒ lines are SKEW.  ∎

10. Applications: Perpendicular Distance and Foot of Perpendicular

The dot product combines naturally with the vector equation of a line to solve classic geometric problems.

10.1 Angle between two lines

The angle between two lines is the acute angle between their direction vectors:

\( \cos\theta = \dfrac{|\mathbf{d}_1 \cdot \mathbf{d}_2|}{|\mathbf{d}_1|\,|\mathbf{d}_2|}, \qquad \theta \in [0, \pi/2]. \)

Two lines are perpendicular iff \(\mathbf{d}_1 \cdot \mathbf{d}_2 = 0\) and parallel iff \(\mathbf{d}_1 = \lambda \mathbf{d}_2\) for some \(\lambda\).

10.2 Foot of perpendicular from a point to a line

Let \(\ell: \mathbf{r} = \mathbf{a} + t\mathbf{d}\) be a line and \(P\) a point (with position vector \(\mathbf{p}\)). The foot of the perpendicular \(F\) from \(P\) to \(\ell\) is the unique point of \(\ell\) closest to \(P\). Two equivalent methods:

  • Orthogonality condition. \(F = \mathbf{a} + t\mathbf{d}\) for some \(t\), and \(\overrightarrow{FP}\) is perpendicular to the line, i.e. \(\overrightarrow{FP} \cdot \mathbf{d} = 0\). This gives a linear equation in \(t\).
  • Minimising distance. Let \(\mathbf{r}(t) = \mathbf{a} + t\mathbf{d}\); the squared distance from \(P\) to \(\mathbf{r}(t)\) is \(|\mathbf{p} - \mathbf{r}(t)|^2\), a quadratic in \(t\). Differentiate and set equal to zero to find the minimising \(t\) — you get the same condition as above.

The perpendicular distance from \(P\) to \(\ell\) is then \(|\overrightarrow{FP}| = |\mathbf{p} - \mathbf{a} - t\mathbf{d}|\) evaluated at the optimal \(t\).

Worked Example 12 — Foot of perpendicular and shortest distance

Find the foot of the perpendicular from \(P(5, 2, 3)\) to the line \(\ell: \mathbf{r} = (1, 0, 1) + t(2, 1, 2)\). Hence find the shortest distance from \(P\) to \(\ell\).

Exam tip — skew lines only exist in 3D. In two dimensions every pair of lines either meets or is parallel — "skew" is impossible. Any OCR 3D vectors question that asks you to classify a pair of lines expects you to consider all four cases: coincident, parallel, intersecting, skew.

15. Chapter Summary and Examiner's Checklist

Chapter-wide checklist for Vectors.
  • Notation: always underline or bold handwritten vectors; distinguish \(\overrightarrow{AB}\) (a vector) from \(AB\) (a length).
  • Magnitude: \(|\mathbf{a}| = \sqrt{\sum a_i^2}\) — leave in surd form unless asked otherwise.
  • Unit vector: \(\hat{\mathbf{a}} = \mathbf{a}/|\mathbf{a}|\); always verify \(|\hat{\mathbf{a}}| = 1\).
  • Dot product: component sum \(a_1 b_1 + a_2 b_2 + a_3 b_3\); remember it is a scalar.
  • Angle: \(\cos\theta = (\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b})/(|\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|)\); take \(|\cdot|\) for the acute angle between lines.
  • Perpendicular: \(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b} = 0\). Parallel: \(\mathbf{b} = k\mathbf{a}\).
  • Line equation: \(\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{a} + t\mathbf{d}\); reduce \(\mathbf{d}\) to simplest integer form.
  • Point on a line: check all three components produce the same \(t\).
  • Intersection of lines in 3D: solve two equations, check the third; mismatch means skew (provided directions not parallel).
  • Skew lines: state both "not parallel" and "don't intersect" to score full marks.
  • Foot of perpendicular: parameterise the foot, use \(\overrightarrow{FP}\cdot\mathbf{d} = 0\), solve for \(t\).
Typical OCR examiner complaints (from mark reports).
  • Omitting the vector notation (no bold, underline or arrow), so components read as ordinary variables.
  • Confusing "angle between vectors" (\(\theta \in [0, \pi]\)) with "angle between lines" (\(\theta \in [0, \pi/2]\)); giving an obtuse answer for a line angle.
  • Declaring "lines are parallel" as the reason they don't meet, without checking whether they are in fact coincident.
  • Concluding "skew" after merely failing to find a \(t\) — without first confirming the directions are not parallel.
  • For a line question, solving one component for \(t\) and not substituting into the others — missing the consistency check.
  • Rounding \(\cos\theta\) early to 3 d.p. and then quoting \(\theta\) to 5 d.p. — rounding error propagates in \(\arccos\).
  • Losing the minus sign when computing \(\mathbf{b} - \mathbf{a}\); write each component as a bracketed subtraction.
  • Forgetting that the dot product is a scalar — writing answers like "\(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b} = 3\mathbf{i}\)".
Formula snapshot (memorise).
  • Magnitude: \(|\mathbf{a}| = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2 + z^2}\).
  • Unit vector: \(\hat{\mathbf{a}} = \mathbf{a}/|\mathbf{a}|\).
  • Vector between points: \(\overrightarrow{AB} = \mathbf{b} - \mathbf{a}\).
  • Dot product: \(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b} = a_1 b_1 + a_2 b_2 + a_3 b_3 = |\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|\cos\theta\).
  • Angle: \(\cos\theta = (\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b})/(|\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|)\).
  • Perpendicular: \(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b} = 0\).
  • Line: \(\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{a} + t\mathbf{d}\).
  • Foot of perpendicular: \((\mathbf{p} - \mathbf{a} - t\mathbf{d})\cdot\mathbf{d} = 0 \Rightarrow t\).
