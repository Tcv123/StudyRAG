2.1 Statistics

Mathematics — OCR A-Level (H240) — Paper 3: Statistics & Mechanics

Statistics is the mathematics of uncertain data. Where pure mathematics trades in exact statements — "the derivative of \(\sin x\) is \(\cos x\)" — statistics asks the softer but more practical question: given a noisy, partial observation of the world, what can we reasonably conclude? The OCR H240 Statistics content splits into three strands that build on one another. First, data handling: how to collect a sample honestly, summarise it numerically, and visualise it. Second, probability: the calculus of chance that lets us reason about randomness, culminating in two named distributions — the discrete Binomial and the continuous Normal — which between them describe an astonishing slice of real phenomena. Third, statistical inference: formal hypothesis testing, where we put a claim about a population on trial and use sample data to decide whether the evidence against it is strong enough to reject it. Every paper 3 Statistics section rests on these three pillars; this chapter treats them at full A-level depth.

1. Populations, Samples and Sampling Methods

The very first decision any statistician faces is what to count. A population is the entire set of individuals (people, machines, measurements, events, …) about which we want to draw a conclusion; a sample is a subset of that population actually inspected. Because populations are usually too large, too expensive, or simply impossible to examine in full, nearly all real-world statistics is sample-based.

Population and sample. A population is the complete collection of items of interest — every registered voter, every bolt manufactured on a shift, every egg laid by a flock. A sample is any subset of the population actually observed. A statistic computed from a sample (e.g. the sample mean \(\bar{x}\)) is an estimate of the corresponding population quantity (e.g. the population mean \(\mu\)).

A sample is only useful if it is representative: the characteristics of the sample mirror those of the population. A sample that systematically over- or under-represents some group is called biased. Good sampling design minimises bias; poor design produces numbers that, no matter how carefully analysed, describe something other than the population of interest.

Bias and sampling error. Bias is a systematic tendency for a sampling method to favour certain kinds of outcome, producing estimates that are wrong on average. Sampling error is the random variation between one sample and another drawn by the same method from the same population — even a perfect method will not give identical samples each time. Bias is structural and does not shrink with larger samples; sampling error does.

1.1 Simple random sampling

The gold standard: every member of the population is equally likely to be selected, and every sample of a given size is equally likely to arise. In practice one numbers the population 1 to \(N\), then uses a random-number generator (or table) to pick \(n\) distinct numbers without replacement.

Simple random sample. A sample of size \(n\) from a population of size \(N\) such that every possible sample of size \(n\) has the same probability, \(1\big/\binom{N}{n}\), of being chosen. Equivalently, each individual has probability \(n/N\) of being in the sample, and selections are made without replacement.

1.2 Systematic sampling

Order the population 1 to \(N\) (e.g. by a list), choose a starting point at random in the first \(k\) items, then select every \(k\)th item thereafter. Typically \(k = \lfloor N/n \rfloor\).

1.3 Stratified sampling

Divide the population into non-overlapping strata (e.g. age bands, school year groups, male/female). Take a simple random sample from each stratum, sized in proportion to the stratum's share of the population: from a stratum of size \(N_i\) in a population of size \(N\), take \(n_i = n\cdot N_i/N\).

Stratified sampling. The population is partitioned into mutually exclusive strata. Independent simple random samples are drawn from each stratum, with sample sizes proportional to stratum sizes. Stratified sampling reduces sampling error whenever the strata are internally homogeneous but differ between one another.

1.4 Cluster sampling

The population is split into clusters (e.g. schools, neighbourhoods); a random selection of clusters is taken, and every individual within the chosen clusters is surveyed (or a further sample is taken within each). Cheap in time and travel but usually gives a larger sampling error than a simple random sample of the same size.

1.5 Opportunity and quota sampling

Opportunity sampling (also called convenience sampling) takes whoever is readily available — the first 30 people out of the school gate, say. Quota sampling sets targets for sub-groups (e.g. "20 men, 20 women") and fills each quota by convenience. Both are non-random, quick, and cheap, but notoriously prone to bias because the interviewer (consciously or not) selects the sample.

Exam tip — justify the choice of sampling method. OCR frequently asks "state one advantage and one disadvantage" of a named method, or "explain why this sample may not be representative". Good answers identify the mechanism of bias: e.g. "asking shoppers at 10 am on a Tuesday over-represents retirees and stay-at-home parents". Do not simply write "it's biased" — say how.

2. Types of Data and Presentation

Before choosing a statistical technique we must classify the data. The basic split is qualitative (categorical — colour, brand, response "yes/no/maybe") versus quantitative (numerical), with numerical data further split into discrete (counts; values in a separate list, e.g. number of siblings) and continuous (measurements; any real value in an interval, e.g. height in cm).

Qualitative (categorical)Blood type, car colour, brand preference.Bar chart; pie chart.
Discrete numericalNumber of children; dice score; goals scored.Bar chart; vertical line chart.
Continuous numericalHeight, mass, reaction time, temperature.Histogram; cumulative frequency curve; box plot.

2.1 Frequency tables

An ungrouped frequency table lists each distinct value against the number of times it occurs. A grouped frequency table bundles continuous values (or discrete values with a wide range) into class intervals such as \(10 \le x < 15\).

2.2 Histograms

For continuous grouped data, a histogram displays frequency so that area is proportional to frequency. When class widths differ, plotting raw frequency distorts the picture; instead plot frequency density:

Frequency density. For a class of frequency \(f\) and width \(w\), \[ \text{frequency density} = \frac{f}{w}. \] The area of each bar (density \(\times\) width) equals the frequency of the class. Total area equals total frequency.

2.3 Cumulative frequency curves

Plot the upper class boundary (horizontal) against cumulative frequency (vertical); join with a smooth curve. From the curve one reads off the median (at \(N/2\)), quartiles (at \(N/4\) and \(3N/4\)) and any percentile.

2.4 Box-and-whisker plots

A compact summary of a distribution in five numbers.

Five-number summary. For a data set ordered from smallest to largest:
  • Minimum \(x_{\min}\);
  • Lower quartile \(Q_1\) (25th percentile);
  • Median \(Q_2\) (50th percentile);
  • Upper quartile \(Q_3\) (75th percentile);
  • Maximum \(x_{\max}\).
A box plot draws a box from \(Q_1\) to \(Q_3\) with a line at the median and "whiskers" extending to the extreme non-outlier values; outliers are shown as isolated points.

2.5 Scatter diagrams and bivariate data

Bivariate data records two variables \(x\) and \(y\) for each observation. A scatter diagram plots one point per observation; the overall pattern reveals correlation — the tendency for \(y\) to increase or decrease with \(x\).

3. Measures of Location

A measure of location (or measure of central tendency) summarises a distribution by a single typical value.

Mean, median, mode.
  • Mean \(\bar{x} = \dfrac{1}{n}\sum x_i\). Uses every value; sensitive to outliers.
  • Median: the middle value when data are ordered (for \(n\) odd) or the mean of the two middle values (for \(n\) even). Robust to outliers.
  • Mode: the most frequent value (for grouped data, the modal class). A distribution may have no mode, one mode, or several.

For grouped frequency data with class midpoints \(x_i\) and frequencies \(f_i\), the mean is estimated by

\( \bar{x} = \dfrac{\sum f_i x_i}{\sum f_i}. \)

The median of grouped data is found from a cumulative frequency curve or by linear interpolation within the median class. The modal class is simply the class of highest frequency density (use density, not raw frequency, when class widths differ).

4. Measures of Spread

A measure of location is only half the story: two samples can share the same mean yet have very different variability. Measures of spread quantify how stretched-out a distribution is.

Range, interquartile range, variance, standard deviation.
  • Range \(= x_{\max} - x_{\min}\). Very sensitive to outliers.
  • Interquartile range \(\text{IQR} = Q_3 - Q_1\). Contains the middle 50%; robust.
  • Variance \(\sigma^2 = \dfrac{1}{n}\sum (x_i - \mu)^2 = \dfrac{\sum x_i^2}{n} - \mu^2\). Average squared deviation.
  • Standard deviation \(\sigma = \sqrt{\sigma^2}\). Same units as the data.

The identity \(\sum(x_i - \mu)^2 = \sum x_i^2 - n\mu^2\) underpins the calculator-friendly formulae

\( \sigma^2 = \dfrac{\sum x_i^2}{n} - \bar{x}^2, \qquad \sigma^2_{\text{grouped}} = \dfrac{\sum f_i x_i^2}{\sum f_i} - \bar{x}^2. \)

Modern A-level students rarely compute \(\sigma\) by hand: enter the data in a calculator's statistics mode and read \(\sigma_n\) (population standard deviation) or \(\sigma_{n-1} = s\) (sample standard deviation, divides by \(n-1\)). OCR questions specify which is required.

5. Outliers

Outlier (1.5 × IQR rule). A data value is flagged as an outlier if it lies more than \(1.5\) IQRs beyond the quartiles: \[ x < Q_1 - 1.5\cdot\text{IQR} \quad \text{or} \quad x > Q_3 + 1.5\cdot\text{IQR}. \] An alternative convention (sometimes used with approximately Normal data) is "more than 2 standard deviations from the mean", but OCR's default is the IQR rule unless otherwise stated.

Worked Example 3 — Identifying outliers

A data set has \(Q_1 = 18\), \(Q_3 = 30\). Which of the values 5, 12, 40, 52 are outliers?

1.  IQR = 30 − 18 = 12.
2.  1.5·IQR = 18.
3.  Lower fence = Q1 − 18 = 18 − 18 = 0.
4.  Upper fence = Q3 + 18 = 30 + 18 = 48.
5.  Flag values outside [0, 48]:
6.      5   — inside [0, 48], not an outlier.
7.      12  — inside, not an outlier.
8.      40  — inside, not an outlier.
9.      52  — above 48, IS an outlier.
10. Conclusion: 52 is the only outlier.
Exam tip — outliers are flagged, not deleted. Identifying a point as an outlier does not authorise you to discard it. Outliers may be genuine extreme observations (record floods, stellar athletes). Remove only when there is external evidence of an error (miscounting, faulty instrument); otherwise report the value and comment.

6. Correlation and Regression

Given paired data \((x_i, y_i)\), how strongly are the variables related? The product-moment correlation coefficient (PMCC), denoted \(r\), measures the strength and direction of a linear relationship.

Product-moment correlation coefficient. For paired data \((x_1, y_1), \ldots, (x_n, y_n)\), \[ r = \dfrac{S_{xy}}{\sqrt{S_{xx}\,S_{yy}}}, \qquad \text{where } S_{xy} = \sum(x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y}), \] and \(S_{xx}, S_{yy}\) are defined analogously. The value \(r\) lies in \([-1, +1]\). \(r = +1\) indicates perfect positive linear relationship, \(r = -1\) perfect negative, \(r = 0\) no linear relationship.
Value of \(r\)Interpretation
\(|r| \gtrsim 0.8\)Strong linear correlation.
\(0.5 \lesssim |r| \lesssim 0.8\)Moderate linear correlation.
\(0.2 \lesssim |r| \lesssim 0.5\)Weak linear correlation.
\(|r| \lesssim 0.2\)Little or no linear correlation.
Exam tip — correlation is not causation. A high \(|r|\) shows an association, not that \(x\) causes \(y\) or \(y\) causes \(x\). There may be a hidden third (confounding) variable, or the relationship may be coincidental. Always state the direction and strength of correlation in words, and resist claims of causation.

When a linear relationship is present, the least-squares regression line of \(y\) on \(x\) is the straight line that minimises the sum of squared vertical residuals \(\sum (y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2\):

\( y = a + b x, \qquad b = \dfrac{S_{xy}}{S_{xx}}, \qquad a = \bar{y} - b\bar{x}. \)

The regression line is asymmetric in \(x\) and \(y\): the line of \(y\) on \(x\) is designed to predict \(y\) from \(x\), not vice versa. It is appropriate when (i) the scatter diagram suggests a linear relationship, (ii) \(x\) is the controlled or explanatory variable, and (iii) predictions are within the range of observed \(x\) (interpolation). Predictions outside that range (extrapolation) are unreliable.

7. Probability: Axioms and Basic Rules

A random experiment has an outcome that is not known in advance but is drawn from a known sample space \(S\). An event \(A\) is any subset of \(S\); its probability \(P(A)\) is a number satisfying Kolmogorov's axioms:

Probability axioms.
  • \(0 \le P(A) \le 1\) for every event \(A\).
  • \(P(S) = 1\) (something in the sample space is certain to happen).
  • For mutually exclusive events \(A, B\): \(P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B)\).
Consequences: \(P(\varnothing) = 0\); \(P(A') = 1 - P(A)\); for any \(A, B\), \(P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)\).
Independence and conditional probability. Events \(A\) and \(B\) are independent iff \(P(A \cap B) = P(A)\,P(B)\). The conditional probability of \(A\) given \(B\) (with \(P(B) > 0\)) is \[ P(A \mid B) = \dfrac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}. \] Independence is equivalent to \(P(A \mid B) = P(A)\): knowing \(B\) does not change the probability of \(A\).
RelationshipKey identity
Complement\(P(A') = 1 - P(A)\)
Union (general)\(P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)\)
Union (mutually exclusive)\(P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B)\)
Intersection (independent)\(P(A \cap B) = P(A)\,P(B)\)
Conditional\(P(A \mid B) = P(A \cap B)/P(B)\)
Multiplicative\(P(A \cap B) = P(B)\,P(A \mid B)\)

Tree diagrams organise sequential experiments (e.g. two draws without replacement) and Venn diagrams organise set-theoretic combinations of events. Both are part of the A-level toolkit and frequently appear in OCR papers.

8. Discrete Random Variables and the Binomial Distribution

A random variable is a numerical quantity whose value depends on the outcome of a random experiment. A discrete random variable \(X\) takes values in a countable set; its probability function \(P(X = x)\) gives the probability of each possible value, with \(\sum_x P(X = x) = 1\).

Expectation and variance of a discrete random variable. For \(X\) with values \(x_1, x_2, \ldots\) and probabilities \(p_i = P(X = x_i)\), \[ E(X) = \mu = \sum_i x_i\,p_i, \qquad \text{Var}(X) = \sigma^2 = E(X^2) - [E(X)]^2 = \sum_i x_i^2\,p_i - \mu^2. \] Expectation is a weighted average of possible values; variance is the expected squared deviation from the mean.

8.1 The binomial distribution

Many experiments consist of \(n\) independent repetitions of a Bernoulli trial — a single trial with just two outcomes, "success" (probability \(p\)) or "failure" (probability \(1 - p\)). The number of successes \(X\) in the \(n\) trials is then a binomial random variable.

Binomial distribution \(X \sim B(n, p)\). \(X\) is the number of successes in \(n\) independent Bernoulli trials, each with success probability \(p\). The probability mass function is \[ P(X = k) = \binom{n}{k} p^k (1-p)^{n-k}, \qquad k = 0, 1, 2, \ldots, n. \] Mean \(E(X) = np\); variance \(\text{Var}(X) = np(1-p)\). Valid when: fixed \(n\); each trial independent; constant \(p\); only two possible outcomes per trial.
Exam tip — check all four binomial conditions. OCR loves a question that looks binomial but isn't. Sampling without replacement from a small population violates the "constant \(p\)" condition (each draw changes the remaining probabilities); a variable number of trials violates "fixed \(n\)". Always state the conditions \(B(n,p)\) assumes and justify each.

9. The Normal Distribution

Continuous random variables take values in an interval; probabilities are given by areas under a probability density function (pdf). The most important continuous distribution — and an indispensable A-level tool — is the Normal.

Normal distribution \(X \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)\). A continuous random variable with the bell-shaped density \[ f(x) = \dfrac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}\exp\!\left(-\dfrac{(x - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right), \qquad x \in \mathbb{R}. \] It is symmetric about \(\mu\) (so mean = median = mode = \(\mu\)) and has variance \(\sigma^2\). Probabilities are computed by standardising to the standard Normal \(Z = (X - \mu)/\sigma \sim N(0, 1)\) and using tables or calculator functions.
\( Z = \dfrac{X - \mu}{\sigma} \sim N(0, 1). \)

The empirical rule (or 68-95-99.7 rule) gives quick benchmarks for a Normal distribution:

  • Approximately 68% of values lie within \(\pm 1\sigma\) of the mean.
  • Approximately 95% lie within \(\pm 2\sigma\).
  • Approximately 99.7% lie within \(\pm 3\sigma\).

The inverse Normal goes the other way: given a probability \(p\), find the value \(x\) such that \(P(X \le x) = p\). Calculators have a dedicated function; by hand one uses the standard-Normal tables backwards, then unstandardises with \(x = \mu + \sigma z\).

9.1 Normal approximation to the binomial

For large \(n\) with \(p\) not too close to 0 or 1 (rule of thumb: \(np > 5\) and \(n(1-p) > 5\)), the binomial is very nearly bell-shaped, and

\( B(n, p) \approx N\!\left(np,\, np(1-p)\right). \)

Because we are approximating a discrete variable (integer values) by a continuous one, apply a continuity correction: widen each integer \(k\) to the interval \([k - 0.5, k + 0.5]\). For example \(P(X \ge 12)\) becomes \(P(Y > 11.5)\) where \(Y\) is the approximating Normal.

10. Hypothesis Testing

Hypothesis testing is a formal procedure for using sample data to decide between two competing claims about a population parameter. The logical structure is the same no matter which distribution is involved.

Null and alternative hypotheses. The null hypothesis \(H_0\) states a specific value for the unknown parameter — typically the "status quo" or "no effect" position. The alternative hypothesis \(H_1\) states what we suspect is true instead: either a specific inequality (one-tailed: \(p > p_0\) or \(p < p_0\)) or merely difference (two-tailed: \(p \ne p_0\)). We test whether the data are consistent with \(H_0\).
Significance level, critical region, p-value. The significance level \(\alpha\) (commonly 5%, 1%, 10%) is the maximum tolerable probability of rejecting a true \(H_0\) (a Type I error). The critical region is the set of test-statistic values that would lead to rejection of \(H_0\). The p-value is the probability, assuming \(H_0\), of observing a test statistic at least as extreme as the one obtained. Reject \(H_0\) iff the p-value is less than \(\alpha\) (equivalently, the test statistic falls in the critical region).
Type I and Type II errors.
  • Type I error — rejecting \(H_0\) when it is true. Probability \(= \alpha\), the significance level.
  • Type II error — failing to reject \(H_0\) when it is false. Probability denoted \(\beta\); depends on the true parameter value and is generally unknown.
Lowering \(\alpha\) makes Type I errors rarer but raises \(\beta\) (other things equal).
StepAction
1State \(H_0\) and \(H_1\) in terms of a population parameter.
2Decide one-tailed or two-tailed; state significance level \(\alpha\).
3Identify the distribution of the test statistic under \(H_0\).
4Compute the test statistic (or the p-value) from the sample.
5Compare with the critical region (or compare p-value with \(\alpha\)).
6State the decision: reject or do not reject \(H_0\).
7Write a conclusion in context answering the original question.

10.1 Binomial hypothesis test

We test a claim about a population proportion \(p\). Under \(H_0: p = p_0\), the number of successes \(X\) in \(n\) independent trials is \(B(n, p_0)\). Compute the "tail" probability at least as extreme as the observation, and compare with \(\alpha\).

Worked Example 9 — Binomial test: is the coin fair?

A coin is tossed 20 times and lands heads 15 times. Test at the 5% level whether the coin is biased towards heads.

1.  Let p = P(head).
2.  H0: p = 0.5      (coin is fair).
3.  H1: p > 0.5      (biased towards heads — one-tailed).
4.  Significance level:  α = 0.05.
5.
6.  Under H0, X ~ B(20, 0.5).   Observed x = 15.
7.  Compute P(X ≥ 15) = 1 − P(X ≤ 14).
8.      From binomial tables:  P(X ≤ 14) = 0.9793.
9.      So P(X ≥ 15) = 1 − 0.9793 = 0.0207.
10.
11. Compare with α = 0.05:  0.0207 < 0.05.
12. ⇒  Reject H0.
13.
14. Conclusion (in context):  There is evidence at the 5% significance level that
15. the coin is biased in favour of heads.
Exam tip — always conclude in context. Exam mark schemes reserve a mark for the contextual conclusion. "Reject \(H_0\)" scores the statistical conclusion; you must also say what it means in the real problem: "the coin is biased towards heads", "the drug does increase the cure rate", etc. Use tentative language ("evidence that", "suggests that") — we never prove the alternative.

10.2 Normal test for a sample mean

Suppose \(X \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)\) with known \(\sigma\); we have a random sample of size \(n\) with mean \(\bar{x}\) and wish to test \(H_0: \mu = \mu_0\). Under \(H_0\) the sample mean is distributed as \(\bar{X} \sim N(\mu_0, \sigma^2/n)\), so the test statistic

\( z = \dfrac{\bar{x} - \mu_0}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}} \sim N(0, 1) \text{ under } H_0. \)

Compare \(z\) with critical values \(\pm 1.645\) (one-tailed 5%), \(\pm 1.96\) (two-tailed 5%), \(\pm 2.326\) (one-tailed 1%), etc.

Worked Example 10 — Normal test on a sample mean

Cans of drink are supposed to contain 330 ml with standard deviation 3 ml. A sample of 40 cans has mean 328.5 ml. Test at the 5% significance level whether the machine is under-filling.

1.  H0:  μ = 330    (machine fills correctly).
2.  H1:  μ < 330    (machine under-fills — one-tailed).
3.  α = 0.05,  σ = 3,  n = 40,  x̄ = 328.5.
4.
5.  Test statistic:
6.      z = (x̄ − μ0) / (σ/√n)
7.        = (328.5 − 330) / (3/√40)
8.        = −1.5 / 0.4743
9.        ≈ −3.162.
10.
11. Critical value (lower-tail, 5%):  z_crit = −1.645.
12. Since −3.162 < −1.645, test statistic is in the critical region.
13. ⇒  Reject H0.
14.
15. p-value:  P(Z < −3.162) ≈ 0.00078 < 0.05,  same decision.
16.
17. Conclusion: there is strong evidence at the 5% level that the machine is
18. under-filling the cans (mean < 330 ml).

For \(n = 10\) pairs of observations, the sample PMCC is \(r = 0.68\). Test at the 5% level whether there is positive correlation between the variables.

11. A Full Worked Example: Testing Whether a Die is Biased

Worked Example 12 — Binomial test, full write-up

A six-sided die is suspected of being biased in favour of showing a six. The die is rolled 50 times, and a six is observed on 14 rolls. Test at the 5% significance level whether the die is biased towards sixes.

1.  STEP 1 — Define the parameter.
2.     Let p = P(six on a single roll).
3.
4.  STEP 2 — State hypotheses.
5.     H0:  p = 1/6   (die fair with respect to sixes).
6.     H1:  p > 1/6   (biased towards sixes — one-tailed test).
7.
8.  STEP 3 — State significance level and distribution.
9.     α = 0.05.
10.    Under H0, X = number of sixes in 50 rolls  ~  B(50, 1/6).
11.    Observed x = 14.
12.
13. STEP 4 — Compute p-value.
14.    Mean under H0:  np = 50·(1/6) ≈ 8.333.
15.    P(X ≥ 14) = 1 − P(X ≤ 13).
16.    Using a calculator (or binomcdf):
17.        P(X ≤ 13) ≈ 0.9696.
18.        P(X ≥ 14) ≈ 1 − 0.9696 = 0.0304.
19.
20. STEP 5 — Compare with α.
21.    p-value 0.0304 < 0.05 = α.
22.    ⇒ Reject H0.
23.
24. STEP 6 — Conclusion in context.
25.    There is evidence at the 5% significance level that the die is biased
26.    in favour of sixes.  (Had α been 1%, we would NOT reject, since
27.    0.0304 > 0.01 — the strength of evidence is moderate.)
Exam tip — pick the correct tail carefully. "Biased towards sixes" means \(H_1: p > p_0\) (upper tail — compute \(P(X \ge \text{obs})\)). "Biased against sixes" means \(p < p_0\) (lower tail — \(P(X \le \text{obs})\)). "Biased" (no direction) is two-tailed — compute the smaller tail and double, and compare with \(\alpha\). Mis-reading the direction of \(H_1\) is the single most common marks-loser in binomial tests.

13. Chapter Summary and Examiner's Checklist

Chapter-wide checklist for Statistics.
  • Sampling: state method, one advantage, one disadvantage; distinguish bias (structural) from sampling error (random).
  • Data display: for unequal class widths, plot frequency density; box plots need the five-number summary.
  • Outliers: use the 1.5-IQR rule; flag rather than automatically delete.
  • Correlation: quote strength (strong/moderate/weak) and direction; remember "correlation \(\ne\) causation".
  • Regression: \(y\) on \(x\) predicts \(y\) from \(x\); do not extrapolate beyond the data range.
  • Probability: draw tree/Venn diagrams when useful; check independence against \(P(A\cap B) = P(A)P(B)\).
  • Binomial: verify all four conditions (fixed \(n\), two outcomes, independence, constant \(p\)).
  • Normal: always standardise to \(Z\); distinguish "less than" (lower tail) from "more than" (upper tail); state the inverse Normal use when finding quantiles.
  • Hypothesis tests: state \(H_0\), \(H_1\), \(\alpha\); compute test statistic or p-value; compare; reject or not; conclude in context.
  • One- vs two-tailed: decided by the wording of \(H_1\); for two-tailed at level \(\alpha\) each tail has area \(\alpha/2\).
  • Type I error: probability \(\alpha\) of rejecting a true \(H_0\); this is what we control directly.
Typical OCR examiner complaints (from mark reports).
  • Stating hypotheses without specifying the parameter — e.g. "\(H_0\): 0.5" without saying what 0.5 refers to.
  • Using \(>\) in \(H_1\) when "biased" requires a two-tailed \(\ne\).
  • Computing \(P(X = k)\) rather than the tail \(P(X \ge k)\) or \(P(X \le k)\) for a binomial test.
  • Forgetting the continuity correction when using the Normal approximation to the binomial.
  • Using \(\sigma\) where the sample version \(s\) (divisor \(n-1\)) is required, or vice versa.
  • Quoting a conclusion as "\(H_0\) is true" (never correct — we only fail to reject it) or as "proves" (we never prove an alternative).
  • Mixing up one-tailed and two-tailed critical values: e.g. using \(1.96\) for a one-tailed 5% test instead of \(1.645\).
  • Reading frequency (not frequency density) off a histogram with unequal class widths.
  • Omitting the contextual conclusion — "reject \(H_0\)" alone does not fully answer the question.
  • Extrapolating a regression line far outside the observed \(x\)-range and quoting the prediction with full confidence.
Formula snapshot (memorise).
  • Mean, variance (grouped): \(\bar x = \sum fx/\sum f\), \(\sigma^2 = \sum fx^2/\sum f - \bar x^2\).
  • Outlier: more than \(1.5\cdot\text{IQR}\) beyond the quartiles.
  • PMCC: \(r = S_{xy}/\sqrt{S_{xx}S_{yy}} \in [-1, 1]\).
  • Regression: \(b = S_{xy}/S_{xx}\), \(a = \bar y - b\bar x\).
  • Conditional: \(P(A\mid B) = P(A\cap B)/P(B)\).
  • Binomial: \(P(X=k) = \binom{n}{k}p^k(1-p)^{n-k}\), mean \(np\), variance \(np(1-p)\).
  • Standard Normal: \(Z = (X-\mu)/\sigma \sim N(0,1)\).
  • Sample mean: \(\bar X \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2/n)\); \(z = (\bar x - \mu_0)/(\sigma/\sqrt n)\).
  • Common critical z-values: one-tailed 5% \(= 1.645\); two-tailed 5% \(= 1.960\); one-tailed 1% \(= 2.326\).
