1.9 Numerical Methods

Mathematics — OCR A-Level (H240) — Paper 1: Pure Mathematics

Numerical methods are techniques for producing approximate answers when an exact closed-form solution is impossible, impractical, or unknown. Many equations and integrals that arise naturally in applied mathematics — from orbital mechanics, to finance, to machine learning — simply cannot be solved with the algebraic toolkit of A-level. For example, the transcendental equation \(x = \cos x\) has a real root, but no combination of elementary functions expresses it; the integral \(\int e^{-x^2}\, dx\) appears in statistics everywhere (normal distribution) but has no elementary antiderivative. In such cases we fall back on iterative procedures that generate sequences of better and better approximations, together with rigorous methods to bound the error. This chapter develops the three main root-finding techniques on the OCR H240 specification — change of sign, fixed-point iteration, and Newton-Raphson — and introduces numerical quadrature via the trapezium rule.

1. Why Numerical Methods?

Analytic algebra handles polynomial equations up to degree four and a small handful of other cases. Beyond that, most equations cannot be solved exactly in closed form. Classic examples:

  • Transcendental equations mix polynomial and transcendental functions: \(x = \cos x\), \(e^x = 3x\), \(\ln x = 1 - x\). No finite combination of radicals and elementary functions solves these.
  • Quintic and higher polynomials are, in general, not solvable by radicals (Abel-Ruffini theorem, 1824).
  • Integrals without elementary antiderivatives: \(\int e^{-x^2}\, dx\), \(\int \sin(x^2)\, dx\), \(\int \frac{\sin x}{x}\, dx\) — all well-defined but no elementary closed form.

When exact methods fail, we design an algorithm that generates a sequence of approximations \(x_0, x_1, x_2, \ldots\) converging to the true answer. Two kinds of error inevitably creep in:

Truncation error vs rounding error.
  • Truncation error (sometimes called method error) is the error built into the algorithm itself — e.g. stopping a convergent sequence after finitely many steps, or approximating a curve by straight lines. It depends on the method and on how many iterations / strips you use.
  • Rounding error is the error introduced by representing numbers with finitely many digits (e.g. a calculator keeping 10 significant figures). It accumulates with every arithmetic operation.
Both must be kept under control. A good numerical method reduces truncation error quickly enough that rounding error stays dominated by it.
Exam tip — state the degree of accuracy. Every numerical answer in an OCR exam should carry an explicit statement of accuracy: "to 3 decimal places", "to 3 significant figures", or "correct to 4 d.p.". Marks are routinely lost by quoting an answer to too few or too many figures, or by failing to say which. If the question specifies the accuracy, round at the very end — never mid-calculation — and keep at least one extra guard digit throughout the working.

2. Locating Roots — The Change of Sign Method

The simplest way to show that an equation \(f(x) = 0\) has a root in an interval is to use the Intermediate Value Theorem.

Root of an equation. A root of \(f(x) = 0\) is a value \(\alpha\) such that \(f(\alpha) = 0\). Graphically, it is a value of \(x\) where the curve \(y = f(x)\) crosses (or touches) the x-axis.
Change of sign (Intermediate Value Theorem). If \(f\) is continuous on the closed interval \([a, b]\) and \(f(a)\) and \(f(b)\) have opposite signs (that is, \(f(a) \cdot f(b) < 0\)), then there exists at least one root \(\alpha \in (a, b)\) with \(f(\alpha) = 0\).

The reasoning is geometric: a continuous curve cannot jump from a positive value at \(x = a\) to a negative value at \(x = b\) without passing through \(y = 0\) somewhere in between. The theorem is an existence statement only — it guarantees at least one root, not a unique one, and says nothing about its exact location.

Worked Example 1 — Locate a root of \(f(x) = x^3 - 2x - 5\)

Show that the equation \(x^3 - 2x - 5 = 0\) has a root between \(x = 2\) and \(x = 3\).

1.  f(x) = x³ − 2x − 5 is a polynomial, therefore continuous on ℝ.
2.  Evaluate at the endpoints:
3.     f(2) = 8  − 4 − 5 = −1     (negative).
4.     f(3) = 27 − 6 − 5 = 16     (positive).
5.  Since f(2) < 0 and f(3) > 0, there is a change of sign.
6.  By the Intermediate Value Theorem, there is at least one root
7.  α ∈ (2, 3) with f(α) = 0.                                   ∎

// This equation was used by Wallis and Newton in the 1600s to introduce
// iterative methods — the "root between 2 and 3" is α ≈ 2.0946.
Exam tip — always quote f(a) and f(b) and the word "continuous". A fully-marked change-of-sign argument has four parts: (i) state that \(f\) is continuous on \([a, b]\); (ii) compute \(f(a)\) with sign; (iii) compute \(f(b)\) with sign; (iv) conclude "since there is a change of sign, there is a root in \((a, b)\)". Skipping any of these — particularly the continuity clause — costs method marks.

Two important cautions apply to change-of-sign arguments:

  • Continuity is essential. The function \(f(x) = 1/x\) satisfies \(f(-1) = -1 < 0\) and \(f(1) = 1 > 0\), but has no root in \((-1, 1)\) — because it is discontinuous at \(x = 0\), where it diverges to \(\pm\infty\). The change of sign happens across a vertical asymptote, not through the axis.
  • Sign change detects only an odd number of roots. If \(f(a)\) and \(f(b)\) have the same sign, there could still be zero, two, four, or any even number of roots in \((a, b)\). A change of sign finds at least one, but not the total count.

Worked Example 2 — Missing a double root

1.  Consider f(x) = (x − 1)² on the interval [0, 2].
2.  f(0) = 1 > 0;  f(2) = 1 > 0.   No sign change.
3.  Yet f(1) = 0, so there IS a root at x = 1.
4.  Conclusion: change-of-sign is sufficient but not necessary for a root.
5.  Touching the axis (repeated root) is invisible to sign-change tests.

3. Interval Bisection

Once a sign change locates a root in \([a, b]\), we can narrow down its position by repeatedly halving the interval — the bisection method.

Interval bisection algorithm. Given a continuous \(f\) and an interval \([a, b]\) with \(f(a)\cdot f(b) < 0\):
  1. Compute the midpoint \(c = (a + b)/2\).
  2. Evaluate \(f(c)\). If \(f(c) = 0\), the root is \(c\); stop.
  3. If \(f(a)\cdot f(c) < 0\), the sign change is in \([a, c]\); set \(b \leftarrow c\).
  4. Otherwise the sign change is in \([c, b]\); set \(a \leftarrow c\).
  5. Repeat until the interval width \(b - a\) is below the required tolerance.

Because each pass halves the bracket width, after \(n\) iterations the remaining interval has width

\( \displaystyle w_n = \frac{b - a}{2^n}, \)

and the root \(\alpha\) lies within \(w_n/2\) of the current midpoint. This gives a guaranteed error bound — a rare luxury among numerical methods. Convergence is linear: each step wins one bit of accuracy, about 0.3 of a decimal digit per iteration. It is steady but slow.

Worked Example 3 — Three bisection steps on \(f(x) = x^3 - 2x - 5\) in [2, 3]

4. Fixed-Point Iteration

A second family of root-finding techniques works by rewriting \(f(x) = 0\) in the form \(x = g(x)\) — so that a root \(\alpha\) of \(f\) is a fixed point of \(g\), meaning \(g(\alpha) = \alpha\). We then iterate:

\( x_{n+1} = g(x_n), \qquad n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots \)

starting from some initial guess \(x_0\). If the sequence \(\{x_n\}\) converges, its limit must be a fixed point of \(g\) (and therefore a root of \(f\)).

Fixed-point iteration. To solve \(f(x) = 0\) by fixed-point iteration:
  1. Rearrange the equation into the form \(x = g(x)\). There are usually several ways to do so.
  2. Choose a starting value \(x_0\) close to the suspected root.
  3. Compute successive terms \(x_{n+1} = g(x_n)\) until the difference between consecutive values is below a chosen tolerance.
Convergence criterion. Let \(\alpha\) be a fixed point of \(g\) (so \(g(\alpha) = \alpha\)), and suppose \(g\) is differentiable near \(\alpha\). The iteration \(x_{n+1} = g(x_n)\) converges locally to \(\alpha\) if and only if \[ |g'(\alpha)| < 1. \] If \(|g'(\alpha)| > 1\), the iteration diverges away from \(\alpha\) no matter how good the starting guess (unless you happen to start exactly on \(\alpha\)). The case \(|g'(\alpha)| = 1\) is borderline.

Visually, the iteration corresponds to bouncing between the line \(y = x\) and the curve \(y = g(x)\):

  • Staircase diagram: when \(0 < g'(\alpha) < 1\), successive iterates approach \(\alpha\) monotonically from one side, tracing a staircase pattern.
  • Cobweb diagram: when \(-1 < g'(\alpha) < 0\), iterates alternate about \(\alpha\), producing a spiral (cobweb) pattern that closes in on the fixed point.
  • Divergence: if \(|g'(\alpha)| > 1\), the staircase or cobweb walks outward, running away from \(\alpha\).

5. Newton-Raphson Method

Newton-Raphson is a powerful, fast-converging root finder that uses the local tangent line to the curve \(y = f(x)\) at the current estimate.

Newton-Raphson iteration. Given a differentiable \(f\) and a starting estimate \(x_0\) near a root \(\alpha\), \[ x_{n+1} = x_n - \frac{f(x_n)}{f'(x_n)}. \] Geometrically, the next estimate \(x_{n+1}\) is the x-intercept of the tangent line to \(y = f(x)\) at the point \((x_n, f(x_n))\).

Derivation: the tangent at \(x_n\) has equation \(y - f(x_n) = f'(x_n)(x - x_n)\). Setting \(y = 0\) and solving for \(x\) gives the intercept \(x = x_n - f(x_n)/f'(x_n)\) — which we call \(x_{n+1}\).

Quadratic convergence. Near a simple root \(\alpha\) (where \(f'(\alpha) \neq 0\)), Newton-Raphson converges quadratically: the error \(e_n = x_n - \alpha\) satisfies \(|e_{n+1}| \approx K |e_n|^2\) for some constant \(K\). Practically, this means the number of correct decimal places roughly doubles with each iteration once you are close enough.

Worked Example 6 — Approximate \(\sqrt{7}\) by solving \(x^2 - 7 = 0\)

1.  f(x) = x² − 7,   f'(x) = 2x.
2.  Newton-Raphson:
3.     x_(n+1) = x_n − (x_n² − 7)/(2x_n)
4.             = (x_n + 7/x_n) / 2.             (the "Babylonian" formula)
5.  Start x₀ = 3 (since 3² = 9 is close to 7).
6.
7.  n   x_n              x_(n+1) = (x_n + 7/x_n)/2
8.  0   3.00000000       (3 + 7/3)/2       = (3 + 2.3333)/2    = 2.66667
9.  1   2.66666667       (2.6667 + 2.625)/2                     = 2.64583
10. 2   2.64583333       (2.6458 + 2.64567)/2                   = 2.64575
11. 3   2.64575131       (2.64575131 + 2.64575131)/2            = 2.64575131
12.
13. Converged to x ≈ 2.6457513 (which equals √7 to 7 d.p.).
14. Notice how the accuracy roughly doubles each step — quadratic convergence.

// This is the 4000-year-old "Babylonian method" for square roots —
// but it is exactly Newton-Raphson applied to x² − N = 0.

5.1 When Newton-Raphson fails

Despite its speed, Newton-Raphson is not foolproof. It can fail or misbehave in several situations:

  • Zero derivative (\(f'(x_n) = 0\)): the formula divides by zero. Geometrically, the tangent at \(x_n\) is horizontal, so it never meets the x-axis. Choose a different starting value.
  • Poor initial guess: far from \(\alpha\), the tangent line's x-intercept can be far worse than \(x_n\), and subsequent iterates can run away to infinity or hop to a different root.
  • Inflection points: if \(f''\) changes sign near the root, the iteration may oscillate.
  • Repeated roots: if \(\alpha\) is a double root (\(f'(\alpha) = 0\)), quadratic convergence is lost and the method becomes only linearly convergent.

Worked Example 7 — A starting value that fails

1.  Apply Newton-Raphson to f(x) = x³ − 2x + 2 with x₀ = 0.
2.  f'(x) = 3x² − 2.
3.  x₁ = 0 − f(0)/f'(0) = 0 − 2/(−2) = 1.
4.  f(1) = 1 − 2 + 2 = 1;   f'(1) = 3 − 2 = 1.
5.  x₂ = 1 − 1/1 = 0.
6.  x₃ = 0 − (2)/(−2) = 1.
7.  x₄ = 1 − 1 = 0.
8.  The iteration CYCLES between 0 and 1 — it never converges.
9.  The actual root is near α ≈ −1.7693, far from the cycle.

// Lesson: even a well-behaved cubic can trap Newton-Raphson in a 2-cycle.
// A sketch of y = f(x) BEFORE choosing x₀ is always a good idea.
Exam tip — Newton-Raphson can diverge if the starting value is poor. Questions often ask "explain why this iteration fails when \(x_0 = \ldots\)". The standard answers are: (i) \(f'(x_0) = 0\), so the tangent is horizontal and \(x_1\) is undefined; (ii) the tangent at \(x_0\) misses the neighbourhood of the root entirely; (iii) the starting value is on the "wrong" side of a turning point. Always sketch \(y = f(x)\) before applying the method.

6. The Trapezium Rule

Moving from roots to integrals: when an antiderivative is unavailable (or prohibitively hard), we can still estimate the definite integral \(\int_a^b f(x)\, dx\) by approximating the area under the curve with a collection of trapezia.

Trapezium rule. Divide \([a, b]\) into \(n\) strips of equal width \(h = (b - a)/n\). Label the endpoints \(x_0 = a, x_1 = a + h, \ldots, x_n = b\) and let \(y_i = f(x_i)\). Then \[ \int_a^b f(x)\, dx \;\approx\; \frac{h}{2}\Big[y_0 + 2(y_1 + y_2 + \cdots + y_{n-1}) + y_n\Big]. \] The end ordinates \(y_0\) and \(y_n\) are counted once; every interior ordinate is counted twice.

The formula comes from approximating each strip by a trapezium of parallel sides \(y_i\) and \(y_{i+1}\) and height \(h\). The area of one trapezium is \(\tfrac{h}{2}(y_i + y_{i+1})\); summing and collecting repeated terms gives the formula above.

Worked Example 8 — Approximate \(\int_0^1 \dfrac{1}{1+x^2}\, dx\) with 4 strips

The exact value is \([\arctan x]_0^1 = \pi/4 \approx 0.78540\).

1.  n = 4, a = 0, b = 1, so h = 1/4 = 0.25.
2.  Tabulate y_i = 1/(1 + x_i²):
3.     x₀ = 0.00  →  y₀ = 1/1       = 1.00000
4.     x₁ = 0.25  →  y₁ = 1/1.0625  = 0.94118
5.     x₂ = 0.50  →  y₂ = 1/1.25    = 0.80000
6.     x₃ = 0.75  →  y₃ = 1/1.5625  = 0.64000
7.     x₄ = 1.00  →  y₄ = 1/2       = 0.50000
8.
9.  Apply the formula:
10.    I ≈ (h/2) [y₀ + 2(y₁ + y₂ + y₃) + y₄]
11.      = (0.25/2) [1.00000 + 2(0.94118 + 0.80000 + 0.64000) + 0.50000]
12.      = 0.125   [1.00000 + 2(2.38118) + 0.50000]
13.      = 0.125   [1.00000 + 4.76236 + 0.50000]
14.      = 0.125 × 6.26236
15.      ≈ 0.78280.
16.
17. Compare with exact π/4 ≈ 0.78540  →  error ≈ 0.00260  (about 0.3 %).

// The integrand is concave on [0, 1], so the trapezia lie BELOW the curve,
// giving an under-estimate of the true area. This matches our 0.78280 < 0.78540.

6.1 Error behaviour of the trapezium rule

The error in the trapezium rule shrinks as the number of strips increases:

Trapezium rule — error. For a twice-differentiable integrand, the total error \(E_n\) in approximating \(\int_a^b f(x)\, dx\) with \(n\) equal strips satisfies \[ E_n = -\frac{(b - a)^3}{12 n^2}\, f''(\xi) \quad \text{for some } \xi \in (a, b). \] In particular \(E_n = O(h^2)\), so doubling the number of strips divides the error by roughly 4.

6.2 Over- vs under-estimate (concavity)

The trapezia used in the rule are straight-line chords between consecutive points on the curve. Whether they lie above or below the true curve depends on the concavity of \(f\) on the strip:

  • If \(f''(x) > 0\) (curve is convex / "cup up"), each chord lies above the curve — the trapezium rule over-estimates the integral.
  • If \(f''(x) < 0\) (curve is concave / "cap down"), each chord lies below the curve — the trapezium rule under-estimates.
  • If \(f\) is linear (\(f'' = 0\)) the rule is exact.
Exam tip — state the number of strips (or ordinates) and \(h\). Whenever you apply the trapezium rule, write down: (i) the number of strips \(n\) (beware — \(n\) strips means \(n+1\) ordinates); (ii) the strip width \(h = (b-a)/n\); (iii) a neat table of \(x_i\) and \(y_i\) values; (iv) the substitution into the formula. OCR examiners report that missed marks most often come from confusing "strips" with "ordinates", or from dropping an interior term.

7. Simpson's Rule (Brief Mention)

Beyond the trapezium rule lies Simpson's rule, which fits a parabola (quadratic) through every three consecutive ordinates rather than a straight line. The formula (for an even number \(n\) of strips) is

\( \displaystyle\int_a^b f(x)\, dx \approx \frac{h}{3}\Big[y_0 + 4(y_1 + y_3 + \cdots) + 2(y_2 + y_4 + \cdots) + y_n\Big]. \)

It is much more accurate than the trapezium rule (error is \(O(h^4)\) rather than \(O(h^2)\)), so doubling the strips divides the error by 16 instead of 4. Simpson's rule is not on the core OCR H240 specification — it sits on some other specifications (MEI, Edexcel Further) — but you may meet it informally. For standard OCR A-level pure, the trapezium rule is the only quadrature method you are expected to apply.

8. Error Analysis and Convergence

Every numerical algorithm generates a sequence of approximations; the key questions are how fast and how accurately.

Order of convergence. A sequence \(\{x_n\} \to \alpha\) is said to converge with order \(p\) if there is a constant \(K > 0\) with \[ |x_{n+1} - \alpha| \leq K\, |x_n - \alpha|^p \] for all sufficiently large \(n\).
  • \(p = 1\) — linear convergence. Each step multiplies the error by a fixed constant less than 1 (bisection, fixed-point iteration).
  • \(p = 2\) — quadratic convergence. The number of correct digits roughly doubles per step (Newton-Raphson at a simple root).

In practice, you cannot know \(\alpha\) exactly — so you cannot measure the error directly. A standard proxy is the gap between successive iterates: iterate until

\( |x_{n+1} - x_n| < \varepsilon \)

for a chosen tolerance \(\varepsilon\). For a rapidly convergent method this is a safe indicator of accuracy; for a slowly convergent one it may overstate progress. A more reliable check for a root-finding problem is to evaluate \(f(x_n)\) — if \(|f(x_n)|\) is small and \(|f'(x_n)|\) is not tiny, then \(x_n\) is close to a root.

Exam tip — include units and the degree of accuracy. When a numerical method produces an answer with physical meaning (velocity in m s\(^{-1}\), area in m\(^2\), etc.), always write the units on the final line. For pure-number answers, state the accuracy: "to 3 decimal places" or "correct to 4 significant figures". Keep one extra guard digit in the working, then round only at the very end.

Change of sign\(f(a) \cdot f(b) < 0 \Rightarrow \exists\, \alpha \in (a, b)\)\(f\) continuous; existence only.

12. Chapter Summary and Examiner's Checklist

Chapter-wide checklist for Numerical Methods.
  • Change of sign: state continuity, quote \(f(a)\) and \(f(b)\) with signs, conclude root in \((a, b)\).
  • Bisection: tabulate \(a_n, b_n, c_n, f(c_n)\) and the retained half at every step.
  • Fixed-point iteration: before iterating, check \(|g'(\alpha)| < 1\). After iterating, stop when \(|x_{n+1} - x_n|\) is below tolerance.
  • Newton-Raphson: write down \(f'(x)\) and the iteration formula explicitly; beware \(f'(x_n) = 0\) and poor starting values.
  • Trapezium rule: state \(n\) strips and \(h\); build a clean table of \(x_i, y_i\); substitute carefully, with interior terms doubled.
  • Always quote the degree of accuracy — "correct to 3 d.p." etc. — at the end.
  • Concavity check: for the trapezium rule, decide over/under-estimate from the sign of \(f''\).
  • Sketch the function before choosing a starting value for any iterative method.
  • Verify the final root by substituting back and checking \(f(\alpha) \approx 0\).
Typical OCR examiner complaints (from mark reports).
  • Omitting the statement "\(f\) is continuous" when applying the change-of-sign test.
  • Stating that a sign change in \([a, b]\) means exactly one root, rather than at least one.
  • In fixed-point iteration, failing to verify \(|g'(\alpha)| < 1\), or giving only the final answer with no iteration table.
  • Claiming a Newton-Raphson iteration "diverges because the function is not differentiable", when the real reason is \(f'(x_0) = 0\) or a poor starting value.
  • Confusing "number of strips" with "number of ordinates" in the trapezium rule — \(n\) strips produces \(n+1\) ordinates.
  • Doubling the end ordinates \(y_0\) and \(y_n\) (should be counted once) or failing to double the interior ordinates (should be counted twice).
  • Rounding intermediate results to 3 d.p. and then quoting the final answer to 5 d.p. — rounding error propagates.
  • Giving no explicit statement of accuracy ("to 3 d.p.") on the final line.
Formula snapshot (memorise).
  • Change of sign: \(f\) continuous on \([a,b]\), \(f(a)f(b) < 0 \Rightarrow\) at least one root in \((a,b)\).
  • Bisection width: \(w_n = (b-a)/2^n\); midpoint error \(\le w_n / 2\).
  • Fixed-point iteration: \(x_{n+1} = g(x_n)\); converges iff \(|g'(\alpha)| < 1\).
  • Newton-Raphson: \(x_{n+1} = x_n - f(x_n)/f'(x_n)\); quadratic near a simple root.
  • Trapezium rule: \(\int_a^b f\, dx \approx \tfrac{h}{2}[y_0 + 2(y_1 + \cdots + y_{n-1}) + y_n]\), \(h = (b-a)/n\).
  • Trapezium error: \(O(h^2)\); doubling \(n\) divides error by 4.
  • Concavity: \(f'' > 0 \Rightarrow\) over-estimate; \(f'' < 0 \Rightarrow\) under-estimate.
