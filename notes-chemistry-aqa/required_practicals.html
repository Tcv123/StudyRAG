⚗ RP1: Make Up a Volumetric Solution and Carry Out a Simple Acid-Base Titration

Apparatus

  • 250 cm³ volumetric flask with stopper
  • 25.0 cm³ pipette and pipette filler
  • 50 cm³ burette and burette clamp
  • Conical flask (250 cm³)
  • White tile
  • Indicator (e.g., methyl orange or phenolphthalein)
  • Balance (to 2 d.p.), wash bottle, beakers, stirring rod

Method

  1. Weigh the required mass of solid accurately using a balance (record to 2 d.p.).
  2. Dissolve the solid in a small volume of distilled water in a beaker, stirring to ensure complete dissolution.
  3. Transfer the solution to a 250 cm³ volumetric flask using a funnel. Rinse the beaker and funnel several times, adding all washings to the flask.
  4. Add distilled water up to the 250 cm³ graduation mark. Use a pipette for the final drops so the bottom of the meniscus sits on the line. Stopper and invert several times to mix.
  5. Rinse the pipette with the alkali solution, then pipette exactly 25.0 cm³ of alkali into a conical flask.
  6. Add 2-3 drops of indicator to the conical flask.
  7. Rinse the burette with acid solution, then fill it and record the initial reading (to nearest 0.05 cm³).
  8. Perform a rough titration: add acid from the burette, swirling continuously, until the indicator changes colour permanently. Record the final burette reading.
  9. Repeat titrations until at least two concordant results (within 0.10 cm³) are obtained.
  10. Calculate the mean titre from concordant results only.

Safety

  • Acids and alkalis are corrosive/irritant — wear safety goggles and gloves.
  • Use a pipette filler (never mouth-pipette).
  • Wipe up any spills immediately with water.

Analysis

Plot/Calculate: Use the mean titre volume with the balanced equation and known concentration to calculate the unknown concentration. n = c × V (where V is in dm³).

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Misreading the burette scaleRandomRead at eye level; read bottom of meniscus
Overshooting the end pointSystematicAdd acid drop-wise near end point; use half-drops
Incomplete transfer to volumetric flaskSystematicRinse beaker and funnel multiple times with distilled water
Air bubbles in burette tipSystematicRun acid through tap to displace air before starting
Using wrong indicatorSystematicChoose indicator with end point in the pH range of equivalence
Exam Tip: Always quote burette readings to 2 decimal places (e.g., 23.45 cm³) and include units. Concordant means within 0.10 cm³. Only average concordant titres — exclude the rough.

⚗ RP2: Measurement of an Enthalpy Change

Apparatus

  • Polystyrene cup (acts as calorimeter) with lid
  • Thermometer (±0.1 °C) or temperature probe
  • Measuring cylinder (25 cm³ or 50 cm³)
  • Balance (to 2 d.p.)
  • Stirring rod
  • Timer / stopwatch

Method

  1. Measure a known volume (e.g., 25 cm³) of one solution using a measuring cylinder and place in the polystyrene cup.
  2. Record the initial temperature of the solution every minute for 3 minutes (to establish a baseline).
  3. At the 4th minute, add the second reagent (either another solution of known volume, or a weighed mass of solid).
  4. Stir the mixture and continue recording temperature every minute for at least 6 more minutes.
  5. Plot temperature against time. Extrapolate both the pre-mixing and post-mixing lines back to the time of mixing to find the true temperature change (ΔT).
  6. Calculate: q = mcΔT (where m = total mass of solution, c = 4.18 J g⁻¹ K⁻¹ for dilute aqueous solutions).
  7. Convert to ΔH = -q / n (per mole of limiting reagent).

Safety

  • Wear safety goggles — acids and alkalis are corrosive.
  • Take care with exothermic reactions — cup may become hot.
  • Handle concentrated solutions with care.

Analysis

Plot: Temperature (y-axis) vs. Time (x-axis). Extrapolate lines to find ΔT at the time of mixing.

Calculate: q = mcΔT, then ΔH = -q/n in kJ mol⁻¹.

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Heat loss to surroundingsSystematicUse lid, insulation; extrapolation technique on graph
Assuming c = 4.18 J g⁻¹ K⁻¹ and density = 1 g cm⁻³SystematicAcknowledge this assumption; gives approximate result
Thermometer reading lagRandomUse a temperature probe with data logger
Incomplete reactionSystematicUse excess of one reagent; stir thoroughly
Heat absorbed by calorimeterSystematicDetermine calorimeter constant or use better insulation
Exam Tip: The graphical extrapolation method corrects for heat loss. Always draw lines of best fit for both the cooling and baseline sections, and extrapolate back to the time of mixing to find the 'true' ΔT.

⚗ RP3: Investigation of How Rate of Reaction Changes with Temperature

Apparatus

  • Conical flask (100 cm³)
  • Measuring cylinders (10 cm³, 25 cm³)
  • Sodium thiosulfate solution (Na₂S₂O₃, 0.1 mol dm⁻³)
  • Dilute hydrochloric acid (1 mol dm⁻³)
  • Thermometer (±1 °C)
  • Water bath (beakers with water at various temperatures)
  • Paper with a black cross (X) drawn on it
  • Stopwatch

Method

  1. Measure 25 cm³ of sodium thiosulfate solution into a conical flask.
  2. Place the flask in a water bath at the desired temperature (e.g., 20 °C). Allow to equilibrate.
  3. Record the exact temperature of the thiosulfate solution.
  4. Place the conical flask on the paper with the X mark.
  5. Add 5 cm³ of dilute HCl to the flask and immediately start the stopwatch.
  6. Look down through the flask from above. Stop the timer when the cross is no longer visible due to sulfur precipitate forming.
  7. Record the time taken for the cross to disappear.
  8. Repeat at five different temperatures (e.g., 20, 30, 40, 50, 60 °C).
  9. Calculate rate = 1/t for each temperature.

Safety

  • Sulfur dioxide (SO₂) is produced — work in a well-ventilated area or fume cupboard.
  • Dilute HCl is an irritant — wear goggles.
  • Hot water baths — take care to avoid scalding at higher temperatures.

Analysis

Plot: 1/time (y-axis) vs. Temperature in °C (x-axis) — gives an exponential-shaped curve showing rate increases with temperature.

Alternative: Plot ln(1/t) vs. 1/T (in K) for an Arrhenius plot — gradient = -Eₐ/R.

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Judging when the cross disappears (subjective)RandomSame person judges every time; use a light sensor/colorimeter
Temperature changes during reactionSystematicUse a water bath to maintain constant temperature
Reaction time in starting/stopping stopwatchRandomUse data logging; repeat and average
Inaccurate volumesRandomUse a pipette instead of measuring cylinder for more accuracy
Exam Tip: Rate = 1/t is a common proxy for initial rate. A 10 °C rise approximately doubles the rate. For full marks, always state units: rate ∝ 1/t (s⁻¹).

⚗ RP4: Simple Test-Tube Reactions to Identify Cations and Anions

Apparatus

  • Test tubes and test tube rack
  • Nichrome wire loop and Bunsen burner (for flame tests)
  • Dilute NaOH solution, dilute NH₃ solution
  • Dilute HCl, dilute H₂SO₄, dilute HNO₃
  • Silver nitrate solution (AgNO₃), barium chloride solution (BaCl₂)
  • Limewater (Ca(OH)₂ solution)
  • Safety goggles, gloves

Method

  1. Flame tests: Dip a clean nichrome wire in concentrated HCl. Hold in a roaring Bunsen flame until no colour is seen. Dip in the test solution and hold in the flame. Record the colour: Li⁺ = red, Na⁺ = yellow, K⁺ = lilac, Ca²⁺ = orange-red, Ba²⁺ = green.
  2. Cation tests with NaOH: Add a few drops of dilute NaOH to the test solution. Record the precipitate colour. Add excess NaOH — note if precipitate dissolves (Al(OH)₃ is amphoteric: dissolves in excess).
  3. Cation tests with NH₃: Repeat using dilute ammonia instead. Cu(OH)₂ dissolves in excess NH₃ to give a deep blue solution (tetraammine complex).
  4. Carbonate test: Add dilute HCl to the solid/solution. If CO₃²⁻ is present, effervescence occurs. Pass the gas through limewater — turns milky if CO₂.
  5. Sulfate test: Acidify with dilute HNO₃ (to remove carbonates), then add BaCl₂ solution. A white precipitate of BaSO₄ confirms SO₄²⁻.
  6. Halide test: Acidify with dilute HNO₃, then add AgNO₃ solution. Cl⁻ = white precipitate (AgCl), Br⁻ = cream (AgBr), I⁻ = yellow (AgI).

Safety

  • Silver nitrate stains skin — wear gloves.
  • Barium chloride is toxic — handle with care, wash hands.
  • Concentrated HCl is corrosive — use in fume cupboard.
  • Wear safety goggles throughout.

Analysis

Record: Tabulate observations for each test — colour of precipitate, solubility in excess, flame colour, gas tests. Match to known results to identify unknown ions.

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Contaminated nichrome wireSystematicClean wire thoroughly in HCl and flame until no colour
Sodium contamination giving yellow flameSystematicView through blue cobalt glass to mask sodium yellow
Not acidifying before adding BaCl₂ or AgNO₃SystematicAlways acidify first to remove interfering ions
Colour judgment (e.g., cream vs white precipitate)RandomCompare directly with known standards side by side
Exam Tip: Know the difference between NaOH and NH₃ tests — especially that Al(OH)₃ dissolves in excess NaOH but NOT in excess NH₃, while Cu(OH)₂ dissolves in excess NH₃ but NOT in excess NaOH. This is a classic distinguishing question.

⚗ RP5: Distillation of a Product from a Reaction

Apparatus

  • Round-bottomed flask
  • Still head with thermometer adapter
  • Thermometer (to monitor distillate boiling point)
  • Liebig condenser with rubber tubing for water in/out
  • Collecting flask / conical flask
  • Anti-bumping granules
  • Heat source (Bunsen burner, electric heater, or heating mantle)
  • Clamp stand, boss, clamp

Method

  1. Set up the distillation apparatus as shown: round-bottomed flask connected to still head, then to condenser angled downward into collecting flask.
  2. Add anti-bumping granules to the flask to prevent bumping.
  3. Pour the reaction mixture into the round-bottomed flask (no more than half full).
  4. Connect the condenser: cold water enters at the bottom and exits at the top (counter-current flow for maximum cooling).
  5. Turn on the water supply before heating.
  6. Heat gently and increase heat until the liquid begins to boil. Record the temperature when distillate begins to collect.
  7. Collect the fraction that distils at the expected boiling point of the desired product (±2 °C).
  8. Discard the first few drops (forerun) and any fraction outside the target range.

Safety

  • Never heat a sealed system — ensure the apparatus is open to the atmosphere at the collecting end.
  • Flammable liquids — keep away from naked flames; use an electric heater if possible.
  • Anti-bumping granules prevent superheating and sudden boiling.
  • Ensure all glassware joints are secure but not forced.

Analysis

Record: Temperature range at which the product distils. A pure product has a sharp boiling point. Calculate percentage yield from moles of product collected vs. theoretical maximum.

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Product left in flask / condenserSystematicRinse apparatus with small amounts of solvent; lower yield expected
Collecting impure fractions (wrong temperature range)SystematicMonitor thermometer carefully; collect only at correct boiling point
Overheating causing decompositionSystematicHeat gently and gradually; use a water bath for low-boiling-point liquids
Incomplete reaction before distillationSystematicEnsure reaction has gone to completion (reflux first if needed)
Exam Tip: Water in the condenser flows counter-current (enters at the bottom, exits at the top). Anti-bumping granules must be added before heating, never to a hot liquid. Always state that the thermometer bulb should be level with the side-arm outlet.

⚗ RP6: Tests for Alcohol, Aldehyde, Alkene, and Carboxylic Acid

Apparatus

  • Test tubes and test tube rack
  • Water bath (warm, ~60 °C)
  • Tollens' reagent (silver nitrate + ammonia solution — prepare fresh)
  • Fehling's solution A and B (mix equal volumes before use)
  • Bromine water (Br₂(aq))
  • Sodium carbonate solution (Na₂CO₃)
  • Acidified potassium dichromate (K₂Cr₂O₇/H₂SO₄)

Method

  1. Aldehyde test (Tollens'): Add the unknown organic compound to Tollens' reagent in a clean test tube. Warm gently in a water bath. A silver mirror on the tube walls indicates an aldehyde.
  2. Aldehyde test (Fehling's): Add the unknown to a mixture of Fehling's A and B. Heat in a water bath. A brick-red precipitate (Cu₂O) confirms an aldehyde.
  3. Alkene test (bromine water): Add bromine water to the unknown and shake. If the orange colour is decolourised, a C=C double bond (alkene) is present.
  4. Carboxylic acid test (Na₂CO₃): Add the unknown to sodium carbonate solution. Effervescence (CO₂ gas) indicates a carboxylic acid. Confirm by passing gas through limewater.
  5. Alcohol test (K₂Cr₂O₇): Add acidified potassium dichromate to the unknown and warm. Orange to green colour change confirms a primary or secondary alcohol. (Tertiary alcohols show no change.)

Safety

  • Tollens' reagent can form explosive silver fulminate on standing — prepare fresh, use immediately, and wash away promptly.
  • Bromine water is toxic and corrosive — use in fume cupboard, wear gloves.
  • Potassium dichromate is a known carcinogen — handle with gloves, avoid skin contact.
  • Organic unknowns may be flammable — keep away from naked flames.

Analysis

Record: Tabulate the results of each test. Use the combination of positive and negative results to identify the functional group present in the unknown compound.

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Contaminated test tubesSystematicThoroughly clean all glassware; Tollens' requires very clean tubes
Using old Tollens' reagentSystematicAlways prepare fresh immediately before use
Not warming sufficientlySystematicUse a water bath at 60-70 °C; allow adequate time
Bromine water not decolourising fully (excess alkene)SystematicUse excess bromine water relative to sample
Exam Tip: Tollens' and Fehling's both test for aldehydes but NOT ketones. This is because aldehydes can be oxidised to carboxylic acids, but ketones cannot. Always state the specific observation (silver mirror, brick-red precipitate) for full marks.

⚗ RP7: Measuring the Rate of Reaction by a Continuous Monitoring Method

Apparatus

  • Conical flask with delivery tube and bung
  • Gas syringe (100 cm³) OR top-pan balance (to 0.01 g)
  • Cotton wool plug (for mass loss method)
  • Stopwatch / timer
  • Measuring cylinders for reagent volumes
  • Reagents: e.g., CaCO₃ (marble chips) + dilute HCl, or Mg ribbon + dilute HCl

Method

  1. Gas syringe method: Set up the conical flask connected to a gas syringe via a delivery tube.
  2. Measure the required volume of acid into the flask.
  3. Add the solid reactant (e.g., marble chips), quickly attach the bung, and start the stopwatch.
  4. Record the volume of gas collected at regular intervals (e.g., every 30 seconds) until the reaction is complete (no further gas produced).
  5. Mass loss method (alternative): Place the flask (with cotton wool plug) on a balance. Add the acid and solid. Record the mass at regular intervals as CO₂ gas escapes.
  6. Repeat with a control variable changed (e.g., concentration, surface area, temperature) to investigate its effect on rate.

Safety

  • Dilute HCl is an irritant — wear goggles.
  • Gas syringe can pop out if gas is produced too rapidly — secure it with a clamp.
  • If using hydrogen gas, keep away from naked flames.

Analysis

Plot: Volume of gas (or mass loss) on y-axis vs. Time on x-axis.

Calculate: Rate at any point = gradient of the tangent to the curve. Initial rate = gradient at t = 0. The curve levels off when one reactant is used up.

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Gas escaping before bung is fittedSystematicFit bung quickly; use an inverted flask method where solid is dropped in
Gas syringe friction (sticking)SystematicLubricate syringe; use a new one; tap gently
Gas dissolving in solution (e.g., CO₂)SystematicAcknowledged limitation; gas syringe collects what escapes
Uneven marble chip sizesRandomUse same total mass; crush to similar size; weigh accurately
Temperature variation over timeSystematicUse a thermostatted water bath
Exam Tip: On a volume-time graph, the steeper the initial gradient, the faster the initial rate. All curves reach the same final volume if excess acid is used (same moles of solid). If excess solid is used, more concentrated acid gives a higher final volume. Be clear about which is in excess.

⚗ RP8: Measuring the Rate of Reaction by an Initial Rate Method (Iodine Clock)

Apparatus

  • Conical flasks or beakers
  • Measuring cylinders (multiple, for different solutions)
  • Stopwatch
  • Solutions: potassium iodate, sodium bisulfite (or hydrogen peroxide + potassium iodide), starch indicator
  • Distilled water (to maintain constant total volume)
  • White tile

Method

  1. Prepare a series of mixtures with varying concentration of one reactant (e.g., KIO₃). Keep total volume constant by adjusting distilled water volume.
  2. Add starch indicator to each flask.
  3. Add the second reagent (e.g., NaHSO₃) to the first flask and start the stopwatch immediately.
  4. Swirl to mix. Record the time taken for the solution to turn dark blue/black (the iodine clock end point).
  5. Repeat for each concentration.
  6. Calculate the initial rate for each experiment: rate = 1/t.
  7. Plot rate (1/t) against concentration of the varied reactant.

Safety

  • Solutions are low hazard at the concentrations used, but wear goggles.
  • Wash hands after handling all chemicals.
  • Iodine can stain — handle carefully.

Analysis

Plot: 1/t (y-axis) vs. concentration (x-axis). A straight line through the origin indicates first order with respect to that reactant. A curve (quadratic shape) suggests second order.

Calculate: Rate = 1/t. Determine the order of reaction with respect to the varied reactant from the shape of the graph.

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Subjective judgement of colour change end pointRandomSame person judges; use a colorimeter for objective detection
Temperature variation between runsSystematicUse a thermostatted water bath; record temperature each time
Inaccurate volumes (measuring cylinder uncertainty)RandomUse burettes or pipettes for more precise volumes
Delay in starting timer after mixingRandomOne person mixes, another starts timer; or use data logging
Exam Tip: The iodine clock measures the time for a fixed small amount of product (I₂) to form. This is approximately equal to the initial rate because only a tiny fraction of reaction has occurred. Always keep total volume constant when varying concentrations — add water to compensate.

⚗ RP9: Investigating How pH Changes When a Weak Acid Reacts with a Strong Base

Apparatus

  • pH meter (calibrated) or pH probe with data logger
  • Burette (50 cm³) filled with strong base (e.g., 0.10 mol dm⁻³ NaOH)
  • Beaker (250 cm³) with known volume of weak acid (e.g., 25.0 cm³ of 0.10 mol dm⁻³ CH₃COOH)
  • Magnetic stirrer and stirrer bar
  • Clamp stand, boss, clamp
  • Wash bottle with distilled water

Method

  1. Calibrate the pH meter using buffer solutions (pH 4, 7, 10).
  2. Pipette 25.0 cm³ of weak acid into the beaker. Place on magnetic stirrer.
  3. Immerse the pH probe in the acid solution. Record the initial pH.
  4. Fill the burette with NaOH solution. Record the initial burette reading.
  5. Add NaOH in small increments (1 cm³ at first, then 0.5 cm³ or even 0.1 cm³ near the equivalence point).
  6. After each addition, stir, wait for a stable reading, then record the volume added and pH.
  7. Continue well past the equivalence point until pH levels off at a high value.
  8. Plot pH (y-axis) against volume of NaOH added (x-axis).

Safety

  • NaOH is corrosive — wear goggles and gloves.
  • Weak acids are irritants — avoid skin contact.
  • pH probes are fragile — handle with care, store in correct solution.

Analysis

Plot: pH (y-axis) vs. volume of NaOH (x-axis) to produce a titration curve.

Key features: Initial pH > 1 (weak acid), buffer region before equivalence, half-equivalence point where pH = pKₐ, steep rise at equivalence, equivalence point pH > 7 (salt of weak acid + strong base is basic).

Calculate: pKₐ = pH at half-equivalence point. Kₐ = 10⁻ᵖᴷᵃ.

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
pH meter not calibratedSystematicCalibrate with at least two buffer solutions before use
Adding too much NaOH near equivalence pointSystematicUse smaller increments (0.1 cm³) around the steep region
Not allowing pH to stabilise before readingRandomWait 10-15 seconds after each addition; use stirrer
CO₂ absorption from airSystematicUse freshly prepared NaOH; keep beaker covered
Exam Tip: A weak acid/strong base titration curve has equivalence at pH > 7. The buffer region (flat part) shows where pH resists change. At the half-equivalence point, [HA] = [A⁻], so pH = pKₐ. Use phenolphthalein (not methyl orange) as the indicator for this combination.

⚗ RP10: Construct Electrochemical Cells and Measure Their EMF

Apparatus

  • Two beakers (250 cm³)
  • Metal electrodes (e.g., zinc strip and copper strip)
  • Solutions of the metal salts (e.g., 1.0 mol dm⁻³ ZnSO₄ and CuSO₄)
  • Salt bridge (filter paper soaked in KNO₃ solution, or agar-KNO₃ gel in a U-tube)
  • High-resistance voltmeter (or multimeter set to DC voltage)
  • Connecting wires with crocodile clips
  • Emery paper (to clean electrode surfaces)

Method

  1. Clean both metal electrodes with emery paper to remove oxide layer.
  2. Pour 1.0 mol dm⁻³ ZnSO₄ solution into one beaker and 1.0 mol dm⁻³ CuSO₄ solution into the other.
  3. Place the zinc electrode in the ZnSO₄ solution and the copper electrode in the CuSO₄ solution.
  4. Connect the salt bridge between the two solutions (dipping into both beakers).
  5. Connect the electrodes to the voltmeter using wires and crocodile clips.
  6. Record the EMF (voltage) reading. The Zn/Cu cell should give approximately +1.10 V.
  7. Repeat with different metal combinations (e.g., Zn/Fe, Fe/Cu, Mg/Cu) and record EMF for each.
  8. Compare measured values with theoretical values from electrode potential data.

Safety

  • CuSO₄ is harmful — avoid skin contact, wear gloves.
  • Wear safety goggles.
  • Dispose of metal salt solutions according to local regulations.

Analysis

Calculate: E°(cell) = E°(cathode) - E°(anode). Compare measured EMF with calculated values.

Plot: Measured EMF vs. predicted EMF for multiple cells — should give a straight line of gradient 1.

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Oxide layer on electrode surfaceSystematicClean with emery paper immediately before use
Non-standard concentrationsSystematicUse exactly 1.0 mol dm⁻³ solutions; use volumetric flask
Salt bridge drying out or poor contactSystematicEnsure good immersion; use fresh salt bridge
Voltmeter drawing current (lowering reading)SystematicUse a high-resistance voltmeter or potentiometer
Temperature not at 298 KSystematicRecord temperature; acknowledge deviation from standard
Exam Tip: The more reactive metal (more negative E°) is the anode (oxidation). The less reactive metal is the cathode (reduction). Cell EMF = E°(right) - E°(left) when written in conventional notation. A high-resistance voltmeter is essential to prevent current flow, which would alter the EMF.

⚗ RP11: Measure the EMF of an Electrochemical Cell (Standard Electrode Potentials)

Apparatus

  • Standard hydrogen electrode (SHE): platinum electrode, H₂ gas supply at 1 atm, 1.0 mol dm⁻³ HCl
  • Half-cell under test: metal electrode in 1.0 mol dm⁻³ metal ion solution
  • Salt bridge (KNO₃)
  • High-resistance voltmeter
  • Connecting wires and crocodile clips
  • Thermometer (to confirm 298 K / 25 °C)

Method

  1. Set up the standard hydrogen electrode: bubble H₂ gas at 1 atm pressure over a platinised platinum electrode in 1.0 mol dm⁻³ HCl at 298 K.
  2. Set up the test half-cell: place the metal electrode in 1.0 mol dm⁻³ solution of its ions.
  3. Connect the two half-cells with a salt bridge.
  4. Connect both electrodes to a high-resistance voltmeter.
  5. Record the EMF reading and its sign. This gives E° for the test half-cell (since E° of SHE = 0.00 V by definition).
  6. Repeat for different metals/half-cells to build an electrochemical series.
  7. Note: In practice, a secondary reference electrode (e.g., silver/silver chloride) is often used instead of the SHE. Results can be converted.

Safety

  • Hydrogen gas is flammable and explosive — no naked flames nearby; use in well-ventilated area.
  • HCl is corrosive — wear goggles and gloves.
  • Metal salt solutions may be toxic/irritant — handle with care.

Analysis

Record: E° value and sign for each half-cell relative to SHE.

Calculate: E°(cell) = E°(cathode) - E°(anode) for any pair of half-cells.

Compare: Measured values with literature data book values.

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
H₂ pressure not exactly 1 atmSystematicUse a pressure regulator; record actual pressure
Concentration not exactly 1.0 mol dm⁻³SystematicPrepare solutions carefully using volumetric flask
Temperature deviation from 298 KSystematicUse a thermostatted water bath; record temperature
Platinum electrode not properly platinisedSystematicUse a freshly platinised electrode
Junction potential at salt bridgeSystematicUse KNO₃ (ions have similar mobilities); renew salt bridge
Exam Tip: Standard conditions are 298 K, 1 atm (for gases), 1.0 mol dm⁻³ (for solutions). The SHE is assigned E° = 0.00 V by convention. For non-metal half-cells (e.g., Cl₂/Cl⁻), use a platinum electrode. The sign of E° tells you whether the half-cell is more or less easily reduced than H⁺/H₂.

⚗ RP12: Investigate Changing Conditions on a Reversible Reaction at Equilibrium

Apparatus

  • Test tubes and test tube rack
  • Cobalt(II) chloride solution in water/HCl mixture
  • Concentrated HCl (source of Cl⁻ ions)
  • Distilled water
  • Water baths: ice bath (~0 °C) and hot water bath (~80 °C)
  • Beakers for water baths
  • Teat pipettes

Method

  1. Prepare a solution of cobalt(II) chloride that is purple/violet at room temperature (mix CoCl₂ solution with some concentrated HCl to establish equilibrium).
  2. Effect of temperature: Place one tube in an ice bath and another in a hot water bath. Observe colour changes.
    • Cold: shifts equilibrium to the left (exothermic direction) → pink [Co(H₂O)₆]²⁺
    • Hot: shifts equilibrium to the right (endothermic direction) → blue [CoCl₄]²⁻
  3. Effect of concentration (adding Cl⁻): Add drops of concentrated HCl to a tube. Observe the colour change to blue (shifted right, more [CoCl₄]²⁻ formed).
  4. Effect of concentration (adding water): Add distilled water to a tube. Observe the colour change to pink (shifted left, more [Co(H₂O)₆]²⁺ formed).
  5. Record all observations in a table linking the change made, the direction of shift, and the colour observed.

Safety

  • Cobalt(II) chloride is toxic and a suspected carcinogen — wear gloves, avoid inhalation.
  • Concentrated HCl is corrosive and produces fumes — use in fume cupboard.
  • Hot water bath — handle with care to avoid burns.

Analysis

Record: Tabulate: Condition changed → direction of equilibrium shift → colour observed → explanation using Le Chatelier's principle.

The forward reaction is endothermic (ΔH positive). Heating favours the forward (endothermic) direction. Adding Cl⁻ shifts equilibrium to the right. Adding water shifts it to the left.

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Subjective colour judgementRandomUse a colorimeter to measure absorbance; compare against standards
Temperature of water bath not constantSystematicMonitor with thermometer; maintain with ice/heating
Adding too much/too little reagent to see changeSystematicAdd in controlled small amounts; record volumes
Equilibrium not fully re-establishedSystematicAllow sufficient time at each temperature before recording
Exam Tip: Le Chatelier's principle states the system shifts to oppose the imposed change. For temperature: heating shifts equilibrium in the endothermic direction. For concentration: adding a species shifts equilibrium away from that species. A catalyst does NOT shift the position of equilibrium — it only speeds up attainment of equilibrium.
