O2 — Organic Chemistry II

1. Aldehydes and Ketones

1.1 Structure and Properties

Both aldehydes and ketones contain the carbonyl group (C=O). In aldehydes, the carbonyl is at the end of the chain (bonded to at least one H). In ketones, it is between two carbon atoms. The C=O bond is polar (δ+C=Oδ-), making the carbonyl carbon susceptible to nucleophilic attack.

1.2 Nucleophilic Addition

The key reaction of aldehydes and ketones is nucleophilic addition. The nucleophile attacks the δ+ carbon of the C=O group.

Reaction with HCN (hydrogen cyanide):

RCHO + HCN → RCH(OH)CN   (hydroxynitrile / cyanohydrin)

Conditions: HCN in the presence of a trace of KCN (or NaCN) as a base catalyst. The CN- ion is the nucleophile.

Mechanism:

  1. The lone pair on CN- attacks the δ+ carbon of C=O, forming a new C-CN bond. The π bond breaks and both electrons move to the oxygen, creating an alkoxide intermediate (C-O-).
  2. The alkoxide ion is protonated by HCN to form the -OH group, regenerating CN-.

This reaction extends the carbon chain by one carbon. The nitrile group can be hydrolysed to a carboxylic acid or reduced to an amine.

If the carbonyl compound is an aldehyde (not symmetrical), the product contains a chiral centre, producing a racemic mixture because the nucleophile can attack from either side of the planar carbonyl group.

1.3 Distinguishing Aldehydes from Ketones

Tollens' reagent (ammoniacal silver nitrate, [Ag(NH3)2]+): Aldehydes produce a silver mirror (Ag+ reduced to Ag). Ketones give no reaction.

Fehling's solution (alkaline copper(II) sulfate): Aldehydes produce a brick-red precipitate of Cu2O. Ketones give no reaction.

Both tests work because aldehydes can be further oxidised to carboxylic acids, while ketones cannot.

Exam Tip: In nucleophilic addition mechanisms, use full curly arrows showing the lone pair on CN- attacking the δ+ carbonyl carbon, and a curly arrow from the C=O π bond to the oxygen. Show the intermediate alkoxide ion before protonation.

2. Carboxylic Acids and Derivatives

2.1 Carboxylic Acids

Carboxylic acids contain the -COOH functional group. They are weak acids — they partially dissociate in water:

RCOOH(aq) ⇌ RCOO-(aq) + H+(aq)

Reactions of carboxylic acids:

  • With metals: 2CH3COOH + Mg → (CH3COO)2Mg + H2
  • With carbonates: 2CH3COOH + Na2CO3 → 2CH3COONa + H2O + CO2
  • With bases: CH3COOH + NaOH → CH3COONa + H2O
  • With alcohols (esterification): See alcohols section

2.2 Esters

Esters are formed from the reaction of a carboxylic acid (or acyl chloride/acid anhydride) with an alcohol. They contain the -COO- linkage.

Naming esters: The alcohol part comes first (as an alkyl group), followed by the acid part with the suffix -oate. For example, ethyl ethanoate is formed from ethanol and ethanoic acid.

Hydrolysis of esters:

  • Acid hydrolysis: Ester + water (with H2SO4 catalyst, reflux) → carboxylic acid + alcohol. This is reversible.
  • Base (alkaline) hydrolysis: Ester + NaOH (reflux) → sodium salt of carboxylic acid + alcohol. This is irreversible and goes to completion. Also known as saponification.

2.3 Acyl Chlorides

Acyl chlorides (R-COCl) are very reactive derivatives of carboxylic acids. They react vigorously at room temperature with nucleophiles in addition-elimination reactions:

NucleophileReactionProduct
WaterRCOCl + H2O → RCOOH + HClCarboxylic acid + HCl fumes
Alcohol (ROH)RCOCl + R'OH → RCOOR' + HClEster + HCl fumes
Ammonia (NH3)RCOCl + 2NH3 → RCONH2 + NH4ClAmide
Amine (RNH2)RCOCl + 2R'NH2 → RCONHR' + R'NH3ClN-substituted amide

Acyl chlorides are preferred over carboxylic acids for ester synthesis because the reactions are faster, go to completion (irreversible), and produce higher yields.

2.4 Acid Anhydrides

Acid anhydrides (RCO-O-COR) undergo similar reactions to acyl chlorides but are less vigorous and cheaper, making them preferred in industry:

(CH3CO)2O + CH3OH → CH3COOCH3 + CH3COOH

The by-product is a carboxylic acid rather than HCl, making acid anhydrides safer to use.

Exam Tip: Aspirin is made by reacting 2-hydroxybenzoic acid (salicylic acid) with ethanoic anhydride. The -OH group on the benzene ring reacts with the anhydride to form an ester linkage. Know this as a key application.

3. Aromatic Chemistry

3.1 Structure of Benzene

Benzene (C6H6): A planar, cyclic molecule with a ring of 6 carbon atoms. Each carbon is sp2 hybridised with one p orbital perpendicular to the ring. The p orbitals overlap to form a delocalised π system above and below the plane of the ring.

Evidence for the delocalised model (not Kekule's alternating double bonds):

  • All C-C bonds in benzene are the same length (140 pm), intermediate between C-C (154 pm) and C=C (134 pm).
  • Benzene's enthalpy of hydrogenation is 208 kJ mol-1, which is 152 kJ mol-1 less exothermic than predicted for cyclohexatriene (3 × cyclohexene). This shows benzene is more stable than expected due to delocalisation energy.
  • Benzene does not readily undergo addition reactions (which would break the delocalisation). Instead, it undergoes substitution reactions to preserve the stable π system.

3.2 Electrophilic Substitution

Benzene's delocalised π system attracts electrophiles, but the reaction proceeds via substitution (not addition) to preserve the aromatic stability.

Nitration:

C6H6 + HNO3 → C6H5NO2 + H2O

Conditions: Concentrated HNO3 + concentrated H2SO4, heated to 50°C (below 55°C to prevent disubstitution).

The electrophile is the nitronium ion, NO2+, generated by:

HNO3 + H2SO4 → NO2+ + HSO4- + H2O

Mechanism: The π electrons of the benzene ring attack NO2+, forming a positively charged intermediate (Wheland intermediate). Then H+ is lost from the ring carbon to restore the aromatic system. The H+ reacts with HSO4- to regenerate H2SO4 (catalyst).

Nitrobenzene can be reduced to phenylamine (aniline) using tin (Sn) and concentrated HCl, followed by NaOH.

Friedel-Crafts Acylation:

C6H6 + RCOCl → C6H5COR + HCl

Conditions: Acyl chloride (RCOCl) with an AlCl3 catalyst (halogen carrier), reflux. The electrophile is RCO+ (acylium ion), generated when AlCl3 accepts a lone pair from the Cl of the acyl chloride:

RCOCl + AlCl3 → RCO+ + AlCl4-

Friedel-Crafts acylation introduces a C=O group onto the ring. This is useful for building carbon chains on aromatic rings.

Friedel-Crafts Alkylation:

C6H6 + RCl → C6H5R + HCl

Conditions: Halogenoalkane (RCl) with AlCl3 catalyst. The electrophile is R+ (carbocation). This attaches an alkyl group to the ring.

Exam Tip: In electrophilic substitution mechanisms, draw the curly arrow from the benzene ring (the π system) to the electrophile. Show the intermediate with a positive charge on the ring and H still attached. Then show H+ being lost with a curly arrow. AlCl3 is regenerated at the end.

4. Amines

4.1 Structure and Classification

Amine: An organic compound containing a nitrogen atom with a lone pair. Derived from ammonia by replacing one or more H atoms with alkyl or aryl groups.
  • Primary amine: RNH2 (one alkyl group on nitrogen)
  • Secondary amine: R2NH (two alkyl groups)
  • Tertiary amine: R3N (three alkyl groups)

4.2 Preparation of Amines

From halogenoalkanes: Excess ammonia + halogenoalkane, heat in a sealed tube:

CH3Br + 2NH3 → CH3NH2 + NH4Br

Excess ammonia is used to minimise further substitution (secondary, tertiary amines, quaternary ammonium salts).

Reduction of nitriles: Using LiAlH4 in dry ether, or H2/Ni catalyst:

RCN + 4[H] → RCH2NH2

This produces a primary amine with one more carbon than the original nitrile.

Reduction of nitrobenzene: Sn / conc. HCl, then NaOH produces phenylamine (an aromatic amine).

4.3 Base Properties of Amines

Amines act as Bronsted-Lowry bases because the lone pair on nitrogen can accept a proton:

CH3NH2 + H2O ⇌ CH3NH3+ + OH-

Basicity order: Aliphatic amines > ammonia > aromatic amines (phenylamine).

  • Aliphatic amines are stronger bases than ammonia because alkyl groups are electron-donating (positive inductive effect), pushing electron density towards nitrogen, making the lone pair more available to accept H+.
  • Phenylamine is a weaker base because the lone pair on nitrogen is partially delocalised into the benzene ring, making it less available to accept H+.

Amines react with acids to form salts: CH3NH2 + HCl → CH3NH3+Cl-

5. Polymers

5.1 Addition Polymers

Formed from alkene monomers. The double bond opens and monomers join together. No by-products. Non-biodegradable. (Covered in O1.)

5.2 Condensation Polymers

Formed when monomers with two functional groups join together with the loss of a small molecule (usually water).

Polyesters: Formed from a dicarboxylic acid and a diol (or a hydroxy acid):

nHOOC-R-COOH + nHO-R'-OH → -(OC-R-COO-R'-O)n- + nH2O

Example: Terylene (PET) from benzene-1,4-dicarboxylic acid and ethane-1,2-diol. The ester linkage (-COO-) is formed.

Polyamides (nylons): Formed from a dicarboxylic acid and a diamine:

nHOOC-R-COOH + nH2N-R'-NH2 → -(OC-R-CONH-R'-NH)n- + nH2O

The amide linkage (-CONH-) is formed. Example: Nylon 6,6 from hexanedioic acid and 1,6-diaminohexane.

5.3 Biodegradability and Disposal

  • Addition polymers: Non-biodegradable (no polar bonds for microorganisms to attack). Can be recycled, incinerated, or sent to landfill.
  • Condensation polymers: Can be hydrolysed (the ester or amide bonds can be broken by water/acid/base). More biodegradable than addition polymers.
  • Photodegradable polymers: Break down when exposed to light.
Exam Tip: To identify the monomers from a condensation polymer: find the repeating unit, identify the linkage (ester -COO- or amide -CONH-), break the linkage, and add back OH or H where the small molecule was lost. You must be able to draw monomers from a polymer and vice versa.

6. Amino Acids and Proteins

6.1 Structure of Amino Acids

Amino acids contain both an amine group (-NH2) and a carboxylic acid group (-COOH) attached to the same carbon (the α-carbon). General formula: H2N-CHR-COOH, where R is the side chain.

Zwitterion: A dipolar ion with both a positive and a negative charge. At the isoelectric point, amino acids exist as zwitterions: +H3N-CHR-COO-. The -NH2 is protonated and the -COOH is deprotonated.

In acidic solution: +H3N-CHR-COOH (cation, positively charged — the -COO- is protonated).

In basic solution: H2N-CHR-COO- (anion, negatively charged — the -NH3+ is deprotonated).

All amino acids (except glycine, R=H) have a chiral centre at the α-carbon and exist as enantiomers.

6.2 Peptide Bonds and Proteins

Amino acids link together by condensation reactions forming peptide bonds (-CONH-):

H2N-CHR-COOH + H2N-CHR'-COOH → H2N-CHR-CONH-CHR'-COOH + H2O

Proteins are polypeptides — long chains of amino acids joined by peptide bonds. The peptide bond can be hydrolysed by heating with 6 mol dm-3 HCl under reflux.

8. NMR Spectroscopy

8.1 Carbon-13 NMR

13C NMR shows the number of different carbon environments in a molecule. Each chemically non-equivalent carbon gives a separate peak. The number of peaks equals the number of distinct carbon environments.

Chemical shift (δ) indicates the type of carbon environment. Values are given in the data booklet.

Carbon environmentApproximate δ (ppm)
C-C (alkyl, R-CH3)5-40
C-O (alcohol, ether)50-90
C=C (aromatic, alkene)110-160
C=O (carbonyl)160-220

8.2 Proton (1H) NMR

Proton NMR provides three key pieces of information:

1. Chemical shift (δ): The position of the peak, measured in ppm relative to TMS (tetramethylsilane, Si(CH3)4, defined as δ = 0). TMS is used as a reference because it gives a single, sharp peak at a field strength away from most other protons, it is inert, and easily removed (low boiling point).

Proton environmentApproximate δ (ppm)
R-CH3 (alkyl)0.7-1.6
R-CH2-R1.2-1.8
R-CH2-Cl/Br3.0-4.2
R-O-CH33.3-3.9
R-OH0.5-6.0 (variable)
R-CHO9.0-10.0
Ar-H (benzene ring)6.5-8.0
R-COOH10.0-12.0

2. Integration (relative peak area): The area under each peak is proportional to the number of hydrogen atoms in that environment. This is shown as a stepped integration trace or a numerical ratio.

3. Splitting pattern (spin-spin coupling): Adjacent non-equivalent protons cause splitting of peaks. The n+1 rule: a proton with n equivalent neighbours is split into (n+1) peaks.

Number of adjacent HSplitting patternName
01 peakSinglet (s)
12 peaksDoublet (d)
23 peaksTriplet (t)
34 peaksQuartet (q)

Example: In ethanol (CH3CH2OH), the CH3 group (3H, triplet — split by 2 adjacent CH2 protons), the CH2 group (2H, quartet — split by 3 adjacent CH3 protons), and the OH (1H, singlet — does not couple due to rapid proton exchange).

Exam Tip: O-H and N-H protons often appear as broad singlets and do not show splitting because of rapid proton exchange with the solvent. They can be identified using D2O shake: adding D2O causes the O-H/N-H peak to disappear as the proton is exchanged for deuterium.

9. Chromatography

9.1 Thin Layer Chromatography (TLC)

A solid stationary phase (silica or alumina on a plate) and a liquid mobile phase (solvent). The sample is spotted on the baseline and the plate is placed in a solvent. Components separate based on their relative affinity for the stationary and mobile phases.

Rf = distance moved by substance / distance moved by solvent front

Rf values can be compared with known values to identify substances. If spots are not visible, a UV lamp or locating agent (e.g. iodine vapour, ninhydrin for amino acids) is used.

9.2 Gas Chromatography (GC)

Used to separate and identify volatile compounds. The stationary phase is a liquid or solid coated inside a long column. The mobile phase is an inert carrier gas (e.g. nitrogen, helium). The sample is injected and vaporised. Components separate based on their boiling points and affinity for the stationary phase.

The retention time identifies each component (by comparison with standards). The area under each peak is proportional to the amount of that component.

GC-MS: Gas chromatography coupled with mass spectrometry. GC separates the components, and MS identifies each one by its mass spectrum (fragmentation pattern and molecular ion peak). This is a powerful technique for identifying unknown organic compounds in mixtures.

9.3 High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC)

Similar to column chromatography but uses high pressure to force the mobile phase (a liquid solvent) through a column packed with a solid stationary phase. It is used for non-volatile or thermally unstable compounds that cannot be analysed by GC. Components are detected as they elute from the column (e.g. by UV absorption).

Exam Tip: Know the differences between TLC, GC, and HPLC: TLC is qualitative and quick; GC is for volatile samples and gives quantitative data; HPLC is for non-volatile or heat-sensitive samples. GC-MS combines separation with identification.
