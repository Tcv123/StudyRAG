1. Properties of Period 3 Oxides

1.1 Types of Bonding in Period 3 Oxides

OxideNa2OMgOAl2O3SiO2P4O10SO3Cl2O7
BondingIonicIonicIonic (some covalent character)Giant covalentSimple covalentSimple covalentSimple covalent

The bonding changes from ionic (Na, Mg) through ionic with covalent character (Al) to giant covalent (Si) to simple covalent (P, S, Cl). This reflects the increasing electronegativity across the period: the difference in electronegativity between the element and oxygen decreases, making the bonding progressively more covalent.

General trend: Metal oxides on the left of the period are basic (or amphoteric for Al2O3). Non-metal oxides on the right are acidic. This is a key trend across the period.

Amphoteric oxide: An oxide that can react with both acids and bases. Al2O3 reacts with acids to form aluminium salts and with bases to form aluminate ions [Al(OH)4]-.

1.3 Period 3 Chlorides

ChlorideNaClMgCl2AlCl3SiCl4PCl5
BondingIonicIonicCovalent (in vapour)Simple covalentSimple covalent
Reaction with waterDissolves (neutral)Dissolves (slightly acidic, pH ~6.5)Vigorous, acidic fumesVigorous, acidic fumesVigorous, acidic fumes

Ionic chlorides (NaCl, MgCl2) dissolve in water. NaCl gives a neutral solution. MgCl2 gives a slightly acidic solution because the high charge density of Mg2+ attracts water molecules strongly, weakening the O-H bond and releasing H+.

Covalent chlorides (AlCl3, SiCl4, PCl5) react vigorously with water (hydrolysis), producing white misty fumes of HCl:

SiCl4(l) + 2H2O(l) → SiO2(s) + 4HCl(g)
PCl5(s) + 4H2O(l) → H3PO4(aq) + 5HCl(g)
Exam Tip: AlCl3 is ionic in the solid but behaves as a covalent compound when vaporised (it forms Al2Cl6 dimers). Its reaction with water is hydrolysis, producing an acidic solution and fumes of HCl. Know the distinction between dissolving (ionic) and hydrolysis (covalent).

2. Transition Metals

2.1 Definition and Electron Configuration

Transition metal: A d-block element that forms at least one stable ion with a partially filled d sub-shell.

Note: Scandium (Sc3+ has [Ar] 3d0) and zinc (Zn2+ has [Ar] 3d10) are d-block elements but NOT transition metals because their ions do not have partially filled d orbitals.

When writing electron configurations of transition metal ions, remove the 4s electrons first, then 3d electrons if necessary.

2.2 Properties of Transition Metals

Transition metals have several characteristic properties, all arising from the partially filled d orbitals:

1. Variable oxidation states: Transition metals can form ions with different charges because the 3d and 4s electrons are close in energy, so different numbers can be removed. Common examples:

MetalCommon oxidation states
Ti+2, +3, +4
V+2, +3, +4, +5
Cr+2, +3, +6
Mn+2, +4, +7
Fe+2, +3
Co+2, +3
Cu+1, +2

2. Coloured ions: Transition metal ions in solution are coloured because the d orbitals split into different energy levels when ligands bond to the metal ion. Electrons can be promoted between these split d levels, absorbing specific wavelengths of visible light. The colour observed is the complementary colour to the one absorbed.

IonOxidation stateColour in solution

The colour depends on: the metal ion, its oxidation state, the ligands attached, and the coordination number.

3. Catalytic behaviour: Transition metals and their compounds are effective catalysts because they can change oxidation state, providing an alternative reaction pathway with lower activation energy. They can also provide a surface for adsorption of reactant molecules.

Heterogeneous catalyst: A catalyst in a different phase from the reactants (usually a solid catalyst with liquid or gaseous reactants).
Homogeneous catalyst: A catalyst in the same phase as the reactants.

2.3 Complex Ions

Complex ion: A central metal ion surrounded by ligands. A ligand is a molecule or ion that donates a lone pair of electrons to the metal ion to form a dative (coordinate) covalent bond.
Coordination number: The total number of dative bonds from ligands to the central metal ion.

Types of ligands:

TypeDescriptionExamples
MonodentateForms one dative bond (has one lone pair that bonds)H2O, NH3, Cl-, CN-, OH-
BidentateForms two dative bonds per ligandEthane-1,2-diamine (en, H2NCH2CH2NH2), ethanedioate (C2O42-)
MultidentateForms multiple dative bonds per ligandEDTA4- (hexadentate, forms 6 bonds)

Common shapes of complexes:

  • Coordination number 6: Octahedral (e.g. [Cu(H2O)6]2+, [Fe(CN)6]3-)
  • Coordination number 4: Tetrahedral (e.g. [CoCl4]2-, [CuCl4]2-) or square planar (e.g. cisplatin [Pt(NH3)2Cl2])
  • Coordination number 2: Linear (e.g. [Ag(NH3)2]+, [CuCl2]-)

2.4 Ligand Substitution Reactions

Ligands can be exchanged in complex ions. When water ligands are replaced by other ligands:

Copper(II) complexes:

[Cu(H2O)6]2+ + 4NH3 → [Cu(NH3)4(H2O)2]2+ + 4H2O

Blue solution → deep blue solution. Only 4 of the 6 water ligands are replaced by ammonia (partial substitution). The coordination number remains 6.

[Cu(H2O)6]2+ + 4Cl- → [CuCl4]2- + 6H2O

Blue solution → yellow/green solution. The shape changes from octahedral to tetrahedral because Cl- is a larger ligand. Coordination number changes from 6 to 4.

The chelate effect: Substitution by bidentate or multidentate ligands is thermodynamically favourable because it increases entropy. Replacing 6 monodentate water ligands with 3 bidentate en ligands increases the number of independent species in solution (from 7 to 4), increasing disorder.

Exam Tip: When describing ligand substitution, always state: the change in colour, any change in coordination number, any change in shape, and the type of ligand exchange (partial or complete). Write the full equation with square brackets and charges.

3.3 Acidity of Transition Metal Aqua Ions

Transition metal aqua ions such as [Fe(H2O)6]3+ are acidic in solution. The highly charged metal ion polarises the O-H bonds in the water ligands, weakening them. This makes it easier for the water to lose a proton (H+) to a free water molecule:

[Fe(H2O)6]3+(aq) + H2O(l) ⇌ [Fe(H2O)5(OH)]2+(aq) + H3O+(aq)

M3+ ions are more acidic than M2+ ions because the higher charge density polarises the water ligands more.
