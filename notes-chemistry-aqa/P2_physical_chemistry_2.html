P2 — Physical Chemistry II

1. Kinetics

1.1 Rate of Reaction

Rate of reaction: The change in concentration of a reactant or product per unit time. Units: mol dm-3 s-1.

Factors affecting rate: concentration (or pressure for gases), temperature, surface area, and catalysts.

1.2 Collision Theory and Activation Energy

For a reaction to occur, particles must collide with sufficient energy (greater than or equal to the activation energy, Ea) and with the correct orientation.

Activation energy (Ea): The minimum energy that colliding particles must have for a reaction to occur.

1.3 Maxwell-Boltzmann Distribution

The Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution shows the spread of molecular energies in a gas at a given temperature. Key features:

  • The curve starts at the origin (no molecules have zero energy).
  • The peak represents the most probable energy.
  • The curve is asymmetric with a long tail to the right (no maximum energy).
  • The area under the curve represents the total number of molecules and is constant.
  • The area to the right of Ea represents molecules with enough energy to react.

Effect of temperature increase: The peak shifts right and becomes lower and broader. More molecules have energy ≥ Ea, so the rate increases.

Effect of a catalyst: Provides an alternative pathway with a lower Ea. The distribution curve stays the same, but the Ea line moves left, so more molecules have sufficient energy.

1.4 Rate Equations and Orders

Order of reaction (with respect to a reactant): The power to which the concentration of that reactant is raised in the rate equation. Can only be determined experimentally.

For a reaction A + B → products:

Rate = k[A]m[B]n

Where k = rate constant, m = order with respect to A, n = order with respect to B. The overall order = m + n.

The value of k increases with temperature.

1.6 The Arrhenius Equation

k = Ae-Ea/RT

Taking the natural logarithm gives a linear form:

ln k = ln A - Ea / RT

A graph of ln k (y-axis) against 1/T (x-axis) gives a straight line with gradient = -Ea/R and y-intercept = ln A, where A is the pre-exponential (Arrhenius) factor.

Exam Tip: To calculate Ea from a graph: gradient = -Ea/R, so Ea = -gradient × R. Use R = 8.314 J K-1 mol-1. The answer will be in J mol-1; divide by 1000 to convert to kJ mol-1.

1.7 Rate-Determining Step

Rate-determining step: The slowest step in a multi-step reaction mechanism. It controls the overall rate of reaction.

Only species that appear in or before the rate-determining step appear in the rate equation. If the rate equation is Rate = k[A][B], the rate-determining step involves one molecule of A and one molecule of B.

2. Equilibria

2.1 Dynamic Equilibrium

Dynamic equilibrium: A state reached in a closed system when the rate of the forward reaction equals the rate of the reverse reaction, so the concentrations of reactants and products remain constant (though not necessarily equal).

2.2 Kc — The Equilibrium Constant

For the reaction: aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD

Kc = [C]c[D]d / [A]a[B]b

Where square brackets denote equilibrium concentrations in mol dm-3. Kc has no units if the powers balance, but usually has units that must be calculated.

A large Kc means the equilibrium lies to the right (products favoured). A small Kc means the equilibrium lies to the left (reactants favoured).

2.3 Kp — Equilibrium Constant in Terms of Partial Pressures

For gaseous equilibria:

pA = xA × Ptotal

Where pA = partial pressure of A, xA = mole fraction of A.

Kp = pCc × pDd / (pAa × pBb)

2.4 Le Chatelier's Principle

Le Chatelier's Principle: If a system at equilibrium is subjected to a change in conditions, the position of equilibrium shifts to counteract the change and restore equilibrium.
ChangeEffect on positionEffect on K
Increase concentration of reactantShifts right (towards products)No change
Increase temperature (exothermic forward)Shifts left (endothermic direction)K decreases
Increase temperature (endothermic forward)Shifts right (endothermic direction)K increases
Increase pressureShifts to side with fewer moles of gasNo change
Add catalystNo change in positionNo change
Exam Tip: Only temperature changes the value of K. Concentration, pressure, and catalysts change the position of equilibrium but not K itself. A catalyst only speeds up the rate at which equilibrium is reached.

3. Oxidation, Reduction and Redox

3.1 Oxidation States

Oxidation state (oxidation number): A measure of the degree of oxidation of an atom in a compound. It represents the charge an atom would have if all bonds were fully ionic.

Rules for assigning oxidation states:

  • Uncombined elements = 0
  • Simple ions = the charge on the ion
  • Oxygen = -2 (except in peroxides = -1, and OF2 = +2)
  • Hydrogen = +1 (except in metal hydrides = -1)
  • Fluorine = always -1
  • Sum of oxidation states in a compound = 0; in a polyatomic ion = the charge
Oxidation: Loss of electrons / increase in oxidation state.
Reduction: Gain of electrons / decrease in oxidation state. (Remember OIL RIG)

3.2 Half-Equations and Ionic Equations

Redox reactions can be split into two half-equations. To balance half-equations:

  1. Balance the atoms other than O and H.
  2. Balance oxygen by adding H2O.
  3. Balance hydrogen by adding H+.
  4. Balance charge by adding electrons.

Combine half-equations by ensuring the number of electrons lost equals the number gained.

4. Thermodynamics

4.1 Born-Haber Cycles

A Born-Haber cycle applies Hess's law to calculate lattice enthalpy indirectly, since it cannot be measured directly. The cycle links:

  • Enthalpy of formation (ΔHf)
  • Enthalpy of atomisation (ΔHat)
  • First and subsequent ionisation energies (IE)
  • Electron affinities (EA) — enthalpy change when a gaseous atom gains an electron
  • Lattice enthalpy (ΔHlatt)
Lattice enthalpy of formation: The enthalpy change when one mole of an ionic lattice is formed from its gaseous ions under standard conditions. Always exothermic (negative).
Lattice enthalpy of dissociation: The enthalpy change when one mole of an ionic lattice is separated into gaseous ions. Always endothermic (positive).

Lattice enthalpy is affected by ionic charge (higher charge = stronger attraction = more exothermic) and ionic radius (smaller ions = closer together = stronger attraction = more exothermic).

4.2 Enthalpy of Solution and Hydration

ΔHsol = -ΔHlatt(formation) + ΔHhyd(cation) + ΔHhyd(anion)
Enthalpy of hydration: The enthalpy change when one mole of gaseous ions is dissolved in sufficient water to form an infinitely dilute solution.

4.3 Entropy

Entropy (S): A measure of the disorder or number of ways energy can be distributed in a system. Units: J K-1 mol-1.

Entropy increases when: solids melt, liquids boil, the number of moles of gas increases, a solid dissolves.

ΔSsystem = ΣS(products) - ΣS(reactants)

4.4 Gibbs Free Energy

ΔG = ΔH - TΔS

A reaction is thermodynamically feasible (spontaneous) when ΔG ≤ 0.

Exam Tip: When using ΔG = ΔH - TΔS, ensure units are consistent. ΔH is usually in kJ mol-1 and ΔS in J K-1 mol-1. Convert ΔS to kJ by dividing by 1000, or ΔH to J by multiplying by 1000.

5. Acids and Bases

5.1 Bronsted-Lowry Theory

Bronsted-Lowry acid: A proton (H+) donor.
Bronsted-Lowry base: A proton (H+) acceptor.

Strong acids (e.g. HCl, H2SO4, HNO3) fully dissociate in water. Weak acids (e.g. CH3COOH, H2CO3) partially dissociate — an equilibrium is established.

5.2 pH Calculations

pH = -log10[H+]     and     [H+] = 10-pH

Strong acid: [H+] = concentration of acid (for monobasic). For H2SO4, [H+] = 2 × concentration.

Strong base: Calculate [OH-], then use Kw to find [H+].

Kw = [H+][OH-] = 1.00 × 10-14 mol2 dm-6 at 298 K

5.3 The Acid Dissociation Constant Ka

For a weak acid HA:

Ka = [H+][A-] / [HA]

Assumptions: [H+] = [A-] (negligible dissociation of water) and [HA]equilibrium ≈ [HA]initial (weak acid, very little dissociation).

pKa = -log10 Ka

A smaller Ka (larger pKa) means a weaker acid.

5.4 Buffer Solutions

Buffer solution: A solution that resists changes in pH when small amounts of acid or base are added. It consists of a weak acid and its conjugate base (or a weak base and its conjugate acid).

An acidic buffer is made by mixing a weak acid with its sodium/potassium salt (e.g. CH3COOH + CH3COONa), or by partially neutralising a weak acid with a strong base.

How a buffer works:

  • If H+ is added: the conjugate base (A-) reacts with it: A- + H+ → HA. This removes the added H+.
  • If OH- is added: the weak acid (HA) reacts with it: HA + OH- → A- + H2O. This removes the added OH-.

To calculate buffer pH, use the Henderson-Hasselbalch approach or rearrange Ka:

[H+] = Ka × [HA] / [A-]
Exam Tip: Blood is buffered at pH 7.4 by the H2CO3/HCO3- system. This is a common application question. Explain the buffer action using the equilibrium and the role of both the acid and conjugate base.

6. Electrode Potentials

6.1 Standard Electrode Potential

Standard electrode potential (E°): The EMF of a half-cell connected to a standard hydrogen electrode under standard conditions (298 K, 100 kPa, 1.00 mol dm-3 solutions).

The standard hydrogen electrode (SHE) is assigned a potential of 0.00 V. It consists of hydrogen gas at 100 kPa bubbled over a platinum electrode in 1.00 mol dm-3 H+(aq).

6.2 Electrochemical Cells

Ecell = E°(positive terminal / cathode) - E°(negative terminal / anode)

Or equivalently: Ecell = E°(reduction at cathode) - E°(reduction at anode)

The more positive half-cell is the positive terminal (cathode, where reduction occurs). The more negative half-cell is the negative terminal (anode, where oxidation occurs). Electrons flow from the more negative to the more positive electrode through the external circuit.

6.3 Feasibility of Reactions

A reaction is thermodynamically feasible if Ecell is positive. However, a positive Ecell does not guarantee a reaction will occur in practice — kinetic factors (e.g. high activation energy) may prevent it.

Exam Tip: When predicting feasibility, always state that a positive Ecell only indicates thermodynamic feasibility. The reaction may be kinetically too slow to observe. Also note that non-standard conditions may alter the electrode potential and thus the feasibility.
ΔG = -nFEcell

Where n = number of moles of electrons transferred, F = Faraday constant (96485 C mol-1). This links electrode potentials to free energy.
