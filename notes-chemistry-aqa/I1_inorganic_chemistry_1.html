I1 — Inorganic Chemistry I

1. Periodicity

1.1 Classification of the Periodic Table

The periodic table arranges elements in order of increasing atomic number. Periods are horizontal rows, and groups are vertical columns. Elements in the same group have the same number of outer-shell electrons and therefore similar chemical properties.

The periodic table is divided into blocks based on the sub-shell being filled:

  • s-block: Groups 1 and 2 (and helium) — outer electrons in an s sub-shell.
  • p-block: Groups 3 to 0 (13 to 18) — outer electrons in a p sub-shell.
  • d-block: Transition metals — electrons filling the d sub-shell.

1.2 Period 3 Trends

Atomic radius: Decreases across Period 3 from Na to Cl. The nuclear charge increases with each proton added, but the shielding remains approximately the same (electrons added to the same shell). This means a greater effective nuclear charge pulls the outer electrons closer to the nucleus.

First ionisation energy: Generally increases across Period 3 from Na to Ar due to increasing nuclear charge and decreasing atomic radius with similar shielding.

Config. (outer)3s13s23p13p23p33p43p53p6

Two anomalies in Period 3:

  • Mg > Al: Aluminium loses a 3p electron, which is in a higher-energy sub-shell and further from the nucleus than magnesium's 3s electron. The 3p electron is also slightly shielded by the 3s2 electrons, so less energy is needed to remove it.
  • P > S: Sulfur has a paired electron in one of its 3p orbitals. The electron-electron repulsion in this pair makes one of the electrons easier to remove than one of phosphorus's three unpaired 3p electrons.
Exam Tip: When explaining IE anomalies, always compare the specific sub-shells involved and mention either the higher energy of the p sub-shell (Mg/Al drop) or electron-electron repulsion from pairing (P/S drop). Give electron configurations to support your answer.

Electronegativity: Increases across Period 3 from Na to Cl (excluding noble gases which do not generally form bonds). Atoms have a stronger pull on bonding electrons due to increasing nuclear charge and decreasing atomic radius.

Melting point trend across Period 3:

ElementNaMgAlSiP4S8Cl2Ar
StructureMetallicMetallicMetallicGiant covalentSimple molecularSimple molecularSimple molecularSimple molecular
Melting point371 K923 K933 K1687 K317 K392 K172 K84 K

Explanation of melting point trend:

  • Na to Al (metals): Melting point increases. Metallic bonding becomes stronger due to increasing charge on the metal ion (Na+, Mg2+, Al3+), more delocalised electrons, and smaller ionic radius. More energy is needed to overcome the stronger attraction.
  • Si: Very high melting point. Silicon has a giant covalent (macromolecular) structure like diamond. Many strong covalent bonds must be broken, requiring a lot of energy.
  • P4 to Ar (simple molecular): Low melting points. Only weak London (dispersion) forces between molecules need to be overcome. S8 has a higher melting point than P4 because it has more electrons and a larger molecular size, giving stronger London forces. Cl2 and Ar have the lowest melting points due to their small molecular sizes.

ElectronegativityDecreasesBonding electrons are further from the nucleus and more shielded, so the atom attracts them less strongly.
ReactivityIncreasesIt becomes easier to lose the two outer electrons as IE decreases. Atoms are more readily oxidised.
Melting pointGenerally decreasesLarger ions, weaker metallic bonding (though there are some irregularities).

2.2 Reactions of Group 2 Elements with Water

Group 2 metals react with water to form a metal hydroxide and hydrogen gas. Reactivity increases down the group.

M(s) + 2H2O(l) → M(OH)2(aq) + H2(g)
  • Magnesium: Reacts very slowly with cold water (a few bubbles of H2, thin layer of Mg(OH)2 forms). Reacts vigorously with steam: Mg(s) + H2O(g) → MgO(s) + H2(g)
  • Calcium: Reacts steadily with cold water, producing Ca(OH)2 (slightly soluble, forms a white precipitate / milky solution) and bubbles of hydrogen.
  • Strontium: Reacts more vigorously than calcium with cold water.
  • Barium: Reacts vigorously with cold water, producing Ba(OH)2 (more soluble) and hydrogen.

Exam Tip: Barium sulfate is so insoluble that it is used as a "barium meal" in medical X-rays. Despite barium ions being toxic, BaSO4 is safe because it does not dissolve and release Ba2+ ions.

2.4 Thermal Decomposition of Group 2 Carbonates and Nitrates

Carbonates: Group 2 carbonates decompose on heating to form the metal oxide and carbon dioxide:

MCO3(s) → MO(s) + CO2(g)

The thermal stability increases down the group (higher temperatures needed). BeCO3 decomposes most easily; BaCO3 requires the highest temperature.

Explanation: The larger cations down the group polarise the carbonate anion less. Smaller, more highly charged cations (like Be2+) distort the electron cloud of the large CO32- anion more, weakening the C-O bond and making decomposition easier.

Nitrates: Group 2 nitrates decompose on heating to form the metal oxide, nitrogen dioxide, and oxygen:

2M(NO3)2(s) → 2MO(s) + 4NO2(g) + O2(g)

The same trend in thermal stability applies: stability increases down the group.

Flame tests work because the heat excites electrons to higher energy levels. When they fall back down, they emit light of characteristic wavelengths (colours).

2.6 Uses of Group 2 Compounds

  • Mg(OH)2: Used in antacid remedies to neutralise excess stomach acid (HCl). Also known as milk of magnesia.
  • Ca(OH)2: Used to neutralise acidic soils in agriculture (liming) and to treat acidic waste water and flue gases.
  • CaO (quicklite / quicklite): Used in the manufacture of cement and to neutralise acidic soils.
  • CaCO3: Used in the Blast Furnace to remove acidic impurities (SiO2) from iron ore.

3.2 Trends in Electronegativity and Reactivity

Electronegativity decreases down Group 7: F is the most electronegative element. Going down, the atoms are larger with more shielding, so they attract bonding electrons less strongly.

Reactivity decreases down the group. Halogens react by gaining an electron to form halide ions (X-). Fluorine gains an electron most easily because: its small atomic radius means the incoming electron is close to the nucleus, and despite electron-electron repulsion in the small 2p orbital, the very high electronegativity and nuclear attraction dominate. Going down the group, the incoming electron enters a shell further from the nucleus with more shielding, making it harder to attract and capture.

Oxidising agent: A substance that oxidises another substance by gaining electrons itself. Halogens are strong oxidising agents. Their oxidising ability decreases down the group.

3.3 Displacement Reactions of Halogens

A more reactive halogen can displace a less reactive halide from solution. This demonstrates the trend in oxidising ability.

KCl(aq)KBr(aq)KI(aq)
Cl2(aq)No reactionCl2 displaces Br- (orange)Cl2 displaces I- (brown)
Br2(aq)No reactionNo reactionBr2 displaces I- (brown)
I2(aq)No reactionNo reactionNo reaction

Example ionic equation for Cl2 displacing Br-:

Cl2(aq) + 2Br-(aq) → 2Cl-(aq) + Br2(aq)

Chlorine is reduced (gains electrons, it is the oxidising agent). Bromide is oxidised (loses electrons).

Exam Tip: In displacement reactions, the more reactive halogen always oxidises the less reactive halide ion. You can confirm observations by adding cyclohexane: Cl2 and Br2 dissolve to give different colours. Bromine is orange in cyclohexane, iodine is purple/violet.

3.4 Halide Ions as Reducing Agents

Halide ions can act as reducing agents by donating electrons. Reducing ability increases down the group (I- > Br- > Cl-) because the outer electron is further from the nucleus and more easily lost.

Reactions of halide ions with concentrated sulfuric acid (H2SO4):

HalideReaction with conc. H2SO4ProductsObservations
NaClAcid-base only (not a redox reaction)NaHSO4 + HClSteamy white fumes of HCl
NaBrAcid-base + redoxNaHSO4 + HBr, then HBr reduces H2SO4 → Br2 + SO2 + H2OSteamy fumes, then orange-brown fumes (Br2), choking gas (SO2)
NaIAcid-base + further redoxNaHSO4 + HI, then HI reduces H2SO4 → I2 + SO2, then S, then H2SPurple/black solid (I2), yellow solid (S), rotten egg smell (H2S)

Iodide is a stronger reducing agent than bromide, which is why it can reduce sulfuric acid further (all the way to H2S, oxidation state of S goes from +6 to -2). Chloride is too weak a reducing agent to reduce H2SO4 at all.

3.6 Disproportionation

Disproportionation: A reaction in which the same element is simultaneously oxidised and reduced.

Chlorine with water:

Cl2(aq) + H2O(l) → HCl(aq) + HClO(aq)

Chlorine (oxidation state 0) is oxidised to +1 in HClO and reduced to -1 in HCl. This is a disproportionation reaction.

Chlorine with cold dilute NaOH:

Cl2(aq) + 2NaOH(aq) → NaCl(aq) + NaClO(aq) + H2O(l)

This produces sodium chlorate(I), NaClO, which is the active ingredient in bleach. Again, chlorine disproportionates from 0 to -1 and +1.

Uses of chlorine:

  • Water treatment and purification — chlorine kills bacteria by forming HClO, a strong oxidising agent.
  • The benefits of chlorinating water (preventing waterborne diseases like cholera and typhoid) outweigh the risks (formation of small amounts of potentially harmful chlorinated organic compounds).
