O1 — Organic Chemistry I

1. Nomenclature and Functional Groups

1.1 IUPAC Naming Rules

Organic molecules are named using the IUPAC system. The name has three parts:

  1. Prefix: Identifies the longest carbon chain (meth- = 1C, eth- = 2C, prop- = 3C, but- = 4C, pent- = 5C, hex- = 6C).
  2. Suffix: Identifies the functional group (-ane, -ene, -ol, -al, -one, -oic acid, -amine).
  3. Side chains and substituents: Named with a position number and prefix (e.g. 2-methyl, 3-chloro).

Homologous series: A family of organic compounds with the same functional group, the same general formula, and similar chemical properties. Each successive member differs by CH2.

2. Isomerism

Isomers: Molecules with the same molecular formula but different structural arrangements.

2.1 Structural Isomerism

Structural isomers have the same molecular formula but different structural formulae. There are three types:

  • Chain isomerism: Different arrangements of the carbon chain (e.g. butane and methylpropane, both C4H10).
  • Position isomerism: Same carbon chain but the functional group is in a different position (e.g. butan-1-ol and butan-2-ol).
  • Functional group isomerism: Same molecular formula but different functional groups (e.g. propanal C2H5CHO and propanone CH3COCH3, both C3H6O).

2.2 E/Z Isomerism (Geometric Isomerism)

E/Z isomerism arises in alkenes when there is restricted rotation about the C=C double bond, and each carbon of the double bond has two different groups attached.

  • Z-isomer (zusammen): The higher priority groups on each carbon are on the same side of the double bond.
  • E-isomer (entgegen): The higher priority groups are on opposite sides.

Priority is determined by atomic number (higher atomic number = higher priority), following the Cahn-Ingold-Prelog rules.

2.3 Optical Isomerism

Chiral centre (asymmetric carbon): A carbon atom bonded to four different groups. This produces two non-superimposable mirror image forms called enantiomers.

Enantiomers have identical physical properties (melting point, boiling point, solubility) and identical chemical properties, except they rotate the plane of plane-polarised light in opposite directions. One is the (+) enantiomer (rotates clockwise), the other is the (-) enantiomer (rotates anticlockwise).

Racemic mixture (racemate): A 50:50 mixture of the two enantiomers. It shows no overall rotation of plane-polarised light because the rotations cancel out.
Exam Tip: To identify a chiral centre, look for a carbon bonded to four DIFFERENT groups. Draw the two enantiomers as mirror images. In reactions that create a new chiral centre from a planar molecule (e.g. nucleophilic addition to an aldehyde), a racemic mixture is formed because the reagent can attack equally from both sides.

3. Alkanes

3.1 Properties

Alkanes are saturated hydrocarbons (contain only C-C single bonds and C-H bonds). General formula: CnH2n+2. They are relatively unreactive because C-C and C-H bonds are strong and non-polar (similar electronegativities of C and H).

Boiling points increase with chain length (more London forces) and decrease with branching (less surface area for intermolecular contact).

3.2 Combustion

Complete combustion (excess oxygen):

CnH2n+2 + (3n+1)/2 O2 → nCO2 + (n+1)H2O

Alkanes are used as fuels. Complete combustion produces CO2 (a greenhouse gas contributing to climate change) and H2O.

Incomplete combustion (limited oxygen) produces CO (toxic, colourless, odourless gas that binds irreversibly to haemoglobin) and/or C (soot/particulates that cause respiratory problems).

3.3 Free Radical Substitution

Alkanes react with halogens (Cl2, Br2) in the presence of UV light via a free radical mechanism. This is the only reaction of alkanes at A-level (besides combustion).

Free radical: A reactive species with an unpaired electron. They are formed by homolytic fission of a covalent bond.

Mechanism (three stages):

Initiation: UV light causes homolytic fission of the Cl-Cl bond:

Cl2 → 2Cl•   (UV light)

Propagation: Chain reactions that regenerate free radicals:

CH4 + Cl• → •CH3 + HCl
•CH3 + Cl2 → CH3Cl + Cl•

Termination: Two free radicals combine:

Cl• + Cl• → Cl2     •CH3 + Cl• → CH3Cl     •CH3 + •CH3 → C2H6

Limitations of free radical substitution:

  • Further substitution can occur (CH3Cl → CH2Cl2 → CHCl3 → CCl4), giving a mixture of products.
  • For longer alkanes, hydrogen atoms on different carbons can be substituted, giving positional isomers.
  • This makes free radical substitution a poor method for preparing specific halogenoalkanes.
Exam Tip: Use single-headed curly arrows (fishhooks) for free radical mechanisms to show the movement of single electrons. Use the dot notation (•) to show unpaired electrons on radicals. You must show all three stages clearly labelled.

4. Alkenes

4.1 Properties

Alkenes are unsaturated hydrocarbons containing at least one C=C double bond. General formula: CnH2n. The double bond consists of a sigma (σ) bond (head-on overlap) and a pi (π) bond (sideways overlap of p orbitals). The π bond is an area of high electron density, making alkenes susceptible to attack by electrophiles.

4.2 Electrophilic Addition

Electrophile: An electron-pair acceptor; a species attracted to areas of high electron density. Examples: H+, Br+, NO2+, carbocations.

The general mechanism for electrophilic addition to alkenes:

  1. The π bond electrons attract the electrophile.
  2. A pair of electrons from the π bond forms a new bond with the electrophile.
  3. The other part of the electrophile (now a negative species) bonds to the other carbon of the double bond.

Key electrophilic addition reactions:

ReagentConditionsProductProduct type

Markownikoff's rule: When HBr adds across an unsymmetrical alkene, the major product has the hydrogen atom added to the carbon already bonded to more hydrogen atoms. The intermediate carbocation is more stable when the positive charge is on the more substituted carbon (tertiary > secondary > primary stability due to the inductive effect of alkyl groups).

Test for unsaturation: Add bromine water (orange). If the solution decolourises, a C=C double bond is present (electrophilic addition of Br2).

4.3 Addition Polymerisation

Many alkene molecules (monomers) join together to form a long chain polymer. The double bond opens up and each monomer bonds to the next:

nCH2=CH2 → -(CH2-CH2)n-

Addition polymers are unreactive and non-biodegradable, causing environmental problems. They can be recycled mechanically, used as fuel (incineration), or processed using feedstock recycling (cracking back to monomers).

5. Halogenoalkanes

5.1 Classification

Halogenoalkanes are classified as primary, secondary, or tertiary depending on the number of carbon atoms bonded to the carbon carrying the halogen:

  • Primary: The halogen-bearing carbon is bonded to one other carbon (e.g. CH3CH2Br).
  • Secondary: Bonded to two other carbons (e.g. (CH3)2CHBr).
  • Tertiary: Bonded to three other carbons (e.g. (CH3)3CBr).

5.2 Nucleophilic Substitution

Nucleophile: An electron-pair donor; a species with a lone pair that is attracted to a δ+ carbon. Examples: OH-, CN-, NH3, H2O.

The C-X bond is polar (δ+C-Xδ-) because halogens are more electronegative than carbon. Nucleophiles attack the δ+ carbon.

SN2 mechanism (primary halogenoalkanes):

  • One-step mechanism. The nucleophile attacks the δ+ carbon from the opposite side to the halogen (backside attack).
  • A transition state forms where the nucleophile is partially bonded and the halogen is partially leaving.
  • Rate = k[halogenoalkane][nucleophile] — second order, bimolecular.
  • Produces inversion of configuration at the carbon centre.

SN1 mechanism (tertiary halogenoalkanes):

  • Two-step mechanism. Step 1: The C-X bond breaks heterolytically to form a carbocation (slow, rate-determining step). Step 2: The nucleophile rapidly attacks the carbocation.
  • Rate = k[halogenoalkane] — first order, unimolecular.
  • Tertiary carbocations are more stable (inductive effect of three alkyl groups stabilises the positive charge).
  • Produces a racemic mixture if a chiral centre is formed (nucleophile can attack from either side of the planar carbocation).

Secondary halogenoalkanes undergo a mixture of SN1 and SN2.

5.3 Elimination

Halogenoalkanes can also undergo elimination instead of substitution, forming an alkene:

CH3CHBrCH3 + KOH → CH3CH=CH2 + KBr + H2O

Conditions: Hot ethanolic KOH (dissolved in ethanol). The OH- acts as a base, removing a hydrogen from a carbon adjacent to the halogen. The C-X bond breaks and the electrons form the double bond.

Exam Tip: The conditions determine whether substitution or elimination occurs. Aqueous NaOH (warm) favours substitution (OH- acts as nucleophile). Ethanolic KOH (hot) favours elimination (OH- acts as base). Tertiary halogenoalkanes favour elimination and SN1; primary favour SN2.

5.4 Reactivity of Halogenoalkanes

The rate of hydrolysis (substitution with water/OH-) depends on the strength of the C-X bond:

C-X bondBond enthalpy (kJ mol-1)Rate of hydrolysis
C-F484Very slow (strongest bond)
C-Cl338Slow
C-Br276Moderate
C-I238Fast (weakest bond)

Iodoalkanes react fastest because the C-I bond is the weakest and easiest to break.

6. Alcohols

6.1 Classification

  • Primary (1°): The -OH group is on a carbon bonded to one other carbon (e.g. butan-1-ol).
  • Secondary (2°): The -OH is on a carbon bonded to two other carbons (e.g. butan-2-ol).
  • Tertiary (3°): The -OH is on a carbon bonded to three other carbons (e.g. 2-methylpropan-2-ol).

6.2 Oxidation of Alcohols

The oxidising agent is acidified potassium dichromate (K2Cr2O7 / H2SO4). The orange Cr2O72- is reduced to green Cr3+.

Alcohol typeDistillation productReflux product
PrimaryAldehyde (distil off immediately)Carboxylic acid (further oxidation)
SecondaryKetone (no further oxidation possible)
TertiaryNo reaction (cannot be oxidised by K2Cr2O7)

[O] is used as shorthand for the oxidising agent in equations:

Primary alcohol + [O] → Aldehyde + H2O   (distillation)
Aldehyde + [O] → Carboxylic acid   (reflux)

6.3 Dehydration

Alcohols can be dehydrated to form alkenes using a strong acid catalyst:

CH3CH2OH → CH2=CH2 + H2O

Conditions: Concentrated H2SO4 or concentrated H3PO4 at elevated temperature. Alternatively, pass alcohol vapour over hot Al2O3. This is an elimination reaction.

6.4 Esterification

An alcohol reacts with a carboxylic acid in the presence of a concentrated H2SO4 catalyst to form an ester and water:

CH3COOH + CH3CH2OH ⇌ CH3COOCH2CH3 + H2O

This is a reversible reaction (equilibrium). Esters have sweet, fruity smells and are used as flavourings, solvents, and in perfumes.

6.5 Combustion

Alcohols burn completely in excess oxygen to produce CO2 and H2O. Ethanol is used as a biofuel (carbon neutral in principle, as the CO2 released was absorbed by the plant during photosynthesis).

7. Organic Analysis

7.1 Mass Spectrometry

The molecular ion peak (M+) in a mass spectrum gives the relative molecular mass of the compound. The molecular ion is formed when the molecule loses one electron: M → M+ + e-.

Fragmentation patterns help identify the structure. Common fragment losses: 15 (CH3), 17 (OH), 29 (CHO or C2H5), 45 (OC2H5).

Exam Tip: You do not need to memorise exact wavenumbers — they are given in the data booklet. Focus on recognising the broad O-H absorptions (alcohols vs carboxylic acids) and the sharp C=O peak. The shape of the absorption is as important as its position.
