P1 — Physical Chemistry I

1. Atomic Structure

1.1 Fundamental Particles

Atoms consist of a nucleus containing protons and neutrons, surrounded by electrons in energy levels (shells).

ParticleRelative MassRelative ChargeLocation
Proton1+1Nucleus
Neutron10Nucleus
Electron1/1836 (negligible)-1Shells/orbitals
Atomic (proton) number (Z): The number of protons in the nucleus of an atom. This defines the element.
Mass number (A): The total number of protons and neutrons in the nucleus of an atom.

Number of neutrons = A - Z. In a neutral atom, the number of electrons equals the number of protons.

1.2 Isotopes

Isotopes: Atoms of the same element with the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons. They have the same atomic number but different mass numbers.

Isotopes have identical chemical properties because they have the same electron configuration, but different physical properties (e.g. density, rate of diffusion) due to their different masses.

1.3 Mass Spectrometry

A mass spectrometer measures the relative masses and abundances of isotopes or molecules. The stages are:

  1. Ionisation: The sample is vaporised and bombarded with high-energy electrons, knocking off an electron to form positive ions: X(g) → X+(g) + e-
  2. Acceleration: Ions are accelerated by an electric field so they all have the same kinetic energy.
  3. Deflection: Ions are deflected by a magnetic field. Lighter ions and ions with higher charges are deflected more.
  4. Detection: Ions reach the detector, producing a current proportional to their abundance.

Relative atomic mass (Ar) is calculated from the mass spectrum:

Ar = Σ(isotopic mass × relative abundance) / Σ(relative abundance)
Exam Tip: When calculating Ar from a mass spectrum, remember to divide by the total abundance (often 100 if percentages are given). Show all working clearly.

1.4 Electron Configuration

Electrons occupy orbitals within sub-shells within shells. The main energy levels (shells) are numbered n = 1, 2, 3, etc. Each shell contains sub-shells:

Shell (n)Sub-shells availableMax electrons
11s2
22s, 2p8
33s, 3p, 3d18
44s, 4p, 4d, 4f32

Each orbital holds a maximum of 2 electrons with opposite spins (Pauli exclusion principle). Sub-shell capacities: s = 2, p = 6, d = 10, f = 14.

The filling order follows the Aufbau principle: 1s, 2s, 2p, 3s, 3p, 4s, 3d, 4p, 5s, 4d...

Note that 4s fills before 3d, but when transition metal atoms are ionised, 4s electrons are removed first.

Exam Tip: Chromium is [Ar] 3d5 4s1 and copper is [Ar] 3d10 4s1 — half-filled and fully-filled d sub-shells are more stable. Learn these exceptions.

1.5 Ionisation Energies

First ionisation energy: The energy required to remove one mole of electrons from one mole of gaseous atoms to form one mole of gaseous 1+ ions.
X(g) → X+(g) + e-

Factors affecting ionisation energy:

  • Nuclear charge: More protons = greater attraction = higher IE
  • Atomic radius (distance): Greater distance from nucleus = weaker attraction = lower IE
  • Shielding: More inner electron shells = greater shielding = lower IE

Trends:

  • Across a period: IE generally increases (increasing nuclear charge, same shielding, decreasing atomic radius).
  • Down a group: IE decreases (more shells, greater shielding, larger atomic radius outweigh increasing nuclear charge).

Anomalies across Period 2 and 3:

  • Be > B: Boron loses a 2p electron which is higher energy and easier to remove than beryllium's 2s electron.
  • N > O: Oxygen has a paired electron in 2p which experiences repulsion, making it easier to remove than nitrogen's unpaired 2p electron.

Successive ionisation energies show large jumps when electrons are removed from a new (inner) shell, providing evidence for electron shell structure.

2. Amount of Substance

2.1 The Mole and Avogadro's Constant

One mole: The amount of substance that contains 6.022 × 1023 particles (Avogadro's constant, NA). This is the number of atoms in exactly 12 g of carbon-12.
Relative atomic mass (Ar): The weighted mean mass of an atom of an element relative to 1/12 of the mass of an atom of carbon-12.
Relative molecular mass (Mr): The weighted mean mass of a molecule relative to 1/12 of the mass of an atom of carbon-12.

2.2 Moles, Mass, and Molar Mass

n = m / M

Where n = number of moles, m = mass in grams, M = molar mass in g mol-1.

2.3 Moles and Gas Volumes

At room temperature and pressure (RTP: 298 K, 101.3 kPa), one mole of any gas occupies approximately 24.0 dm3.

n = V / 24.0   (at RTP, V in dm3)

2.4 The Ideal Gas Equation

pV = nRT

Where p = pressure (Pa), V = volume (m3), n = moles, R = 8.314 J K-1 mol-1, T = temperature (K).

Exam Tip: Unit conversions are critical: 1 kPa = 1000 Pa; 1 dm3 = 0.001 m3 = 1 × 10-3 m3; 1 cm3 = 1 × 10-6 m3. Temperature must be in Kelvin: K = °C + 273.

2.5 Moles in Solutions

n = c × V

Where n = moles, c = concentration (mol dm-3), V = volume (dm3). If V is in cm3, divide by 1000.

2.6 Empirical and Molecular Formulae

Empirical formula: The simplest whole number ratio of atoms of each element in a compound.
Molecular formula: The actual number of atoms of each element in one molecule of a compound. It is a whole number multiple of the empirical formula.

To find the empirical formula from percentage composition or masses: divide each element's mass (or %) by its Ar, then divide all results by the smallest value to get the simplest ratio.

2.7 Balanced Equations and Calculations

A balanced equation shows the mole ratio (stoichiometry) of reactants and products. Steps for reacting mass calculations:

  1. Write the balanced equation.
  2. Calculate moles of the known substance.
  3. Use the mole ratio to find moles of the unknown substance.
  4. Convert moles to the required quantity (mass, volume, concentration).

2.8 Percentage Yield and Atom Economy

Percentage yield = (actual yield / theoretical yield) × 100%
Atom economy: A measure of how much of the reactant atoms end up in the desired product.
Atom economy = (Mr of desired product / Σ Mr of all products) × 100%

High atom economy means less waste. Addition reactions have 100% atom economy. Substitution and elimination reactions have lower atom economy because they produce by-products.

Exam Tip: Don't confuse percentage yield with atom economy. Yield depends on practical losses; atom economy depends on the type of reaction and is calculated from the equation alone.

3. Bonding

3.1 Ionic Bonding

Ionic bonding: The electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions, formed by the transfer of electrons from a metal to a non-metal.

Ionic compounds form giant ionic lattices. Properties:

  • High melting and boiling points (strong electrostatic forces in all directions).
  • Conduct electricity when molten or dissolved (ions free to move) but not when solid (ions fixed in lattice).
  • Often soluble in water (polar water molecules can surround and stabilise the ions).

3.2 Covalent Bonding

Covalent bond: A shared pair of electrons between two atoms, where the bonding pair is attracted to both nuclei.

A dative (coordinate) covalent bond is a covalent bond where both electrons in the shared pair come from the same atom. For example, in NH4+, the nitrogen donates a lone pair to H+.

3.3 Metallic Bonding

Metallic bonding: The electrostatic attraction between a lattice of positive metal ions and a sea of delocalised electrons.

Properties: high melting points, good conductors of electricity and heat (delocalised electrons carry charge and kinetic energy), malleable and ductile (layers of ions can slide over each other).

3.4 Shapes of Molecules — VSEPR Theory

Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion (VSEPR) theory: electron pairs around a central atom repel each other and arrange themselves as far apart as possible to minimise repulsion.

Lone pairs repel more than bonding pairs: LP-LP > LP-BP > BP-BP.

3.5 Electronegativity and Bond Polarity

Electronegativity: The ability of an atom to attract the bonding pair of electrons in a covalent bond towards itself.

Electronegativity increases across a period (increasing nuclear charge, decreasing atomic radius) and decreases down a group (increasing shielding and atomic radius).

A polar bond forms when atoms with different electronegativities share electrons unequally, creating partial charges (δ+ and δ-). A molecule is polar overall only if the dipole moments do not cancel out. Symmetrical molecules like CO2 and CCl4 have polar bonds but are non-polar overall.

3.6 Intermolecular Forces

London (dispersion) forces: Temporary, induced dipole-dipole interactions present in all molecules. Strength increases with the number of electrons (larger Mr) and surface area of contact.

Permanent dipole-dipole forces: Attractions between the δ+ end of one polar molecule and the δ- end of another. Stronger than London forces for molecules of similar size.

Hydrogen bonding: A special, strong type of dipole-dipole interaction that occurs when hydrogen is bonded to a highly electronegative atom (N, O, or F) with a lone pair. The lone pair on N, O, or F is attracted to the δ+ hydrogen on an adjacent molecule.

Hydrogen bonding explains:

  • The anomalously high boiling points of NH3, H2O, and HF compared to other hydrides in their groups.
  • The relatively low density of ice (hydrogen bonds hold water molecules in an open lattice structure).
  • The high surface tension of water.
Exam Tip: Hydrogen bonds are NOT between the H and the lone pair on the same molecule. They are between the δ+ H on one molecule and a lone pair on N, O, or F of another molecule. Draw them clearly as a dotted line between molecules.

4. Energetics

4.1 Enthalpy Changes

Enthalpy change (ΔH): The heat energy change in a reaction at constant pressure. Measured in kJ mol-1.
Standard conditions: 100 kPa pressure, a stated temperature (usually 298 K), substances in their standard states. Denoted by the θ symbol: ΔH°.

Exothermic reactions: ΔH is negative (heat released to surroundings, temperature rises). Endothermic reactions: ΔH is positive (heat absorbed from surroundings, temperature falls).

Enthalpy changeDefinition
ΔHf° (formation)The enthalpy change when one mole of a compound is formed from its elements in their standard states under standard conditions.
ΔHc° (combustion)The enthalpy change when one mole of a substance is completely burned in excess oxygen under standard conditions.
ΔHneut° (neutralisation)The enthalpy change when one mole of water is formed by the reaction of an acid with a base under standard conditions.
ΔHat° (atomisation)The enthalpy change when one mole of gaseous atoms is formed from the element in its standard state.

4.2 Calorimetry

The enthalpy change of a reaction in solution can be measured using a calorimeter (polystyrene cup):

q = mcΔT

Where q = heat energy (J), m = mass of solution (g), c = specific heat capacity of water (4.18 J g-1 K-1), ΔT = temperature change (K or °C).

Then: ΔH = -q / n, where n = moles of the limiting reagent.

Exam Tip: Sources of error in calorimetry: heat loss to surroundings, incomplete combustion, assumption that solution has the same density and specific heat capacity as water. An insulated cup and lid reduce heat loss.

4.3 Hess's Law

Hess's Law: The total enthalpy change for a reaction is independent of the route taken, provided the initial and final conditions are the same. This is a consequence of the law of conservation of energy.

Hess's law allows us to calculate enthalpy changes that cannot be measured directly. Two common routes:

Using enthalpies of formation:

ΔHr° = ΣΔHf°(products) - ΣΔHf°(reactants)

Using enthalpies of combustion:

ΔHr° = ΣΔHc°(reactants) - ΣΔHc°(products)
Exam Tip: Note the reversal: for formation data, it's products minus reactants. For combustion data, it's reactants minus products. Draw an energy cycle diagram if unsure.

4.4 Bond Enthalpies

Mean bond enthalpy: The average energy required to break one mole of a particular type of bond in the gaseous phase, measured over a range of different compounds.
ΔH = Σ(bond enthalpies broken) - Σ(bond enthalpies formed)

Breaking bonds is endothermic (requires energy). Making bonds is exothermic (releases energy). If more energy is released forming bonds than is required to break bonds, the reaction is exothermic overall.

Bond enthalpy calculations give approximate values because mean bond enthalpies are averages. The exact energy of a bond depends on the molecular environment. Calculations using Hess's law with formation or combustion data are more accurate.

Exam Tip: Bond enthalpy questions require all species to be in the gaseous state. Draw out all the bonds in the structural formulae of reactants and products, count every bond carefully, and don't forget double bonds count as one bond of that type (e.g. C=O in CO2 has two C=O bonds).
