1.5 Legal, Moral, Cultural and Ethical Issues

Computer Science — OCR — Component 01 (Computer Systems)

Introduction — Four Overlapping Lenses

Topic 1.5 is different from the rest of the H446 specification: there is almost no code, no hardware diagrams and no algorithmic complexity. Instead, you are expected to reason about the impact of computing on individuals, organisations, society and the environment — and to know a small number of UK Acts of Parliament by name and by year. Exam questions in this area are typically 6–10 mark extended-response questions which reward balanced arguments, named legislation and real-world examples.

Legal — required or forbidden by the law of a particular jurisdiction. Enforceable by courts; breach leads to fines or imprisonment. Objective and written down.
Moral — what an individual personally believes is right or wrong. Shaped by upbringing, religion and conscience. Subjective and internal.
Ethical — a shared, usually professional, set of principles for what is right or wrong in practice (e.g. medical ethics, the BCS Code of Conduct). Codified by a group but not law.
Cultural — accepted customs and expectations of a specific society. What is considered acceptable varies between countries and communities (e.g. privacy norms, acceptable speech, gender equality in the workplace).

These four categories overlap but are not identical. Something can be legal but immoral (mass data collection that complies with terms of service), illegal but widely considered moral (downloading an out-of-print textbook for a blind student), ethical but culturally uncommon (transparent algorithmic decisions), or cultural but unethical (workplace surveillance norms that violate dignity). A good exam answer identifies which lens is in play.

When a question asks for "moral and ethical issues", do not just list laws. Examiners are looking for reasoning about fairness, harm, autonomy and responsibility — i.e. the values behind the law, or the places where law is silent.

1.5.1 Computing-Related Legislation

The OCR specification lists four principal Acts you must know by name and year: the Data Protection Act 2018, the Computer Misuse Act 1990, the Copyright, Designs and Patents Act 1988, and the Regulation of Investigatory Powers Act 2000. You should also be aware of the Freedom of Information Act 2000 and the Equality Act 2010, and the more recent Online Safety Act 2023 and Investigatory Powers Act 2016.

Timeline of Major UK Computing Legislation

1. Data Protection Act 2018 (incorporating UK GDPR)

The Data Protection Act 2018 (DPA 2018) is the UK's implementation of the EU General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR). It regulates how personal data is collected, stored, processed and shared. Personal data is any information relating to an identified or identifiable living individual — names, addresses, IP addresses, cookies, biometric data, health records, online identifiers.

The Act replaced the Data Protection Act 1998 and came into force on 25 May 2018. After Brexit, the GDPR was retained as the UK GDPR, supplemented by the DPA 2018.

The Seven GDPR Principles

Data controllers must process personal data in accordance with seven binding principles:

  1. Lawfulness, fairness and transparency — there must be a lawful basis (consent, contract, legal obligation, vital interests, public task, or legitimate interests) and individuals must be told what you are doing with their data.
  2. Purpose limitation — data must be collected for specified, explicit and legitimate purposes and not further processed in an incompatible way.
  3. Data minimisation — collect only what is adequate, relevant and limited to what is necessary. Do not "just in case" collect birthdays to send a marketing card.
  4. Accuracy — data must be accurate and kept up to date. Inaccurate data must be corrected or erased without delay.
  5. Storage limitation — data must not be kept in a form that identifies individuals for longer than is necessary.
  6. Integrity and confidentiality (security) — appropriate technical and organisational measures must be taken against unauthorised processing, accidental loss, destruction or damage (encryption, access control, backups).
  7. Accountability — the controller must be able to demonstrate compliance with all six preceding principles (records of processing, DPIAs, training).

Data Subjects' Rights

Data subjects are the living individuals to whom personal data relates. Under the UK GDPR they have the following rights, which can be exercised free of charge:

  • Right of access (subject access request, SAR) — ask what data an organisation holds about them; controller must respond within one month.
  • Right to rectification — correct inaccurate or incomplete data.
  • Right to erasure ("right to be forgotten") — delete data no longer necessary, or that was unlawfully processed.
  • Right to restriction of processing — pause processing while a dispute is resolved.
  • Right to data portability — receive their data in a structured, commonly used, machine-readable format (e.g. CSV, JSON) to take to another provider.
  • Right to object — stop processing (especially for direct marketing or automated profiling).
  • Rights relating to automated decision making and profiling — not to be subject to a solely automated decision with legal or similarly significant effects, unless specific conditions apply; and to have human intervention.

Enforcement and Penalties

The Information Commissioner's Office (ICO) enforces the Act. The maximum fines are tiered:

  • Standard tier: up to £8.7m or 2% of global annual turnover (whichever is higher) — e.g. failure to keep records or notify a breach.
  • Higher tier: up to £17.5m or 4% of global annual turnover (whichever is higher) — e.g. breach of principles, rights violations, unlawful international transfers.

Organisations must notify the ICO of a personal data breach within 72 hours of becoming aware of it if there is a risk to individuals' rights and freedoms.

British Airways — ICO fine, 2020 (£20m)

In 2018, malicious code was injected into the BA website, skimming the payment-card details of approximately 400,000 customers. The ICO found BA had inadequate security measures (multi-factor authentication and monitoring were missing) — a breach of the integrity and confidentiality principle. Initial notice of intent was £183m (c. 1.5% of global turnover); this was reduced to £20m in 2020 partly due to COVID-19 economic impact on the airline. The fine demonstrated that Principle 6 (security) is not just good practice — it is a legal obligation.

Exemptions

The DPA 2018 contains narrow exemptions where principles or rights are disapplied, including:

  • National security (processing for MI5/MI6/GCHQ purposes)
  • Crime and taxation (police investigations, HMRC)
  • Journalism, literature and art (where publication is in the public interest)
  • Research, history and statistics (with safeguards such as pseudonymisation)
  • Legal privilege and judicial proceedings
Students often confuse the Data Protection Act (personal data of identifiable living people) with the Computer Misuse Act (unauthorised access/modification of any computer material). If the scenario is about someone's personal data being leaked or mishandled, the DPA is likely relevant. If it is about hacking a system, the CMA is likely relevant. Sometimes both apply — e.g. an attacker who hacks a database breaches the CMA, and the controller may separately have breached the DPA by failing to secure it.

2. Computer Misuse Act 1990

The Computer Misuse Act 1990 (CMA) criminalises unauthorised access to, and modification of, computer material. It was introduced after the 1988 case R v Gold and Schifreen, in which two hackers who broke into Prestel (BT's viewdata system) were acquitted because existing laws did not cover computer intrusion. The CMA was updated by the Police and Justice Act 2006 and significantly expanded by the Serious Crime Act 2015.

Key elements for every offence:

  1. The access/action is unauthorised (the accused did not have permission).
  2. The accused knew at the time it was unauthorised (mens rea).
  3. The accused caused a computer to perform a function (s.1) or did an act in relation to a computer (s.3).

R v Cuthbert, 2005 — "Tsunami donation site"

Following the December 2004 Boxing Day tsunami, security consultant Daniel Cuthbert donated to a disaster-relief appeal through an online portal. When no confirmation page appeared, he suspected the site was a phishing scam and ran a basic directory traversal test (appending ../../../ to the URL) to see whether the server filtered suspicious input. The host's intrusion detection system flagged him; he was traced, arrested and convicted under s.1 CMA. Cuthbert received a £400 fine and £600 costs. The case is often cited to illustrate that good intentions are not a defence under the CMA — authorisation is a strict requirement. It also fuels ongoing debate about whether the Act chills ethical (white-hat) security research.

WannaCry, May 2017 — NHS disruption

The WannaCry ransomware worm spread rapidly through networks that had not patched the Windows SMBv1 EternalBlue vulnerability (leaked from the NSA). It encrypted files and demanded Bitcoin. The NHS was heavily affected: an estimated 19,000 appointments cancelled, ambulances diverted, and some trusts reverted to paper records. In 2017, UK security researcher Marcus Hutchins accidentally activated a "kill switch" domain, halting propagation. The attackers (attributed to the Lazarus Group, North Korea) would be liable under s.3 / s.3ZA CMA. The episode illustrates how one act of unauthorised modification can impair critical national infrastructure — and shows why s.3ZA was added in 2015.

3. Copyright, Designs and Patents Act 1988

The Copyright, Designs and Patents Act 1988 (CDPA) gives automatic legal protection to the creators of original literary, dramatic, musical and artistic works, including computer software and databases. No registration is required — copyright exists the moment the work is fixed in a tangible form (saved to disk, written down, recorded).

What the Act Protects

  • Literary works — including source code and object code, technical documentation, novels, lyrics
  • Dramatic works — plays, screenplays, choreography
  • Musical works — compositions (separate copyright from sound recordings)
  • Artistic works — images, photographs, sculptures, graphic design, icons, UI artwork
  • Films and sound recordings
  • Broadcasts and typographical arrangements of published editions
  • Databases (where there is "substantial investment" — sui generis database right)

Infringement

Primary infringement (civil) occurs when a person, without permission, does any restricted act: copying, distributing, performing, showing, communicating to the public, or adapting the work. Secondary infringement (can be criminal) includes importing, selling or dealing in infringing copies, or providing premises or equipment for infringement.

Penalties range from damages and injunctions in civil cases to up to 10 years imprisonment for serious commercial copyright infringement (under the Digital Economy Act 2017 which increased online infringement penalties).

Fair Dealing / Permitted Acts

The CDPA contains narrow fair dealing exceptions. Unlike US fair use, these are a closed list:

  • Research and private study (non-commercial)
  • Criticism, review and quotation (with sufficient acknowledgement)
  • Reporting of current events (except photographs)
  • Caricature, parody or pastiche
  • Illustration for instruction (education, non-commercial)
  • Text and data mining for non-commercial research
  • Accessible copies for people with disabilities
  • Backup copies of legitimately owned software (s.50A)
  • Decompilation solely to achieve interoperability with another program (s.50B) — strict conditions; you may not use it to produce a competing program

Metallica v Napster, 2000 — Music Piracy

File-sharing service Napster let users swap MP3 files peer-to-peer. The band Metallica and rapper Dr. Dre sued, and the RIAA followed. Napster was found liable for contributory and vicarious copyright infringement and was forced into bankruptcy by mid-2002. The case shaped how UK and US courts treat platforms whose main use is infringement, and accelerated legitimate streaming (iTunes 2003, Spotify 2008). Under CDPA terms, Napster users were infringing by copying and distributing; Napster itself was liable for authorising infringement.

4. Regulation of Investigatory Powers Act 2000 & Investigatory Powers Act 2016

The Regulation of Investigatory Powers Act 2000 (RIPA) is the framework that governs interception of communications, directed surveillance, and the acquisition of communications data by UK public authorities (police, intelligence agencies, HMRC, some local councils). Parts of RIPA have been superseded by the Investigatory Powers Act 2016 (IPA), nicknamed the "Snooper's Charter".

Key Powers under RIPA / IPA

  • Interception of communications — opening post, phone tapping, reading emails/messages in transit. Requires a warrant signed by the Secretary of State and a Judicial Commissioner (the "double lock" under the IPA).
  • Communications data acquisition — obtaining metadata (who contacted whom, when, for how long). Lower threshold than interception.
  • Internet Connection Records (ICRs) — logs, retained for 12 months by ISPs, of which services or websites a device accessed (not full URLs). Required under the IPA 2016.
  • Bulk interception / bulk acquisition / bulk equipment interference — mass collection, usually by GCHQ, of data crossing international links; authorised for serious national-security purposes.
  • Equipment interference (hacking) — intelligence agencies may lawfully hack computers and phones, subject to warrants.
  • Encryption notices — authorities may require a "technical capability" to remove electronic protection (a highly controversial provision that critics argue undermines end-to-end encryption).

Safeguards

  • Investigatory Powers Commissioner's Office (IPCO) independently oversees use of powers.
  • Investigatory Powers Tribunal (IPT) hears complaints that powers were used unlawfully.
  • "Double lock" — most warrants need both ministerial approval and a Judicial Commissioner's approval.

Controversy — Mass Surveillance vs National Security

The RIPA / IPA debate is the clearest A-Level example of security vs privacy trade-offs. Supporters argue lawful surveillance is essential to detect terrorism, organised crime and child sexual exploitation, and that the "double lock" provides proportionate oversight. Critics — including Liberty, Big Brother Watch and the European Court of Human Rights — argue that bulk powers amount to mass suspicionless surveillance incompatible with Article 8 of the European Convention on Human Rights (right to private life), and that ICRs give a disturbingly detailed picture of an individual's life.

Edward Snowden, 2013 — NSA/GCHQ Revelations

Former NSA contractor Edward Snowden leaked classified documents to journalists Glenn Greenwald and Laura Poitras, revealing the scope of programmes such as PRISM (US collection from major tech firms), Tempora (GCHQ tapping fibre-optic cables at UK landing stations) and XKeyscore (NSA analytic tool). The revelations prompted global debate, court challenges to bulk surveillance, a redesign of tech-company encryption, and ultimately the passage of the Investigatory Powers Act 2016 (which both legalised and constrained many of the programmes disclosed). Snowden sought asylum in Russia and remains wanted in the US under the Espionage Act. Moral dimension: he broke the law but argued he served the public interest — a whistleblowing dilemma directly relevant to the BCS Code's "public interest" principle.

5. Freedom of Information Act 2000

The Freedom of Information Act 2000 (FOIA) gives any person a right of access to recorded information held by UK public authorities. This includes government departments, the NHS, state schools, universities, the police, and local councils. A request must be answered within 20 working days, with a fee only if the cost exceeds statutory limits (approximately £450).

Exemptions include national security, law enforcement, personal data (deferred to the DPA), commercial confidentiality, and information likely to cause "prejudice". The FOIA is the counterpart to the DPA: the DPA gives individuals rights over data about themselves; the FOIA gives everyone rights over information about public authorities. Investigative journalism and accountability campaigns (MPs' expenses scandal 2009, police use-of-force statistics) rely heavily on FOIA requests.

6. Equality Act 2010 — Relevance to Computing

The Equality Act 2010 consolidates earlier anti-discrimination law and lists nine protected characteristics (age, disability, gender reassignment, marriage/civil partnership, pregnancy/maternity, race, religion/belief, sex, sexual orientation). Computing relevance:

  • Accessibility — public-sector websites must comply with the Public Sector Bodies Accessibility Regulations 2018 (derived from the Equality Act) and WCAG 2.1 AA. Alt-text, keyboard navigation, sufficient colour contrast, screen-reader compatibility.
  • Algorithmic discrimination — automated systems (hiring, credit scoring, facial recognition) must not indirectly discriminate on protected characteristics.
  • Reasonable adjustments — employers must provide assistive technology where needed.

7. Online Safety Act 2023 (awareness)

The Online Safety Act 2023 places duties of care on "user-to-user" and search services to protect users (especially children) from illegal content and "legal but harmful" content. Ofcom is the regulator, with powers to fine up to £18 million or 10% of global turnover. Duties include risk assessments, age assurance, and takedown of priority illegal content (child sexual abuse, terrorism, fraud, revenge pornography). Controversial among privacy advocates for provisions that could require scanning of end-to-end-encrypted messages.

1.5.2 Moral and Ethical Issues

Beyond the letter of the law, computing raises profound questions about how we should use technology. The OCR specification asks students to understand ethical implications in several domains. A high-mark answer:

  1. Identifies the issue (what is being done and by whom).
  2. Weighs both sides — benefits and harms.
  3. References legislation or a code of conduct where relevant.
  4. Concludes with a reasoned judgement — not sitting on the fence.

Computing in the Workforce — Automation

Automation means substituting software or robotics for tasks previously done by humans. Three waves are evident:

  • Industrial automation — car-factory welding robots, pick-and-place machines, warehouse robotics (Amazon Kiva).
  • Retail/service automation — self-checkout, ATMs, chatbots, automated phone systems.
  • Cognitive automation (2020s) — generative AI (LLMs, image generators) performing writing, coding, design and analysis tasks previously reserved for degree-educated "knowledge workers".

Arguments For

  • Higher productivity → cheaper goods and services
  • Removes dull, dirty or dangerous work (e.g. robotic welding in extreme heat)
  • Consistency and 24/7 operation
  • Frees humans for creative, interpersonal work
  • New job categories (AI prompt engineers, robotics technicians)

Arguments Against

  • Short-term job displacement concentrated in specific sectors and regions
  • Widening inequality — capital owners benefit; displaced workers often cannot retrain
  • Loss of skills / tacit knowledge (e.g. handcraft trades)
  • Social effects — loss of dignity, community, purpose
  • Concentration of economic power in a few tech firms
  • Retraining costs often borne by taxpayer, not the automating company
Technological unemployment — unemployment caused by technological change that destroys jobs faster than new ones are created. Keynes warned of it in 1930; economists still dispute whether the 21st-century wave is qualitatively different from earlier transitions (agricultural → industrial → service → information).

Artificial Intelligence and Automated Decision Making

Machine-learning systems are increasingly used for consequential decisions: recruitment shortlisting, loan approvals, insurance premiums, criminal-risk scoring, medical triage, social-welfare eligibility, predictive policing. Ethical issues cluster around four themes:

1. Bias

Training data reflects historical human decisions, which often contain discrimination. Examples:

  • COMPAS (US criminal risk tool) — ProPublica's 2016 investigation found it wrongly flagged Black defendants as high-risk at nearly twice the rate of white defendants.
  • Amazon recruiting tool (2018) — scrapped after it was found to downrank CVs mentioning "women's" (e.g. "women's chess club"), because it had trained on a male-dominated 10-year dataset.
  • Facial recognition — NIST's 2019 study of 189 algorithms found higher false-positive rates for Black and Asian faces, and for women, than for white males.
  • Apple Card credit limits (2019) — couples reported very different limits for husband vs wife despite shared finances; investigation found no overt sex-based variable, but proxy features correlated.

2. Explainability

Deep-learning models are often "black boxes". The GDPR includes a limited right to explanation for solely-automated decisions with significant effects. This is hard to deliver for a 70-billion-parameter neural network; research fields such as interpretable ML and XAI (Explainable AI) attempt to bridge the gap.

3. Accountability

When an AI system causes harm, who is liable? The developer, the deployer, the user, the data supplier? Current UK/EU approaches treat AI as a tool — the deployer remains legally responsible — but the EU AI Act 2024 introduces risk-tiered obligations and may influence UK practice.

4. Power and Autonomy

Generative AI reshapes creative industries, journalism, education and public discourse. Concerns include deepfakes undermining trust in evidence, scale-industrial disinformation, erosion of teacher–student assessment integrity, and concentration of cutting-edge capability in a small number of firms controlling frontier compute.

Microsoft "Tay", March 2016 — AI gone wrong

Microsoft released Tay, a chatbot on Twitter designed to learn from conversation. Within 16 hours, coordinated trolls exploited Tay's online learning to make it post racist, antisemitic and sexually explicit tweets; Microsoft took it offline. The incident illustrates (a) the brittleness of models that learn live from untrusted inputs, (b) the need for content moderation and adversarial testing before deployment, and (c) that the deploying company retains moral and reputational responsibility even when "the AI" did the posting.

Uber self-driving fatality, Tempe AZ, March 2018

An Uber autonomous-test SUV in self-driving mode struck and killed pedestrian Elaine Herzberg, who was walking a bicycle across the road at night. The system's perception pipeline detected her but misclassified her repeatedly (vehicle → bicycle → unknown) and delayed braking. A safety driver was behind the wheel but was watching a streaming show. Prosecutors declined to charge Uber as a corporation; the safety driver pleaded guilty to endangerment (2023). The case raises core A-Level questions: who is liable when a self-driving car kills? The driver, the company, the sensor suppliers, the software engineers, the regulator? Jurisdictions are still converging on an answer.

Environmental Effects

Computing's environmental footprint has four main pressure points:

1. Energy Consumption

  • Data centres consumed roughly 1–1.5% of global electricity pre-AI-boom; figures for 2024–25 are rising sharply due to LLM training and inference.
  • Bitcoin mining alone consumes more electricity annually than some countries (Argentina-scale by some estimates). Proof-of-work's energy cost is the core target of critics; Ethereum's 2022 move to proof-of-stake cut its energy use by ~99.95%.
  • Mitigations: renewable-powered data centres (Google carbon-neutral since 2007, 24/7 clean-energy matching target 2030), liquid cooling, workload scheduling to follow renewable generation.

2. E-Waste

  • The UN reported 62 million tonnes of e-waste generated globally in 2022 — growing five times faster than recycling.
  • E-waste contains toxic metals (lead, cadmium, mercury) and valuable ones (gold, silver, copper, palladium).
  • Informal recycling in Agbogbloshie (Ghana), Guiyu (China, historically), and parts of Pakistan and India involves burning cables for copper, releasing dioxins and poisoning groundwater.
  • The UK Waste Electrical and Electronic Equipment (WEEE) Regulations 2013 require producers to fund collection and treatment; retailers must offer take-back.

3. Rare-Earth and Mineral Extraction

  • Cobalt (lithium-ion batteries) — ~70% comes from the Democratic Republic of Congo, with documented child labour and dangerous artisanal mining.
  • Lithium — extraction in the Atacama (Chile/Bolivia/Argentina) depletes groundwater in arid regions.
  • Rare-earth elements (neodymium, dysprosium) — processing concentrated in China; radioactive tailings; geopolitical leverage.
  • Ethical sourcing frameworks: EU Conflict Minerals Regulation, OECD Due Diligence Guidance, "Fairphone" business model.

4. Positive Environmental Effects of Computing

  • Smart grids balance renewable supply and demand, reducing fossil peaking.
  • Remote work (post-2020) cut commuter emissions; offset by increased home energy and cloud use.
  • Precision agriculture — sensors and drones reduce fertiliser, pesticide and water use.
  • Climate modelling — HPC simulations underpin IPCC assessments.
  • Digital documents — reduced paper demand (though the "paperless office" has been over-promised for decades).

Censorship and the Internet

Three distinct actors can censor:

State Censorship

  • Great Firewall of China — blocks Google, Facebook, Twitter, Wikipedia (partially), Western news; uses DNS poisoning, SNI filtering, deep packet inspection and mass TCP resets. Combined with domestic alternatives (Baidu, WeChat, Weibo) and the "50-cent army" of online commenters.
  • Russia — RuNet isolation exercises; blocking of Twitter/X and Meta platforms since 2022.
  • Iran, North Korea, Turkey, Myanmar — various models, from near-total isolation to targeted blocks during protests.
  • UK — court-ordered ISP blocking of piracy sites; age-verification under the Online Safety Act; ISP family-filter defaults.

Platform Moderation (Private Censorship)

Social platforms apply their own terms of service. Decisions to de-platform individuals, down-rank content, or apply "misinformation" labels shape public discourse. Defenders argue private firms have free-speech rights; critics argue a handful of platforms now function as the public square and should be regulated accordingly. The Online Safety Act 2023 formalises some moderation duties; Section 230 in the US shields platforms from liability for user content.

Net Neutrality

Net neutrality is the principle that ISPs must treat all internet traffic equally, without throttling or prioritising particular sources, destinations or applications. The UK/EU still broadly upholds it (Open Internet Regulation 2015/2120). The US Federal Communications Commission has oscillated — classified ISPs as common carriers (2015), reversed (2017), and partially restored rules (2024).

Without net neutrality, ISPs could charge Netflix extra for "fast-lane" delivery, potentially disadvantaging start-ups that cannot afford the toll.

Monitoring and Surveillance

Workplace Monitoring

  • Keystroke logging, screenshot capture, email scanning, webcam checks — widely used in remote-work era.
  • Legal under UK law if proportionate, necessary, and covered by a Data Protection Impact Assessment; must be transparent under GDPR Principle 1.
  • Excessive monitoring can breach Article 8 ECHR (privacy) and cause psychological harm.

Public-Space Facial Recognition

  • UK police trials (Metropolitan Police, South Wales Police) challenged successfully in Bridges v South Wales Police (Court of Appeal, 2020) on DPA/Equality grounds.
  • "Chilling effect" — the knowledge that one is being watched alters behaviour, potentially suppressing legitimate protest and free assembly.

The Surveillance Economy

Beyond the state, ad-tech firms construct detailed behavioural profiles from cookies, device fingerprints, location, and purchase histories. Targeted advertising funds much of the free web; the ethical question is whether users have meaningfully consented given pervasive dark patterns in cookie banners.

Cambridge Analytica / Facebook, 2018

A personality-quiz app (thisisyourdigitallife), run by academic Aleksandr Kogan, collected data not just from ~270,000 consenting users but — under Facebook's then-loose platform rules — from their friends, totalling ~87 million profiles. The data were passed to Cambridge Analytica and used to build psychographic models for targeted political advertising, reportedly in the 2016 US presidential campaign and the UK Brexit referendum. The ICO fined Facebook £500,000 (the pre-GDPR maximum; a 2018 GDPR fine would have been potentially billions). Facebook later settled a US FTC case for $5 billion. The case illustrates: DPA principles (lawfulness, transparency, purpose limitation), platform responsibility for third-party developers, and democratic legitimacy of microtargeting.

Piracy and Copyright Infringement

Digital piracy ranges from recreational file-sharing to industrial counterfeiting:

  • Music — Napster → LimeWire → BitTorrent → Spotify. Streaming subscriptions have reduced (but not eliminated) audio piracy.
  • Film and TV — BitTorrent, illegal IPTV ("Firestick streams"). Fragmentation of legal streaming services (Netflix, Disney+, Apple, HBO, Amazon, Paramount, Peacock) is widely blamed for resurgent piracy.
  • Software — cracked installers, keygens. Subscription SaaS (Office 365, Adobe CC) reduces but does not eliminate.
  • Academic publishing — Sci-Hub and Library Genesis distribute paywalled scholarly articles to researchers without institutional subscriptions. Illegal, widely used globally, and the subject of ongoing debate about open access.
  • Video games — Denuvo and similar DRM; console jailbreaks.

Ethical arguments

  • For piracy (moral): access inequality; obsolescence (a 1992 game unavailable for sale); archival purposes; protest against digital-locker DRM that stops reselling; academic freedom in the Global South (Sci-Hub).
  • Against piracy: creators deprived of income; smaller studios hit harder than majors; undermines investment in new works; breach of CDPA 1988.

Offensive Communications, Cyberbullying and Online Harms

The internet's global reach and anonymity make both harassment and hate speech easier. UK offences include:

  • Malicious Communications Act 1988 — sending indecent, grossly offensive or threatening communications.
  • Communications Act 2003, s.127 — grossly offensive, menacing or false messages via public electronic communications network.
  • Protection from Harassment Act 1997 — covers cyberstalking.
  • Online Safety Act 2023 — duty of care; criminalises cyberflashing and "false communications" intended to cause harm.

Cyberbullying among school-age children is strongly correlated with mental-health harm, including self-harm. Case law increasingly holds platforms partly accountable (Molly Russell inquest 2022, which found Instagram content "contributed" to a 14-year-old's suicide).

Cultural Issues and the Digital Divide

The digital divide is the gap between those with access to, and skills to use, modern digital technology and those without. It operates on three levels: access (hardware, broadband), skills (digital literacy) and outcomes (economic, educational, civic).
  • Geographic — rural UK broadband lags urban; Project Gigabit targets 85% gigabit coverage by 2025. Globally, sub-Saharan Africa still has mobile-internet penetration below 50%.
  • Economic — a laptop, smartphone and home broadband cost hundreds of pounds annually; UK "poverty-premium" sees the poorest paying more per GB.
  • Generational — older adults may struggle with banking apps as branches close.
  • Disability — Equality Act requires reasonable adjustments; not all services comply.
  • Linguistic — the web is still skewed toward English; LLMs are dramatically worse in low-resource languages.
  • Cultural acceptance — attitudes to privacy, surveillance, gambling and speech vary between cultures; a service designed in California may be inappropriate or illegal in Germany or Saudi Arabia.

COVID-19 sharply exposed the divide: pupils without devices or broadband fell behind during remote schooling, and the gap persisted post-pandemic.

Professional Conduct

Professional ethics bridge the gap between personal morality and the law. Two codes are worth knowing:

British Computer Society (BCS) Code of Conduct

The BCS is the UK's Chartered Institute for IT. Its Code of Conduct has four pillars that all members must uphold:

Crucially, Pillar 1 (Public Interest) takes precedence. A BCS member who discovers that their employer's software will endanger the public and is ordered not to disclose is ethically required to put public safety first — the classic whistleblowing duty.

ACM Code of Ethics

The Association for Computing Machinery (USA) publishes a broader, more detailed code (2018 revision) with seven general principles including "avoid harm", "be honest and trustworthy", "respect privacy", and "honour confidentiality". UK students should know it exists and is internationally influential, especially in academia.

Whistleblowing

Both codes recognise a duty to speak up. UK law (Public Interest Disclosure Act 1998) protects qualifying whistleblowers from dismissal. Famous computing-related whistleblowers:

  • Edward Snowden (2013) — NSA mass surveillance. US position: Espionage Act violation. Ethical position: highly contested.
  • Chelsea Manning (2010) — disclosed US military logs to WikiLeaks. Convicted under Espionage Act; sentence later commuted.
  • Frances Haugen (2021) — former Facebook product manager disclosed internal research showing Instagram harm to teenagers; testified to UK Parliament and US Senate. Operated within whistleblower-protection frameworks.

Volkswagen "Dieselgate", 2015 — Engineered Deceit

Volkswagen installed "defeat device" software in 11 million diesel cars. When the engine-control unit detected the regulatory steering/throttle pattern of a laboratory emissions test, it enabled a cleaner-running mode; in normal driving, NOx emissions were up to 40 times the legal limit. The US EPA uncovered it in 2015; VW paid over £25 billion in fines, settlements and buybacks. Several engineers were prosecuted (Oliver Schmidt, 7 years US prison). Ethical issues: engineers wrote code knowing its deceptive purpose — a direct breach of BCS Pillar 1 (public interest, environment) and Pillar 2 (integrity). "Just following orders" is not an acceptable defence under any major professional code.

Ashley Madison data breach, July 2015

Ashley Madison, a dating site for married people seeking affairs, was breached by "The Impact Team". 37 million user records were dumped online, including names, emails and partial card details. Consequences included two reported suicides, divorces, resignations, and lawsuits. Ethical issues interweave: the attackers claimed a moral cause (the site's "paid delete" feature was not honoured); victims' privacy was obliterated irrespective of personal morality about infidelity; and the company was found by the Australian/Canadian regulators to have inadequate security practices (breach of what would become GDPR Principle 6). The case is used in ethics lectures worldwide to distinguish legal (the attackers committed a crime; the site was legal), moral (private judgements on infidelity) and ethical (professional obligation to secure data) dimensions.

Extended Case Studies on Professional Conduct

Therac-25, 1985–1987 — Radiation overdoses

The Therac-25 was a computer-controlled radiotherapy machine. Six known accidents between 1985 and 1987 delivered massive radiation overdoses (up to 100 times the prescribed dose); three patients died and others suffered severe injuries. The root causes were a race condition in the control software (inherited from the older Therac-20 which had a hardware interlock to mask it), inadequate testing, and the manufacturer's initial denial. No formal independent code review had been conducted. The case is the canonical example in software engineering ethics courses of why Pillar 1 of the BCS Code — due regard for public health — must outrank commercial pressure. Modern medical-device standards (IEC 62304) and the UK MHRA regime descend directly from Therac lessons.

Post Office Horizon IT scandal, 1999–present

Between 1999 and 2015, the UK Post Office prosecuted over 900 sub-postmasters for theft and false accounting based on apparent shortfalls reported by its Horizon accounting software (built by Fujitsu). Many were bankrupted, imprisoned, or took their own lives. Horizon contained numerous bugs producing phantom shortfalls; Post Office management knew some of this and withheld evidence from the courts. The 2019 Bates v Post Office High Court ruling and subsequent 2024 public inquiry exposed systemic failures. Under the BCS Code, Fujitsu engineers who knew of the defects had a Pillar 1 duty to the public that arguably overrode their Pillar 3 duty to the employer. The case has prompted calls for a statutory duty of candour for software suppliers to the public sector, and for the reversal of a legal presumption (dating from 1999) that computer evidence is reliable unless proven otherwise.

Boeing 737 MAX MCAS, 2018–2019

Two crashes (Lion Air 610, October 2018; Ethiopian 302, March 2019) killing 346 people were traced to the Manoeuvring Characteristics Augmentation System (MCAS), flight-control software added to compensate for larger engines. MCAS could command repeated nose-down trim based on a single angle-of-attack sensor — violating basic redundancy principles — and pilots were not informed of its existence. Internal Boeing messages showed engineers had raised concerns; commercial pressure to avoid pilot-retraining costs won out. Boeing paid over $20 billion in fines, settlements and grounding costs. The ethical lesson: engineers' duty to "speak up" (BCS Pillar 1) is only meaningful if the organisational culture lets those voices be heard.

Frameworks for Ethical Reasoning

When evaluating a computing scenario, philosophers offer complementary frameworks:

FrameworkCore questionComputing example
Consequentialism / UtilitarianismDoes the action maximise overall wellbeing?Self-driving cars that reduce total deaths even if they shift who dies (trolley problem variants).
Deontology (Kant)Is the action consistent with a universal duty or rule? Does it respect persons as ends?Never use users' data in ways to which they could not reasonably consent — regardless of profit.
Virtue ethicsWhat would a person of good character do here?A developer acts with honesty and practical wisdom when disclosing a security vulnerability responsibly.

A-Level answers do not need to name these frameworks, but being able to deploy "but from a rights-based view..." or "a utilitarian would argue..." shows maturity worth a mark.

Emerging Issues (2020s)

Generative AI and Intellectual Property

Models such as GPT-4, Claude and Stable Diffusion were trained on massive scrapes of the web — including copyrighted text and images. Ongoing UK/US/EU litigation (Getty Images v Stability AI, New York Times v OpenAI, Authors Guild v OpenAI) will determine whether training constitutes infringement, fair dealing / fair use, or something requiring a new licensing regime. The UK government's 2024–25 consultation on a "text and data mining exception with opt-out" proved deeply controversial among artists.

Deepfakes and Synthetic Media

Realistic AI-generated video and voice create risks for fraud (CEO "voice clone" authorising a transfer), non-consensual intimate imagery, election interference, and evidentiary trust. The Online Safety Act 2023 criminalises sharing non-consensual sexual deepfakes; the UK government announced in 2024 intent to criminalise their creation as well.

Autonomous Weapons

Fully autonomous "lethal autonomous weapon systems" (LAWS) raise profound ethical questions. UN discussions have not produced a treaty; campaigns such as "Stop Killer Robots" argue meaningful human control must be legally required before a machine takes a human life.

Right to Repair

Manufacturers' serialisation, software locks and parts-pairing restrict independent repair and fuel e-waste. The UK Right to Repair regulations 2021 and EU Right to Repair Directive 2024 require spare-parts availability for many appliances; similar pressure is now focused on smartphones.

Synthesis — Applying the Four Lenses

When you see a scenario in the exam, systematically apply each lens:

LensQuestion to askExample answer tokens
LegalWhich Act(s) apply? Who is the data controller / defendant? What penalty?"The company breached Principle 6 of the UK GDPR, risking a fine up to 4% of global turnover under the DPA 2018."
MoralWhat would a reasonable individual think is right here? Whose autonomy is affected?"Although lawful, tracking employees' keystrokes is intrusive and may violate their personal sense of dignity."
EthicalWhat do professional codes say? What are the duties to public interest / employer / profession?"Under BCS Pillar 1 the engineer has a duty to disclose the flaw to protect public safety, even over employer objections."
CulturalWould this be viewed differently in another society? What expectations is the technology built on?"Facial recognition in shops is routine in parts of Asia but widely rejected in the UK/EU, reflecting differing cultural norms about public-space privacy."
Examiners reward balance. If asked "Discuss the ethical implications of X" (6–9 marks), structure your answer as: identify the issue → one benefit / argument for → one harm / argument against → reference to law or code → real-world example → reasoned conclusion. Avoid one-sided rants.

Worked Exam Example

Question: A supermarket deploys a new AI system that uses facial recognition at self-checkouts to estimate shoppers' ages for alcohol purchases and to flag "known shoplifters" against a private watch-list. Discuss the legal, moral and ethical issues raised. [9 marks]

Model answer skeleton:

  1. Legal — DPA 2018 / UK GDPR: biometric data is special category data under Article 9 and requires explicit consent or another lawful condition. A private watchlist faces additional accuracy, transparency and fairness concerns (Principles 1 and 4). The ICO has already issued reprimands for similar deployments (e.g. Southern Co-op, 2024).
  2. Legal — Equality Act 2010: if the system has higher false-positive rates for certain ethnicities (as NIST evidence suggests), the retailer could be liable for indirect discrimination.
  3. Moral: customers may feel their dignity violated by being treated as suspects by default; shoppers whose faces are falsely matched face public humiliation. Counterpoint: reduces retail shrinkage and prevents unlawful sale of alcohol to minors, protecting staff from confrontational ID checks.
  4. Ethical (BCS): engineers deploying the system are bound by Pillar 1 (public interest) to consider disparate-impact harms and by Pillar 2 to acknowledge the limits of their model's accuracy. An ethical deployment would include transparent signage, an appeal mechanism, bias testing, and an independent audit — not a black-box watchlist.
  5. Cultural: UK norms are sceptical of public facial recognition (compare China's widespread acceptance). The proportionality of surveillance in a supermarket context should reflect those local norms.
  6. Conclusion: The age-estimation feature could be acceptable with consent, minimal data retention, and no-ID fallback; the watchlist feature is likely unlawful without extensive safeguards and is hard to justify ethically given the risks of misidentification.

Further Worked Exam Example

Question: A student discovers that their college's online reporting form has a weakness allowing anyone with the URL pattern to view other students' submissions. They navigate to a few URLs to verify the issue, then email the IT department. Discuss the legal and ethical issues. [6 marks]

Model answer skeleton:

  1. Legal (CMA 1990): Visiting URLs they are not authorised to access is technically an offence under s.1 CMA (unauthorised access), even if no further use is made of the information — parallel to the Daniel Cuthbert case 2005. Good intent is not a defence.
  2. Legal (DPA 2018): The college may separately be in breach — exposed submissions are personal data, and failing to secure them breaches Principle 6 (integrity and confidentiality). They would have to notify the ICO within 72 hours if risk to subjects is likely.
  3. Ethical (BCS Pillar 1): Disclosing the flaw responsibly serves the public interest — all students benefit from the fix. An ethical researcher would document minimally and report promptly, not exfiltrate data.
  4. Moral: The student's intentions were benign, yet the action was unlawful — a classic "legal / moral" gap. A remedy would be establishing a formal responsible disclosure policy so ethical researchers have safe-harbour.
  5. Conclusion: The student acted morally but illegally. Better practice: report the suspected vulnerability to IT without verifying it beyond the minimum, or first obtain written authorisation to test.

  • Can you name all six core Acts with their years without looking?
  • Can you recite the seven GDPR principles?
  • Can you distinguish CMA s.1 / s.2 / s.3 / s.3ZA and give an example of each?
  • Can you explain copyright duration and one fair dealing exception relevant to software?
  • Can you summarise the four BCS pillars and state which has priority?

Final revision tip: legal/ethical questions reward vocabulary density. Practice reading your answers aloud and tallying named Acts, named principles, named case studies, and named professional codes. Answers with five named items typically outscore those with zero by a large margin — even if the argument quality is similar.
