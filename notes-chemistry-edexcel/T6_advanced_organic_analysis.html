6.1 Aromatic Chemistry — Benzene

Structure of Benzene

Benzene (C6H6) is a planar, hexagonal molecule. Kekule originally proposed alternating single and double C-C bonds, but this model has problems:

  • Benzene does not undergo addition reactions typical of alkenes (e.g. does not decolourise bromine water)
  • All six C-C bonds are the same length (140 pm), intermediate between a single bond (154 pm) and a double bond (134 pm)
  • The enthalpy of hydrogenation of benzene is less exothermic than expected for three isolated C=C bonds (by about 150 kJ mol−1), indicating benzene is more stable than the Kekule structure predicts
Delocalisation model: Each carbon in benzene is sp2 hybridised with one unhybridised p orbital perpendicular to the ring plane. The six p orbitals overlap sideways to form a delocalised π system of electrons above and below the ring plane. This delocalisation stabilises benzene (thermodynamic stability) and makes it resist addition reactions (kinetic stability).

Electrophilic Substitution of Benzene

Benzene undergoes electrophilic substitution rather than addition, because substitution preserves the stable delocalised π system. The general mechanism involves:

  1. An electrophile attacks the π electron ring
  2. A curly arrow from the π system to the electrophile forms a new C-E bond
  3. An intermediate carbocation (Wheland intermediate) forms, losing aromaticity temporarily
  4. A proton is lost to regenerate the aromatic ring (H+ leaves, restoring the delocalised system)

Nitration

C6H6 + HNO3 → C6H5NO2 + H2O

Conditions: concentrated HNO3 + concentrated H2SO4, 50°C (reflux). The electrophile is the nitronium ion, NO2+, generated by:

HNO3 + H2SO4 → NO2+ + HSO4− + H2O
Exam Tip: Temperature control is important in nitration. Above 50°C, dinitrobenzene may form. Below 50°C, the reaction is too slow. Always state the temperature in your answer.

Halogenation (Bromination)

C6H6 + Br2 → C6H5Br + HBr

Conditions: anhydrous AlBr3 or FeBr3 catalyst (Lewis acid / halogen carrier), reflux. The catalyst polarises Br2 to generate Br+ electrophile:

Br2 + AlBr3 → Br+ + AlBr4−

Friedel-Crafts Alkylation

Introduces an alkyl group onto the benzene ring using a halogenoalkane (e.g. CH3Cl) with an AlCl3 catalyst. The electrophile is a carbocation (CH3+).

C6H6 + CH3Cl → C6H5CH3 + HCl    (AlCl3 catalyst)

Friedel-Crafts Acylation

Introduces an acyl group (RCO-) using an acyl chloride (e.g. CH3COCl) with AlCl3 catalyst. The electrophile is the acylium ion (RCO+).

C6H6 + CH3COCl → C6H5COCH3 + HCl    (AlCl3 catalyst)

Acylation is preferred over alkylation in synthesis because: (1) only monosubstitution occurs (the acyl group deactivates the ring), whereas alkylation can give polysubstitution; (2) the product ketone can be reduced to the alkyl group if needed.

6.2 Aldehydes and Ketones

Aldehyde: Contains a -CHO group (carbonyl at the end of a chain).
Ketone: Contains a >C=O group (carbonyl within the chain).
Both contain the carbonyl group (C=O), which is polar (δ+ on C, δ− on O), making the carbon susceptible to nucleophilic addition.

Nucleophilic Addition

The C=O is attacked by nucleophiles. The key reaction is with HCN (hydrogen cyanide):

RCHO + HCN → RCH(OH)CN    (a hydroxynitrile / cyanohydrin)

Conditions: NaCN in dilute H2SO4 (generates HCN in situ — safer than using HCN directly). The nucleophile is CN−, which attacks the δ+ carbon of C=O. After CN− bonds to carbon, H+ from HCN protonates the resulting alkoxide to give the -OH group.

This reaction extends the carbon chain by one and creates a new chiral centre, producing a racemic mixture (the nucleophile can attack from either side of the planar carbonyl).

2,4-DNP Test (Brady's Reagent)

2,4-dinitrophenylhydrazine (2,4-DNP) reacts with any carbonyl compound (both aldehydes and ketones) to form a yellow/orange precipitate (a 2,4-dinitrophenylhydrazone). This confirms the presence of a C=O group. The precipitate can be recrystallised and its melting point measured to identify the specific carbonyl compound.

Exam Tip: 2,4-DNP tests for the presence of a carbonyl group (aldehyde OR ketone). It does not distinguish between them. Use Tollens' or Fehling's to distinguish aldehydes from ketones.

6.3 Carboxylic Acids and Esters

Carboxylic acids contain the -COOH functional group. They are weak acids (partially dissociate in water). They react with bases, carbonates, and metals to form salts.

Reactions of Carboxylic Acids

ReactionEquation
With NaOHRCOOH + NaOH → RCOONa + H2O
With Na2CO32RCOOH + Na2CO3 → 2RCOONa + H2O + CO2
Esterification with alcoholRCOOH + R'OH ⇌ RCOOR' + H2O (H2SO4 catalyst, reflux)

Esters

Esters have the general formula RCOOR'. They are formed by esterification (acid + alcohol) or by reaction of an acyl chloride with an alcohol (faster, goes to completion). Esters can be hydrolysed back to the acid and alcohol:

  • Acid hydrolysis: RCOOR' + H2O ⇌ RCOOH + R'OH (dilute H2SO4, reflux) — reversible
  • Base hydrolysis (saponification): RCOOR' + NaOH → RCOONa + R'OH (NaOH, reflux) — irreversible, goes to completion

6.4 Acyl Chlorides

Acyl chlorides (RCOCl) are very reactive derivatives of carboxylic acids. They react vigorously at room temperature with nucleophiles via nucleophilic addition-elimination:

NucleophileProductOther Product

ROH (alcohol)Ester (RCOOR)HCl
NH3Primary amide (RCONH2)HCl
RNH2 (amine)N-substituted amide (RCONHR)HCl

Acyl chlorides are preferred over carboxylic acids for ester synthesis because the reaction is faster, irreversible, and gives higher yields.

6.5 Amines

Amine: An organic compound derived from ammonia (NH3) by replacing one or more H atoms with alkyl/aryl groups. Primary (RNH2), secondary (R2NH), tertiary (R3N). The lone pair on nitrogen makes amines basic and nucleophilic.

Preparation of Amines

  • From halogenoalkanes: RX + excess NH3 → RNH2 + HX (heated in sealed tube, ethanol solvent). Excess NH3 is used to minimise further substitution.
  • Reduction of nitriles: RCN + 4[H] → RCH2NH2 (LiAlH4 in dry ether, or H2/Ni catalyst)
  • Reduction of nitrobenzene: C6H5NO2 + 6[H] → C6H5NH2 + 2H2O (Sn + conc. HCl, reflux, then NaOH)

Basicity of Amines

Amines act as Bronsted-Lowry bases by accepting a proton using their lone pair:

RNH2 + H2O ⇌ RNH3+ + OH−

Aliphatic amines are stronger bases than ammonia (alkyl groups are electron-donating, increasing electron density on N). Aromatic amines (e.g. phenylamine) are weaker bases than ammonia because the nitrogen lone pair is delocalised into the benzene ring, making it less available for proton acceptance.

6.6 Amino Acids and Proteins

Amino acid: A molecule containing both an amine (-NH2) group and a carboxylic acid (-COOH) group, usually on the same carbon (α-amino acid). At neutral pH, amino acids exist as zwitterions (H3N+-CHR-COO−).

Amino acids (except glycine) have a chiral carbon and exist as optical isomers. Proteins are formed by condensation polymerisation of amino acids, linked by peptide bonds (-CONH-).

H2N-CHR-COOH + H2N-CHR'-COOH → H2N-CHR-CO-NH-CHR'-COOH + H2O

Peptide bonds can be hydrolysed by heating with 6 mol dm−3 HCl under reflux for 24 hours.

6.7 Polymers

Addition Polymers

Formed from alkene monomers. The C=C opens and monomers join in a chain. No small molecule is lost. Examples: poly(ethene), poly(propene), PVC, PTFE. Non-biodegradable — persist in the environment.

Condensation Polymers

Formed when monomers with two functional groups join, releasing a small molecule (usually H2O):

  • Polyesters: Formed from a diol + dicarboxylic acid (or hydroxy acid). Ester links (-COO-). Example: PET (Terylene) from ethane-1,2-diol and benzene-1,4-dicarboxylic acid.
  • Polyamides: Formed from a diamine + dicarboxylic acid (or amino acid). Amide links (-CONH-). Example: nylon-6,6 from 1,6-diaminohexane and hexanedioic acid. Proteins are natural polyamides.

Condensation polymers are biodegradable (the ester or amide links can be hydrolysed). They can be broken down by hydrolysis with acid or base.

Recycling and Disposal

  • Mechanical recycling: Sorting, melting, remoulding (suitable for thermoplastics)
  • Chemical recycling: Breaking polymers back into monomers (hydrolysis of condensation polymers)
  • Energy recovery: Incineration to generate energy (produces CO2 and potentially toxic gases)
  • Biodegradable polymers: PLA (polylactic acid) and starch-based polymers decompose naturally

6.11 Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR) Spectroscopy

1H NMR (Proton NMR)

1H NMR: Detects hydrogen atoms in different chemical environments. The number of peaks indicates the number of distinct hydrogen environments. Chemical shift (δ, in ppm) indicates the type of environment. Integration (area under peak) gives the ratio of hydrogens. Splitting pattern reveals the number of neighbouring non-equivalent hydrogens.

Exam Tip: O-H and N-H protons do not usually show splitting and appear as broad singlets. They can be identified by the D2O shake test: add D2O to the sample, and the O-H/N-H peak disappears as the proton exchanges with deuterium.

13C NMR: Detects carbon-13 atoms in different chemical environments. The number of peaks equals the number of distinct carbon environments. No splitting occurs in proton-decoupled 13C spectra (standard mode). Chemical shift indicates the type of carbon environment.

6.12 Chromatography

Chromatography separates mixtures based on the different affinities of components for a stationary phase and a mobile phase.

  • Thin Layer Chromatography (TLC): Stationary phase = silica or alumina on a plate. Mobile phase = solvent. Used for quick identification and monitoring reactions. Rf value = distance moved by spot / distance moved by solvent front.
  • Column Chromatography: Stationary phase = silica/alumina packed in a column. Mobile phase = solvent. Used for separating and purifying mixtures. Components elute at different rates.
  • Gas Chromatography (GC): Stationary phase = high boiling point liquid on an inert solid in a heated column. Mobile phase = inert carrier gas (e.g. N2, He). Retention time identifies each component; peak area gives relative amount. Often coupled with mass spectrometry (GC-MS) for identification.
Exam Tip: In GC-MS, the gas chromatograph separates the components of a mixture and the mass spectrometer identifies each component by its fragmentation pattern. GC-MS is widely used in forensics, drug testing, and environmental analysis. Always mention both separation and identification when describing GC-MS.
