⚗ CP1: Determine the Molar Mass of a Volatile Liquid or Gas

Apparatus

  • Gas syringe (100 cm³) in an oven at known temperature, OR round-bottomed flask with foil cap (Dumas method)
  • Hypodermic syringe for injecting volatile liquid
  • Self-sealing rubber septum
  • Balance (to 0.001 g)
  • Thermometer, barometer (to record T and P)
  • Boiling water bath (for Dumas method)

Method (Gas Syringe)

  1. Place the gas syringe in an oven at a known temperature above the boiling point of the liquid.
  2. Record the temperature of the oven and the atmospheric pressure.
  3. Weigh the hypodermic syringe containing a small volume of volatile liquid.
  4. Inject the liquid through the septum into the hot gas syringe. It vaporises immediately.
  5. Re-weigh the hypodermic syringe. Mass of liquid = difference.
  6. Record the volume of gas in the gas syringe once it reaches thermal equilibrium.
  7. Use the ideal gas equation: pV = nRT, so n = pV/(RT). Then M = mass/n.

Safety

  • Volatile liquids are flammable — no naked flames, use electric oven.
  • Handle hypodermic syringe with care — sharp needle.
  • Oven is hot — use heat-resistant gloves.

Analysis

Calculate: n = pV/(RT), where p in Pa, V in m³, T in K, R = 8.314 J mol⁻¹ K⁻¹. Then M = mass/n.

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Gas syringe friction (underreads volume)SystematicTap syringe gently; use well-lubricated syringe
Temperature not uniform in ovenSystematicAllow time for equilibrium; place thermometer near syringe
Liquid not fully vaporisedSystematicEnsure oven temperature is well above boiling point
Vapour escaping around septumSystematicCheck seal is airtight before injecting
Ideal gas assumptionSystematicAcknowledged limitation; more valid at high T, low P
Exam Tip: Remember to convert all units: volume to m³ (1 cm³ = 1×10⁻⁶ m³), temperature to K, pressure to Pa. The ideal gas equation assumes no intermolecular forces, so results are approximate.

⚗ CP2: Acid-Base Titration (Finding Concentration)

Apparatus

  • 50 cm³ burette, clamp and stand
  • 25.0 cm³ pipette and pipette filler
  • Conical flasks (250 cm³)
  • White tile
  • Suitable indicator (methyl orange or phenolphthalein)
  • Standard solution and unknown solution

Method

  1. Rinse the burette with the acid to be used, then fill and record the initial reading to 0.05 cm³.
  2. Rinse the pipette with the alkali solution. Pipette exactly 25.0 cm³ of alkali into a clean conical flask.
  3. Add 2-3 drops of indicator.
  4. Place the flask on a white tile under the burette.
  5. Perform a rough titration — add acid fairly quickly until the indicator permanently changes colour. Record the final burette reading.
  6. Repeat with accurate titrations: add acid drop-wise near the expected end point.
  7. Obtain at least two concordant results (within 0.10 cm³).
  8. Calculate the mean titre and use n = cV to find the unknown concentration.

Safety

  • Acids and alkalis are corrosive — wear goggles.
  • Use a pipette filler, never mouth-pipette.

Analysis

Calculate: n(known) = c × V. Use stoichiometry to find n(unknown), then c(unknown) = n/V.

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Overshooting end pointSystematicAdd drop-wise near end; use half-drops
Parallax error reading buretteRandomRead at eye level; read bottom of meniscus
Not rinsing burette with correct solutionSystematicRinse with the solution to be measured
Wet conical flask diluting alkaliNot an errorDoesn't affect moles of alkali — no improvement needed
Exam Tip: A wet conical flask does NOT affect the result because it does not change the number of moles of alkali present. However, a wet pipette or wet burette WOULD cause errors because they dilute the solution and change the concentration.

⚗ CP3: Enthalpy of Neutralisation (Calorimetry)

Apparatus

  • Polystyrene cup with lid (calorimeter)
  • Thermometer (±0.1 °C) or temperature probe
  • Measuring cylinder (25 cm³ or 50 cm³)
  • 1.0 mol dm⁻³ HCl and 1.0 mol dm⁻³ NaOH
  • Stopwatch
  • Stirring rod

Method

  1. Measure 25 cm³ of 1.0 mol dm⁻³ NaOH into the polystyrene cup.
  2. Record the temperature every minute for 3 minutes to get a baseline.
  3. At minute 4, add 25 cm³ of 1.0 mol dm⁻³ HCl. Stir and replace lid.
  4. Record temperature every minute for at least 6 more minutes.
  5. Plot temperature vs. time. Extrapolate the cooling line back to the mixing time to find the true ΔT.
  6. Calculate: q = mcΔT (m = total mass = 50 g if density ≈ 1 g cm⁻³, c = 4.18 J g⁻¹ K⁻¹).
  7. Calculate moles of water formed: n = c × V for limiting reagent.
  8. ΔH(neutralisation) = -q/n in kJ mol⁻¹.

Safety

  • 1 mol dm⁻³ acid and alkali are irritants — wear goggles.
  • Exothermic reaction — handle with care.

Analysis

Plot: Temperature vs. time. Extrapolate to find ΔT. Expected ΔH(neutralisation) for strong acid + strong base ≈ -57.1 kJ mol⁻¹.

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Heat loss to surroundingsSystematicUse lid; extrapolation method; use vacuum flask
Assuming c = 4.18 and density = 1SystematicAcknowledged assumption for dilute solutions
Heat absorbed by cup and thermometerSystematicDetermine calorimeter constant; use temperature probe
Inaccurate volume measurementsRandomUse a pipette for more precise volumes
Exam Tip: The enthalpy of neutralisation for any strong acid + strong base is approximately the same (-57.1 kJ mol⁻¹) because the reaction is always H⁺ + OH⁻ → H₂O. Weak acids/bases give less exothermic values because energy is needed to ionise them.

⚗ CP4: Determine the Enthalpy of Combustion of an Alcohol

Apparatus

  • Spirit burner containing alcohol (e.g., methanol, ethanol, propan-1-ol)
  • Copper calorimeter (or metal can)
  • Thermometer (±0.5 °C)
  • Measuring cylinder (100 cm³)
  • Balance (to 0.01 g)
  • Draught shield
  • Stirring rod, clamp stand

Method

  1. Measure 100 cm³ of water into the copper calorimeter. Record initial temperature.
  2. Weigh the spirit burner (with cap on) and record mass to 0.01 g.
  3. Place the calorimeter above the spirit burner. Use a draught shield to minimise heat loss.
  4. Light the spirit burner and heat the water, stirring gently.
  5. When the temperature has risen by approximately 20 °C, extinguish the flame (replace cap).
  6. Record the final temperature and re-weigh the spirit burner.
  7. Calculate: q = mcΔT (m = 100 g, c = 4.18 J g⁻¹ K⁻¹). Mass of fuel burned = initial - final mass.
  8. Moles of fuel = mass / Mᵣ. ΔH(combustion) = -q/n in kJ mol⁻¹.
  9. Repeat for different alcohols and compare.

Safety

  • Alcohols are flammable — keep away from other sources of ignition.
  • Spirit burner flame can be hard to see — handle carefully.
  • Hot apparatus — use tongs/heatproof mat.

Analysis

Plot: ΔH(combustion) vs. number of carbon atoms — should show a linear trend (each extra CH₂ adds approximately the same amount of energy).

Compare: Experimental values with data book values — typically much lower due to heat loss.

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Heat loss to surroundings (dominant error)SystematicBetter insulation; draught shield; use bomb calorimeter
Incomplete combustion (soot/CO formed)SystematicEnsure adequate air supply; clean wick
Evaporation of alcohol from wickSystematicCap the burner promptly; weigh quickly
Heat absorbed by calorimeter/thermometerSystematicUse calorimeter constant; copper has low specific heat
Exam Tip: Experimental values are always less exothermic (smaller magnitude) than literature values. The main reason is heat loss to surroundings. Always explain that incomplete combustion also contributes. The trend across a homologous series is linear because each additional CH₂ group releases similar energy on combustion.

⚗ CP5: Rate of Reaction — Initial Rates Method (Iodine Clock)

Apparatus

  • Beakers or conical flasks
  • Measuring cylinders (multiple)
  • Stopwatch
  • Potassium iodate (KIO₃) solution, sodium bisulfite (NaHSO₃) solution, starch indicator
  • Distilled water
  • White tile

Method

  1. Prepare a table of volumes: vary concentration of one reactant (e.g., KIO₃) while keeping total volume constant by adding water.
  2. Add starch indicator to the flask containing the NaHSO₃ solution.
  3. Pour the KIO₃ into the bisulfite/starch mixture and start the stopwatch.
  4. Swirl to mix. Record the time for the blue-black colour to appear.
  5. Repeat for each set of volumes. Record all times.
  6. Calculate rate = 1/t for each experiment.
  7. Plot rate vs. [KIO₃] to determine the order with respect to that reactant.

Safety

  • Solutions are low hazard — wear goggles as standard.
  • Wash hands after use.

Analysis

Plot: Rate (1/t) vs. concentration. Straight line through origin = first order. Curve of increasing gradient = second order.

Plot: Rate vs. [X]² — if straight line, second order confirmed.

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Subjective colour change judgementRandomSame observer; use colorimeter
Temperature variation between experimentsSystematicThermostatted water bath
Delay starting timerRandomPractice; data logging
Inaccurate volume measurementsRandomUse burettes instead of measuring cylinders
Exam Tip: The iodine clock measures the time for a fixed (tiny) amount of I₂ to form. Since the extent of reaction is very small, this approximates the initial rate. Always state why total volume must be constant: to ensure fair comparison of concentrations.

⚗ CP6: Rate of Reaction — Continuous Monitoring (Gas Collection)

Apparatus

  • Conical flask with bung and delivery tube
  • Gas syringe (100 cm³)
  • Stopwatch
  • Measuring cylinder
  • Calcium carbonate (marble chips) and dilute HCl (or suitable gas-producing reaction)

Method

  1. Set up the conical flask connected via delivery tube to the gas syringe.
  2. Measure the acid volume using a measuring cylinder and pour into the flask.
  3. Add the marble chips, insert the bung quickly, and start the timer.
  4. Record the volume of CO₂ gas in the syringe every 15-30 seconds.
  5. Continue until the reaction is complete (no more gas produced).
  6. Repeat with different concentrations or particle sizes to investigate the effect on rate.

Safety

  • Dilute HCl is an irritant — wear goggles.
  • Gas syringe can be displaced by rapid gas production — clamp securely.

Analysis

Plot: Volume of gas vs. time. Initial rate = gradient of tangent at t = 0.

Compare: Different curves for different conditions (concentration, temperature, surface area).

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Gas leaks around bungSystematicCheck seal; use grease
Gas escaping before bung fittedSystematicWork quickly; invert flask method
Syringe stickingSystematicLubricate; tap gently
CO₂ dissolving in waterSystematicAcknowledged; less significant at higher gas volumes
Exam Tip: When comparing curves, if the same mass of excess solid is used with different acid concentrations: all curves reach the same final volume (same moles of CaCO₃). Higher concentration gives steeper initial gradient (faster rate) but same end point.

⚗ CP7: Qualitative Analysis (Flame Tests, Cation/Anion Tests)

Apparatus

  • Nichrome wire loop, Bunsen burner
  • Test tubes, test tube rack
  • Dilute NaOH, dilute NH₃
  • AgNO₃(aq), BaCl₂(aq), dilute HNO₃, dilute HCl
  • Limewater

Method

  1. Flame tests: Clean nichrome wire in conc. HCl and flame until colourless. Dip in sample, hold in flame, record colour.
  2. NaOH test: Add NaOH to sample — observe precipitate colour. Add excess — does it dissolve?
  3. NH₃ test: Add NH₃ to sample — observe precipitate. Add excess — Cu(OH)₂ dissolves to deep blue.
  4. Halide test: Acidify with HNO₃, add AgNO₃. White = Cl⁻, cream = Br⁻, yellow = I⁻.
  5. Sulfate test: Acidify with HNO₃, add BaCl₂. White precipitate = SO₄²⁻.
  6. Carbonate test: Add dilute HCl. Fizzing → pass gas through limewater → milky = CO₃²⁻.

Safety

  • AgNO₃ stains skin — gloves.
  • BaCl₂ is toxic — wash hands thoroughly.
  • Conc. HCl fumes — fume cupboard.

Analysis

Record: Tabulate all observations systematically. Match to known results to identify ions.

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Na contamination in flame testSystematicUse cobalt blue glass to filter yellow
Dirty wireSystematicClean until no flame colour visible
Not acidifying before AgNO₃ testSystematicAlways acidify with HNO₃ first
Colour judgementRandomCompare with known standards side by side
Exam Tip: Acidify with nitric acid (not HCl) before the halide test with AgNO₃ — HCl would add Cl⁻ ions and give a false positive. Similarly, acidify with HNO₃ before the sulfate test to remove carbonates (which would also give a white precipitate with Ba²⁺).

⚗ CP8: Electrolysis of Aqueous Solutions

Apparatus

  • Beaker (250 cm³)
  • Two carbon (graphite) electrodes or copper electrodes
  • DC power supply (6V) or battery
  • Connecting wires with crocodile clips
  • Ammeter (optional)
  • Various electrolyte solutions (CuSO₄, NaCl, H₂SO₄)
  • Test tubes for gas collection (inverted over electrodes)

Method

  1. Pour electrolyte solution into the beaker to a depth covering the electrodes.
  2. Place two electrodes in the solution, held apart by a clamp or electrode holder.
  3. Connect the electrodes to the DC power supply.
  4. Switch on and observe what happens at each electrode for 5-10 minutes.
  5. Test any gases produced: O₂ relights a glowing splint; H₂ gives a squeaky pop; Cl₂ bleaches damp litmus.
  6. Record observations at both electrodes.
  7. Repeat with different electrolytes and compare products.

Safety

  • Chlorine gas is toxic — work in fume cupboard when electrolyzing NaCl.
  • Do not touch electrodes while power is on.
  • CuSO₄ is harmful — avoid skin contact.

Analysis

Record: Observations at cathode and anode for each electrolyte. Identify products using gas tests or visual observation (e.g., copper deposits at cathode from CuSO₄).

Explain: Use electrode potentials and selective discharge rules to explain why certain ions are discharged preferentially.

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Gas dissolving before collectionSystematicUse inverted test tube filled with solution over electrode
Electrode contaminationSystematicClean electrodes before use; use fresh graphite rods
Products reacting with each otherSystematicUse a diaphragm to separate anode and cathode compartments
Variable currentSystematicUse an ammeter and rheostat to maintain constant current
Exam Tip: At the cathode (negative), cations are reduced — the least reactive metal ion or H⁺ is discharged first. At the anode (positive), anions are oxidised — halide ions are discharged in preference to OH⁻ (unless very dilute). With copper electrodes and CuSO₄, copper dissolves at the anode and deposits at the cathode — mass stays constant.

⚗ CP9: Find the Ksp of Calcium Hydroxide by Titration

Apparatus

  • Saturated calcium hydroxide solution (limewater — prepared in advance)
  • Filter funnel and filter paper
  • 25.0 cm³ pipette and filler
  • Burette (50 cm³) filled with standardised HCl (known concentration)
  • Conical flask, white tile
  • Methyl orange indicator

Method

  1. Prepare a saturated solution of Ca(OH)₂ by adding excess solid to distilled water, stirring, and leaving to reach equilibrium.
  2. Filter the solution to remove undissolved Ca(OH)₂. The filtrate is a saturated solution.
  3. Pipette 25.0 cm³ of the filtrate into a conical flask.
  4. Add 2-3 drops of methyl orange indicator.
  5. Titrate with standardised HCl from the burette until the indicator changes from yellow to orange/red.
  6. Repeat for concordant results.
  7. Calculate: Ca(OH)₂ + 2HCl → CaCl₂ + 2H₂O. Find [OH⁻], then [Ca²⁺] = [OH⁻]/2.
  8. Ksp = [Ca²⁺][OH⁻]² — substitute values.

Safety

  • Ca(OH)₂ is irritant — wear goggles.
  • HCl is corrosive — handle with care.

Analysis

Calculate: n(HCl) = c × V(titre). n(Ca(OH)₂) = n(HCl)/2. [Ca(OH)₂] = n/0.025. Then [Ca²⁺] = [Ca(OH)₂], [OH⁻] = 2[Ca(OH)₂]. Ksp = [Ca²⁺][OH⁻]².

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Solution not truly saturatedSystematicUse excess solid; allow time for equilibrium; temperature control
Undissolved solid passing through filterSystematicUse fine filter paper; filter twice if needed
CO₂ absorbed from air (forming CaCO₃)SystematicPrepare fresh; keep stoppered; filter just before use
Temperature not at 25 °C (Ksp varies with T)SystematicRecord temperature; use thermostatted water bath
Exam Tip: The stoichiometry is crucial: 1 mol Ca(OH)₂ reacts with 2 mol HCl. Since Ca(OH)₂ → Ca²⁺ + 2OH⁻, the [OH⁻] is twice the [Ca(OH)₂]. Ksp = [Ca²⁺][OH⁻]² — don't forget to square the hydroxide concentration.

⚗ CP10: Construct Electrochemical Cells and Measure EMF

Apparatus

  • Two beakers, metal electrodes (e.g., Zn, Cu, Fe, Mg)
  • 1.0 mol dm⁻³ metal salt solutions
  • Salt bridge (KNO₃)
  • High-resistance voltmeter, connecting wires, crocodile clips
  • Emery paper

Method

  1. Clean electrodes with emery paper.
  2. Set up two half-cells with metal electrodes in their respective ion solutions.
  3. Connect with salt bridge and wires to voltmeter.
  4. Record the EMF. Note which electrode is positive (cathode).
  5. Repeat with different metal combinations.
  6. Compare measured EMF with E°(cell) = E°(cathode) - E°(anode).

Safety

  • CuSO₄ and other metal salts may be harmful — gloves and goggles.
  • Dispose of solutions appropriately.

Analysis

Calculate: E°(cell) = E°(right) - E°(left). Plot measured vs. theoretical EMF — should be linear with gradient ≈ 1.

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Oxide layer on electrodesSystematicClean with emery paper just before use
Non-standard concentrationsSystematicPrepare accurately using volumetric flask
Voltmeter drawing currentSystematicUse high-resistance voltmeter
Salt bridge drying outSystematicPrepare fresh; ensure good contact
Exam Tip: The salt bridge completes the circuit by allowing ion migration, maintaining electrical neutrality in both half-cells. Without it, the cell voltage drops immediately to zero. Use KNO₃ because K⁺ and NO₃⁻ have similar ionic mobilities and do not react with common cell components.

⚗ CP11: Investigate How Temperature Affects Rate (Thiosulfate + HCl)

Apparatus

  • Conical flask, measuring cylinders
  • Sodium thiosulfate solution, dilute HCl
  • Paper with X mark
  • Stopwatch, thermometer
  • Water baths at different temperatures (20, 30, 40, 50, 60 °C)

Method

  1. Measure 25 cm³ Na₂S₂O₃ into a conical flask. Place in water bath to equilibrate to desired temperature.
  2. Record the temperature. Place the flask on the cross.
  3. Add 5 cm³ dilute HCl, start the timer.
  4. Look down from above. Stop the timer when the cross disappears due to sulfur precipitate.
  5. Repeat at different temperatures.
  6. Calculate rate = 1/t for each temperature.

Safety

  • SO₂ produced is toxic — well-ventilated room or fume cupboard.
  • HCl is irritant — goggles.
  • Hot water — care with higher temperature experiments.

Analysis

Plot: 1/t vs. temperature (°C) — shows exponential increase. For Arrhenius analysis: plot ln(1/t) vs. 1/T (K) — straight line, gradient = -Eₐ/R.

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Subjective end-point (cross disappearing)RandomSame observer; use light sensor
Temperature changing during reactionSystematicMaintain temperature in water bath
Human reaction timeRandomRepeat and average; use data logging
Inaccurate temperature measurementRandomUse digital thermometer; allow equilibrium time
Exam Tip: This is a classic Arrhenius experiment. A 10 °C rise roughly doubles the rate. For the Arrhenius plot, use T in Kelvin and plot ln(rate) vs 1/T. The gradient = -Eₐ/R, so Eₐ = -gradient × R. Remember R = 8.314 J mol⁻¹ K⁻¹.

⚗ CP12: Investigate Factors Affecting Kc

Apparatus

  • Test tubes, test tube rack
  • Cobalt(II) chloride solution
  • Concentrated HCl, distilled water
  • Water baths (ice, hot water)
  • Teat pipettes

Method

  1. Prepare cobalt chloride equilibrium mixture (purple at room temperature).
  2. Temperature effect: Place tubes in ice bath (shifts to pink — exothermic direction favoured) and hot water bath (shifts to blue — endothermic direction favoured). Only temperature changes Kc.
  3. Concentration effect (adding Cl⁻): Add conc. HCl — shifts to blue (but Kc unchanged, just Q adjusts to match Kc).
  4. Concentration effect (adding H₂O): Add water — shifts to pink (Q adjusts, Kc unchanged).
  5. Record all colour changes and explain using Le Chatelier's principle.

Safety

  • CoCl₂ is toxic and carcinogenic — wear gloves.
  • Conc. HCl — fume cupboard.

Analysis

Key distinction: Changing temperature changes Kc (and shifts equilibrium). Changing concentration shifts the position of equilibrium but does NOT change Kc (at constant temperature).

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Subjective colour assessmentRandomUse colorimeter for quantitative measurement
Temperature not maintainedSystematicKeep in water bath; check with thermometer
Equilibrium not re-establishedSystematicAllow sufficient time before recording
Uncontrolled volumes of reagent addedSystematicUse measured volumes with a pipette
Exam Tip: A common mistake is saying that adding HCl changes Kc. It does NOT — only temperature changes Kc. Adding HCl increases [Cl⁻], so Q < Kc temporarily, and the reaction shifts right until Q = Kc again. Heating an endothermic forward reaction increases Kc.

⚗ CP13: Prepare a Pure Organic Solid and Test Its Purity

Apparatus

  • Beaker, hot plate or Bunsen burner
  • Suitable solvent (e.g., water, ethanol)
  • Filter funnel and paper (for hot filtration)
  • Buchner funnel, filter flask, vacuum pump
  • Melting point apparatus (Thiele tube or digital)
  • Capillary tubes, watch glass, spatula

Method

  1. Dissolve the crude solid in the minimum volume of hot solvent.
  2. If insoluble impurities are present, filter the hot solution through a pre-warmed funnel (hot filtration).
  3. Allow the filtrate to cool slowly — crystals of the pure product form.
  4. Collect the crystals by vacuum (Buchner) filtration. Wash with a small volume of cold solvent to remove soluble impurities.
  5. Dry the crystals on filter paper or in a desiccator.
  6. Weigh to determine yield.
  7. Test purity by measuring the melting point. Pack crystals into a capillary tube and heat slowly.
  8. A pure substance has a sharp melting point matching the literature value. Impurities broaden and lower the melting point.

Safety

  • Hot solvents — risk of burns; use water bath heating for flammable solvents.
  • Organic solvents are flammable — no naked flames.
  • Glass capillary tubes are sharp — handle carefully.

Analysis

Record: Melting point range. A range of 1-2 °C indicates high purity. Compare with literature value.

Calculate: Percentage yield = (actual mass / theoretical mass) × 100.

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Product lost during transfersSystematic (low yield)Minimise transfers; scrape crystals carefully
Crystals not fully dry (lowers melting point)SystematicDry thoroughly in desiccator; press between filter papers
Heating too fast in melting point testSystematicHeat slowly (1 °C per minute) near expected mp
Too much solvent used (low yield)SystematicUse minimum volume to dissolve the solid
Exam Tip: Recrystallisation purifies by using the difference in solubility at different temperatures. The product must be more soluble in hot solvent and less soluble in cold. Washing with cold solvent removes soluble impurities without dissolving too much product. A sharp, correct melting point confirms purity.

⚗ CP14: Prepare a Pure Organic Liquid (Distillation/Reflux)

Apparatus

  • Round-bottomed flask, anti-bumping granules
  • Reflux condenser (vertical) for step 1
  • Distillation condenser (angled, Liebig) for step 2
  • Still head, thermometer adapter, thermometer
  • Collecting flask
  • Separating funnel (if liquid-liquid extraction needed)
  • Drying agent (anhydrous CaCl₂, Na₂SO₄, or MgSO₄)
  • Heat source

Method

  1. Reflux: Set up a vertical condenser on top of the round-bottomed flask. Heat the reaction mixture for the required time. Vapours condense and return to the flask — ensures complete reaction without losing volatile reagents.
  2. Distillation: Rearrange apparatus for distillation (angled condenser). Heat and collect the fraction at the correct boiling point.
  3. Purification: If the product is organic, use a separating funnel to wash with sodium carbonate (removes acid impurities), then with water.
  4. Drying: Add anhydrous drying agent (e.g., MgSO₄) to remove water. Filter off the drying agent.
  5. Redistillation: Distil again to obtain pure product at the expected boiling point.

Safety

  • Many organic reagents are flammable — use electric heating or water bath.
  • Never heat a sealed system.
  • Use a fume cupboard for volatile/toxic organic compounds.
  • When using a separating funnel, invert and open the tap to release pressure regularly.

Analysis

Record: Boiling point of the collected fraction. A sharp boiling point indicates purity.

Calculate: Percentage yield = (actual yield / theoretical yield) × 100. Atom economy for the reaction.

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Product lost during transfersSystematicMinimise apparatus transfers; rinse with small amounts
Incomplete reaction (not refluxed long enough)SystematicReflux for adequate time; use excess of cheaper reagent
Water contaminationSystematicUse adequate drying agent; dry apparatus
Wrong fraction collectedSystematicMonitor thermometer carefully; discard fore-run
Exam Tip: Reflux and distillation use the same condenser in different orientations: vertical for reflux (returns liquid to flask), angled for distillation (collects distillate). The purpose of reflux is to allow prolonged heating without loss of volatile substances. Always explain why each purification step is done.

⚗ CP15: Tests to Identify Functional Groups

Apparatus

  • Test tubes, water bath (60-70 °C)
  • Tollens' reagent (freshly prepared), Fehling's A+B
  • Bromine water, acidified K₂Cr₂O₇
  • 2,4-dinitrophenylhydrazine (2,4-DNP) solution
  • Na₂CO₃ solution, iodine/NaOH solution

Method

  1. Alkene (C=C): Add bromine water — decolourises.
  2. Aldehyde: Tollens' (silver mirror) or Fehling's (brick-red ppt).
  3. Carbonyl (aldehyde or ketone): 2,4-DNP — orange precipitate.
  4. Primary/secondary alcohol: Acidified K₂Cr₂O₇ — orange to green.
  5. Carboxylic acid: Na₂CO₃ — effervescence (CO₂).
  6. Methyl ketone / secondary alcohol with CH₃ adjacent: Iodoform test (I₂/NaOH) — yellow precipitate of CHI₃.

Safety

  • K₂Cr₂O₇ is carcinogenic — gloves, fume cupboard.
  • Tollens' forms explosive if stored — use immediately, wash away.
  • Bromine water is corrosive — fume cupboard, gloves.
  • 2,4-DNP contains methanol — flammable, toxic.

Analysis

Record: Positive/negative for each test. Use the pattern of results to identify the functional group.

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Contaminated equipmentSystematicClean all glassware thoroughly
Wrong temperature for testSystematicUse appropriate water bath; don't overheat
Insufficient reagentSystematicUse excess test reagent relative to sample
Misidentifying subtle colour changesRandomCompare with known positive and negative controls
Exam Tip: 2,4-DNP tests for any C=O (aldehyde or ketone). To distinguish aldehyde from ketone, follow up with Tollens' (positive for aldehyde only). The iodoform test is positive for CH₃CO- groups (methyl ketones and ethanol/secondary alcohols with adjacent CH₃). The yellow precipitate is triiodomethane (CHI₃).

⚗ CP16: Investigate pH Changes During Acid-Base Titrations

Apparatus

  • Calibrated pH meter or pH probe with data logger
  • Burette (50 cm³) with strong base (NaOH)
  • Beaker with measured volume of acid (strong or weak)
  • Magnetic stirrer
  • Buffer solutions for calibration

Method

  1. Calibrate the pH meter with buffer solutions (pH 4, 7, 10).
  2. Pipette 25.0 cm³ of acid into the beaker. Place pH probe in solution.
  3. Record the initial pH.
  4. Add NaOH from burette in 1 cm³ increments (smaller increments of 0.2 cm³ near the equivalence point).
  5. Stir, wait for stable reading, record pH and volume after each addition.
  6. Continue until pH levels off at a high value.
  7. Plot pH vs. volume of NaOH to produce the titration curve.
  8. Repeat with different acid/base combinations (strong/strong, weak/strong, strong/weak, weak/weak) to compare curve shapes.

Safety

  • NaOH and acids are corrosive/irritant — goggles and gloves.
  • pH probes are delicate — handle carefully.

Analysis

Key curve features: Strong acid + strong base: equivalence at pH 7, steep curve. Weak acid + strong base: equivalence at pH > 7, buffer region visible, half-equivalence pH = pKₐ. Strong acid + weak base: equivalence at pH < 7.

Determine: Suitable indicator has its end point within the steep section of the curve.

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
pH meter not calibratedSystematicCalibrate with two or three buffer standards
Large volume increments near equivalenceSystematicUse 0.1-0.2 cm³ additions near the steep region
pH not equilibrated before readingRandomWait for stable reading; use stirrer
CO₂ from air affecting pH of NaOHSystematicUse freshly prepared NaOH; keep stoppered
Exam Tip: Choose the indicator whose colour change range falls within the steep part of the titration curve. Phenolphthalein (pH 8-10) is suitable for weak acid/strong base. Methyl orange (pH 3-4.4) is suitable for strong acid/weak base. For strong/strong, either works because the steep section spans a wide pH range.
