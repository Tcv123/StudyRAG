3.1 Periodicity — Period 3

Periodicity: The repeating pattern of physical and chemical properties of elements across periods in the periodic table, as a consequence of repeating electron configurations.

Trends Across Period 3 (Na to Ar)

PropertyTrend Na → ArExplanation
Atomic radiusDecreasesIncreasing nuclear charge pulls electrons closer; same shielding (same shell)

ElectronegativityIncreases (Na to Cl)Increasing nuclear charge, decreasing atomic radius

Electrical conductivityHigh for Na, Mg, Al; none for Si→ArMetals have delocalised electrons; non-metals do not (Si is a semiconductor)

3.2 Group 1 — Alkali Metals

Group 1 metals (Li, Na, K, Rb, Cs) have one electron in their outer s sub-shell. They are very reactive metals that readily lose this electron to form M+ ions.

Trends Down Group 1

  • Atomic radius increases: Additional electron shells
  • Ionisation energy decreases: Outer electron is further from nucleus, more shielding
  • Reactivity increases: Easier to lose the outer electron
  • Melting point decreases: Metallic bonding weakens as ionic radius increases

Reactions of Group 1 Metals

With water: 2M(s) + 2H2O(l) → 2MOH(aq) + H2(g). Vigour increases down the group. Lithium fizzes gently, sodium melts into a ball and fizzes vigorously, potassium ignites with a lilac flame.

With oxygen: Lithium forms Li2O (oxide), sodium forms Na2O2 (peroxide), potassium forms KO2 (superoxide).

With chlorine: 2M(s) + Cl2(g) → 2MCl(s). All form white ionic chlorides.

3.3 Group 2 — Alkaline Earth Metals

Group 2 metals (Be, Mg, Ca, Sr, Ba) have two electrons in their outer s sub-shell. They form M2+ ions.

Trends Down Group 2

PropertyTrend Down GroupExplanation
Atomic radiusIncreasesMore electron shells
First ionisation energyDecreasesGreater distance, more shielding
ReactivityIncreasesEasier to remove outer electrons
Melting pointDecreases (general trend)Weaker metallic bonding

Reactions of Group 2

With water: M(s) + 2H2O(l) → M(OH)2(aq) + H2(g). Mg reacts very slowly with cold water (faster with steam). Ca fizzes steadily. Sr and Ba react vigorously.

With oxygen: 2M(s) + O2(g) → 2MO(s). All burn in oxygen with characteristic flame colours. The oxides are white solids.

Thermal Decomposition of Group 2 Carbonates and Nitrates

Group 2 carbonates decompose on heating: MCO3(s) → MO(s) + CO2(g). The thermal stability increases down the group (BaCO3 requires the highest temperature). This is because larger cations have lower charge density and are less able to polarise and weaken the carbonate ion.

Solubility Trends

Compound TypeSolubility Trend Down Group 2
Hydroxides M(OH)2Increases (Mg(OH)2 sparingly soluble, Ba(OH)2 soluble)
Sulfates MSO4Decreases (MgSO4 soluble, BaSO4 insoluble)
Exam Tip: The test for sulfate ions uses BaCl2(aq) with dilute HCl. A white precipitate of BaSO4 confirms sulfate is present. The HCl removes any carbonate ions that could also form a white precipitate with Ba2+.

3.4 Group 7 — Halogens

The halogens (F, Cl, Br, I) exist as diatomic molecules (X2) and have 7 electrons in their outer shell. They gain one electron to form X− ions or share one electron in covalent bonds.

Trends Down Group 7

PropertyTrendExplanation
Atomic radiusIncreasesMore electron shells
ElectronegativityDecreasesGreater atomic radius and shielding
Boiling pointIncreases (F2 and Cl2 gases, Br2 liquid, I2 solid)Stronger London forces with more electrons
Reactivity (as oxidising agents)DecreasesHarder to gain an electron as atom is larger
ColourDarkens: pale yellow → green → red-brown → purple/black—

Halogen Displacement Reactions

A more reactive halogen can displace a less reactive halide from solution. This is because the more reactive halogen is a stronger oxidising agent.

KCl(aq)KBr(aq)KI(aq)

Reactions of Halogens with NaOH

Cold dilute NaOH (disproportionation):

Cl2(aq) + 2NaOH(aq) → NaCl(aq) + NaClO(aq) + H2O(l)

Chlorine is simultaneously oxidised (0 to +1 in NaClO) and reduced (0 to −1 in NaCl). This is disproportionation.

Disproportionation: A reaction in which the same element is simultaneously oxidised and reduced.

Hot concentrated NaOH:

3Cl2(aq) + 6NaOH(aq) → 5NaCl(aq) + NaClO3(aq) + 3H2O(l)

Tests for Halide Ions

Add dilute HNO3 (to remove interfering ions) then AgNO3(aq):

Exam Tip: Always add dilute nitric acid before silver nitrate in the halide test. If you use HCl, the Cl− from HCl will give a false positive. Never use HCl or H2SO4 for this test.

3.5 Transition Metals

Transition metal: A d-block element that forms at least one stable ion with an incomplete d sub-shell. Scandium (Sc3+: [Ar] 3d0) and zinc (Zn2+: [Ar] 3d10) are d-block but not transition metals.

Electron Configurations of Transition Metals

Transition metals fill the 3d sub-shell after 4s. When forming ions, electrons are lost from 4s before 3d. For example: Fe = [Ar] 3d6 4s2, Fe2+ = [Ar] 3d6, Fe3+ = [Ar] 3d5.

Properties of Transition Metals

  • Variable oxidation states: Due to the small energy gap between 3d and 4s sub-shells. Common examples: Fe2+/Fe3+, Cu+/Cu2+, Mn ranges from +2 to +7.
  • Coloured compounds: Arise from d-d transitions. When ligands surround the metal ion, d orbitals split into two energy levels. Electrons absorb visible light to transition between levels. The colour seen is the complementary colour of the light absorbed.
  • Catalytic activity: Transition metals can act as catalysts because they can change oxidation state, providing an alternative reaction pathway. Examples: Fe in the Haber process, V2O5 in the Contact process, Mn in decomposition of H2O2.
  • Complex ion formation: Transition metal ions form complex ions with ligands.

Complex Ions and Ligands

Complex ion: A central metal ion surrounded by ligands, bonded by dative (coordinate) bonds from the ligand lone pairs to the metal ion.
Ligand: A species that donates a lone pair of electrons to a central metal ion to form a dative bond.
Coordination number: The total number of dative bonds from ligands to the central metal ion.

Ligand Substitution

Ligands can be exchanged in complex ions. Common substitution reactions with copper(II):

[Cu(H2O)6]2+ + 4Cl− ⇌ [CuCl4]2− + 6H2O

Blue solution → yellow-green solution. The coordination number changes from 6 to 4 because Cl− is a larger ligand than H2O.

[Cu(H2O)6]2+ + 4NH3 → [Cu(NH3)4(H2O)2]2+ + 4H2O

Pale blue → deep blue. Only 4 of the 6 water ligands are replaced (partial substitution).

Exam Tip: The chelate effect: polydentate ligands form more stable complexes than monodentate ligands. Replacing monodentate ligands with polydentate ones increases entropy (more free particles in solution), making ΔG more negative.
