T1 — Atomic Structure, Bonding and Energetics

1.1 Atomic Models and Fundamental Particles

The atom consists of a dense, positively charged nucleus containing protons and neutrons, surrounded by electrons in energy levels (shells and sub-shells). The development of atomic models progressed from Dalton's solid sphere, through Thomson's plum pudding model, to Rutherford's nuclear model, and finally Bohr's model with quantised energy levels.

Mass number (A): The total number of protons and neutrons in the nucleus of an atom.
Atomic number (Z): The number of protons in the nucleus, which defines the element.
Isotopes: Atoms of the same element with the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons.

Relative Atomic Mass and Isotopic Abundance

Relative atomic mass (Ar): The weighted mean mass of an atom of an element relative to 1/12th the mass of a carbon-12 atom.
Ar = Σ(isotopic mass × percentage abundance) / 100

Mass spectrometry is used to determine isotopic masses and their relative abundances. A mass spectrum shows peaks corresponding to each isotope, with the height of each peak proportional to its abundance. From these data, you can calculate Ar.

Exam Tip: When calculating Ar from a mass spectrum, always check whether abundances are given as percentages or as raw peak heights. If raw heights, divide each by the total of all heights first.

1.2 Electron Configuration

Electrons occupy atomic orbitals, which are regions of space where there is a high probability of finding an electron. Each orbital can hold a maximum of two electrons with opposite spins (Pauli exclusion principle).

Sub-shells and Orbitals

The order of filling follows the Aufbau principle: 1s, 2s, 2p, 3s, 3p, 4s, 3d, 4p, 5s, 4d, 5p, 6s, 4f, 5d, 6p... Electrons fill the lowest energy sub-shell first.

Hund's rule states that electrons occupy orbitals of the same sub-shell singly before pairing. Each singly occupied orbital has electrons with parallel spin.

Exam Tip: Remember the exceptions for chromium (Cr: [Ar] 3d5 4s1) and copper (Cu: [Ar] 3d10 4s1). Half-filled and fully-filled d sub-shells provide extra stability.

Electron Configuration Notation

Full notation writes out every sub-shell, e.g. iron: 1s2 2s2 2p6 3s2 3p6 3d6 4s2. Shorthand uses the previous noble gas core, e.g. [Ar] 3d6 4s2.

When transition metals form ions, electrons are removed from the 4s sub-shell before the 3d. For example, Fe2+ is [Ar] 3d6, not [Ar] 3d4 4s2.

1.3 Ionisation Energies

First ionisation energy: The energy required to remove one mole of electrons from one mole of gaseous atoms to form one mole of gaseous 1+ ions.
M(g) → M+(g) + e−

Factors Affecting Ionisation Energy

  • Nuclear charge: More protons = stronger attraction = higher IE
  • Atomic radius: Greater distance from nucleus = weaker attraction = lower IE
  • Shielding: More inner electron shells = greater shielding = lower IE
  • Sub-shell structure: Paired electrons in the same orbital repel, making them easier to remove

Trends in Ionisation Energy

Across a period: General increase due to increasing nuclear charge with the same shielding. Two notable drops occur: between Group 2 and Group 3 (the Group 3 element loses a p-electron, which is higher in energy than the s-electron of Group 2), and between Group 5 and Group 6 (Group 6 has a paired p-electron, easier to remove due to electron-electron repulsion).

Down a group: Decrease because atomic radius increases and shielding increases, outweighing the increase in nuclear charge.

Successive Ionisation Energies

Successive ionisation energies always increase because each subsequent electron is removed from an increasingly positive ion. A large jump occurs when an electron is removed from a new inner shell (closer to nucleus, less shielding), which reveals the number of outer-shell electrons and hence the group of the element.

Exam Tip: If asked to deduce the group of an element from successive IE data, look for the biggest jump between consecutive IEs. If the big jump is between the 3rd and 4th IE, the element is in Group 3.

1.4 Ionic Bonding

Ionic bonding: The electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions, formed by the transfer of electrons from a metal to a non-metal.

Ionic compounds form giant ionic lattices. Each ion is surrounded by ions of the opposite charge. The lattice structure gives ionic compounds high melting points, as a large amount of energy is needed to overcome the strong electrostatic attractions throughout the lattice.

Properties of Ionic Compounds

  • High melting and boiling points (strong electrostatic forces)
  • Conduct electricity when molten or dissolved (ions are free to move)
  • Do not conduct when solid (ions are fixed in position)
  • Often soluble in water (polar water molecules can surround and stabilise ions)
  • Brittle (displacement of layers causes like charges to align, causing repulsion)

1.5 Covalent Bonding

Covalent bond: A shared pair of electrons between two atoms, where the electrostatic attraction between the shared electrons and the nuclei of both atoms holds the atoms together.

Types of Covalent Bond

  • Single bond: One shared pair of electrons (sigma bond, σ)
  • Double bond: Two shared pairs (one σ + one π)
  • Triple bond: Three shared pairs (one σ + two π)
  • Dative (coordinate) bond: Both electrons in the shared pair come from one atom. Shown by an arrow from donor to acceptor. Example: NH4+, where NH3 donates a lone pair to H+.

Shapes of Molecules — VSEPR Theory

VSEPR (Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion): Electron pairs around a central atom arrange themselves as far apart as possible to minimise repulsion. Lone pairs repel more than bonding pairs.

1.6 Metallic Bonding

Metallic bonding: The electrostatic attraction between a lattice of positive metal ions (cations) and a sea of delocalised electrons.

Metallic bond strength increases with: (1) greater number of delocalised electrons per atom, and (2) smaller ionic radius. This explains why melting points increase from Na to Mg to Al across Period 3.

Properties of Metals

  • Electrical conductivity: Delocalised electrons can flow through the structure when a potential difference is applied
  • Thermal conductivity: Delocalised electrons transfer kinetic energy rapidly through the lattice
  • Malleability/ductility: Layers of ions can slide over each other without breaking bonds, as the sea of electrons adjusts
  • High melting points: Strong electrostatic attraction throughout the lattice (varies with charge and size of ions)

1.7 Intermolecular Forces

Intermolecular forces are the weak attractions between molecules. They determine physical properties such as boiling point, solubility, and viscosity.

London (Dispersion) Forces

Present in all molecules. Caused by temporary, instantaneous dipoles arising from random fluctuations in electron distribution. A temporary dipole induces a dipole in a neighbouring molecule. Strength increases with number of electrons (larger molecules/greater Mr) and with greater surface area of contact.

Permanent Dipole-Dipole Forces

Occur between polar molecules. The δ+ end of one molecule attracts the δ− end of another. Stronger than London forces alone for molecules of similar size.

Hydrogen Bonding

Hydrogen bond: A strong intermolecular force between a hydrogen atom bonded to a highly electronegative atom (N, O, or F) and a lone pair on another N, O, or F atom on a neighbouring molecule.

Hydrogen bonding explains the anomalously high boiling points of NH3, H2O, and HF compared to other hydrides in their groups. It also explains the relatively low density of ice (hydrogen bonds hold water molecules in an open lattice structure).

Exam Tip: When explaining boiling point trends, always name the specific intermolecular force, explain how it arises, and relate it to the energy needed to overcome those forces.

1.8 Electronegativity and Bond Polarity

Electronegativity: The ability of an atom to attract the bonding pair of electrons in a covalent bond towards itself.

Electronegativity increases across a period (greater nuclear charge, same shielding) and decreases down a group (greater atomic radius, more shielding). Fluorine is the most electronegative element.

A polar bond has an uneven distribution of electron density. If the molecule is symmetrical, individual bond dipoles may cancel, making the molecule non-polar overall (e.g. CO2, CCl4). If asymmetrical, the molecule has a net dipole moment (e.g. H2O, CHCl3).

1.9 Enthalpy Changes

Enthalpy change (ΔH): The heat energy change measured at constant pressure. Exothermic reactions have ΔH < 0 (heat released). Endothermic reactions have ΔH > 0 (heat absorbed).

Standard Enthalpy Changes

Enthalpy ChangeDefinition (per mole, standard conditions: 298 K, 100 kPa)
ΔcH° (combustion)Complete combustion of one mole of a substance in excess oxygen
ΔfH° (formation)Formation of one mole of a compound from its elements in their standard states
ΔneutH° (neutralisation)Reaction of an acid and base to form one mole of water
ΔatH° (atomisation)Formation of one mole of gaseous atoms from the element in its standard state

Calorimetry

In calorimetry experiments, the heat change is calculated using:

q = mcΔT

where q = heat energy (J), m = mass of solution (g), c = specific heat capacity (4.18 J g−1 K−1 for water), and ΔT = temperature change (K or °C).

To find the enthalpy change per mole, divide q by the number of moles of the limiting reagent:

ΔH = −q / n

The negative sign is included because if the solution temperature increases (exothermic), the enthalpy change of the reaction is negative.

Exam Tip: Common sources of error in calorimetry: heat loss to surroundings, incomplete combustion, assuming the specific heat capacity of the solution equals that of water, and assuming the density of the solution is 1 g cm−3.

1.10 Hess's Law

Hess's Law: The total enthalpy change of a reaction is independent of the route taken, provided the initial and final conditions are the same. This is a consequence of the law of conservation of energy.

Using Formation Enthalpies

ΔrH° = ΣΔfH°(products) − ΣΔfH°(reactants)

Using Combustion Enthalpies

ΔrH° = ΣΔcH°(reactants) − ΣΔcH°(products)

Note the reversal: for combustion data, reactants minus products (the opposite of formation data). This is because combustion enthalpies provide a route down from reactants and products to a common set of combustion products (CO2 and H2O).

Exam Tip: When drawing Hess's law cycles, always include state symbols and ensure arrows point in the correct direction. If you need to reverse a reaction, change the sign of ΔH.

1.11 Bond Enthalpies

Mean bond enthalpy: The average energy required to break one mole of a particular type of bond in the gaseous state, measured over a range of different compounds containing that bond.
ΔrH = Σ(bond enthalpies broken) − Σ(bond enthalpies formed)

Bond enthalpy calculations give approximate values because mean bond enthalpies are averages. The actual energy of a specific bond depends on the molecular environment. For example, the O-H bond in water has a different exact bond enthalpy from the O-H bond in ethanol.

Breaking bonds is endothermic (energy input required). Forming bonds is exothermic (energy released). If more energy is released forming new bonds than is required to break old bonds, the reaction is exothermic overall.

Exam Tip: Bond enthalpy calculations only apply to reactions where all species are in the gaseous state. If a question gives you a liquid product, you need additional data (enthalpy of vaporisation) for an accurate answer.
