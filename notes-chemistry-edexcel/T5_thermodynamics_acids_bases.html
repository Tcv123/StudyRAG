5.1 Born-Haber Cycles

Born-Haber cycle: An enthalpy cycle used to calculate lattice enthalpy indirectly using Hess's law. It combines atomisation enthalpies, ionisation energies, electron affinities, and enthalpy of formation.

Key Enthalpy Terms

Enthalpy TermDefinitionSign
ΔfH° (formation)Formation of 1 mol of compound from elements in standard statesUsually negative (exothermic)
ΔatH° (atomisation)Formation of 1 mol of gaseous atoms from element in standard stateAlways positive (endothermic)
IE (ionisation energy)Removal of 1 mol of electrons from 1 mol of gaseous atoms/ionsAlways positive (endothermic)
EA (electron affinity)Addition of 1 mol of electrons to 1 mol of gaseous atoms/ions. 1st EA is exothermic; 2nd EA is endothermic1st: negative; 2nd: positive
ΔLEH° (lattice enthalpy)Formation of 1 mol of ionic lattice from gaseous ions (exothermic definition) or the dissociation of 1 mol of ionic lattice into gaseous ions (endothermic definition)Depends on definition used
Exam Tip: Edexcel typically uses the exothermic definition of lattice enthalpy (formation of lattice from gaseous ions, so the value is negative). Check the wording carefully in the question — some mark schemes accept either convention if applied consistently.

Constructing a Born-Haber Cycle

For NaCl, the cycle connects the following steps:

  1. Elements in standard states: Na(s) + 1/2 Cl2(g)
  2. Atomisation of Na: Na(s) → Na(g) [ΔatH°]
  3. Atomisation of Cl: 1/2 Cl2(g) → Cl(g) [ΔatH°]
  4. First ionisation of Na: Na(g) → Na+(g) + e− [IE1]
  5. First electron affinity of Cl: Cl(g) + e− → Cl−(g) [EA1]
  6. Lattice enthalpy: Na+(g) + Cl−(g) → NaCl(s) [ΔLEH°]

By Hess's law: ΔfH° = ΔatH°(Na) + ΔatH°(Cl) + IE1(Na) + EA1(Cl) + ΔLEH°

Factors Affecting Lattice Enthalpy

Lattice enthalpy becomes more exothermic (larger magnitude) with:

  • Smaller ionic radii: Ions are closer together, so electrostatic attraction is stronger
  • Higher ionic charges: Greater charge means stronger attraction (e.g. MgO has a much larger lattice enthalpy than NaCl because both ions are 2+/2− rather than 1+/1−)

5.2 Enthalpy of Hydration and Solution

Enthalpy of hydration (ΔhydH°): The enthalpy change when 1 mol of gaseous ions is dissolved in sufficient water to form an infinitely dilute solution. Always exothermic (ion-dipole attractions form).
Enthalpy of solution (ΔsolH°): The enthalpy change when 1 mol of an ionic solid dissolves in sufficient water to form an infinitely dilute solution.
ΔsolH° = −ΔLEH° + ΔhydH°(cation) + ΔhydH°(anion)

(Using the exothermic lattice enthalpy convention, where ΔLEH° is negative, so −ΔLEH° is positive — representing breaking the lattice.)

A substance dissolves if the enthalpy of hydration of the ions is large enough to compensate for the energy needed to break the lattice. If ΔsolH is negative or only slightly positive, dissolving is energetically favourable or can be driven by entropy.

5.3 Entropy

Entropy (S): A measure of the disorder or number of ways of arranging energy in a system. Units: J K−1 mol−1. Greater disorder = higher entropy.

Entropy Trends

  • S(gas) >> S(liquid) > S(solid) — gases have the most disorder
  • Entropy increases with temperature (more energy arrangements)
  • Entropy increases when the number of moles of gas increases in a reaction
  • Dissolving a solid in a solvent generally increases entropy
  • More complex molecules have higher entropy than simpler ones

Entropy Change of a Reaction

ΔSsystem = ΣS°(products) − ΣS°(reactants)

If ΔS is positive, the system becomes more disordered. If ΔS is negative, the system becomes more ordered. Note that a reaction can still be spontaneous even with negative ΔS if it is sufficiently exothermic.

5.4 Gibbs Free Energy

Gibbs free energy (ΔG): A thermodynamic quantity that determines whether a reaction is thermodynamically feasible (spontaneous). A reaction is feasible when ΔG < 0.
ΔG = ΔH − TΔS

where ΔH = enthalpy change (J mol−1), T = temperature (K), ΔS = entropy change (J K−1 mol−1).

Exam Tip: Ensure units are consistent. If ΔH is in kJ mol−1 and ΔS is in J K−1 mol−1, convert ΔS to kJ K−1 mol−1 by dividing by 1000 before substituting into the equation.

Feasibility at Different Temperatures

ΔHΔSFeasibility
Negative (exo)PositiveFeasible at all temperatures (ΔG always negative)
Negative (exo)NegativeFeasible at low temperatures (when |ΔH| > |TΔS|)
Positive (endo)PositiveFeasible at high temperatures (when TΔS > ΔH)
Positive (endo)NegativeNever feasible (ΔG always positive)

The temperature at which a reaction becomes feasible (where ΔG = 0) can be found by rearranging:

T = ΔH / ΔS
Exam Tip: Thermodynamic feasibility (ΔG < 0) does not guarantee a reaction will occur. Kinetic barriers (high activation energy) may prevent it. Always state this limitation when asked to discuss feasibility.

5.5 Acids and Bases — Bronsted-Lowry Theory

Bronsted-Lowry acid: A proton (H+) donor.
Bronsted-Lowry base: A proton (H+) acceptor.
Conjugate acid-base pair: Two species that differ by one proton. When an acid donates a proton, it forms its conjugate base; when a base accepts a proton, it forms its conjugate acid.

Example: HCl + H2O → H3O+ + Cl−. HCl is the acid (donates H+), H2O is the base (accepts H+). Cl− is the conjugate base of HCl, H3O+ is the conjugate acid of H2O.

5.6 pH Calculations

pH = −log10[H+]     and     [H+] = 10−pH

pH of Strong Acids

For a strong monoprotic acid (e.g. HCl): [H+] = concentration of acid. So pH = −log[acid].

For a strong diprotic acid (e.g. H2SO4): [H+] = 2 × concentration of acid (assuming full dissociation of both protons).

pH of Strong Bases

Use the ionic product of water:

Kw = [H+][OH−] = 1.00 × 10−14 mol2 dm−6 at 25°C

Find [OH−] from the base concentration, then calculate [H+] = Kw / [OH−], then find pH.

5.7 Ka and pKa

Acid dissociation constant (Ka): The equilibrium constant for the dissociation of a weak acid in water. For HA ⇌ H+ + A−:
Ka = [H+][A−] / [HA]

A larger Ka means a stronger acid (greater degree of dissociation).

pKa = −log10Ka     and     Ka = 10−pKa

A smaller pKa means a stronger acid.

Calculating pH of a Weak Acid

For a weak acid HA with concentration c and Ka known, we assume [H+] = [A−] and [HA] ≈ c (since dissociation is small):

Ka = [H+]2 / c     so     [H+] = √(Ka × c)
Exam Tip: The assumptions [H+] = [A−] and [HA] ≈ c are only valid for weak acids. State these assumptions explicitly in calculations. They break down for very dilute solutions or moderately strong weak acids.

5.8 Buffer Solutions

Buffer solution: A solution that resists changes in pH when small amounts of acid or base are added. It consists of a weak acid and its conjugate base (or a weak base and its conjugate acid).

How Buffers Work

An acidic buffer contains a weak acid (HA) and its salt (e.g. NaA, providing A−):

  • When acid (H+) is added: The conjugate base A− reacts with H+ to form HA. This removes the added H+, so pH barely changes.
  • When base (OH−) is added: The weak acid HA reacts with OH− to form A− + H2O. This removes the added OH−, so pH barely changes.

Calculating the pH of a Buffer

Using the Ka expression: Ka = [H+][A−] / [HA], rearranging gives:

[H+] = Ka × [HA] / [A−]

Then pH = −log[H+]. The Henderson-Hasselbalch equation is an equivalent form:

pH = pKa + log10([A−] / [HA])
Exam Tip: When preparing a buffer, you can mix a weak acid with its sodium salt, or you can partially neutralise a weak acid with a strong base. In the latter case, calculate the moles of HA remaining and moles of A− formed.

Key Features of a Weak Acid-Strong Base Curve

  • Starts at a higher pH than a strong acid (only partially dissociated)
  • Buffer region: a relatively flat section before equivalence where pH changes slowly (the solution acts as a buffer)
  • Half-equivalence point: halfway to equivalence, [HA] = [A−], so pH = pKa
  • Equivalence point: pH > 7 (the salt of a weak acid and strong base is slightly basic)
  • Steep section around equivalence point is shorter than for strong acid-strong base

Indicators

Indicator: A weak acid (HIn) that has a different colour to its conjugate base (In−). The end point colour change occurs when pH = pKin ± 1. For a titration, choose an indicator whose pKin falls within the steep vertical section of the titration curve.
