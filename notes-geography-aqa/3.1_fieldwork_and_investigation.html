Fieldwork & Investigation (AQA Paper 3)

AQA Paper 3 assesses your ability to design, execute, and evaluate geographical investigations. You must demonstrate understanding of the entire enquiry process, from formulating a question through to critical evaluation of your findings. This section covers the methodological, statistical, and presentational skills required.

1. The Enquiry Process

Geographical enquiry: A systematic investigation that follows a structured process to answer a geographical question or test a hypothesis, using primary and/or secondary data.

The enquiry process follows a logical sequence from question formulation through to critical evaluation. Each stage builds on the previous one, and weaknesses at any stage can undermine the entire investigation.

Formulating Questions and Hypotheses

A good geographical question is specific, measurable, and rooted in geographical theory. Hypotheses should be testable statements that predict a relationship or pattern.

  • Null hypothesis (H0): States there is no significant relationship or difference (e.g., "There is no significant correlation between distance from the CBD and land values")
  • Alternative hypothesis (H1): States there is a significant relationship or difference (e.g., "There is a significant negative correlation between distance from the CBD and land values")
Exam Tip: Always frame your hypothesis as a testable, two-part statement (null + alternative). Examiners reward students who can clearly operationalise their variables and state the expected direction of the relationship.

Risk Assessment and Ethical Considerations

All fieldwork investigations must include a risk assessment identifying potential hazards (physical, environmental, social) and mitigation strategies. Ethical considerations include obtaining consent for questionnaires, respecting private property, minimising environmental impact, and ensuring data anonymity.

2. Sampling Techniques

Sampling: The process of selecting a subset of individuals, items, or locations from a larger population for study, since it is usually impractical to collect data from the entire population.

The choice of sampling technique directly affects the reliability and representativeness of your data. Each method has distinct advantages and limitations.

Sampling Method Description Advantages Disadvantages
Random Each member of the population has an equal chance of selection. Uses random number tables/generators to select sample points. Eliminates researcher bias; statistically valid May leave gaps in spatial coverage; requires a sampling frame
Systematic Samples are taken at regular intervals (e.g., every 10th house, every 50m along a transect). Good spatial coverage; easy to implement in the field May coincide with periodic patterns in data; not truly random
Stratified Population is divided into sub-groups (strata), then random samples are taken from each in proportion to their size. Ensures all sub-groups are represented; more representative than simple random Requires prior knowledge of the population; more complex to organise
Quota Researcher sets a target number for each sub-group and selects participants until each quota is filled. Quick and easy to conduct; useful for questionnaire surveys High researcher bias; not statistically representative
Opportunistic Samples are collected as and when the opportunity arises (convenience sampling). Very easy; useful when access is limited Highly biased; not representative; results cannot be generalised
Exam Tip: In Paper 3, you must justify your sampling technique by linking it to your specific investigation. State why your chosen method is appropriate for your study area, data type, and research question. Always acknowledge limitations.

3. Data Collection: Primary vs Secondary

Primary data: Data collected first-hand by the researcher through direct observation, measurement, or surveys in the field.
Secondary data: Data collected by someone else and made available for the researcher to use, such as census data, published statistics, maps, or satellite imagery.

Primary Data Collection Methods

Method Description Use Cases
Questionnaires Structured, semi-structured, or unstructured questions administered to participants Perception studies, quality of life, land use surveys
Field sketches Annotated drawings made in the field to record landscape features Coastal landscapes, urban morphology, geomorphological features
Measurements Quantitative recordings using instruments (e.g., clinometer, flowmeter, thermometer) River velocity, beach profiles, microclimate studies
Transect surveys Recording data along a predetermined line at regular intervals Vegetation transects, urban transects, beach profiles
Environmental quality surveys Bipolar or Likert scale assessments of environmental quality at different locations Urban regeneration studies, comparing residential areas
Photography Systematic photographic record of sites for comparison and analysis Repeat photography studies, land use change, evidence gathering

Secondary Data Sources

  • Census data (ONS) - population characteristics, deprivation indices
  • OS maps - topography, land use, settlement patterns
  • GIS datasets - Environment Agency flood maps, Index of Multiple Deprivation
  • Satellite imagery - Google Earth, Sentinel-2 for land use change
  • Climate data - Met Office records, historical weather data
  • Published academic studies - provide theoretical context and comparative data

4. Data Presentation Techniques

Effective data presentation communicates patterns, trends, and relationships clearly. The choice of presentation technique should be appropriate for the data type and the geographical patterns you wish to highlight.

Choosing Appropriate Presentation Methods

Data Type Suitable Methods Example
Discrete / categorical Bar chart, pie chart, pictogram Land use types in different zones
Continuous / time-series Line graph, histogram, climate graph River discharge over a year
Bivariate (two variables) Scatter graph, best-fit line Discharge vs. velocity at sites
Spatial / locational Choropleth, isoline, proportional symbol map, GIS overlay Deprivation index by ward
Qualitative / perception Word clouds, annotated photos, mental maps, radar charts Perception of place identity

GIS (Geographical Information Systems)

GIS: A computer-based system for capturing, storing, analysing, and displaying geographically referenced data. GIS enables the overlay of multiple data layers to identify spatial patterns and relationships.

GIS is increasingly expected in A-Level investigations. Key capabilities include:

  • Layering: Overlaying different datasets (e.g., flood risk zones + population density + infrastructure)
  • Spatial analysis: Buffer zones, nearest neighbour analysis, density mapping
  • Geocoding: Converting addresses/postcodes into map coordinates
  • Data visualisation: Choropleth maps, heat maps, 3D terrain models

5. Statistical Techniques

Statistical tests allow you to move beyond description to determine whether observed patterns are statistically significant or could have occurred by chance. The choice of test depends on the type of data and the relationship being investigated.

Measures of Central Tendency and Dispersion

Measure Formula / Method When to Use
Mean Sum of values / Number of values Normally distributed interval/ratio data
Median Middle value when ranked Skewed data or ordinal data
Mode Most frequently occurring value Categorical/nominal data
Range Maximum - Minimum Quick measure of spread
Interquartile range Q3 - Q1 Spread of middle 50%; less affected by outliers
Standard deviation σ = √[Σ(x - x̄)² / n] Precise measure of dispersion around the mean

Spearman's Rank Correlation Coefficient (rs)

Spearman's Rank: A non-parametric test that measures the strength and direction of the relationship between two sets of ranked data. The result (rs) ranges from -1 (perfect negative correlation) through 0 (no correlation) to +1 (perfect positive correlation).
rs = 1 - (6Σd²) / (n(n² - 1))

Where: d = difference between paired ranks, n = number of paired observations.

Exam Tip: When using Spearman's Rank, always: (1) state your hypotheses clearly, (2) show your working in a table format, (3) state the critical value and significance level (typically 0.05 for 95% confidence), and (4) explain what the result means in geographical terms. A common error is forgetting to deal with tied ranks (use the average of the tied positions).

Chi-Squared (χ²) Test

Chi-squared test: A statistical test used to determine whether there is a significant difference between observed frequencies and expected frequencies in categorical data. It tests the association between two categorical variables.
χ² = Σ [(O - E)² / E]

Where: O = observed frequency, E = expected frequency.

Conditions for use:

  • Data must be in frequencies (counts), not percentages
  • No expected frequency should be less than 5
  • Data must be independent (each observation in only one category)
  • Sample size should ideally be at least 20

Degrees of freedom: (number of rows - 1) x (number of columns - 1)

Worked Example: You survey 100 people in a town about their preferred transport mode, categorised by age group. Chi-squared tests whether the distribution of transport preferences differs significantly between age groups. If χ² calculated > χ² critical at 0.05 significance level, you reject the null hypothesis and conclude there is a significant association between age and transport preference.

Mann-Whitney U Test

Mann-Whitney U: A non-parametric test that determines whether there is a significant difference between two independent samples. It compares the medians of two groups when data is ordinal or when interval/ratio data does not meet the assumptions for a parametric test.
U1 = n1n2 + [n1(n1 + 1) / 2] - R1

Where: n1 and n2 = sample sizes, R1 = sum of ranks for sample 1.

When to use:

  • Comparing two independent groups (e.g., environmental quality scores in two different areas)
  • Data is at least ordinal
  • Samples are independent of each other
  • You want to test for a difference (not a relationship)

Choosing the Right Statistical Test

Purpose Test Data Requirements
Correlation between two variables Spearman's Rank At least ordinal; paired data; minimum ~10 pairs
Difference between observed and expected frequencies Chi-squared Nominal (categorical) frequencies; no E < 5
Difference between two independent groups Mann-Whitney U At least ordinal; two independent samples
Measure of central tendency Mean, median, mode Depends on data distribution and type
Measure of spread Standard deviation, IQR Interval/ratio data (SD); ordinal+ (IQR)

Significance Levels and Confidence

In geography, the standard significance level is 0.05 (5%), meaning there is a 95% probability that the result did not occur by chance. Some investigations use stricter levels:

  • 0.05 (5%) - Standard; 95% confidence (most common in A-Level)
  • 0.01 (1%) - Strict; 99% confidence (used for stronger claims)
  • 0.10 (10%) - Lenient; 90% confidence (sometimes used with small samples)

6. Analysis and Conclusions

Data Analysis Skills

Analysis involves describing, explaining, and interpreting patterns in your data. A strong analysis should:

  • Describe the patterns shown by your data presentation (what does the graph/map show?)
  • Identify anomalies and outliers, suggesting possible explanations
  • Link to geographical theory - use established models and concepts to explain patterns
  • Cross-reference primary and secondary data to support or challenge findings
  • Use statistical results to provide evidence for or against your hypothesis

Drawing Conclusions

Conclusions must directly address the original research question or hypothesis. They should:

  • State clearly whether the hypothesis is supported or rejected
  • Summarise the key findings with reference to specific data evidence
  • Relate findings back to geographical theory and wider context
  • Acknowledge the limitations of the conclusion
  • Suggest how findings compare to or extend existing geographical knowledge

7. Evaluation

Evaluation is a critical component of Paper 3 and must demonstrate mature, reflective thinking about the strengths and weaknesses of the entire investigation.

Key Areas to Evaluate

Aspect Questions to Consider
Methodology Was the sampling method appropriate? Was the sample size sufficient? Were there better methods available?
Data collection Were measurements accurate? Was there observer bias? Were conditions consistent?
Data presentation Were the chosen techniques appropriate? Did they effectively communicate the findings?
Statistical analysis Were the correct tests used? Were the assumptions met? Was the sample size adequate for the test?
Conclusions Are findings generalisable? How representative was the study area? What further research is needed?

Accuracy, Reliability, and Validity

Accuracy: How close measurements are to the true value. Affected by instrument precision and human error.
Reliability: Whether the investigation would produce the same results if repeated under the same conditions. Improved by larger sample sizes and standardised methods.
Validity: Whether the investigation actually measures what it claims to measure. A study can be reliable but not valid if it consistently measures the wrong thing.
Exam Tip: In your evaluation, avoid simply listing problems. Instead, explain the specific impact each limitation had on your results and suggest concrete improvements. For example, do not just say "the sample size was small" - explain that with only 15 data pairs, the Spearman's critical value was high (0.521), making it harder to achieve significance, and that a larger sample of 30+ would reduce the critical value and improve statistical power.

8. Fieldwork Investigation Examples

Physical Geography Example - River Study: Investigating how channel characteristics change downstream along a river. Hypothesis: channel width, depth, and velocity increase with distance downstream (supporting the Bradshaw Model). Methods include systematic sampling at 10 sites using ranging poles, metre rulers, hydroprop/float method, and clinometers. Data presented using scatter graphs with Spearman's Rank to test for significant correlations. Secondary data from EA river gauge data provides triangulation.
Human Geography Example - Changing Places: Investigating how perceptions of place vary between different groups within a locality. Hypothesis: long-term residents perceive the area more positively than newcomers. Methods include stratified questionnaires using Likert scales, semi-structured interviews, and environmental quality surveys (EQS) at multiple sites. Data presented using radar diagrams, divergent bar charts, and annotated photographs. Mann-Whitney U test used to compare perception scores between the two groups. Secondary data from census ward profiles and Index of Multiple Deprivation supports the analysis.
Exam Tip: Paper 3 questions often ask you to evaluate unfamiliar fieldwork scenarios. Practise applying your methodological knowledge to new contexts. Think about what sampling method, data presentation, and statistical test would be most appropriate for any given investigation, and always justify your choices.
