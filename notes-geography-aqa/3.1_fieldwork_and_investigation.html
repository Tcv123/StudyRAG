Fieldwork & Investigation (AQA Paper 3)

AQA Paper 3 assesses your ability to design, execute, and critically evaluate geographical investigations. You must demonstrate understanding of the entire enquiry process, from formulating a question through to evaluation. This section covers the methodological, statistical, and presentational skills required. You will answer questions on your own two fieldwork investigations (one physical, one human) and on an unfamiliar investigation presented in the exam.

1. The Enquiry Process

Geographical enquiry: A systematic investigation that follows a structured process to answer a geographical question or test a hypothesis, using primary and/or secondary data. The enquiry process is iterative — findings may prompt refinement of the question or methodology.

Formulating Questions and Hypotheses

A good geographical question is specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and rooted in geographical theory. It should focus on a relationship, pattern, or process that can be investigated with available data.

  • Null hypothesis (H0): States there is no significant relationship, difference, or pattern. E.g. "There is no significant correlation between distance from the CBD and land values." This is the hypothesis you test statistically.
  • Alternative hypothesis (H1): States there IS a significant relationship, difference, or pattern. E.g. "There is a significant negative correlation between distance from the CBD and land values." This is what you expect to find based on theory.

Good questions often derive from geographical models or theories: Does the Bradshaw model apply to this river? Does the Burgess model explain land use patterns in this town? Does environmental quality decline with distance from a park?

Exam Tip: Always frame your hypothesis as a testable, two-part statement (null + alternative). Examiners reward students who clearly operationalise their variables (define exactly what they will measure and how) and state the expected direction of the relationship. Weak hypotheses are vague ("I think the river will change downstream"); strong hypotheses are specific and justified ("There is a significant positive correlation between distance downstream and channel width, because discharge increases with contributing area").

2. Sampling Techniques

Sampling: The process of selecting a subset of individuals, locations, or measurements from a larger population for study, since it is usually impractical (and unnecessary) to collect data from every possible point. The choice of sampling technique directly affects the reliability, representativeness, and statistical validity of results.

Exam Tip: In Paper 3, you MUST justify your sampling technique by linking it to your specific investigation. Do not just describe the method — explain WHY it was appropriate for your study area, data type, and research question. Always acknowledge limitations and explain how they might affect your results. E.g. "We used systematic sampling at 50 m intervals along the river to ensure even spatial coverage of the study reach. A limitation is that this may miss localised features between sample points."

3. Data Collection: Primary vs Secondary

Primary data: Data collected first-hand by the researcher through direct observation, measurement, or surveys in the field. You have control over methodology, timing, and accuracy, but collection is time-consuming and limited in scope.
Secondary data: Data collected by someone else and made available for use. Includes census data (ONS), published statistics, Ordnance Survey maps, satellite imagery (Google Earth), Environment Agency flood/river data, weather station records, IMD data, historical photographs. Allows access to large datasets and historical trends, but you have no control over collection methods and data may not precisely match your needs.

5. Statistical Analysis

Statistics help you move beyond description to identify significant patterns, relationships, and differences in your data. AQA expects you to understand and apply several key statistical techniques:

Measures of Central Tendency and Dispersion

  • Mean — sum of values / number of values. Affected by extreme values (outliers). Best for normally distributed data.
  • Median — middle value when ranked in order. Not affected by outliers. Better for skewed data.
  • Mode — most frequent value. Useful for categorical data.
  • Range — highest value minus lowest. Simple but influenced by outliers.
  • Interquartile range (IQR) — Q3 minus Q1 (middle 50% of data). More robust measure of spread, unaffected by outliers.
  • Standard deviation — measures average deviation from the mean. Low SD = data clustered close to mean; high SD = data spread widely. For normally distributed data, ~68% of values fall within 1 SD of the mean, ~95% within 2 SD.

Spearman's Rank Correlation Coefficient (rs)

Spearman's Rank: A non-parametric test that measures the strength and direction of the relationship between two ranked variables. Produces a value between -1 (perfect negative correlation) and +1 (perfect positive correlation). 0 = no correlation.
rs = 1 − (6∑d²) / (n(n² − 1))

Where d = difference between ranks for each pair, n = number of pairs. After calculating rs, compare it to the critical value table at the chosen significance level (usually 0.05 or 95% confidence) for your sample size. If |rs| > critical value, the correlation is statistically significant and you can reject the null hypothesis.

Interpreting rs: 0–0.2 = very weak, 0.2–0.4 = weak, 0.4–0.6 = moderate, 0.6–0.8 = strong, 0.8–1.0 = very strong. Remember: correlation does not prove causation.

Chi-Squared Test (χ²)

Chi-Squared: A test of association between two categorical variables. It compares observed frequencies with expected frequencies (what you would expect if there were no association). Used with nominal data in contingency tables.
χ² = ∑ (O − E)² / E

Where O = observed frequency, E = expected frequency (row total × column total / grand total). Compare the calculated χ² with the critical value from the table, using degrees of freedom = (rows − 1) × (columns − 1). If calculated χ² > critical value at the 0.05 significance level, the association is statistically significant.

Mann-Whitney U Test

Mann-Whitney U: A non-parametric test comparing two independent samples to determine if there is a significant difference between them. Used when data is ordinal or not normally distributed. E.g. comparing pebble sizes at two different beach locations, or EQI scores in two different streets.

Statistical Significance and Confidence Levels

  • Significance level (p-value) — the probability that the result occurred by chance. At p = 0.05 (5%), there is a 95% chance the result is genuine and a 5% chance it is due to random variation.
  • Rejecting H0 — if your test statistic exceeds the critical value at your chosen significance level, you reject the null hypothesis and accept the alternative hypothesis.
  • Degrees of freedom — a value calculated from sample size that determines which critical value to use. For Spearman's: df = n − 2. For chi-squared: df = (r − 1)(c − 1).
  • Confidence level — the complement of the significance level. At p = 0.05, the confidence level is 95%. At p = 0.01, it is 99%.
Exam Tip: When presenting statistical results, always: (1) state the test used and why, (2) state the calculated value, (3) state the critical value and significance level, (4) state the degrees of freedom or sample size, (5) state whether you accept or reject H0, and (6) explain what this means in geographical terms. E.g. "Spearman's rs = -0.87, which exceeds the critical value of 0.648 at p = 0.05 with n = 12. Therefore, I reject H0 and conclude there is a significant negative correlation between distance downstream and pebble size. This supports the Bradshaw model prediction that attrition reduces sediment size downstream."

6. Conclusions and Evaluation

Drawing Conclusions

  • Relate findings back to your original question/hypothesis — do your results support or contradict it?
  • Link findings to geographical theory or models — does your data support the Bradshaw model, Burgess model, or relevant theory?
  • Identify patterns, anomalies, and unexpected results — explain possible reasons for anomalies.
  • Consider alternative explanations for your findings.
  • Avoid overgeneralising from a small sample or limited study area.

Critical Evaluation

Evaluation is one of the highest-order skills assessed. You should critically assess:

  • Accuracy — how close were measurements to the true value? Equipment limitations (e.g. flow meters have ±5% accuracy), human error (reading scales incorrectly), environmental conditions (wind, rain affecting measurements).
  • Reliability — would repeating the investigation produce similar results? Did you take enough repeat measurements? Were conditions consistent across all sites?
  • Representativeness — can your findings be generalised to a wider area or population? Was your sample size large enough? Was your sampling strategy appropriate? Did you avoid spatial/temporal bias?
  • Validity — did you actually measure what you intended to measure? Did your methodology address your research question?
  • Data limitations — gaps in data, weather constraints, time restrictions, access issues, equipment availability, seasonal timing.
  • Improvements — what would you do differently? Larger sample size, more sites, different time of year, better equipment, additional data sources, longer study period.
  • Extensions — what further research questions arise from your findings? How could you build on this investigation?
Exam Tip: The evaluation section is where many students lose marks by being too superficial. Do not just write "I would increase my sample size." Instead, explain: "My sample size of 10 sites along a 3 km stretch was insufficient to capture the full downstream variation. Increasing to 20 sites at 150 m intervals would provide more robust data for statistical analysis (Spearman's rank requires a minimum of 10 pairs for meaningful results, and our n=10 was at this threshold). Additionally, collecting data in both summer and winter would reveal seasonal variations in discharge and velocity, which our single-day fieldwork could not capture."
Exam Tip: For Paper 3 Section B (the unfamiliar fieldwork investigation), you will be given a scenario and asked to critically evaluate someone else's methodology, data, and conclusions. Practice reading data presentation techniques, identifying strengths and weaknesses of methodologies, and suggesting improvements. The key skills are: interpreting graphs/maps/statistics you have not seen before, identifying sampling bias, assessing whether conclusions are supported by the data, and suggesting alternative methodologies.
Exam Tip: AQA expects you to demonstrate that your fieldwork is connected to the wider specification. For a river investigation, link to water cycle processes (infiltration, runoff, throughflow) and the drainage basin system. For an urban investigation, link to place identity, inequality, or sustainability. This synoptic connection lifts your evaluation from competent to excellent.

7. Spearman's Rank: Full Worked Example

Below is a complete worked example of Spearman's Rank Correlation, the most commonly required statistical test in AQA Geography Paper 3.

Research Question and Data

Research question: "Is there a significant relationship between distance downstream from the source and mean pebble size at a river in the Lake District?"

Null hypothesis (H0): There is no significant correlation between distance downstream and mean pebble size.

Alternative hypothesis (H1): There is a significant negative correlation between distance downstream and mean pebble size (pebbles become smaller with distance as attrition and abrasion reduce size).

Calculation

Interpreting the Result

rs = −1.0 is a perfect negative correlation (in this simplified example). In real fieldwork, rs values between −0.7 and −0.9 are typical for downstream pebble-size studies, indicating a strong negative correlation with some variation.

Critical value check: For n = 10 at p = 0.05 (95% confidence level), the critical value from Spearman's Rank table is 0.648. Since |−1.0| = 1.0 > 0.648, we reject H0.

Conclusion: There is a statistically significant negative correlation between distance downstream and mean pebble size (rs = −1.0, n = 10, p < 0.05). We accept H1. This supports the theoretical prediction that attrition and abrasion reduce pebble size with increasing distance downstream, consistent with the Bradshaw model's prediction of decreasing sediment particle size downstream.

Exam Tip: Note two important conventions when ranking: (1) When ranking data for Spearman's, rank 1 is assigned to the smallest value if you are correlating two variables that should both increase (or both decrease) together. Be consistent — decide before you start whether rank 1 = smallest or largest, and apply this consistently to BOTH variables. (2) For tied values: if two values are equal, assign them the average of the ranks they would have occupied (e.g. if values ranked 3rd and 4th are equal, both get rank 3.5). This must be done correctly or your rs calculation will be wrong.

Research Question and Observed Data

Research question: "Is there a significant association between proximity to a city park and residents' reported use of outdoor green space for exercise?"

H0: There is no significant association between proximity to the park and frequency of outdoor exercise.

H1: There is a significant association between proximity to the park and frequency of outdoor exercise.

Observed frequencies (from questionnaire survey, n = 120):

Expected frequency (E) for each cell = (Row total × Column total) / Grand total

  • Far from park, exercises frequently: (60 × 66) / 120 = 33

Step 3: Compare to Critical Value

Degrees of freedom = (rows − 1) × (columns − 1) = (2−1) × (2−1) = 1

Critical value at p = 0.05, df = 1: 3.84

Critical value at p = 0.01, df = 1: 6.63

Our calculated χ² = 10.91 > 6.63 (p = 0.01 critical value). Therefore we reject H0 at the 99% confidence level.

Conclusion: There is a highly significant association between proximity to the park and frequency of outdoor exercise (χ² = 10.91, df = 1, p < 0.01). We accept H1. People living within 300 m of the park are more likely to exercise outdoors 3+ times per week. This is consistent with the concept that accessible urban green space promotes physical activity and health.

Exam Tip: Chi-squared has strict requirements that examiners test: (1) data must be in frequencies (counts), not percentages or proportions — convert percentages back to counts if the total sample size is given; (2) no expected frequency should be less than 5 — if any cell has E < 5, you must either collapse categories or use an alternative test; (3) observations must be independent — each person/location should only appear in one cell. Violating these conditions invalidates the test. In your evaluation, mention any cells that approached the E < 5 threshold as a limitation.

Evaluating Secondary Data Sources: Key Questions

  • Who collected it, and why? A government report on immigration may undercount irregular migrants; a company's environmental performance data may be selectively positive. Identify the collector and their purpose.
  • When was it collected? The 2021 UK Census is the most recent but already 3 years old; the IMD was last updated in 2019 (using 2017–18 data). Areas may have changed substantially.
  • What methodology was used? Census data is comprehensive but self-reported (people may misreport). Police crime statistics undercount unreported crimes (typically only 40% of crimes are reported).
  • What are the spatial and temporal units? IMD data is at LSOA level (~1,500 people); averaging across larger areas may mask significant intra-area variation.
  • Are there gaps or known biases? The 2021 Census estimated 10% non-response in some London boroughs; LA-level data may mask significant neighbourhood variation.
  • Has it been published in a peer-reviewed or quality-assured context? ONS, EA, and academic journals are more reliable than tabloid news coverage or advocacy group statistics.
Exam Tip: In the Paper 3 unfamiliar investigation section, you will often be asked to evaluate secondary data sources. Apply the questions above systematically. Do not simply say "secondary data is less reliable than primary data" — this is too vague. Instead, identify SPECIFIC limitations of the specific secondary source presented (e.g. "The crime statistics used may undercount actual crime because many crimes go unreported; the researcher has no way of knowing the unreported crime rate in this area").

Box-and-Whisker Plots

Box-and-whisker plots (box plots) are particularly useful in geographical fieldwork for comparing the distribution and spread of two or more datasets. They display five summary statistics: minimum, lower quartile (Q1), median (Q2), upper quartile (Q3), and maximum.

Reading this box-and-whisker plot:

  • Site A (upstream) has a median pebble size of 68 mm and IQR of 25 mm (Q1=55, Q3=80), indicating a wide spread of pebble sizes.
  • Site B (downstream) has a median pebble size of 24 mm and IQR of 20 mm (Q1=15, Q3=35), indicating smaller pebbles with less variation.
  • The fact that the boxes (IQR) do not overlap is strong visual evidence of a significant difference in distributions between the two sites, consistent with downstream fining.
  • A Mann-Whitney U test could be used to formally test whether this difference is statistically significant.

Examiners reward specific, justified improvements. Avoid generic statements. For each improvement, explain:

  1. What you would change
  2. How specifically (the methodology)
  3. Why this would improve the investigation (the limitation it addresses)
  4. What you would expect to find differently (the predicted impact on results)

Example of a weak improvement statement: "I would collect more data."

Example of a strong improvement statement: "I would increase the number of transect sites from 8 to 20, spaced at 500 m intervals along the full 10 km study reach, rather than our current irregular spacing. This would improve the spatial representativeness of our dataset and provide a sufficient sample size (n=20) to detect moderate correlations in Spearman's Rank (critical value at n=20, p=0.05 is 0.450, compared to 0.738 at n=8). Currently, our irregular spacing means that a 3 km section of the middle reach is represented by only 2 data points, which may be insufficient to capture the full variation in that section."

Exam Tip: Paper 3 Section A (your own fieldwork) and Section B (unfamiliar investigation) both reward the same evaluation skills. For Section B, you are given an investigation you have not seen before and asked to evaluate its methodology. The skills are identical: identify what the researcher did, evaluate its strengths and limitations, suggest improvements, and interpret whether the conclusions are supported by the data. Practise by evaluating past-paper scenarios as well as your own fieldwork.
