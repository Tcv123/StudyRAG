1.2 Coastal Systems & Landscapes

1. Coasts as Systems

Coastal system: An open system where energy and material cross the boundary. The coast is shaped by the interaction of marine processes (waves, tides, currents), sub-aerial processes (weathering, mass movement), and human activity.

Key system components:

  • Inputs: wave energy, tidal energy, wind energy, sediment from rivers/cliff erosion, solar energy
  • Outputs: sediment removed by longshore drift, erosion products carried offshore, evaporation
  • Stores: beaches, sand dunes, spits, bars, mudflats, salt marshes
  • Transfers: longshore drift, onshore/offshore sediment movement, aeolian transport
Sediment cell (littoral cell): A largely self-contained stretch of coastline within which sediment movement occurs. England and Wales have 11 major sediment cells. Sediment rarely crosses cell boundaries. Understanding sediment cells is essential for coastal management.

2. Waves

Wave: An undulation in the water surface caused by wind friction transferring energy to the water. The size of a wave depends on wind speed, wind duration, and fetch (the distance of open water over which the wind blows).

Wave Characteristics

  • Wave height — vertical distance between trough and crest
  • Wave length — horizontal distance between successive crests
  • Wave frequency — number of waves per minute
  • Wave period — time between successive wave crests passing a fixed point
  • Wave steepness — ratio of wave height to wave length

Wave Refraction

When waves approach an irregular coastline (headlands and bays), they bend due to differential friction with the seabed. Waves slow down in shallower water near headlands, causing wave energy to concentrate on the headland. In bays, waves spread out and energy is dissipated, encouraging deposition.

3. Coastal Processes

Marine Erosion

  • Hydraulic action — the force of water and trapped air being compressed into cracks in the rock. The pressure exerted can widen joints and fractures.
  • Abrasion (corrasion) — rock fragments carried by waves scrape and wear away the cliff face, like sandpaper. Most effective during storms.
  • Attrition — rock fragments carried by the sea collide with each other, becoming smaller, smoother, and more rounded over time.
  • Corrosion (solution) — weak acids in seawater dissolve soluble rocks like limestone and chalk. The process is enhanced by CO2 in the water.

Sub-aerial Processes

  • Weathering — in-situ breakdown of rock:
    • Mechanical: freeze-thaw (water expands by 9% when frozen, widening cracks), salt crystallisation, wetting and drying
    • Chemical: carbonation (CO2 + H2O forms carbonic acid dissolving CaCO3), oxidation, hydrolysis
    • Biological: root growth widens cracks, burrowing organisms, organic acids from lichens
  • Mass movement — downslope movement of material under gravity:
    • Rockfall: sudden collapse of rock from a steep cliff face
    • Landslide: block of material slides along a planar failure surface
    • Rotational slump: material slides along a curved failure surface, common in clay cliffs
    • Mudflow: saturated soil flows downslope as a viscous mass

Sediment Transport

Longshore drift: The movement of sediment along the coast by wave action. Waves approach at an angle (determined by the prevailing wind), carrying sediment up the beach with the swash. Backwash carries sediment straight back down under gravity. The net result is a zig-zag movement of material along the shore.

Sediment is also transported by:

  • Traction — large particles rolled along the seabed
  • Saltation — pebbles bounced along the seabed
  • Suspension — fine particles carried within the water
  • Solution — dissolved minerals carried invisibly in the water

4. Erosional Landforms

Headlands and Bays

Form along coastlines with alternating bands of resistant (hard) and less resistant (soft) rock. Differential erosion removes softer rock faster, creating bays, while harder rock remains as protruding headlands. Wave refraction then concentrates energy on headlands, eventually eroding them.

Cliff Recession and Wave-cut Platforms

Waves attack the base of a cliff, eroding a wave-cut notch through hydraulic action and abrasion. As the notch deepens, the overhanging cliff becomes unstable and collapses. Repeated cycles of notch erosion and cliff collapse cause the cliff to retreat landward, leaving a gently sloping wave-cut platform at the base (exposed at low tide).

Caves, Arches, Stacks, and Stumps

  1. Cave — hydraulic action and abrasion exploit a weakness (joint/fault) in a headland, creating a cave.
  2. Arch — erosion from both sides of a headland breaks through, forming an arch.
  3. Stack — the roof of the arch collapses, leaving an isolated pillar of rock (stack).
  4. Stump — the stack is eroded at its base, eventually collapsing to form a stump (visible at low tide).
Case Study: Old Harry Rocks, Dorset
Old Harry Rocks are chalk stacks on the eastern end of the Isle of Purbeck. They demonstrate the full sequence of erosional landforms: caves, arches (Old Harry's Wife collapsed in 1896 leaving a stump), and stacks. The Jurassic Coast here shows concordant and discordant coastline features. The chalk headland of Handfast Point is being eroded by hydraulic action and abrasion, with wave refraction concentrating energy on the headland. The nearby Studland Bay is a depositional feature formed in the shelter of the headland.

5. Depositional Landforms

Beaches

Beaches form where constructive waves deposit more material than destructive waves remove. They are the most common depositional landform.

  • Sandy beaches — gentle gradient (1–5°), fine sediment, wide, common in sheltered bays
  • Shingle/pebble beaches — steeper gradient (10–20°), coarse sediment, narrow, often at base of cliffs
  • Berms — ridges of material deposited at the high tide mark
  • Cusps — semi-circular depressions formed by swash and backwash interaction

Spits

A spit is a finger-like extension of beach material projecting into the sea or across a river mouth. They form where longshore drift carries sediment past a change in coastline direction (e.g. a river mouth). The end of the spit may be curved by wave refraction or secondary wind/wave direction. A sheltered area of calm water behind the spit often develops into a salt marsh.

Bars and Tombolos

  • Bar — a spit that grows across a bay, completely enclosing it. Water trapped behind forms a lagoon (e.g. Slapton Ley, Devon).
  • Tombolo — a bar connecting the mainland to an island (e.g. Chesil Beach connects Portland to mainland Dorset).

Sand Dunes

Form where wind blows dry sand from the beach inland. Obstacles trap the sand, which accumulates into dunes. A succession of dune types develops from the shore inland: embryo dunes, fore dunes (yellow dunes), grey dunes, dune slacks, and mature dunes. Pioneer species like sea couch grass and marram grass stabilise early dunes through their root systems.

Case Study: Chesil Beach, Dorset
Chesil Beach is a 29 km tombolo connecting Portland to the mainland at West Bay. It is one of the largest shingle structures in Europe, with pebbles graded by size (smaller at the west end, larger at the Portland end) due to longshore drift and wave energy variations. Behind the bar lies the Fleet Lagoon. Chesil Beach is a relict feature, likely formed at the end of the last Ice Age when sea levels rose. It is currently subject to overwashing during storms, threatening the village of Chiswell.

6. Coastal Landscapes of Deposition — Estuaries and Salt Marshes

Estuary: A partially enclosed coastal body of water where freshwater from a river meets and mixes with saltwater from the sea. Estuaries are characterised by tidal mudflats, salt marshes, and complex sediment dynamics.

Salt marshes develop in sheltered estuarine environments where fine sediment (mud, silt) accumulates. Halophytic (salt-tolerant) plants colonise the marsh in a process of ecological succession: pioneer species (e.g. Spartina, glasswort) trap more sediment, raising the surface. Over time, a diverse marsh community develops with distinct zones based on tidal exposure.

7. Sea Level Change

Eustatic Change

A global change in sea level caused by changes in the volume of water in the oceans. Causes include thermal expansion of ocean water due to warming, and melting of ice sheets and glaciers.

Isostatic Change

A local change in sea level caused by vertical land movement. During glaciation, the weight of ice depresses the land. When ice melts, the land slowly rebounds (isostatic uplift) — still occurring in Scotland at ~1 mm/yr.

Emergent Coastlines (sea level fall relative to land)

  • Raised beaches — former beaches now elevated above current sea level, with old cliff lines behind them
  • Relict cliffs — abandoned cliff faces no longer reached by waves

Submergent Coastlines (sea level rise relative to land)

  • Rias — drowned river valleys with a V-shaped cross-profile that narrows and shallows inland (e.g. Milford Haven, Wales)
  • Fjords — drowned glacial valleys with a U-shaped cross-profile, very deep with steep sides and a shallow threshold at the mouth (e.g. Sognefjord, Norway)
  • Dalmatian coasts — where valleys run parallel to the coast and flooding creates long, narrow islands

Shoreline Management Plans (SMPs)

SMPs divide the coastline into units and assign one of four management strategies:

  1. Hold the line — maintain existing defences
  2. Advance the line — build new defences seaward of existing ones
  3. Managed realignment — allow controlled retreat to a new defence line
  4. No active intervention — let natural processes proceed (do nothing)
Case Study: Holderness Coast, East Yorkshire
The Holderness Coast is one of Europe's fastest eroding coastlines, retreating at an average of 1.8 m/year. The coast is made of soft boulder clay, highly susceptible to hydraulic action, abrasion, and slumping when saturated. Over 30 villages recorded in the Domesday Book have been lost to the sea. Key management approaches: Hornsea has a sea wall and groynes protecting the town, while areas like Mappleton received rock armour in 1991 (£2 million) which starved Cowden (downdrift) of sediment, accelerating erosion there. Spurn Point, a spit at the southern end, is managed through a policy of managed retreat since 2015 — the road was allowed to breach and the area is now tidal.
Exam Tip: For coastal management questions, always evaluate the sustainability of approaches. Consider: cost-benefit analysis, environmental impact, social implications (who wins and who loses?), and whether strategies work with or against natural processes. The concept of sediment cells is key — protecting one area can have knock-on effects elsewhere.
