2.3 Population & the Environment

1. Population Change

Population change: The increase or decrease in the number of people in an area over time. Determined by the balance between birth rate, death rate, and net migration.
Natural change = Birth rate − Death rate
Population change = (Births − Deaths) + (Immigration − Emigration)
Global population reached 8 billion in November 2022 and is projected to peak at ~10.4 billion around 2086 before gradually declining (UN 2024 revision).

Factors Affecting Birth Rates

  • Education (especially female) — the single strongest predictor of fertility decline. Women with secondary education have on average 1.5 fewer children than those with no education. Education delays marriage, increases contraceptive use, and empowers women with career alternatives.
  • Access to contraception — availability and cultural acceptance of family planning. In Sub-Saharan Africa, 24% of women who want to avoid pregnancy lack access to modern contraception (unmet need).
  • Economic development — as countries develop, children shift from economic assets (farm labour) to economic costs (education, childcare). The "cost of childhood" rises sharply with urbanisation and modernisation.
  • Cultural and religious factors — attitudes to family size, roles of women, religious teachings on contraception (e.g. Catholic Church, some Islamic interpretations). However, cultural factors interact with economic conditions — Catholic Italy and Spain have among the world's lowest TFRs.
  • Government policies — pro-natalist (France: generous paid parental leave, childcare subsidies, tax breaks for larger families; Sweden: 480 days shared parental leave) or anti-natalist (China's former one-child policy, India's forced sterilisation programme 1975–77).
  • Urbanisation — urban living reduces birth rates: smaller housing, higher cost of children, less need for farm labour, greater access to contraception and education, more career opportunities for women.
  • Infant mortality — high IMR encourages larger families as "insurance" against child deaths. As IMR falls, parents gain confidence that children will survive, and fertility declines with a lag.
  • Status of women — where women have greater legal rights, workforce participation, and decision-making power, fertility tends to be lower.

Factors Affecting Death Rates

  • Healthcare quality — access to modern medicine, vaccinations (global childhood immunisation now covers ~85%), antibiotics, surgery, maternal care. Universal healthcare systems dramatically reduce preventable deaths.
  • Nutrition and food security — adequate calorie and micronutrient intake. The Green Revolution (1960s–80s) doubled food production and prevented widespread famine.
  • Clean water and sanitation — reduces waterborne diseases (cholera, typhoid, dysentery). 2 billion people still lack safely managed drinking water (2023).
  • Disease prevalence — HIV/AIDS devastated life expectancy in Southern Africa (Botswana's LE fell from 65 to 49 between 1990 and 2002, now recovered to 62 with antiretroviral treatment). Malaria kills ~600,000/yr, mostly children in Sub-Saharan Africa.
  • Conflict and instability — war, civil unrest, displacement raise death rates directly and by destroying healthcare, food systems, and infrastructure.
  • Age structure — countries with ageing populations (Japan, Germany, Italy) have higher crude death rates even with excellent healthcare, because a large proportion of the population is elderly.
  • Lifestyle factors — in HICs, non-communicable diseases (heart disease, cancer, diabetes, obesity-related conditions) are now the leading causes of death. Smoking, alcohol, diet, and sedentary lifestyles.

2. The Demographic Transition Model (DTM)

Demographic Transition Model: A theoretical model describing how population change progresses through stages as a country develops economically. It shows the relationship between birth rates, death rates, and total population over time. Originally based on the experience of Western European countries during industrialisation.

Limitations of the DTM

  • Based on the Western European historical experience — may not apply to countries with different cultures, economies, or political systems.
  • Does not account for migration — a major component of population change in many countries (e.g. UAE, Singapore, UK).
  • Assumes all countries follow the same linear pathway — ignores unique political/cultural contexts (e.g. HIV/AIDS reversed death rate decline in Southern Africa; China's one-child policy artificially accelerated the transition).
  • Stage 5 is contested — not universally accepted. Some demographers argue it should include the possibility of fertility recovery.
  • Does not predict how long each stage lasts — UK took 200 years; Thailand transitioned from Stage 2 to 4 in just 40 years.
  • Eurocentrism — the model was developed to describe European industrialisation and may impose a Western trajectory on diverse development paths.
  • Does not account for the epidemiological transition — the shift from infectious diseases to chronic/non-communicable diseases as the main causes of death.

3. Population Structure

Population pyramid: A graphical representation of the age-sex structure of a population. The shape reveals key characteristics: a wide base indicates a youthful population (high birth rates), a column shape indicates a stable population (low birth and death rates), and a top-heavy or inverted shape indicates an ageing/declining population.

Ageing Populations

Many HICs and some MICs face rapidly ageing populations (increasing proportion aged 65+). This results from sustained low birth rates and increasing life expectancy. Japan is the world's oldest country: 29% of the population is 65+, with a median age of 49.

Consequences of ageing:

  • Rising dependency ratio — fewer working-age people supporting more elderly dependents. Japan's ratio is projected to reach 1 worker per 1 retiree by 2050 (currently 2:1).
  • Increased government spending on pensions (Japan spends ~10% of GDP on pensions), healthcare (the elderly consume 3–5x more healthcare per capita), and social care (dementia care costs alone are projected to double by 2040 in the UK).
  • Labour shortages — shrinking workforce potentially slowing economic growth. Germany's working-age population is projected to shrink by 7 million by 2035.
  • Changing demand for services — more care homes, hospitals, and accessible infrastructure; fewer schools, maternity wards, and children's services.
  • Need for immigration to fill workforce gaps — Germany admitted 1 million+ migrants in 2015 partly to address labour shortages.
  • "Grey vote" — elderly voters become politically dominant, potentially skewing policy priorities towards pensions and healthcare rather than education and young families.

Youthful Populations

Many LICs have youthful populations (high proportion under 15). Niger has the world's youngest population: 50% are under 15, with a median age of just 15.4 years.

Consequences:

  • Enormous pressure on education (building schools, training teachers), healthcare (maternal and child services), and employment (creating millions of jobs for young people entering the workforce).
  • "Youth bulge" — a disproportionately large young adult cohort. If education and employment are inadequate, this can lead to social unrest, political instability, and migration (Arab Spring protests were partly driven by youth unemployment). But if harnessed, it creates a demographic dividend.
  • Demographic dividend — if education and employment are provided for the large working-age cohort, the low dependency ratio can drive rapid economic growth. East Asian tigers (South Korea, Taiwan, Singapore) benefited from this in the 1970s–90s.
  • High dependency ratio in the short term (many children to support relative to workers).
  • Momentum of growth — even after fertility falls, population continues to grow for decades because the large young cohort is yet to have children.

4. Migration

Migration: The permanent or semi-permanent movement of people from one place to another. International migration crosses national borders; internal migration occurs within a country (e.g. rural-urban migration). There are an estimated 281 million international migrants globally (2023), representing 3.6% of the world's population.

Lee's Model of Migration (Push-Pull Theory, 1966)

Everett Lee proposed that migration decisions are influenced by four factors:

  • Push factors at the origin — poverty, unemployment, conflict, persecution, natural disasters, environmental degradation, lack of services
  • Pull factors at the destination — employment, higher wages, safety, education, healthcare, family reunification, freedom, quality of life
  • Intervening obstacles — distance, cost of travel, border controls, immigration policies, language barriers, family ties, discrimination
  • Personal factors — age (young adults most mobile), education, gender, risk tolerance, family situation, information networks

Case Study: EU Migration to the UK (2004–2020)
Following EU enlargement in 2004 (A8 countries including Poland, Lithuania, Latvia joined), the UK experienced significant immigration. Between 2004 and 2016, an estimated 1 million Polish nationals moved to the UK, making Polish the second most common language. Positive impacts: filled labour shortages in agriculture (picking and packing), construction, hospitality, healthcare (12.5% of NHS staff in 2019 were EU nationals), and food processing; net fiscal contributors (paid more in taxes than received in benefits — UCL study found A8 migrants contributed 12% more in taxes than they received in public services); cultural diversity, entrepreneurship (Polish shops, restaurants, churches). Negative impacts: pressure on housing and public services in some areas (e.g. Boston, Lincolnshire — which voted 76% Leave in the EU referendum), concerns about wage depression in low-skill sectors (disputed by most economists), contributed to the Brexit vote (2016) as immigration was a key campaign issue. Post-Brexit: EU net migration fell from 189,000/yr (2015) to near zero (2021), causing acute labour shortages in agriculture (crops left unharvested), hospitality (30% vacancy rate), social care (165,000 vacancies), and HGV driving. Non-EU migration subsequently increased to compensate.
Case Study: Syrian Refugee Crisis (2011–present)
The Syrian Civil War (from 2011) created the largest refugee crisis since World War II. By 2024: 6.5 million Syrian refugees fled to neighbouring countries (Turkey 3.3M, Lebanon 800,000, Jordan 670,000, Germany 850,000) and 6.9 million were internally displaced. Impacts on host countries: Turkey spent $40+ billion hosting refugees; Lebanon's population increased by 25%, overwhelming infrastructure, schools, and healthcare; Jordan's water-scarce resources were strained further. Germany admitted 890,000 asylum seekers in 2015 alone ("Willkommenskultur" policy of Angela Merkel), sparking a political backlash (rise of AfD party, debate about integration). Impacts on Syria: devastating brain drain (doctors, engineers, teachers fled), destroyed human capital, generation of children missing education. Broader significance: the crisis exposed the limitations of the international refugee system, contributed to anti-immigration politics across Europe, and demonstrated how conflict creates cascading humanitarian, economic, and political consequences across multiple countries.

5. Population-Environment Relationships

Key Theories

Malthus (1798) — An Essay on the Principle of Population: Population grows geometrically (1, 2, 4, 8, 16...) while food production grows arithmetically (1, 2, 3, 4, 5...). This means population will always tend to outstrip food supply, leading to "positive checks" (famine, disease, war) that reduce population back to the level the land can support. Malthus also identified "preventive checks" (moral restraint, delayed marriage) that could voluntarily limit growth. A fundamentally pessimistic/neo-Malthusian view. Neo-Malthusians (Paul Ehrlich's "The Population Bomb" 1968, Club of Rome's "Limits to Growth" 1972) updated this argument for the modern era, warning of ecological collapse.
Boserup (1965) — The Conditions of Agricultural Growth: Population growth is not a threat but a stimulus for agricultural innovation. When population pressure increases, people develop new techniques to produce more food from the same land (e.g. irrigation, fertilisers, crop rotation, selective breeding, terracing, double-cropping). "Necessity is the mother of invention." An optimistic view that has been partially supported by the Green Revolution, but questioned by concerns about environmental sustainability.
Club of Rome / Limits to Growth (1972): Computer modelling (by Dennis Meadows et al.) predicted that continued exponential population and economic growth would lead to resource depletion and environmental collapse by the mid-21st century if growth trajectories were not changed. A neo-Malthusian perspective. The models have been updated (2004, 2012) and broadly track real-world trends, though critics argue technology can shift resource limits. The concept of "planetary boundaries" (Rockstrom et al., 2009) identifies 9 Earth system thresholds, of which at least 6 have already been crossed.
Simon (1981) — The Ultimate Resource: Julian Simon argued that human ingenuity is the "ultimate resource." More people means more ideas, innovation, and problem-solving capacity. Population growth drives economic growth and technological progress. He won a famous bet with Paul Ehrlich (1980) that commodity prices would fall over a decade despite population growth (they did). A cornucopian/techno-optimist view.

Ecological Footprint and Carrying Capacity

Ecological footprint: The area of biologically productive land and water required to produce the resources consumed and absorb the waste generated by a given population. Measured in global hectares (gha) per person. The USA's ecological footprint is ~8.1 gha/person; India's is ~1.2 gha/person; the global average is ~2.8 gha/person. Earth's total biocapacity is only ~1.6 gha/person, meaning humanity is in ecological overshoot — using resources 1.75x faster than Earth can regenerate them. "Earth Overshoot Day" (when humanity has used a year's worth of resources) fell on 1 August 2024.
Carrying capacity: The maximum population size that an environment can sustain indefinitely, given the available resources. It is not fixed — technology can increase it (Green Revolution, renewable energy) while environmental degradation can reduce it (soil erosion, water depletion, climate change). For Earth as a whole, estimates range from 4 billion (at US consumption levels) to 15 billion (at subsistence levels) — carrying capacity depends on lifestyle choices as much as absolute resource limits.

6. Global Population Futures and Population Policies

The UN projects global population will reach ~9.7 billion by 2050 and peak at ~10.4 billion around 2086 before gradually declining (2024 revision). Almost all growth will occur in Sub-Saharan Africa (projected to double from 1.2 billion to 2.4 billion by 2050) and South Asia. Meanwhile, populations in Europe, East Asia, and parts of Latin America are already declining or will soon.

The world is experiencing a dramatic fertility decline: global TFR fell from 4.7 (1970) to 2.3 (2024). Over 80 countries are now below replacement fertility (2.1). South Korea's TFR of 0.7 (2023) is the lowest ever recorded in any country. This creates a "demographic divergence" — some countries face youth bulges and rapid growth, while others face ageing and decline.

Case Study: China's Population Policies
China's one-child policy (1979–2015) was the world's most ambitious anti-natalist programme. Methods: financial penalties for additional children (fines of 3–10x annual income), rewards for compliance (priority housing, healthcare, education), workplace monitoring, and in some areas forced sterilisations and abortions. Results: TFR fell from 5.9 (1970) to 1.6 (2000). The policy prevented an estimated 400 million births. Unintended consequences: severe gender imbalance (sex-selective abortions led to 120 boys per 100 girls at peak, creating a "bare branches" problem — 30–40 million men unable to find partners), rapidly ageing population (65+ population grew from 5% in 1980 to 15% in 2024, projected 30% by 2050), "4-2-1" family structure (one child supporting 2 parents and 4 grandparents), "little emperor" syndrome, rural enforcement abuses and human rights violations. Policy reversal: two-child policy (2016), three-child policy (2021), removal of all limits (2024), now actively encouraging births through cash incentives (up to $1,400 per child in some provinces), extended maternity leave (158+ days), childcare subsidies, and housing discounts. Despite all this, TFR continues to fall (~1.0 in 2023) as high living costs, housing prices, education expenses, changing attitudes toward marriage, and rising female workforce participation keep families small. China's population peaked in 2022 (1.426 billion) and is now declining — projected to halve to ~700 million by 2100. China demonstrates that once fertility decline becomes culturally embedded, it is extremely difficult to reverse.
Case Study: Uganda — Rapid Population Growth
Uganda (population ~48 million, 2024) has one of the world's fastest growth rates (~3.2%/yr) and highest TFRs (~4.7). It is transitioning from DTM Stage 2 to early Stage 3: death rates have fallen significantly (under-5 mortality dropped from 175 to 43 per 1,000 between 1990 and 2023) but birth rates remain high. Reasons for persistent high fertility: limited contraceptive access (only 35% of married women use modern contraception; 28% unmet need), cultural preference for large families (children as security, status, and farm labour), low female secondary education enrollment (41%), 75% rural population, early marriage (34% of girls married before 18), and limited government investment in family planning. Consequences: 47% of population is under 15, creating enormous pressure on education (classroom sizes of 100+ common), healthcare (1 doctor per 10,000 people vs WHO recommendation of 1 per 1,000), employment (900,000 young people enter the job market annually, but only 75,000 formal jobs are created), and the environment (deforestation rate of 2.7%/yr for farmland expansion, soil erosion, Lake Victoria pollution). Potential: if education expands (especially for girls) and contraceptive access improves, Uganda could capture a demographic dividend from its large young workforce. But without these investments, the youth bulge risks becoming a source of instability.
Case Study: Japan — Population Decline and Super-ageing
Japan is the world's most extreme example of population ageing and decline. TFR is 1.2 (well below replacement of 2.1). Population peaked at 128.1 million (2008) and is now 123 million (2024), projected to fall to 87 million by 2070 and 50 million by 2100. 29.1% of the population is 65+ (the highest proportion globally). Causes: high cost of living and child-rearing in cities, long working hours (incompatible with family life), high female education and career aspirations, limited childcare, cultural shift away from marriage (30% of Japanese aged 30–34 have never married), small housing. Consequences: 900,000 more deaths than births annually (2023), severe labour shortages (especially in healthcare, construction, agriculture — 1.5 million unfilled jobs), rising pension and healthcare costs (social security spending: 24% of GDP), abandoned rural towns ("ghost villages" — 8 million vacant homes), shrinking domestic market and tax base. Government response: robotics and AI to replace workers, gradual opening to immigration (foreign workers doubled to 2 million between 2015 and 2023, but Japan remains culturally resistant to large-scale immigration), childcare expansion (free preschool from 2019), financial incentives (some municipalities offer $7,000+ per child), but TFR has continued to fall. Japan's Prime Minister declared in 2023 that addressing the fertility crisis was "now or never" — a challenge that may prove impossible to solve through policy alone once cultural norms have shifted.
Exam Tip: AQA expects you to evaluate different perspectives on population-environment relationships. Malthusian and Boserupian views represent opposite ends of a spectrum. The reality is nuanced: the Green Revolution supports Boserup (technology increased food production), but environmental limits are real and growing (climate change, water scarcity, soil degradation support neo-Malthusian concerns). Use specific evidence to build a balanced argument. The best answers acknowledge that the relationship depends on consumption patterns and technology, not just population numbers.
Exam Tip: Always consider the social, ethical, and political dimensions of population policies. China's one-child policy achieved demographic goals but at enormous human cost. Pro-natalist policies (France, Sweden) have limited success because fertility decisions are deeply personal. Discuss whether governments should intervene in family size, and whose rights take priority. This ethical dimension elevates answers beyond mere description.
Exam Tip: When discussing population change, always distinguish between population SIZE and population GROWTH RATE. Global population is still growing in absolute terms but the growth rate has halved since the 1960s. Similarly, distinguish between the PERCENTAGE of elderly and the ABSOLUTE NUMBER. Japan has a high percentage of elderly but a smaller population than Nigeria, which has a much larger absolute number of young people. Precision with data impresses examiners.
