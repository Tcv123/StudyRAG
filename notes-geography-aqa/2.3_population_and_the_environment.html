2.3 Population & the Environment

1. Population Change

Population change: The increase or decrease in the number of people in an area over time. It is determined by the balance between birth rate, death rate, and migration.
Natural change = Birth rate − Death rate
Population change = (Births − Deaths) + (Immigration − Emigration)

Key Demographic Indicators

IndicatorDefinitionGlobal Average (approx.)
Crude Birth Rate (CBR)Number of live births per 1,000 population per year~18 per 1,000
Crude Death Rate (CDR)Number of deaths per 1,000 population per year~8 per 1,000
Rate of Natural Increase (RNI)CBR − CDR, expressed as a percentage~1.0%
Total Fertility Rate (TFR)Average number of children born per woman during her lifetime~2.3
Infant Mortality Rate (IMR)Number of deaths of infants under 1 year per 1,000 live births~28 per 1,000
Life ExpectancyAverage number of years a person is expected to live from birth~73 years
Replacement Level FertilityTFR of ~2.1 needed to maintain population without migration—

Factors Affecting Birth Rates

  • Education — particularly female education. Women with secondary education have significantly fewer children.
  • Access to contraception — availability and cultural acceptance of family planning
  • Economic development — as countries develop, the cost of raising children increases and their economic value decreases
  • Cultural/religious factors — attitudes to family size, roles of women, religious teachings on contraception
  • Government policies — pro-natalist (e.g. France, Sweden offering childcare, parental leave) or anti-natalist (e.g. China's former one-child policy)
  • Urbanisation — urban living tends to reduce birth rates (smaller housing, less need for farm labour)
  • Infant mortality — high IMR encourages larger families as "insurance"

Factors Affecting Death Rates

  • Healthcare quality — access to modern medicine, vaccinations, surgery, maternal care
  • Nutrition — food security and dietary quality
  • Clean water and sanitation — reduces waterborne diseases
  • Disease prevalence — HIV/AIDS, malaria, TB still cause high death rates in some LICs
  • Conflict and instability — war, civil unrest
  • Age structure — ageing populations (Japan, Germany) have higher crude death rates even with excellent healthcare

2. The Demographic Transition Model (DTM)

Demographic Transition Model: A model describing how population change progresses through stages as a country develops economically. It shows the relationship between birth rates, death rates, and total population over time.

DTM Stage Details

StageBRDRPopulationCharacteristics
1: High fluctuatingHighHighLow, stablePre-industrial. High BR due to lack of contraception, need for labour. High DR from disease, famine, poor sanitation.
2: Early expandingHighFallingRapidly growingDR falls due to improved healthcare, sanitation, nutrition. BR stays high. Large natural increase.
3: Late expandingFallingLowStill growing (slowing)BR falls due to urbanisation, education, contraception, changing role of women. Natural increase narrows.
4: Low fluctuatingLowLowHigh, stableBoth rates low. Population stable. Ageing population structure. Most HICs.
5: Decline (debated)Very lowExceeds BRDecliningBR falls below replacement level. Ageing population, dependency ratio rises. E.g. Japan (TFR 1.2).

Limitations of the DTM

  • Based on the experience of Western European countries — may not apply universally
  • Does not account for migration (a major component of population change)
  • Assumes all countries will follow the same pathway — ignores unique political/cultural contexts
  • Stage 5 is contested and not universally accepted
  • Does not predict how long each stage lasts (some countries transition rapidly, others slowly)
  • Does not account for the impact of HIV/AIDS on death rates (e.g. Sub-Saharan Africa)
Exam Tip: When using the DTM, always link stages to specific named countries with supporting data. Explain why rates change, not just that they do. The DTM is a model, not a prediction — acknowledge its limitations.

3. Population Structure

Population pyramid: A graphical representation of the age-sex structure of a population. The shape reveals key characteristics: a wide base indicates a youthful population (high birth rates), while a column or top-heavy shape indicates an ageing population.

Ageing Populations

Many HICs and some MICs face ageing populations (increasing proportion of people aged 65+). This results from falling birth rates and increasing life expectancy.

Consequences:

  • Rising dependency ratio (fewer working-age people supporting more elderly)
  • Increased spending on pensions, healthcare, and social care
  • Labour shortages, potentially slowing economic growth
  • Changing demand for services (more care homes, fewer schools)
  • Need for immigration to fill workforce gaps

Youthful Populations

Many LICs have youthful populations (high proportion under 15). This results from high birth rates and falling infant mortality.

Consequences:

  • Pressure on education, healthcare, and employment
  • Potential for a "youth bulge" leading to social unrest if jobs are unavailable
  • Demographic dividend if education and employment are provided — young workforce drives economic growth
  • High dependency ratio (many children to support)

4. Migration

Migration: The permanent or semi-permanent movement of people from one place to another. International migration crosses national borders; internal migration occurs within a country (e.g. rural-urban migration).

Lee's Model of Migration (Push-Pull Theory)

Everett Lee (1966) proposed that migration decisions are influenced by:

  • Push factors at the origin (poverty, conflict, persecution, unemployment, natural disasters)
  • Pull factors at the destination (jobs, safety, education, healthcare, family reunification)
  • Intervening obstacles (distance, cost, border controls, language barriers, family ties)
  • Personal factors (age, education, risk tolerance, stage in life cycle)

Case Study: EU Migration to the UK (pre-Brexit)
Following EU enlargement in 2004 (A8 countries, including Poland, joined), the UK experienced significant immigration. Between 2004 and 2016, an estimated 1 million Polish nationals moved to the UK. Positive impacts: filled labour shortages in agriculture, construction, hospitality, and healthcare; net fiscal contributors (paid more in taxes than received in benefits); cultural diversity. Negative impacts: pressure on housing and public services in some areas (e.g. Boston, Lincolnshire), concerns about wage depression in low-skill sectors, contributed to the Brexit vote (2016). Post-Brexit, EU migration fell significantly, causing labour shortages in agriculture, hospitality, and care work.

5. Population-Environment Relationships

Key Theories

Malthus (1798) — Essay on the Principle of Population: Population grows geometrically (1, 2, 4, 8, 16...) while food production grows arithmetically (1, 2, 3, 4, 5...). This means population will always outstrip food supply, leading to "positive checks" (famine, disease, war) that reduce population. A pessimistic/neo-Malthusian view.
Boserup (1965) — The Conditions of Agricultural Growth: Population growth stimulates agricultural innovation. When population pressure increases, people develop new techniques to produce more food (e.g. irrigation, fertilisers, crop rotation). "Necessity is the mother of invention." An optimistic/cornucopian view.
Club of Rome / Limits to Growth (1972): Computer modelling predicted that continued population and economic growth would lead to resource depletion and environmental collapse by the mid-21st century. A neo-Malthusian perspective arguing for limits on growth.

Ecological Footprint and Carrying Capacity

Ecological footprint: The area of biologically productive land and water required to produce the resources consumed and absorb the waste generated by a given population. Measured in global hectares (gha) per person. If everyone lived like the average American, we would need ~5 Earths. The global average is ~2.7 gha/person, but Earth's biocapacity is only ~1.6 gha/person, meaning we are in ecological overshoot.
Carrying capacity: The maximum population size that an environment can sustain indefinitely, given the available resources (food, water, space, energy). It is not fixed — technology can increase it, while environmental degradation can reduce it.

6. Global Population Futures

The UN projects global population will reach ~9.7 billion by 2050 and ~10.4 billion by 2100. Almost all growth will occur in Africa and South Asia. Many countries (Japan, South Korea, most of Europe) face population decline without migration.

Population Policies

Case Study: China's Population Policies
China's one-child policy (1979–2015) was the world's most ambitious anti-natalist policy. Methods: financial penalties for more than one child, rewards for compliance, forced sterilisations and abortions in some cases. Results: TFR fell from 5.9 (1970) to 1.6 (2000). But consequences included: gender imbalance (sex-selective abortions led to 120 boys per 100 girls), rapidly ageing population, "4-2-1" family structure (one child supporting two parents and four grandparents). China reversed course: two-child policy in 2016, three-child policy in 2021, and now actively encouraging births through subsidies and childcare support. Despite this, TFR continues to fall (~1.0 in 2023), as high living costs and changing attitudes keep families small.
Case Study: Uganda — Rapid Population Growth
Uganda (population ~47 million, 2024) has one of the world's fastest growth rates (~3.2%/yr) and highest TFRs (~5.0). It is in DTM Stage 2/early 3: death rates have fallen (improved healthcare, reduced HIV mortality) but birth rates remain high (limited contraception access, cultural preference for large families, low female education levels, 75% rural population). Consequences: 48% of the population is under 15, creating enormous pressure on education, healthcare, and employment. Environmental impacts: deforestation for farmland, soil erosion, water stress. Government response has been limited; Uganda's family planning programme has low funding. However, the potential for a demographic dividend exists if education and employment are expanded.
Exam Tip: AQA expects you to evaluate different perspectives on the population-environment relationship. Malthusian and Boserupian views represent opposite ends of a spectrum. The reality is usually more nuanced — technology can increase food production (supporting Boserup), but environmental limits are real (supporting Malthus). Use specific evidence to support a balanced argument.
Exam Tip: Always consider the social, ethical, and political dimensions of population policies. China's one-child policy achieved demographic goals but at enormous human cost. Pro-natalist policies (like France's generous childcare) have limited success because fertility decisions are deeply personal. Discuss whether governments should intervene in family size.
