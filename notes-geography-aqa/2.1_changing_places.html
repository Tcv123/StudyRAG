2.1 Changing Places

1. The Nature and Importance of Places

Place: A location that has been given meaning by people. Places are more than just physical locations — they carry social, cultural, economic, and emotional significance. Place is a central concept in human geography, connecting identity, belonging, and experience to specific locations.
Space vs Place: Space is an abstract, undefined area with no particular meaning. Place is a space that has been given meaning through human experience, memories, and attachment. A coordinate on a map is a space; your home is a place.

Key Concepts of Place

  • Location — the geographical position of a place (absolute: coordinates; relative: in relation to other places)
  • Locale — the physical setting in which social relations and interactions occur (e.g. a workplace, park, or shopping centre)
  • Sense of place — the subjective, emotional attachment people feel towards a place. This is personal and varies between individuals.
  • Perception of place — how an individual or group views a place, shaped by experience, media, education, and identity

Insider and Outsider Perspectives

PerspectiveDescriptionCharacteristics
InsiderSomeone who lives in or has deep personal experience of a placeSubjective, emotional, detailed knowledge, sense of belonging, shaped by everyday experience
OutsiderSomeone who observes a place from outside, without personal connectionMore objective but potentially superficial, shaped by media and stereotypes, may lack nuance
Exam Tip: AQA expects you to understand that place meaning is contested — different groups (residents, developers, tourists, local government) may have very different views of the same place. Always consider multiple perspectives in your answers.

2. Place Meaning and Representation

Factors Shaping Place Character

The character of a place is shaped by a combination of physical and human factors:

Physical Factors (Endogenous)

  • Physical geography — relief, drainage, climate, soil, natural resources
  • Location — coastal, inland, altitude, latitude, proximity to transport routes
  • Environmental quality — green spaces, air quality, flood risk, natural beauty
  • Built environment — architecture, land use, infrastructure, heritage buildings

Human/Demographic Factors

  • Population characteristics — age structure, ethnicity, socioeconomic status, education levels
  • Cultural factors — religion, language, customs, traditions, community organisations
  • Economic factors — employment types, income levels, economic history (industrial, agricultural, service-based)
  • Political factors — governance, planning policy, investment decisions, political ideology
  • Historical factors — past events, heritage, collective memory (e.g. industrial revolution, conflict, migration waves)

Exogenous (External) Factors

Places are also shaped by forces operating beyond the local scale:

  • Globalisation — global flows of capital, people, ideas, and culture can transform local places (e.g. chain stores replacing independent shops, cultural homogenisation)
  • Migration — in-migration and out-migration alter the demographic, cultural, and economic character of a place
  • Government policy — national policies on housing, transport, regeneration, and planning shape local places
  • Technology — digital connectivity, remote working, and social media reshape how places function and are perceived
  • Shifting flows of people, money, ideas — investment decisions, tourism patterns, and cultural trends operating at regional, national, and global scales

3. Representation of Place

Place representation: How a place is portrayed or depicted by different agents (media, artists, government, residents). Representations may reinforce, challenge, or create specific images of a place, and they influence perceptions and decisions about that place.

Formal (Official) Representations

  • Maps and census data — quantitative, seemingly objective, but choices about what to include/exclude carry bias
  • Government statistics — IMD (Index of Multiple Deprivation), ONS data on employment, health, education
  • Planning documents — local plans, regeneration strategies present an official vision of a place
  • Tourism marketing — councils and agencies create selective, positive images to attract visitors and investment

Informal (Unofficial) Representations

  • Film, TV, and literature — fictional representations shape place image powerfully (e.g. "Downton Abbey" and the Yorkshire countryside, "Peaky Blinders" and Birmingham)
  • Photography and art — subjective visual representations that emphasise particular aspects of a place
  • Music — lyrics and cultural associations link places to genres (e.g. Manchester and Britpop, Nashville and country)
  • Social media — user-generated content, reviews, hashtags, and viral posts create collective perceptions
  • Oral histories and personal accounts — individual stories reveal lived experience of a place
  • Newspaper reporting — can stigmatise or celebrate places through selective coverage
Exam Tip: When analysing a representation of place (e.g. a photograph, map, text extract, or advert in the exam paper), always consider: Who created it? Why? What is included/excluded? What impression does it give? How might different people interpret it? Is it biased?

4. Quantitative and Qualitative Approaches to Studying Place

Quantitative Data

Numerical data that can be measured and analysed statistically:

  • Census data (population, ethnicity, age, employment, housing)
  • Index of Multiple Deprivation (IMD) — a composite measure of deprivation across income, employment, education, health, crime, housing, and living environment
  • Land use surveys, traffic counts, environmental quality index scores
  • House price data, unemployment rates, crime statistics

Qualitative Data

Non-numerical data that captures meaning, perception, and experience:

  • Interviews and oral histories with residents
  • Photographs and visual surveys
  • Analysis of text (newspaper articles, literature, social media posts)
  • Field sketches, mental maps, soundscapes
  • Participant observation and ethnographic research
Mental map: A person's internal representation of an area, shaped by their experiences and perceptions. Mental maps reveal what places and features are important to an individual and can expose social and spatial inequalities.

5. Place Identity and Belonging

Factors Influencing Sense of Place

  • Age — older residents may have deeper emotional attachments built over decades; young people may identify more with virtual spaces or aspire to leave
  • Gender — men and women may experience the same place differently (e.g. safety perceptions, access to services)
  • Sexuality — LGBTQ+ communities may feel welcome in some places (e.g. Brighton) and excluded in others
  • Ethnicity and religion — cultural identity strongly shapes sense of belonging and place attachment. Ethnic enclaves can provide belonging but also isolation.
  • Socioeconomic status — wealthier residents may perceive a place more positively; deprivation can create negative perceptions and desire to leave
  • Length of residence — long-term residents develop deeper roots; newcomers may feel like outsiders

Placelessness

Placelessness: The loss of unique place identity, leading to standardised, homogeneous landscapes that could be "anywhere". Associated with globalisation, chain stores, out-of-town retail parks, motorway service stations, and clone towns. Geographer Edward Relph (1976) described this as places becoming increasingly alike and lacking authentic character.

Key Theoretical Perspectives

Theorist/ConceptKey Ideas
Yi-Fu Tuan (1977) — TopophiliaThe affective bond between people and place. Our experience of place is shaped by perception, attitude, and values. Places become meaningful through lived experience.
Edward Relph (1976) — Place and PlacelessnessAuthentic sense of place comes from insider knowledge. Mass culture, tourism, and globalisation create placelessness. Distinguished between "existential insideness" (deep belonging) and "existential outsideness" (alienation).
Doreen Massey (1991) — Global Sense of PlacePlaces are not static or bounded; they are shaped by external connections and flows. A "progressive" sense of place embraces diversity and change rather than nostalgic, exclusionary identities. Places are nodes in a network of global relations.
David Harvey (1989) — Time-space compressionAdvances in transport and communication have "shrunk" the world, changing how we experience distance and place. This process benefits some (the mobile, wealthy) while marginalising others.

6. Changing Places — Processes of Change

Agents of Change

  • Government and local authorities — planning policy, regeneration schemes, infrastructure investment, Enterprise Zones
  • Corporate investment — TNCs locating in or leaving a place, retail developments
  • Community groups — grassroots activism, neighbourhood plans, conservation campaigns
  • Individuals — entrepreneurs, artists, cultural figures who reshape a place's identity
  • Developers — property developers and regeneration companies transforming physical landscapes

Processes of Change

Gentrification: The process by which wealthier people move into a run-down urban area, renovating housing and changing the social character. Property values rise, local services change (independent cafes, galleries replace discount stores), and original lower-income residents may be displaced through rising rents.
Regeneration: The planned improvement of a declining urban or rural area through investment in infrastructure, housing, employment, and public spaces. Often led by government or public-private partnerships.
Deindustrialisation: The decline of traditional manufacturing industries, leading to job losses, population decline, dereliction, and social deprivation. Affected many UK cities from the 1970s onwards (e.g. Sheffield steel, Liverpool docks, Newcastle shipbuilding).
Rebranding: Changing the image and reputation of a place to attract investment, tourism, and new residents. Often involves marketing campaigns, flagship developments, and cultural initiatives.

7. Placemaking

Placemaking: The deliberate process of designing, creating, and managing public spaces to promote community well-being, social interaction, and a strong sense of place. It involves collaboration between planners, architects, residents, and businesses.

Strategies for Placemaking

  • Flagship developments — iconic buildings or projects that transform a place's image (e.g. Tate Modern, Guggenheim Bilbao)
  • Heritage conservation — preserving historic buildings and landscapes to maintain identity and attract tourism
  • Public art and cultural events — murals, sculptures, festivals that create distinctive local character
  • Green spaces — parks, urban forests, and community gardens that improve well-being and environmental quality
  • Community-led initiatives — neighbourhood plans, community land trusts, social enterprises
  • Transport improvements — new rail links, cycling infrastructure, pedestrianisation

8. Case Studies

Case Study: Stratford, East London — Olympic Regeneration
Stratford was one of the most deprived areas in the UK before the 2012 Olympics. The area suffered from deindustrialisation, contaminated brownfield land, poor transport, and high deprivation. The Olympic bid aimed to use the Games as a catalyst for regeneration. Key changes: Queen Elizabeth Olympic Park (560 acres of public space), Westfield Stratford City shopping centre (one of Europe's largest), new housing (East Village — 2,800 homes on the former Athletes' Village), improved transport (Crossrail/Elizabeth Line, DLR extensions, Stratford International). Outcomes: significant physical transformation, job creation (~30,000), improved infrastructure. However, critics argue that regeneration has displaced existing communities, raised housing costs, and benefits have not equally reached long-term residents. Average house prices in Newham rose significantly, and the promised affordable housing targets were not fully met.
Case Study: Cornwall — Rural Place Change
Cornwall is a rural county in SW England experiencing significant place change. Historically dependent on tin mining (declined by 1990s), fishing, and agriculture. Today, tourism dominates the economy (5 million visitors/year). Key changes: rebranding as a cultural destination (Tate St Ives, Eden Project, Minack Theatre), growth of second-home ownership (up to 13% of homes in some parishes), in-migration of retirees, and seasonal employment patterns. Positive impacts: reduced unemployment, cultural vitality, environmental conservation (AONB status). Negative impacts: high house prices (average 12x local income), hollowed-out communities in winter, loss of Cornish identity and language, transport challenges, seasonal job insecurity. The tension between preserving authentic place identity and embracing economic change is a key theme.
Case Study: Detroit, USA — Deindustrialisation and Renewal
Detroit exemplifies the cycle of decline following deindustrialisation. Once the centre of the US auto industry (peak population 1.85 million in 1950), Detroit lost over 60% of its population as car manufacturers relocated. The city filed for bankruptcy in 2013. Impacts: widespread dereliction, high crime, racial inequality, failing public services. Recent renewal: urban farming (700+ community gardens), arts-led regeneration (Heidelberg Project), tech start-ups, the revival of downtown, and new investment. Detroit's story shows that decline is not permanent but recovery is uneven — downtown is transforming while many neighbourhoods remain deeply deprived.
Exam Tip: AQA requires you to study a local place (where you live or study) and a contrasting distant place. For the exam, be prepared to use specific data (census, IMD, photos) and qualitative evidence (interviews, personal experience) to compare how and why these places are changing. Always link changes to wider processes (globalisation, government policy, economic shifts).
Exam Tip: The Changing Places topic is heavily focused on concepts and perspectives. Use geographical terminology precisely: sense of place, placelessness, insider/outsider, representation, identity. Reference theoretical perspectives (Tuan, Relph, Massey) where appropriate to show analytical depth.
