2.1 Changing Places

1. The Nature and Importance of Places

Place: A location that has been given meaning by people through lived experience, memory, and attachment. Places are more than physical locations — they carry social, cultural, economic, and emotional significance. Place is a central concept in human geography, connecting identity, belonging, and experience to specific locations. As geographer Yi-Fu Tuan argued, a place is a "centre of meaning" constructed by experience.
Space vs Place: Space is an abstract, undefined area with no particular human meaning — a geometric concept. Place is a space that has been given meaning through human experience, memory, naming, and attachment. A coordinate on a map is a space; your home, your school, your neighbourhood are places. The transformation from space to place occurs through human engagement.

Key Concepts of Place

  • Location — the geographical position of a place. Absolute location: precise coordinates (latitude/longitude). Relative location: position in relation to other places, transport links, or features (e.g. "20 minutes from Manchester city centre").
  • Locale — the physical setting in which social relations and interactions occur. It includes the built environment, layout, and tangible characteristics of a place (e.g. a marketplace, a park, a factory floor, a high street). The locale shapes how people use and experience the place.
  • Sense of place — the subjective, emotional attachment people feel towards a place. This is deeply personal and varies between individuals. It develops through repeated experience, memories, relationships, and cultural associations.
  • Perception of place — how an individual or group views and interprets a place, shaped by direct experience, media representations, education, cultural background, and identity. Perception may or may not match reality.
  • Spirit of place (genius loci) — the unique atmosphere or character that distinguishes one place from another. This is what makes Paris feel different from New York, or a rural village different from a housing estate.

Insider and Outsider Perspectives

PerspectiveDescriptionCharacteristicsExample
InsiderSomeone who lives in or has deep personal experience of a placeSubjective, emotional, detailed knowledge, sense of belonging, shaped by everyday experience, may take features for grantedA lifelong resident of a mining village who remembers the pit, the community spirit, the decline

Relph (1976) described a spectrum from existential insideness (deep, unselfconscious belonging — "this is where I truly belong") to existential outsideness (feeling alienated, estranged, and disconnected from a place). Most people's experience falls somewhere between these extremes.

Exam Tip: AQA expects you to understand that place meaning is contested — different groups (long-term residents, newcomers, developers, tourists, local government, ethnic minorities) may have very different views of the same place. Always consider multiple perspectives in your answers and explain why perspectives differ.

2. Factors Shaping Place Character

The character of a place is shaped by a combination of endogenous (internal) and exogenous (external) factors that interact and change over time:

Endogenous (Internal) Factors

  • Physical geography — relief, drainage, climate, soil, natural resources, natural hazard exposure. Physical characteristics fundamentally shape what a place can be: a coastal settlement differs from an inland valley town.
  • Location and accessibility — proximity to transport routes (motorways, railways, airports, ports), distance from other settlements, connectivity to markets and services.
  • Built environment — architecture, land use, infrastructure, heritage buildings, street layout, urban design. Buildings embody the economic and cultural history of a place (e.g. Victorian terraces reflect industrialisation, glass towers reflect globalised finance).
  • Demographic characteristics — age structure, ethnicity, socioeconomic composition, education levels, population density. These determine the social character of a place.
  • Cultural factors — religion, language, customs, traditions, community organisations, local identity and pride, artistic and musical heritage.
  • Economic characteristics — employment types (primary/secondary/tertiary/quaternary), income levels, economic history, dominant industries, levels of deprivation.
  • Historical factors — past events and processes that have left a mark: industrialisation, deindustrialisation, wartime bombing, migration waves, past planning decisions, heritage designations.

Exogenous (External) Factors

Places are also shaped by forces operating beyond the local scale:

  • Globalisation — global flows of capital, people, ideas, and culture can transform local places. TNCs opening or closing factories, chain stores replacing independents, cultural homogenisation, foreign direct investment decisions made in distant boardrooms.
  • International and national migration — in-migration and out-migration alter demographic, cultural, and economic character. Post-war migration transformed areas like Brick Lane (London), Sparkbrook (Birmingham), and Moss Side (Manchester).
  • Government policy — national and local policies on housing, transport, planning, regeneration, enterprise zones, devolution, and austerity shape places profoundly. E.g. New Town designations (Milton Keynes, 1967), Enterprise Zones, Housing Market Renewal.
  • Technology — digital connectivity, remote working, and social media reshape how places function and are perceived. The rise of e-commerce has devastated high streets while boosting warehouse districts.
  • Shifting flows of investment — decisions by investors, corporations, and governments to invest in or withdraw from specific places can transform them rapidly. Capital is highly mobile in a globalised world.
  • Media representation — how places are portrayed in news, film, TV, literature, and social media profoundly influences external perception and can attract or deter investment, tourism, and migration.

3. Representation of Place

Place representation: How a place is portrayed, depicted, or "constructed" by different agents (media, government, artists, residents, businesses). Representations can reinforce, challenge, or create specific images of a place. They are always selective — choosing what to include and exclude — and they carry the perspective, purpose, and bias of their creator. Representations shape perceptions and influence real-world decisions about investment, migration, tourism, and policy.

Formal (Official/Quantitative) Representations

  • Maps and GIS data — seemingly objective but choices about scale, projection, colour, and what to include carry implicit bias.
  • Census data and government statistics — ONS data on population, employment, health, education, crime. The Index of Multiple Deprivation (IMD) ranks every neighbourhood in England across 7 domains.
  • Planning documents — local development plans, regeneration strategies, housing strategies present an official vision of what a place should become.
  • Tourism marketing — councils and agencies create selective, positive images to attract visitors and investment. They emphasise heritage, scenery, and culture while omitting deprivation and social problems.
  • Academic studies — geographical surveys, demographic analyses, environmental assessments.

Informal (Unofficial/Qualitative) Representations

  • Film and television — fictional representations shape place image powerfully and enduringly. "Downton Abbey" romanticised the English countryside, "Peaky Blinders" rebranded Birmingham's industrial past, "The Full Monty" captured Sheffield's post-industrial decline.
  • Literature and poetry — writers create powerful sense of place through descriptive language. Dickens' London, Hardy's Wessex, Brontë's Yorkshire moors.
  • Photography and art — subjective visual representations that emphasise particular aspects. L.S. Lowry's paintings defined the industrial north in popular imagination.
  • Music — lyrics and cultural associations link places to genres and emotions. Manchester and indie/Britpop (Oasis, The Smiths), Liverpool and The Beatles, Nashville and country music.
  • Social media — user-generated content, Instagram images, TripAdvisor reviews, Twitter hashtags, and viral posts create real-time collective perceptions that can boost or damage a place's reputation.
  • Oral histories and personal accounts — individual stories reveal the lived experience of a place, capturing perspectives that statistics miss.
  • Newspaper reporting — can stigmatise or celebrate places through selective coverage. Negative reporting about crime or deprivation can become self-fulfilling.
Exam Tip: When analysing a representation of place in the exam (e.g. a photograph, map extract, text, or advertisement), always consider: (1) Who created it? (2) Why? What is its purpose? (3) What is included/emphasised? (4) What is excluded/downplayed? (5) What impression does it give? (6) How might different groups interpret it? (7) Is it biased? Every representation is selective — identify the selection.

4. Approaches to Studying Place

Quantitative Data

Numerical data that can be measured, counted, and analysed statistically:

  • Census data (population size, age, ethnicity, employment, housing tenure, qualifications)
  • Index of Multiple Deprivation (IMD) — composite measure across income, employment, education, health, crime, housing, and living environment. Published for every Lower Super Output Area (LSOA) in England.
  • Land use surveys, traffic counts, pedestrian flow counts, environmental quality index (EQI) scores
  • House price data (Land Registry), unemployment rates (ONS), crime statistics (police.uk)
  • GIS mapping, satellite imagery, OS maps

Qualitative Data

Non-numerical data that captures meaning, perception, experience, and emotion:

  • Semi-structured interviews and oral histories with residents, workers, and visitors
  • Photographs and visual surveys (photo elicitation: asking participants to photograph what matters to them)
  • Analysis of text (newspaper articles, literature, social media posts, council documents)
  • Field sketches, annotated photographs, soundscapes (recording the audio environment of a place)
  • Mental maps — asking people to draw their perception of an area reveals what matters to them, what they know, and what they avoid
  • Participant observation and ethnographic research (immersing oneself in a community)
Mental map: A person's internal, subjective representation of an area, shaped by their experiences, perceptions, and values. Mental maps reveal what places and features are important to an individual, where they feel safe or unsafe, and can expose social and spatial inequalities. They are inherently incomplete and biased — that is their value.

5. Place Identity, Attachment, and Belonging

Factors Influencing Sense of Place

  • Age — older residents may have deeper emotional attachments built over decades of lived experience; young people may identify more with virtual spaces or aspire to leave for opportunities elsewhere.
  • Gender — men and women may experience the same place differently (e.g. women's safety perceptions in public spaces, access to services, use of transport).
  • Sexuality — LGBTQ+ communities may feel welcome and visible in some places (e.g. Brighton, Manchester's Gay Village, Soho) and marginalised or unsafe in others.
  • Ethnicity and religion — cultural identity strongly shapes sense of belonging and place attachment. Ethnic enclaves (e.g. Brick Lane/Banglatown, Chinatown, Little Italy) can provide belonging, community support, and cultural continuity, but may also indicate limited integration or structural inequality.
  • Socioeconomic status — wealthier residents may perceive a place more positively and have more power to shape it; deprivation can create negative perceptions, stigma, and desire to leave.
  • Length of residence — long-term residents develop deeper roots and stronger attachment; newcomers may feel like outsiders, especially if the community is resistant to change.
  • Personal experience — positive memories (childhood, friendships, achievements) strengthen attachment; negative experiences (crime, discrimination, loss) can weaken it.

Placelessness and Globalisation

Placelessness: The loss of unique place identity, leading to standardised, homogeneous landscapes that could be "anywhere". Associated with globalisation, chain stores (clone towns), out-of-town retail parks, motorway service stations, identical suburban housing estates, and international hotel chains. Geographer Edward Relph (1976) argued that mass culture, mass tourism, and corporate standardisation strip places of their authentic character, creating "an environment without significant places."

The New Economics Foundation (NEF) coined the term "clone town" in 2004, finding that many UK high streets had become dominated by identical chain stores, making one town indistinguishable from another. Their research found that for every £1 spent in a local independent shop, 50–70p recirculated locally, compared to only 5–15p from chain stores.

6. How and Why Places Change

Processes of Change

  • Deindustrialisation — the decline of manufacturing industry, particularly in traditional industrial areas. In the UK, manufacturing employment fell from 7.9 million (1971) to 2.7 million (2020). Caused by globalisation (cheaper production overseas), automation, competition, and the shift to a service economy. Created widespread unemployment, deprivation, and loss of community identity in places like Sheffield, Glasgow, Teesside, and the Welsh Valleys.
  • Globalisation — integration into global economic networks can bring investment and jobs (e.g. Sunderland attracting Nissan in 1986) or cause decline (e.g. textile industries relocating to Bangladesh and Vietnam).
  • Government policy — planning decisions, regeneration programmes, infrastructure investment, austerity measures, housing policy, and devolution all reshape places. The New Town movement (1946–1970) created 32 new towns in the UK.
  • Gentrification — the process by which wealthier people move into a previously deprived area, renovating housing and changing the social character. Property values rise, independent shops are replaced by boutiques and coffee shops, and existing low-income residents may be displaced by rising rents.
  • Regeneration — planned investment to revitalise a declining area, improving infrastructure, housing, employment, and public spaces. Can be property-led, culture-led, community-led, or transport-led.
  • Migration — both international and internal migration transform the demographic, cultural, and economic character of places.
  • Suburbanisation, counter-urbanisation, and reurbanisation — shifts in where people choose to live reshape the character of both the places they leave and the places they move to.
  • Technological change — the rise of e-commerce, remote working, and the gig economy are transforming town centres, office districts, and residential areas.
Case Study: Shoreditch, London — Gentrification and Creative Regeneration
Shoreditch in East London has undergone dramatic transformation since the 1990s. Once a deprived, post-industrial area with high unemployment and low property values, it attracted artists, designers, and tech start-ups seeking cheap studio space. By the 2000s, galleries, bars, restaurants, and creative industries had established a thriving "scene". Property values soared: average house prices in Hackney (the borough containing Shoreditch) rose from ~£100,000 (2000) to ~£550,000 (2023). Tech companies (including "Silicon Roundabout" firms) located there, attracted by the creative cachet. Positive impacts: economic revitalisation, cultural vibrancy, job creation, improved public realm, reduced crime. Negative impacts: displacement of low-income residents (social housing waiting lists grew), loss of affordable workspace for the artists who created the area's appeal, independent shops replaced by chains, homogenisation of a previously diverse area, rising homelessness alongside rising wealth. Shoreditch illustrates the paradox of gentrification: the qualities that make a place attractive to newcomers are often destroyed by their arrival.
Case Study: Detroit, USA — Deindustrialisation and Decline
Detroit was the centre of the American automobile industry, home to General Motors, Ford, and Chrysler. Population peaked at 1.85 million (1950). Deindustrialisation, automation, competition from Japanese manufacturers, and the 2008 financial crisis caused catastrophic decline. Population fell to 639,000 (2020) — a loss of 65%. In 2013, Detroit became the largest US city to file for bankruptcy ($18 billion debt). Impacts: 78,000 abandoned buildings, 40% of streetlights non-functional, average home price of $7,500 (some houses sold for $1), crime rates among the highest in the US, life expectancy 6 years below national average. Recovery attempts: $1.8 billion in investment since bankruptcy, downtown revival (Dan Gilbert relocated Quicken Loans HQ, bringing 17,000 jobs), emerging tech sector, urban agriculture (1,400 urban farms), but benefits concentrated in central Detroit while outer neighbourhoods remain in severe decline. Detroit demonstrates how the withdrawal of a dominant industry can devastate an entire city's identity, economy, and population.
Case Study: Stratford, East London — Olympic Regeneration
Stratford was one of London's most deprived areas before being selected as the site for the 2012 Olympic Games. The Lower Lea Valley was a post-industrial landscape of contaminated brownfield land, scrapyards, and fragmented communities. Investment: £9.3 billion total Olympic investment; the Olympic Park (now Queen Elizabeth Olympic Park) created 560 acres of parkland, wetlands, and sports venues. The Athletes' Village became East Village — 2,818 homes (1,379 affordable). Westfield Stratford City shopping centre (175,000 m²) opened in 2011 with 300+ shops and 10,000 jobs. Transport: the Javelin shuttle, improved DLR and Overground, and Crossrail (Elizabeth Line) connections. Positive impacts: 11,000 new homes planned, 10,000+ new jobs, improved parkland and ecology (300+ species in the wetlands), educational facilities (UCL East campus). Negative impacts: displacement of existing businesses and residents (450 businesses relocated, many did not survive), rising property prices (average Newham house price rose from £215,000 in 2012 to £420,000 in 2023), gentrification concerns, legacy promises of affordable housing partially unmet (only 37% of new homes were affordable, below the 50% target). The regeneration was largely top-down and property-led, with limited community input in early stages.

7. Place-Making and Place Management

Place-making: The deliberate process of shaping a place's identity, character, and public spaces to improve quality of life, attract investment, and create a sense of community. It can be top-down (led by government or developers) or bottom-up (led by local communities and grassroots organisations).

Approaches to Place-Making

  • Flagship/property-led development — large-scale iconic projects to attract investment and change perceptions (e.g. the Shard, Tate Modern, Guggenheim Bilbao). Can catalyse wider regeneration but may not benefit local residents.
  • Culture-led regeneration — using arts, heritage, festivals, and creative industries to transform places (e.g. Baltic Gateshead, Northern Quarter Manchester, Hull UK City of Culture 2017).
  • Community-led initiatives — neighbourhood plans, community land trusts, co-operatives, social enterprises, community gardens, and local campaigns. More inclusive but often lack resources.
  • Heritage-led approaches — preserving and celebrating historical buildings, landscapes, and traditions to maintain identity (e.g. listed buildings, conservation areas, World Heritage Sites).
  • Transport-led regeneration — new transport links catalysing development (e.g. Crossrail/Elizabeth Line stimulating regeneration in East London, HS2 planned impacts on Birmingham).

Conflicts in Place-Making

Place-making is inherently contested. Key tensions include:

  • Preservation vs development — protecting heritage character vs allowing modernisation and growth.
  • Local needs vs external investment — community priorities may differ from developer interests.
  • Gentrification vs affordability — improving an area may displace the communities it aims to help.
  • Authenticity vs commercialisation — marketing a place's character may commodify and ultimately destroy it.
  • Whose place? — different groups contest ownership and meaning. Long-term residents, newcomers, businesses, government, and tourists may all claim different visions for a place.
Exam Tip: In the AQA Changing Places exam, you will likely be asked to analyse an unseen resource (photograph, map, data extract, text) and explain what it reveals about a place. Practice reading visual and textual sources critically: What does it tell us? What does it NOT tell us? Whose perspective is it? What further information would you need?
Exam Tip: For the 20-mark essay, you need to demonstrate knowledge of both your local place study and a contrasting distant place. Use specific named evidence: statistics (IMD scores, population data, house prices, employment figures), named locations, and named theorists (Tuan, Relph, Massey, Harvey). Structure: clear argument with a thesis statement, supporting paragraphs with evidence, counterarguments, and a judged conclusion.
Exam Tip: AQA values synoptic thinking in Changing Places. Link place change to other parts of the specification: globalisation (population topic), urbanisation (urban environments topic), climate change (water/carbon topic), migration (population topic). Show that places do not exist in isolation — they are shaped by wider processes.
