1.3 Hazards

1. The Concept of Hazard

Natural hazard: A natural event or process that has the potential to cause loss of life, injury, or damage to property, infrastructure, and livelihoods. A hazard only becomes a disaster when it significantly impacts a vulnerable population.
Disaster: A hazard event causing significant damage and disruption, exceeding a community's ability to cope using its own resources. The UN threshold: 10+ deaths, 100+ people affected, a state of emergency declared, or international assistance called for.

The hazard risk equation:

Risk = (Hazard × Vulnerability) / Capacity to Cope

Key concepts:

  • Vulnerability — the susceptibility of a population to the impacts of a hazard (influenced by poverty, population density, building quality, governance)
  • Resilience — the ability of a community to absorb the impact and recover
  • Capacity to cope — resources, knowledge, and infrastructure available for preparation, response, and recovery

Hazard Perception

People perceive hazard risk differently depending on experience, wealth, education, personality, and cultural/religious beliefs. Three common responses to hazards:

  • Fatalism — accepting hazards as inevitable or as "acts of God", doing little to prepare
  • Adaptation — adjusting behaviour and modifying the environment to reduce risk (e.g. building earthquake-resistant structures)
  • Fear — an extreme response that may lead to avoidance or out-migration from hazardous areas

Park's Hazard Response Model

This model describes the stages a community goes through after a disaster event: pre-disaster normality, the disaster event, a decline in quality of life, relief and rehabilitation, and finally reconstruction. The depth and duration of the decline depends on the severity of the event and the community's resilience. Recovery may return to the same level, exceed it ("build back better"), or remain below it.

2. Plate Tectonics

Plate tectonics: The theory that the Earth's lithosphere is divided into large rigid plates that move relative to one another on the asthenosphere (semi-molten upper mantle). Plate movements are driven by convection currents in the mantle, slab pull, and ridge push.

Earth's Internal Structure

  • Inner core — solid iron and nickel, ~5,500°C, ~1,200 km radius
  • Outer core — liquid iron and nickel, generates Earth's magnetic field, ~2,200 km thick
  • Mantle — semi-molten silicate rock, ~2,900 km thick. Upper mantle includes the rigid lithosphere and plastic asthenosphere
  • Crust — thin outer layer. Oceanic crust: thin (5–10 km), dense (basaltic). Continental crust: thick (25–70 km), less dense (granitic)

Driving Forces of Plate Movement

  • Mantle convection — heat from the core creates convection currents in the mantle. Rising magma at ridges pushes plates apart; sinking material at subduction zones pulls plates down.
  • Slab pull — the weight of a subducting plate (dense oceanic crust) pulls the rest of the plate with it. Now considered the dominant force.
  • Ridge push — gravity causes newly formed crust at mid-ocean ridges (which sits higher) to slide away from the ridge.

3. Volcanic Hazards

Volcano: An opening in the Earth's crust through which magma, ash, and gases are ejected. Volcanic eruptions range from gentle effusive flows (basaltic lava, low silica) to violent explosive eruptions (rhyolitic magma, high silica content).

Volcanic Hazards

  • Lava flows — streams of molten rock. Basaltic lava flows quickly but can be diverted. Rhyolitic lava is slower but more explosive.
  • Pyroclastic flows — superheated mixtures of gas, ash, and rock fragments travelling at 100–700 km/h, temperatures up to 1,000°C. Extremely lethal, virtually impossible to escape.
  • Lahars — volcanic mudflows formed when ash mixes with water (rainfall, snowmelt, or crater lake overflow). Can travel far from the volcano at high speed.
  • Tephra and ash fall — rock fragments ejected into the atmosphere. Ash can collapse roofs, disrupt aviation, contaminate water, and damage crops over huge areas.
  • Volcanic gases — CO2, SO2, H2S. Can cause acid rain, respiratory problems, and climate cooling (sulphate aerosols reflect sunlight).
  • Tsunami — volcanic eruptions on islands or submarine volcanoes can trigger tsunami waves.
Case Study: Mount Merapi, Indonesia (2010)
Mount Merapi is one of the world's most active composite volcanoes, located on a destructive plate boundary (Indo-Australian plate subducting beneath the Eurasian plate). The 2010 eruption produced pyroclastic flows that killed 353 people and displaced 350,000. Lahars caused further damage during the monsoon season. Response: the Indonesian government evacuated 20,000 people before the eruption and established a 20 km exclusion zone. However, many residents returned prematurely to tend livestock. The eruption highlighted the tension between scientific warnings and local livelihoods. Indonesia's volcano monitoring has since improved, with real-time seismic and GPS data.

4. Seismic Hazards

Earthquake: A sudden release of energy in the Earth's crust, generating seismic waves. Most earthquakes occur at plate boundaries due to friction and stress buildup along faults.

Key Terminology

  • Focus (hypocentre) — the point within the Earth where the earthquake originates. Shallow-focus (<70 km), intermediate (70–300 km), deep (>300 km).
  • Epicentre — the point on the Earth's surface directly above the focus.
  • Seismic waves — P-waves (primary, compressional, fastest), S-waves (secondary, shear), and surface waves (Love and Rayleigh waves, most destructive).

Measuring Earthquakes

  • Moment magnitude scale (Mw) — measures total energy released. Logarithmic (each whole number = ~32x more energy). Open-ended. Has replaced the Richter scale for large quakes.
  • Modified Mercalli Intensity scale — measures observed effects (shaking, damage) on a I–XII scale. Useful for mapping damage patterns but subjective.

Earthquake Hazards

  • Ground shaking — the primary hazard, causing building collapse (the leading cause of death in earthquakes)
  • Liquefaction — saturated, loose soil behaves like a liquid during shaking, causing buildings to sink/tilt (e.g. Christchurch 2011)
  • Landslides — shaking destabilises slopes, triggering mass movements
  • Tsunami — submarine earthquakes displacing the ocean floor generate enormous waves
  • Fires — ruptured gas mains and electrical damage cause fires that can spread rapidly (e.g. San Francisco 1906)
Case Study: Nepal Earthquake (2015)
A magnitude 7.8 earthquake struck on 25 April 2015, with the epicentre 80 km NW of Kathmandu and a shallow focus of 8.2 km. Caused by the collision of the Indian and Eurasian plates. Impacts: 8,900 deaths, 22,000 injuries, 600,000 homes destroyed, heritage sites in Kathmandu Valley devastated. Landslides and avalanches on Mount Everest killed 22. Nepal's GDP dropped by 8%. Response challenges: poor infrastructure, mountainous terrain hindered aid delivery, corruption slowed reconstruction. Long-term: Nepal adopted new building codes and strengthened its disaster preparedness. International aid exceeded $4 billion but reconstruction took years.
Case Study: Tohoku Earthquake and Tsunami, Japan (2011)
A magnitude 9.0 earthquake struck off the coast of NE Japan on 11 March 2011 (Pacific plate subducting beneath the Okhotsk/North American plate). It triggered a devastating tsunami with waves up to 40 m high. Impacts: ~18,500 dead/missing, 470,000 displaced, Fukushima nuclear meltdown, $235 billion damage (costliest natural disaster ever). Despite Japan's advanced preparedness (building codes, tsunami walls, early warning systems), the magnitude exceeded expectations. The 10 m sea walls at Fukushima were overtopped by 14 m waves. Japan's response was rapid but highlighted that even the most prepared nations face limits.

5. Tropical Storms

Tropical storm: An intense low-pressure weather system with sustained wind speeds of at least 119 km/h (74 mph). Known as hurricanes (Atlantic/NE Pacific), typhoons (NW Pacific), or cyclones (Indian Ocean/S Pacific).

Formation Conditions

  • Sea surface temperature ≥ 27°C to a depth of ~60 m
  • Location at least 5° from the equator (for the Coriolis effect to initiate spin)
  • Low wind shear (uniform wind speed at different altitudes)
  • Atmospheric instability (warm, moist air rising rapidly)
  • Pre-existing disturbance (e.g. tropical wave, ITCZ convergence)

Structure of a Tropical Storm

  • Eye — calm centre, 20–40 km diameter, clear skies, descending warm air, lowest pressure
  • Eyewall — ring of the most intense cumulonimbus clouds, strongest winds, heaviest rainfall
  • Rainbands — spiral bands of cloud and rain extending hundreds of km from the centre

Tropical Storm Hazards

  • Extreme winds — Category 5 storms exceed 252 km/h. Destroy buildings, uproot trees, create dangerous flying debris.
  • Storm surge — a dome of water pushed ashore by wind and low pressure. Often the deadliest hazard. Can exceed 6 m.
  • Intense rainfall and flooding — can dump 500+ mm of rain. Causes river and flash flooding, landslides.
  • Tornadoes — can be spawned within rainbands.

Case Study: Typhoon Haiyan, Philippines (2013)
One of the strongest tropical cyclones ever recorded, with sustained winds of 315 km/h (Category 5). Struck the Visayas region on 8 November 2013. Impacts: over 6,300 deaths, 4 million displaced, 1.1 million homes damaged/destroyed, storm surge of up to 5 m devastated Tacloban city. The Philippines' poverty (25% below the poverty line), informal housing, and high population density amplified vulnerability. International response: over $1.5 billion in aid, but distribution was hampered by destroyed infrastructure. Long-term: relocation programmes, improved early warning systems, "build back better" approach adopted.

6. Wildfires

Wildfire: An uncontrolled fire in an area of combustible vegetation. They are a natural part of many ecosystems (e.g. savannahs, boreal forests) but are increasingly intensified by climate change, droughts, and human land management.

Causes of Wildfires

  • Natural: lightning strikes, volcanic eruptions, spontaneous combustion of dry material
  • Human: arson, unattended campfires, agricultural burning, power line sparks, discarded cigarettes (~85% of wildfires have human causes)
  • Climate factors: prolonged drought, high temperatures, low humidity, strong winds (e.g. Santa Ana winds in California, Fohn winds in Mediterranean)

The Fire Triangle

Three elements needed: fuel (vegetation, dead organic matter), oxygen (supplied by wind), and heat (ignition source plus ambient temperature). Remove any element and the fire cannot sustain itself.

Case Study: Australian Bushfires (2019–2020)
The "Black Summer" bushfires burned over 18.6 million hectares across NSW, Victoria, and other states. Causes: record-breaking drought and heat (2019 was Australia's hottest and driest year on record), combined with strong winds. Impacts: 34 deaths, over 3,000 homes destroyed, an estimated 3 billion animals killed or displaced, AUS$100 billion damage. Sydney experienced hazardous air quality for weeks. The fires released an estimated 830 million tonnes of CO2. Management: Australia uses controlled burning (hazard reduction), firebreaks, building codes in fire-prone areas, and community warning systems. The scale of these fires overwhelmed resources, leading to military deployment. The link to climate change intensified political debate about fossil fuel dependence.

7. Hazard Management Strategies

The Hazard Management Cycle

  1. Mitigation — long-term measures to reduce impact (e.g. building codes, land-use planning, flood defences)
  2. Preparedness — planning for the event (e.g. early warning systems, evacuation plans, emergency drills, stockpiling supplies)
  3. Response — immediate actions during/after the event (e.g. search and rescue, emergency shelters, medical aid)
  4. Recovery — returning to normality (e.g. rebuilding, economic recovery, psychological support). Short-term (relief) and long-term (rehabilitation/reconstruction).

Exam Tip: AQA often asks you to compare the impacts of hazards in countries at different levels of development. Use specific named examples and consider: governance quality, wealth, infrastructure, technology, education, population density, and the nature of the hazard itself. A moderate earthquake in a poor country can kill more people than a severe one in a wealthy country.
Exam Tip: Remember that hazard impacts are categorised as primary (direct results of the hazard, e.g. building collapse from shaking) and secondary (indirect consequences, e.g. fires after earthquake, disease outbreaks from contaminated water). Always distinguish between these in your answers.
