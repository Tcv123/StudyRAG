2.2 Contemporary Urban Environments

1. Urbanisation

Urbanisation: The increasing proportion of a country's population living in urban areas. In 2007, the global urban population exceeded 50% for the first time; by 2024 it reached ~57%, and by 2050 it is projected to reach 68% (UN). Urbanisation is driven by rural-urban migration and natural increase within cities. Currently, ~4.4 billion people live in urban areas.

Causes of Urbanisation

  • Rural push factors: agricultural mechanisation (reducing farm labour demand), land shortages and fragmentation, lack of services (schools, hospitals), natural disasters and environmental degradation, poverty, conflict and insecurity.
  • Urban pull factors: employment opportunities in manufacturing and services, higher wages (urban wages in LICs can be 2–4x rural wages), education and training opportunities, healthcare access, social amenities and cultural attractions, perceived better quality of life.
  • Natural increase: young migrant populations (15–30 age group) have higher birth rates. In many LIC cities, natural increase now contributes more to urban growth than migration.
  • Reclassification: expanding urban boundaries absorb previously rural settlements.

Megacity: An urban agglomeration with a population exceeding 10 million. In 1970 there were 3 megacities (Tokyo, New York, Osaka); by 2024 there are 35+, with 27 in Asia and Africa. The fastest-growing megacities are in LICs/MICs: Lagos, Dhaka, Kinshasa, Karachi.

Related Processes in HICs

Suburbanisation: The outward growth of cities into surrounding rural areas, creating low-density residential landscapes. Driven by rising car ownership (UK car ownership rose from 30% in 1960 to 78% in 2020), desire for space and gardens, cheaper land on the urban fringe, construction of ring roads and rail links, and government housing policy (inter-war council estates, post-war new towns). Creates car-dependent, sprawling communities with limited public transport.
Counter-urbanisation: The movement of people from cities to smaller towns, villages, and rural areas. Accelerated since 2020 by remote working (post-COVID). Driven by: retirement migration, telecommuting, desire for rural lifestyle and community, congestion/pollution/crime in cities, improved broadband, lower house prices (initially). Impacts on rural areas: rising house prices (pricing out local young people), dormitory villages, loss of rural services (as newcomers commute), changed village character, social tension between newcomers and established residents, increased car use.
Reurbanisation: The movement of people back into city centres after a period of decline and depopulation. Driven by urban regeneration, gentrification, young professionals seeking proximity to work and nightlife, conversion of former industrial/warehouse buildings into apartments, and improved urban amenities. Manchester's city centre population grew from 900 (2001) to ~70,000 (2023) through extensive apartment construction.

2. Urban Forms and Internal Structure

Classic Urban Models

Burgess Concentric Zone Model (1925): Based on 1920s Chicago. Urban areas grow outward from the CBD in concentric rings. The transition zone (zone 2) has mixed land use, older housing, and immigrant communities. Outer zones are progressively newer, lower-density, and wealthier. Assumes a flat, featureless plain with uniform transport, a single CBD, and no planning intervention.

Hoyt Sector Model (1939): Modified Burgess by recognising that land use develops in sectors (wedges) along transport routes rather than neat rings. Industry follows railways/roads; high-class housing avoids industry and seeks elevated, attractive sites. Working-class housing clusters near industrial sectors. More realistic for many cities.

Harris and Ullman Multiple Nuclei Model (1945): Cities develop around several distinct centres (nuclei), not just one CBD. Shopping centres, industrial parks, universities, and airports each create their own zones. Most applicable to modern polycentric cities like Los Angeles.

Limitations of all three models: Based on Western cities of specific eras, assume free-market land use (ignore planning), don't account for physical geography (rivers, hills), don't reflect post-industrial restructuring, gentrification, edge cities, or multicultural diversity. Modern cities are far more complex and polycentric. However, they remain useful frameworks for understanding urban structure.

New Urban Landscapes

  • Edge cities — self-contained urban centres on the outskirts of a major city, with offices, retail, entertainment, and housing (e.g. Canary Wharf London, Tysons Corner Virginia). Coined by Joel Garreau (1991).
  • Fortress landscapes — gated communities, extensive CCTV, privatised public spaces, security guards, and defensible architecture reflecting inequality and fear of crime. Common in US cities and increasingly in UK (e.g. St George Wharf, London).
  • Town centre/CBD decline — competition from online retail (UK online retail share grew from 5% in 2008 to 27% in 2023), out-of-town shopping centres, and edge cities. UK town centre vacancy rates averaged ~14% in 2023.
  • Cultural/creative quarters — regenerated areas focused on arts, media, and nightlife (e.g. Northern Quarter Manchester, Shoreditch London, Baltic Triangle Liverpool).
  • Post-industrial landscapes — former industrial areas converted to mixed-use developments (e.g. Salford Quays/MediaCityUK, King's Cross/Coal Drops Yard London, Tate Modern at Bankside).
  • Suburban intensification — increasing density in suburbs through infill development, garden grabbing, and conversion of houses to flats.

3. Social and Economic Issues

Urban Deprivation and the Index of Multiple Deprivation

Multiple deprivation: The combination of several forms of disadvantage — low income, poor housing, unemployment, ill health, limited education, high crime, and poor living environment — concentrated in specific urban areas. Measured in England by the Index of Multiple Deprivation (IMD), which ranks 32,844 Lower Super Output Areas (LSOAs) across 7 domains: income, employment, education, health, crime, housing/services, and living environment.

Inequality in Cities

  • Income inequality — widening gap between richest and poorest neighbourhoods within the same city. London is the most unequal region in the UK: the top 10% earn 9x the bottom 10%.
  • Housing inequality — unaffordable housing (average UK house price to earnings ratio: 8.3:1, rising to 13:1 in London), homelessness (309,000 people in temporary accommodation in England, 2023), overcrowding, poor-quality private renting, and gentrification-driven displacement.
  • Health inequality — life expectancy can vary by 10–20 years between affluent and deprived areas of the same city. In London, male life expectancy ranges from 71.4 years in Barking and Dagenham to 84.5 years in Kensington. The "Marmot Review" (2010) showed health is strongly linked to socioeconomic status.
  • Education inequality — school quality and attainment vary dramatically between postcodes, perpetuating cycles of disadvantage.
  • Digital divide — unequal access to broadband, digital skills, and technology, excluding some communities from online services, education, and employment.
  • Food deserts — deprived urban areas where residents lack access to affordable, nutritious food. Fast food outlets are 5x more concentrated in deprived areas than affluent ones (Public Health England).

Cultural Diversity in Cities

Migration has made cities culturally diverse. London has 270+ nationalities and 300+ languages. This brings benefits (cultural richness, entrepreneurship, labour supply, food/music/arts, innovation, global connections) and challenges (segregation, integration tensions, pressure on services, discrimination, language barriers). Ethnic enclaves (e.g. Southall/Little India, Brixton/Caribbean community, Brick Lane/Banglatown) can provide supportive communities, cultural preservation, and economic networks for newcomers, but may also indicate structural barriers to integration, housing discrimination, or self-segregation.

4. Urban Climate

Urban Heat Island (UHI): The phenomenon where urban areas are significantly warmer than surrounding rural areas. Typically 2–5°C warmer on average, but up to 10°C warmer on calm, clear nights. London's UHI effect averages ~2°C but has reached 9°C during heatwaves. The UHI was first described by Luke Howard in 1818 and has been extensively documented since.

Causes of the UHI Effect

  • Thermal properties of surfaces — concrete, tarmac, brick, and glass absorb shortwave radiation during the day and re-radiate it as longwave radiation at night, much more than vegetation and soil. Dark surfaces (roads, roofs) have low albedo (reflect only 5–15% of incoming radiation).
  • Reduced evapotranspiration — lack of vegetation means less cooling through water evaporation. Cities may have only 10–20% green cover compared to 70–90% in surrounding rural areas.
  • Anthropogenic heat — waste heat from factories, vehicles, air conditioning, heating systems, and human metabolism. In winter, anthropogenic heat in city centres can exceed solar input. London produces ~130 W/m² of anthropogenic heat.
  • Air pollution — particulates and greenhouse gases form a "pollution dome" that traps outgoing longwave radiation and reduces heat loss from the urban surface.
  • Reduced wind speeds — tall buildings increase surface roughness, reducing average wind speeds by 20–30% and limiting heat dispersal. Buildings disrupt airflow and create calm zones.
  • Urban canyon effect — tall buildings on narrow streets create "canyons" that trap radiation through multiple reflections between walls and limit sky view factor, reducing longwave radiation escape.
  • Reduced sky view factor — in dense urban areas, buildings block the view of the sky, trapping heat and reducing radiative cooling at night.

Other Urban Climate Effects

  • Precipitation — cities receive 5–15% more rainfall than rural surroundings due to: convective uplift from UHI warming, hygroscopic particulates acting as condensation nuclei (increasing cloud formation), and mechanical turbulence around buildings. Thunderstorms are more frequent downwind of cities.
  • Wind — urban areas alter wind patterns. The Venturi effect funnels wind through narrow streets, increasing speeds (can reach 2–3x background speed). Tall buildings cause downdrafts and eddies. Average wind speed across the city is reduced due to friction with buildings.
  • Air quality — higher concentrations of pollutants: NOx (from traffic), PM2.5 and PM10 (particulates from diesel engines, construction, tyre wear), ground-level O3 (formed by sunlight reacting with NOx and VOCs), SO2 (from burning fossil fuels). Temperature inversions (warm air above cold air) trap pollution at ground level, especially in winter.
  • Fog and humidity — cities may experience more smog (pollution-related fog), but reduced fog in UHI conditions as warmth prevents condensation. Relative humidity is generally 5–10% lower in cities due to rapid surface water runoff and reduced evapotranspiration.

5. Urban Drainage and Flood Risk

Urbanisation significantly alters the hydrological cycle, increasing flood risk:

  • Impermeable surfaces (roads, pavements, roofs, car parks) cover 40–90% of urban land, reducing infiltration and increasing surface runoff by 2–6 times compared to natural surfaces.
  • Drainage systems (gutters, drains, sewers, culverted streams) accelerate water transfer to rivers, reducing lag time and increasing peak discharge.
  • Reduced storage capacity — loss of wetlands, paved-over front gardens (in London, 12 km² of front gardens were paved between 1998 and 2008), removal of vegetation, and building on floodplains.
  • Result: flashier storm hydrographs (shorter lag time, higher peak discharge, steeper rising limb), higher flood risk, reduced groundwater recharge, and increased pollution in waterways from urban runoff.
  • Climate change amplifies risk — more intense rainfall events increase surface water flooding. The UK Climate Projections (UKCP18) suggest winter rainfall could increase by 10–30% by 2070.

Sustainable Urban Drainage Systems (SuDS)

  • Permeable paving — allows water to infiltrate through car parks, driveways, and footpaths. Water passes through gaps between blocks into a stone reservoir beneath, from where it infiltrates into the ground.
  • Green roofs — vegetation planted on rooftops absorbs rainfall (retaining 50–90% of rainfall) and reduces runoff. Also provides insulation, reduces UHI, and creates habitat. Used extensively in Germany (>100 million m²) and increasingly in UK cities.
  • Swales — shallow, vegetated channels alongside roads that slow and filter surface runoff, allowing infiltration.
  • Retention/detention ponds — hold water temporarily during storms, releasing it slowly to prevent downstream flooding.
  • Rain gardens and bioswales — planted depressions that collect, absorb, and filter runoff from surrounding impermeable surfaces.
  • Rainwater harvesting — collecting roof water in tanks for garden irrigation, toilet flushing, or industrial use, reducing demand on mains supply and delaying runoff.
  • Attenuation tanks — underground storage tanks that hold stormwater temporarily.

6. Urban Waste and Pollution

Waste Management Strategies

  • Landfill — cheapest disposal method but produces methane (potent greenhouse gas), leachate (contaminating groundwater), odour, and uses scarce land. UK landfill has decreased from 80% of waste (2000) to ~23% (2023) as sites near capacity and landfill tax increases.
  • Incineration (energy from waste) — reduces waste volume by ~90%, generates electricity and heat. Modern plants have filters to reduce emissions. Controversial: communities resist local siting (NIMBYism), still produces CO2 and ash residues.
  • Recycling — conserves resources and reduces landfill. UK recycling rate ~44% (2023), below EU leaders (Germany 66%, Slovenia 60%). Challenges: contamination, market fluctuations for recycled materials, and confusion about what can be recycled.
  • Circular economy — designing products for longevity, reuse, repair, remanufacture, and recycling to minimise waste. Aims to eliminate the concept of "waste" entirely.
  • Composting — biological decomposition of organic waste (food scraps, garden waste) into nutrient-rich compost for soil improvement.
  • Extended producer responsibility — making manufacturers responsible for the entire lifecycle of their products, including disposal and recycling.

Air Pollution

Key urban pollutants: NOx (from vehicle engines, especially diesel), PM2.5 and PM10 (fine and coarse particulates from exhaust, tyre/brake wear, construction), SO2 (from burning fossil fuels), ground-level O3 (secondary pollutant formed by sunlight reacting with NOx and VOCs). Health impacts: respiratory disease (asthma, COPD), cardiovascular disease, lung cancer, premature death. The WHO estimates air pollution causes 7 million premature deaths globally per year. In the UK, air pollution contributes to an estimated 28,000–36,000 premature deaths annually. Ella Adoo-Kissi-Debrah (aged 9) became the first person in the UK to have air pollution listed as a cause of death (2020), after living near the South Circular Road in Lewisham, London.

Case Study: London's Ultra Low Emission Zone (ULEZ)
Introduced in central London in April 2019 and expanded London-wide in August 2023. Vehicles that do not meet emission standards (Euro 4 petrol from 2006+, Euro 6 diesel from 2015+) are charged £12.50/day to drive in the zone. Aims: reduce NO2, PM2.5, and CO2 concentrations to improve public health. Results: roadside NO2 concentrations in central London fell ~44% (2017–2023), PM2.5 fell ~27%, 96% of vehicles now compliant. TfL estimates ULEZ prevented 4.3 million tonnes of CO2 emissions (2019–2023). Controversies: disproportionate impact on lower-income drivers who cannot afford compliant vehicles, small businesses reliant on older vans, outer-London residents with limited public transport alternatives. Government scrappage scheme offered £2,000 per vehicle but was oversubscribed and criticised as insufficient. Political backlash: ULEZ was a major issue in the 2023 Uxbridge by-election. The scheme illustrates the tension between environmental improvement and economic/social equity — a key geographical theme.

7. Urban Sustainability

Sustainable city: A city that meets the needs of the present population without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs (Brundtland definition applied to urban areas). Requires balancing economic growth, social equity, and environmental protection across all aspects of urban life: energy, transport, housing, waste, food, water, green spaces, and governance.

Case Study: Freiburg, Germany — Sustainable City
Freiburg (population ~230,000, south-west Germany) is widely regarded as one of Europe's most sustainable cities. Key features: The Vauban district (5,000 residents) is a car-free neighbourhood built on a former military barracks site with: passive solar housing (buildings oriented south, super-insulated, using 65% less energy than conventional homes), green roofs on all buildings, combined heat and power (CHP) plant, car-free streets (70% of residents do not own a car; parking is available only in multi-storey garages on the edge of the district). Transport: 500 km of cycle paths, 32% of trips by bike (highest in Germany), extensive tram network (9 lines, solar-powered), 20% of trips by public transport. Energy: Germany's "solar capital" — 10,000+ solar installations, Fraunhofer Solar Institute research, district heating from biomass and CHP, the solar settlement (Sonnenschiff) produces more energy than it consumes (energy-plus houses). Green spaces: 600+ hectares of protected urban forest, community gardens, biodiversity corridors. Governance: citizen participation is central to all planning decisions (mandatory public consultations, neighbourhood councils). Criticisms: rising housing costs (median rent ~30% above national average due to desirability), gentrification, car-free living is harder for elderly and disabled residents, and Freiburg's success may be difficult to replicate in larger, more complex cities.
Case Study: Mumbai, India — Megacity Challenges
Mumbai (population ~21 million, metropolitan area ~25 million) is India's financial capital, generating ~6.2% of national GDP. It is a megacity facing extreme urban challenges alongside remarkable economic dynamism. Dharavi, often called Asia's largest slum, houses an estimated 600,000–1 million people in just 2.1 km² (population density ~277,000/km²). Challenges: overcrowding, inadequate sanitation (one toilet per 1,000+ people in some areas), open sewers, contaminated water, air pollution (PM2.5 levels regularly 5–10x WHO safe limits), flooding (monsoon rainfall + coastal location + clogged drains + subsidence + sea-level rise), and acute spatial inequality (Antilia, the world's most expensive private residence at $2 billion, overlooks slums). However, Dharavi has a thriving informal economy worth ~$1 billion/year, with 5,000+ businesses in recycling, pottery, leather goods, and textiles employing ~250,000 workers. This demonstrates the complexity of "slums" — they are economically productive and socially cohesive, not just places of deprivation. Response: the controversial Dharavi Redevelopment Project (awarded to Adani Group in 2022) plans to replace slum housing with high-rise apartments, but residents fear displacement and loss of livelihoods. Mumbai's challenges illustrate the fundamental tension between rapid growth, economic development, social justice, and environmental sustainability in developing world megacities.

8. Urban Regeneration and Planning

Strategies for Urban Regeneration

  • Property-led regeneration — flagship developments and commercial investment to attract further private capital (e.g. London Docklands/Canary Wharf in the 1980s–90s: £12 billion investment, 120,000 jobs created, but criticised for ignoring local residents' needs and displacing existing communities).
  • Community-led regeneration — empowering local residents through neighbourhood plans, community land trusts (CLTs hold land permanently for community benefit), social enterprises, and participatory budgeting.
  • Culture-led regeneration — using arts, heritage, and creative industries to transform areas (e.g. Tate Modern at Bankside, Baltic Centre at Gateshead, Hull UK City of Culture 2017 — £300 million economic impact, 5.3 million visits).
  • Transport-led regeneration — new transport links catalysing development. Crossrail/Elizabeth Line (opened 2022, £18.9 billion) is expected to generate £42 billion in economic benefits and has stimulated regeneration in Woolwich, Abbey Wood, and Ealing.
  • Mixed-use development — combining residential, commercial, leisure, and community uses to create vibrant, walkable neighbourhoods (e.g. King's Cross Central, London: 67 acres, 2,000 homes, 50+ shops, 3.4 million sq ft offices, Central Saint Martins university, Coal Drops Yard).
Exam Tip: Urban regeneration questions often require evaluation. Always consider: (1) Who benefits? (Developers? Existing residents? Newcomers?) (2) Who loses? (Is there displacement/gentrification?) (3) Is the regeneration socially inclusive or does it widen inequality? (4) Are environmental improvements genuine or superficial (greenwashing)? (5) Is the regeneration sustainable long-term? Use specific case study evidence with data to support your arguments.
Exam Tip: AQA expects you to understand the connections between different urban issues. For example, the UHI effect links to energy use (more air conditioning demand), health inequality (heat-related illness disproportionately affects the elderly and deprived), urban drainage (intense UHI-driven rainfall overloads drains), and sustainability (green infrastructure addresses UHI, drainage, air quality, and well-being simultaneously). Drawing these connections demonstrates synoptic understanding and earns high marks.
Exam Tip: In 20-mark essays, evaluate whether urban problems are best solved through top-down approaches (government policy, large-scale investment, regulation) or bottom-up approaches (community action, local empowerment, grassroots initiatives). The best answers argue that both are needed and that the most successful regeneration combines strong governance with genuine community participation. Always support with contrasting named examples.
