2.2 Contemporary Urban Environments

1. Urbanisation

Urbanisation: The increasing proportion of a country's population living in urban areas. In 2007, the global urban population exceeded 50% for the first time; by 2050 it is projected to reach 68%. Urbanisation is driven by rural-urban migration and natural increase within cities.

Causes of Urbanisation

  • Rural push factors: agricultural mechanisation, land shortages, lack of services, natural disasters, poverty, conflict
  • Urban pull factors: employment opportunities, better wages, education, healthcare, social amenities, perceived better quality of life
  • Natural increase: young migrant populations have higher birth rates, contributing to urban growth

Related Processes in HICs

Suburbanisation: The outward growth of cities into surrounding rural areas. Driven by car ownership, desire for space, cheaper land on the urban fringe, and improved transport links. Creates low-density, residential landscapes.
Counter-urbanisation: The movement of people from cities to smaller towns and rural areas. Driven by retirement migration, remote working, desire for rural lifestyle, congestion and pollution in cities, and improved broadband. Can increase house prices and change the character of rural settlements.
Reurbanisation: The movement of people back into city centres after a period of decline. Driven by urban regeneration, gentrification, young professionals seeking proximity to work and nightlife, and conversion of former industrial buildings into apartments.

2. Urban Forms and Internal Structure

Classic Urban Models

Burgess Concentric Zone Model (1925): Based on Chicago. Urban areas grow outward from the CBD in concentric rings. Inner zones have older, higher-density housing; outer zones are newer, lower-density suburbs. The model assumes a flat, featureless plain with no transport variations.

Hoyt Sector Model (1939): Modified Burgess by recognising that land use develops in sectors along transport routes rather than in neat rings. Industry follows railways/roads; high-class housing avoids industry. More realistic than Burgess for many cities.

Limitations of both models: Based on Western cities of specific eras, assume single CBD, ignore physical geography (rivers, hills), don't account for planning intervention, gentrification, or edge cities. Modern cities are more polycentric and complex.

New Urban Landscapes

  • Edge cities — self-contained urban centres on the outskirts of a major city, with offices, retail, and housing (e.g. Canary Wharf, London)
  • Fortress landscapes — gated communities, CCTV, privatised public spaces reflecting inequality and fear of crime
  • Town centre/CBD decline — competition from online retail, out-of-town shopping centres, and edge cities
  • Cultural/creative quarters — regenerated areas focused on arts, media, and nightlife (e.g. Northern Quarter, Manchester)
  • Post-industrial landscapes — former industrial areas converted to mixed-use developments (e.g. Salford Quays)

3. Social and Economic Issues

Urban Deprivation

Multiple deprivation: The combination of several forms of disadvantage — low income, poor housing, unemployment, ill health, limited education, and high crime — concentrated in specific urban areas. Measured in the UK by the Index of Multiple Deprivation (IMD).

Inequality in Cities

  • Income inequality — widening gap between richest and poorest neighbourhoods within the same city
  • Housing inequality — unaffordable housing, homelessness, overcrowding, poor-quality private renting
  • Health inequality — life expectancy can vary by 10+ years between affluent and deprived areas of the same city (e.g. in London, Glasgow)
  • Education inequality — school quality varies, affecting social mobility
  • Digital divide — unequal access to broadband and technology

Cultural Diversity

Migration has made cities culturally diverse. This brings benefits (cultural richness, entrepreneurship, labour supply, food/music/arts) and challenges (segregation, integration tensions, pressure on services, discrimination). Ethnic enclaves can provide a supportive community for newcomers but may also indicate limited integration.

4. Urban Climate

Urban Heat Island (UHI): The phenomenon where urban areas are significantly warmer (typically 2–5°C, up to 10°C at night) than surrounding rural areas. Caused by heat absorption by dark surfaces, waste heat from buildings/vehicles, reduced evapotranspiration, and altered wind patterns.

Causes of the UHI Effect

  • Thermal properties of surfaces — concrete, tarmac, and brick absorb and re-radiate heat more than vegetation and soil
  • Reduced evapotranspiration — lack of vegetation means less cooling through water evaporation
  • Anthropogenic heat — waste heat from factories, vehicles, air conditioning, and heating systems
  • Air pollution — particulates form a "pollution dome" that traps outgoing longwave radiation
  • Reduced wind speeds — tall buildings create a rougher surface, reducing air movement and heat dispersal
  • Canyon effect — tall buildings on narrow streets trap radiation through multiple reflections

Other Urban Climate Effects

  • Precipitation — cities can receive 5–15% more rainfall due to convection from UHI, hygroscopic particulates acting as condensation nuclei, and enhanced uplift around buildings
  • Wind — urban areas alter wind patterns. Venturi effect (funnelling through narrow streets increases speed), downdrafts from tall buildings, reduced average wind speed due to friction
  • Air quality — higher concentrations of pollutants (NOx, PM2.5, O3) due to traffic, industry, and heating. Temperature inversions trap pollution at ground level.
  • Fog and cloud — cities may experience more fog (pollution-related) but less in UHI conditions when warmth prevents condensation

5. Urban Drainage and Flood Risk

Urbanisation significantly alters the hydrological cycle:

  • Impermeable surfaces reduce infiltration and increase surface runoff
  • Drains and sewers accelerate water transfer to rivers
  • Reduced storage capacity (loss of wetlands, paved gardens)
  • Result: flashier hydrographs, higher flood risk, reduced groundwater recharge

Sustainable Urban Drainage Systems (SuDS)

  • Permeable paving — allows water to infiltrate through car parks and driveways
  • Green roofs — vegetation on rooftops absorbs rainfall and reduces runoff
  • Swales — shallow vegetated channels that slow and filter surface runoff
  • Retention/detention ponds — hold water temporarily, releasing it slowly
  • Rain gardens — planted depressions that collect and absorb runoff
  • Rainwater harvesting — collecting roof water in tanks for reuse

6. Urban Waste and Pollution

Waste Management Strategies

  • Landfill — cheapest but produces methane, leachate, and uses land. Many sites nearing capacity.
  • Incineration (energy from waste) — reduces volume by 90%, generates electricity, but produces CO2 and pollutants
  • Recycling — conserves resources and reduces landfill. UK recycling rate ~44% (2023), below EU leaders at 60%+.
  • Circular economy — designing products for reuse, repair, and recycling to minimise waste
  • Composting — organic waste breakdown for soil improvement

Air Pollution

Key urban pollutants: NOx (from vehicles), PM2.5 and PM10 (particulates), SO2 (from burning fossil fuels), ground-level O3 (formed by sunlight reacting with NOx and VOCs). Health impacts: respiratory disease, cardiovascular disease, premature death. The WHO estimates air pollution causes 7 million premature deaths globally per year.

Case Study: London's Ultra Low Emission Zone (ULEZ)
Introduced in 2019 and expanded London-wide in 2023, the ULEZ charges vehicles that do not meet emission standards (Euro 4 petrol, Euro 6 diesel) £12.50/day to drive in the zone. Aims: reduce NO2, PM2.5, and CO2 levels. Results: NO2 concentrations fell by ~20% in central London, 94% of vehicles now compliant. Controversies: impact on lower-income drivers, small businesses, and outer-London residents who depend on older vehicles. Scrappage schemes offered but criticised as insufficient. The ULEZ illustrates the tension between environmental and economic/social justice goals.

7. Urban Sustainability

Sustainable city: A city that meets the needs of the present population without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs. This involves balancing economic growth, social equity, and environmental protection.

Case Study: Freiburg, Germany — Sustainable City
Freiburg is widely regarded as one of Europe's most sustainable cities (population ~230,000). Key features: Vauban district (car-free neighbourhood with passive solar housing, green roofs, combined heat and power), 500 km of cycle paths (30% of trips by bike), extensive tram network, solar panels on public buildings (producing more energy than consumed), 600+ hectares of urban forest. The city banned nuclear power in the 1970s and adopted renewable energy as policy. Green spaces and biodiversity corridors are protected. Citizen participation is central to planning. Critics note rising housing costs and gentrification driven by the city's desirability.
Case Study: Mumbai, India — Megacity Challenges
Mumbai (population ~21 million) is India's financial capital and a megacity facing extreme urban challenges. Dharavi, often called Asia's largest slum, houses an estimated 600,000–1 million people in just 2.1 km². Challenges: overcrowding, inadequate sanitation (one toilet per 1,000 people in some areas), open sewers, air pollution (PM2.5 levels regularly 5–10x WHO safe limits), flooding (monsoon + subsidence + clogged drains), and acute inequality (Antilia, the world's most expensive private residence, overlooks slums). However, Dharavi has a thriving informal economy worth ~$1 billion/year (recycling, pottery, leather, textiles). Mumbai's challenges illustrate the tension between rapid growth and sustainable development.

8. Urban Regeneration and Planning

Strategies for Urban Regeneration

  • Property-led regeneration — flagship developments to attract private investment (e.g. Canary Wharf, London Docklands)
  • Community-led regeneration — empowering local residents through neighbourhood plans, community land trusts, social enterprises
  • Culture-led regeneration — using arts, heritage, and creative industries to transform areas (e.g. Tate Modern, Baltic Gateshead)
  • Transport-led regeneration — new transport links catalysing development (e.g. Crossrail/Elizabeth Line stimulating regeneration in East London)
  • Mixed-use development — combining residential, commercial, and leisure to create vibrant, walkable neighbourhoods
Exam Tip: Urban regeneration questions often require evaluation. Always consider: Who benefits? Who loses? Is the regeneration socially inclusive or does it cause gentrification and displacement? Are environmental improvements genuine or superficial? Use specific case study evidence to support your arguments.
Exam Tip: For AQA, you need to understand the connections between different urban issues. For example, the UHI effect links to energy use (air conditioning), health inequality (heat-related illness affects the elderly and deprived disproportionately), and sustainability (green infrastructure addresses UHI, drainage, and well-being simultaneously).
