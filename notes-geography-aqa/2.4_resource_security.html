2.4 Resource Security

1. The Concept of Resource Security

Resource: Anything that is useful to people and can be extracted from the environment to meet human needs and wants. Resources can be renewable (can be replenished within a human lifetime, e.g. solar, wind, timber) or non-renewable (finite, e.g. fossil fuels, minerals).
Resource security: The ability of a country or population to access sufficient, affordable, and reliable resources to meet current and future needs. Insecurity occurs when supply cannot meet demand, leading to shortages, price spikes, and conflict.

2. Energy Security

Energy security: The uninterrupted availability of energy sources at an affordable price. This includes short-term security (ability to react to sudden supply disruptions) and long-term security (timely investment to supply energy in line with economic and environmental needs).

Global Energy Mix

The global energy mix is dominated by fossil fuels (~80%), though renewable energy is growing rapidly:

Factors Affecting Energy Security

  • Physical factors — geology (fossil fuel deposits), climate (solar/wind potential), hydrology (hydropower)
  • Geopolitics — concentration of supply (OPEC controls ~40% of oil production), political instability in oil-rich regions, pipeline politics (Russia-Europe gas dependency)
  • Economic factors — price volatility, investment in infrastructure, cost of renewables vs fossil fuels
  • Environmental factors — climate change mitigation requires energy transition, carbon taxes, regulations
  • Technology — fracking, deep-sea drilling, battery storage, smart grids, nuclear fusion research

Energy Strategies

  • Diversification — using a range of energy sources to reduce dependence on any single fuel
  • Renewable transition — investing in wind, solar, tidal, geothermal to reduce carbon emissions and import dependency
  • Energy efficiency — insulating buildings, efficient appliances, LED lighting, industrial process improvements
  • Nuclear power — low-carbon but controversial (waste storage, safety concerns, high construction costs)
  • Fracking (hydraulic fracturing) — extracting shale gas. Boosted US energy security but raises environmental concerns (water contamination, seismicity, methane leakage)
  • International cooperation — energy trade agreements, interconnectors (e.g. undersea electricity cables between UK and Europe)
Case Study: The UK's Energy Security Challenge
The UK faces a complex energy transition. North Sea oil and gas production peaked around 1999 and has declined, making the UK a net energy importer since 2004. The UK depends on Norway for ~30% of gas imports and is connected to European gas markets via pipelines and LNG terminals. The 2022 energy crisis (triggered by Russia's invasion of Ukraine) exposed vulnerabilities: gas prices surged 400%, causing a cost-of-living crisis. The UK government's response: energy price cap, windfall tax on oil companies, and the 2023 Energy Security Plan targeting 50 GW of offshore wind by 2030. Renewables now generate ~40% of UK electricity (wind is the largest single source). Controversies: debate over new North Sea oil licences (Rosebank field), nuclear new-build (Hinkley Point C delayed and over budget at £33+ billion), and onshore wind planning restrictions.

3. Water Security

Water security: The capacity of a population to safeguard access to adequate quantities of acceptable-quality water for sustaining human well-being, socioeconomic development, and protection against water-borne pollution and water-related disasters. The UN defines water scarcity as less than 1,000 m3/person/year.

Global Water Distribution

Water supply is unevenly distributed. Countries in arid/semi-arid climates (Middle East, North Africa, parts of South Asia) face physical water scarcity, while others have water but lack infrastructure to access it (economic water scarcity, common in Sub-Saharan Africa).

Factors Affecting Water Security

  • Climate — rainfall patterns, aridity, seasonal variability, glacier melt
  • Population growth — more people means greater demand for domestic, agricultural, and industrial water
  • Economic development — industrialisation and rising living standards increase per-capita water use
  • Agriculture — irrigation accounts for ~70% of global freshwater withdrawals
  • Pollution — industrial discharge, agricultural runoff (fertilisers, pesticides), sewage contaminate freshwater sources
  • Climate change — altered precipitation patterns, glacier retreat, more droughts and floods
  • Governance — water management, pricing, infrastructure investment, transboundary agreements

Case Study: Water Conflict — The Nile Basin
The Nile River (6,650 km) flows through 11 countries. Egypt depends on the Nile for 95% of its freshwater, but Ethiopia's Grand Ethiopian Renaissance Dam (GERD, completed 2023) on the Blue Nile has created major geopolitical tensions. Ethiopia argues the dam is essential for its development (generating 6,000 MW of hydroelectricity to lift millions from energy poverty). Egypt fears reduced downstream flow that could devastate agriculture (which relies on Nile irrigation) and threaten the water supply of 100+ million people. Sudan has fluctuated between supporting and opposing the dam. Negotiations have stalled repeatedly. The dispute illustrates how transboundary water resources create conflict, especially when combined with rapid population growth (Ethiopia 120M, Egypt 105M) and climate change (reduced rainfall projections for East Africa).

4. Food Security

Food security: When all people, at all times, have physical, social, and economic access to sufficient, safe, and nutritious food that meets their dietary needs for an active and healthy life (FAO definition). Food insecurity ranges from chronic hunger to malnutrition and obesity.

Global Food System Challenges

  • Unequal distribution — global food production is sufficient to feed everyone, but ~735 million people face chronic hunger while ~1 billion are overweight/obese
  • Food waste — ~1/3 of food produced globally is lost or wasted (~1.3 billion tonnes/year). In HICs, waste occurs at retail/consumer level; in LICs, at production/storage level.
  • Climate change — droughts, floods, heat stress, shifting growing seasons, and pest/disease range expansion threaten crop yields
  • Soil degradation — 33% of global soils are moderately to highly degraded. Erosion, salinisation, and nutrient depletion reduce productivity.
  • Water scarcity — agriculture accounts for 70% of global freshwater use. Aquifer depletion (e.g. Ogallala, North China Plain) threatens future production.
  • Land use competition — urbanisation, biofuel crops, and environmental conservation compete for agricultural land

Strategies to Improve Food Security

  • Green Revolution — high-yield varieties (HYVs), irrigation, fertilisers, and pesticides. Dramatically increased yields in Asia (1960s–80s) but raised environmental concerns (soil depletion, water overuse, chemical pollution).
  • Gene Revolution / GM crops — genetically modified organisms (GMOs) engineered for pest resistance, drought tolerance, or enhanced nutrition (e.g. Golden Rice with vitamin A). Controversial: concerns about biodiversity, corporate control of seed supply, long-term health effects.
  • Agroecology — sustainable farming methods: crop rotation, polyculture, organic practices, integrated pest management. Lower environmental impact but may produce lower yields.
  • Urban agriculture — growing food within cities (allotments, rooftop gardens, vertical farms). Reduces food miles and improves access.
  • Reducing food waste — better storage (hermetic silos in LICs), changed date-labelling, redistribution schemes (e.g. FareShare), composting
  • Fair trade and improved market access — ensuring smallholder farmers receive fair prices for their produce
  • Land reform — redistributing land from large estates to smallholders, securing tenure rights
Case Study: The Sahel — Food Insecurity
The Sahel (semi-arid belt across Africa south of the Sahara, including Mali, Niger, Burkina Faso, and Chad) is one of the world's most food-insecure regions. ~30 million people regularly face hunger. Causes: low and erratic rainfall (200–600 mm/yr), desertification, soil degradation, rapid population growth (Niger's TFR ~7.0, the world's highest), political instability and conflict (Boko Haram, Mali civil war), poverty, and climate change (temperatures rising 1.5x faster than global average). Responses: the Great Green Wall initiative (planting a belt of trees across 8,000 km to combat desertification), farmer-managed natural regeneration (FMNR), improved drought-resistant crop varieties (millet, sorghum), and international food aid. The Great Green Wall has restored ~18 million hectares of degraded land since 2007, but progress is slower than planned. Long-term solutions require addressing governance, conflict, and population growth alongside agricultural innovation.

5. Resource Futures

The Circular Economy

Circular economy: An economic model that eliminates waste by designing products for durability, reuse, repair, and recycling. Contrasts with the traditional "linear economy" (take, make, dispose). Aims to keep materials in use for as long as possible and regenerate natural systems.

Natural Resource Depletion and Sustainability

  • Peak oil theory — the point at which global oil production reaches its maximum and begins to decline. Debate continues about timing, but the IEA suggests peak demand may come first (by 2030) as renewables and EVs grow.
  • Critical minerals — lithium, cobalt, rare earth elements are essential for batteries, electronics, and renewable energy. Concentrated in a few countries (DRC cobalt, China rare earths), creating new geopolitical dependencies.
  • Sustainable development — the UN's 17 Sustainable Development Goals (2015) provide a framework for balancing economic, social, and environmental priorities. SDG 7 (affordable/clean energy), SDG 6 (clean water), SDG 2 (zero hunger) are directly relevant.
Case Study: Bolivia's Lithium — Resource Curse or Opportunity?
Bolivia holds the world's largest lithium reserves (~21 million tonnes) in the Salar de Uyuni salt flat. Lithium is essential for electric vehicle batteries and energy storage, making it a critical resource for the green transition. Bolivia has attempted to nationalise lithium extraction, establishing a state company (YLB) and requiring foreign partners to share technology. Challenges: lack of investment and technology has slowed extraction (Bolivia produced virtually no lithium while Chile and Australia led global production). Indigenous communities near the Salar face potential environmental damage (water use in an arid region, chemical contamination). The case illustrates the "resource curse" risk: natural wealth does not automatically bring development if governance, infrastructure, and political stability are lacking.
Exam Tip: Resource security questions often require you to consider the interconnections between energy, water, and food — the energy-water-food nexus. For example: agriculture needs water and energy; energy production needs water (cooling power stations, hydropower); water treatment needs energy. A shortage in one resource creates cascading effects across all three.
Exam Tip: AQA expects evaluation of strategies at different scales (local, national, global) and perspectives (economic, environmental, social, political). For example, fracking may improve national energy security but create local environmental problems. Always weigh costs against benefits and consider who gains and who loses.
