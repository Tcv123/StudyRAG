1.1 Water & Carbon Cycles

1. Systems Concepts

System: A set of interrelated components working together to form a functioning whole. In geography, the water and carbon cycles are examples of closed systems at a global scale (no matter enters or leaves) and open systems at a local scale (both energy and matter cross the boundary).

Key systems terminology:

  • Inputs — energy or matter entering the system (e.g. precipitation entering a drainage basin)
  • Outputs — energy or matter leaving the system (e.g. river discharge, evapotranspiration)
  • Stores (components) — where energy or matter is held (e.g. soil moisture, aquifers, biomass)
  • Flows (transfers/fluxes) — movement of energy or matter between stores (e.g. infiltration, throughflow)
  • Positive feedback — amplifies change, pushing the system further from equilibrium (e.g. ice-albedo effect)
  • Negative feedback — dampens change, restoring equilibrium (e.g. increased evaporation leading to more cloud cover and cooling)
  • Dynamic equilibrium — a state of balance maintained despite ongoing fluctuations

2. The Global Water Cycle

Hydrological cycle: The continuous movement of water between the atmosphere, land, and oceans through evaporation, condensation, precipitation, and runoff. At a global scale it is a closed system — no water enters or leaves the Earth.

Key Fluxes (Transfers)

  • Evaporation — liquid water to vapour, driven by solar energy. Greatest over warm oceans.
  • Transpiration — water loss through plant stomata. Tropical rainforests are major contributors.
  • Evapotranspiration — combined water loss from evaporation and transpiration.
  • Condensation — vapour cools to form water droplets/ice crystals, creating clouds.
  • Precipitation — water falling from clouds as rain, snow, sleet, or hail.
  • Runoff — water flowing over the surface to rivers and eventually back to oceans.
  • Infiltration — water soaking into the soil from the surface.
  • Percolation — deeper downward movement of water through soil into bedrock and aquifers.
  • Throughflow — lateral movement of water through the soil layer.
  • Groundwater flow — slow lateral movement of water through permeable rock.

3. The Drainage Basin as an Open System

Drainage basin: The area of land drained by a river and its tributaries. It is an open system with inputs (precipitation) and outputs (discharge, evapotranspiration). The boundary is the watershed — a ridge of high land separating adjacent drainage basins.

The water balance equation summarises the relationship between inputs and outputs:

P = Q + E ± ΔS

Where: P = precipitation, Q = discharge (runoff), E = evapotranspiration, ΔS = change in storage.

Factors Affecting Water Balance

  • Climate — seasonal variations in rainfall and temperature affect inputs and evapotranspiration.
  • Rock type — permeable rocks (chalk, limestone) allow infiltration; impermeable rocks (clay, granite) promote surface runoff.
  • Soil type — sandy soils drain quickly; clay soils hold water and generate more surface runoff.
  • Vegetation — intercepts precipitation, increases infiltration through root channels, and promotes transpiration.
  • Land use — urbanisation creates impermeable surfaces, increasing surface runoff and reducing infiltration.

The Storm Hydrograph

Storm hydrograph: A graph showing how a river's discharge changes over time in response to a rainfall event. Key features include the peak discharge, lag time, rising limb, and falling (recession) limb.

4. The Global Carbon Cycle

Carbon cycle: The biogeochemical cycle by which carbon is exchanged between the biosphere, lithosphere, hydrosphere, and atmosphere. Carbon exists in various forms: CO2 gas, organic molecules, carbonates in rocks, and dissolved in oceans.

Key Carbon Fluxes

  • Photosynthesis — plants absorb CO2 from the atmosphere, converting it to organic carbon. (~120 GtC/yr)
  • Respiration — organisms release CO2 back to the atmosphere through metabolic processes. (~120 GtC/yr)
  • Decomposition — dead organic matter is broken down by microorganisms, releasing CO2 and CH4.
  • Combustion — burning of biomass or fossil fuels releases stored carbon as CO2. (~9.5 GtC/yr from fossil fuels)
  • Ocean-atmosphere exchange — CO2 dissolves in cold ocean water (solubility pump) and is released from warm water.
  • Weathering — chemical weathering of silicate rocks consumes CO2. Very slow, geological timescale flux.
  • Volcanism — volcanic eruptions release CO2 from the lithosphere to the atmosphere. (~0.1 GtC/yr)
  • Burial/sedimentation — organic matter and calcium carbonate accumulate on ocean floors, eventually forming sedimentary rocks and fossil fuels over millions of years.

5. Changes to the Carbon Cycle Over Time

Natural Variations

  • Milankovitch cycles — orbital changes (eccentricity, axial tilt, precession) cause glacial-interglacial cycles over 100,000-year periods, altering carbon stores in ice sheets and oceans.
  • Volcanic eruptions — release CO2 from the lithosphere but also inject aerosols that cause short-term cooling.
  • Ocean circulation changes — thermohaline circulation shifts can alter the ocean's ability to absorb/release CO2.

Human Impacts on the Carbon Cycle

  • Fossil fuel combustion — transfers carbon from the geological store to the atmosphere at an accelerated rate (~9.5 GtC/yr). This exceeds natural volcanic output by a factor of ~100.
  • Deforestation — reduces the terrestrial biomass store and the capacity for photosynthesis. Burning releases carbon directly. Globally ~1.5 GtC/yr.
  • Agriculture — ploughing releases soil carbon; livestock produce methane; rice paddies are significant CH4 sources.
  • Cement production — heating limestone (CaCO3) releases CO2. Accounts for ~5% of industrial CO2 emissions.
Exam Tip: The AQA specification requires understanding of carbon cycle changes over different timescales: geological (millions of years), glacial-interglacial (thousands of years), and recent anthropogenic (decades). Always specify which timescale you are discussing.

6. Water, Carbon, and Climate Connections

The Enhanced Greenhouse Effect

Human activities have increased atmospheric concentrations of greenhouse gases (CO2 from ~280 ppm pre-industrial to ~420 ppm today, CH4 from ~700 ppb to ~1,900 ppb). This enhances the natural greenhouse effect, trapping more longwave radiation and raising global temperatures.

Case Study: The Amazon Rainforest — Carbon Sink Under Threat
The Amazon stores an estimated 150–200 GtC in biomass and soil. It absorbs ~2 GtC/yr through photosynthesis. However, deforestation (over 17% of original forest lost) and climate change threaten this vital carbon sink. Recent research suggests parts of the eastern Amazon have shifted from a net carbon sink to a net source, due to fires and degradation. Positive feedbacks (less forest → less rainfall → more drought → more fires) could push the Amazon past a tipping point, releasing massive amounts of stored carbon.
Case Study: River Severn Drainage Basin
The River Severn (354 km, longest in the UK) demonstrates key hydrological concepts. Its source in the Cambrian Mountains (Plynlimon) receives over 2,000 mm rainfall annually. Upper catchment: thin soils on impermeable rock produce flashy hydrographs. Middle course: widening floodplain, more permeable substrates moderate flow. Lower course: tidal influence at the Severn Estuary. Major floods (e.g. 2020) linked to saturated soils, prolonged rainfall, and limited infiltration capacity. Flood management includes engineered defences at Shrewsbury and natural flood management upstream.

7. Human Impacts on the Water Cycle

  • Urbanisation — impermeable surfaces reduce infiltration, increase surface runoff, and reduce groundwater recharge. Urban areas have flashier hydrographs.
  • Deforestation — removes interception, reduces transpiration, increases surface runoff and soil erosion. Can reduce rainfall in tropical regions through reduced moisture recycling.
  • Irrigation — diverts water from rivers/aquifers, altering flow regimes. Can lower water tables (e.g. Ogallala Aquifer, USA) and cause salinisation.
  • Dams and reservoirs — regulate flow, reduce flood peaks, trap sediment, alter downstream ecology. Change evaporation rates.
  • Climate change — alters precipitation patterns, increases evaporation, causes glacier retreat, raises sea levels, and intensifies extreme weather events.

8. Managing Water and Carbon

Strategies to Reduce Carbon Emissions

  • International agreements — Paris Agreement (2015) aims to limit warming to 1.5°C above pre-industrial levels.
  • Carbon capture and storage (CCS) — technology to capture CO2 from power plants and store it underground.
  • Reforestation/afforestation — planting trees to increase carbon sequestration.
  • Renewable energy transition — shifting from fossil fuels to wind, solar, and hydroelectric power.
  • Carbon trading — cap-and-trade schemes (e.g. EU Emissions Trading System) that create financial incentives to reduce emissions.

Strategies to Manage Water Resources

  • Sustainable drainage systems (SuDS) — permeable paving, green roofs, swales, and retention ponds to manage urban runoff.
  • Catchment management — tree planting, soil management, and wetland restoration to slow the flow of water.
  • Water transfer schemes — moving water from surplus to deficit areas (e.g. Kielder Water to NE England).
  • Demand management — metering, leak reduction, water-efficient technologies.
Exam Tip: AQA frequently asks about the interconnections between the water and carbon cycles. Be ready to explain how a change in one cycle affects the other — for example, how deforestation reduces transpiration (water cycle) AND reduces carbon sequestration (carbon cycle), creating a positive feedback loop.
