S1.2: Poisson Distribution

1. Definition

\(X \sim \operatorname{Po}(\lambda)\) if \[P(X = r) = \dfrac{e^{-\lambda} \lambda^r}{r!}, \quad r = 0, 1, 2, \ldots\]

Interpretation: \(X\) counts rare events occurring independently at a constant average rate \(\lambda\) per unit time/space.

2. Conditions for a Poisson Model

  • Events occur independently.
  • Events occur at a constant mean rate in time or space.
  • Events occur singly (two can't happen at the same instant).
  • The probability of an event in a short interval is proportional to its length.

3. Mean and Variance

\(E(X) = \lambda, \qquad \operatorname{Var}(X) = \lambda\)

Mean equals variance — a distinguishing feature.

4. Summing Independent Poissons

If \(X \sim \operatorname{Po}(\lambda_1)\) and \(Y \sim \operatorname{Po}(\lambda_2)\) are independent, then \(X + Y \sim \operatorname{Po}(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2)\).

This follows from the additive property of independent Poisson counts.

5. Scaling the Rate

If events occur at rate \(\lambda\) per unit time and you consider an interval of length \(t\):

\(\text{count in } t \text{ units} \sim \operatorname{Po}(\lambda t)\)
Calls arrive at a switchboard at 3 per minute. Find the probability of at least 5 calls in 2 minutes.
In 2 minutes, the mean is \(\lambda t = 6\). Let \(Y \sim \operatorname{Po}(6)\).
\(P(Y \ge 5) = 1 - P(Y \le 4) = 1 - 0.2851 = 0.7149\) (from Poisson tables).

6. Poisson Approximation to the Binomial

If \(X \sim B(n, p)\) with \(n\) large and \(p\) small (such that \(np \approx\) small or moderate), then \[X \approx \operatorname{Po}(np).\] Rule of thumb: \(n > 50\) and \(np < 10\).
Faulty components are 1% of a batch. Find \(P(\text{at most 2 faulty in 200})\).
\(X \sim B(200, 0.01) \approx \operatorname{Po}(2)\).
\(P(X \le 2) = e^{-2}(1 + 2 + 2) = 5e^{-2} \approx 0.6767\).

7. Hypothesis Tests on \(\lambda\)

General Procedure

  1. State \(H_0: \lambda = \lambda_0\) and \(H_1\) (either one- or two-tailed).
  2. Assume \(H_0\) and compute the probability of observing a value at least as extreme as the data.
  3. Compare this \(p\)-value with the significance level \(\alpha\).
  4. Decide: reject \(H_0\) if \(p < \alpha\), otherwise do not reject.
  5. State your conclusion in context.
A newsagent averages 5 enquiries per hour. Today he records 12 in one hour. Is this significant at 5%?
\(H_0: \lambda = 5\), \(H_1: \lambda > 5\).
Under \(H_0\), \(X \sim \operatorname{Po}(5)\).
\(P(X \ge 12) = 1 - P(X \le 11) = 1 - 0.9945 = 0.0055 < 0.05\).
Reject \(H_0\): there is evidence at the 5% level that the average enquiry rate has increased.

Two-Tailed Test

Compute \(P(X \ge \text{observed})\) or \(P(X \le \text{observed})\), whichever is smaller, and compare with \(\alpha/2\).

OCR Exam Tip: Always state the distribution under \(H_0\) (e.g. "Under \(H_0\), \(X \sim \operatorname{Po}(5)\)") and give the \(p\)-value, not just "significant/not significant".

8. Critical Region Approach

For \(X \sim \operatorname{Po}(\lambda_0)\), find the smallest \(c\) such that \(P(X \ge c) \le \alpha\). The critical region is \(\{c, c+1, \ldots\}\) (upper-tail test).

Note: Poisson takes only integer values, so the actual significance level may be less than \(\alpha\) (it's a discrete distribution).

9. Exam Style Strategy

  • Check whether a Poisson model is even appropriate — if variance ≠ mean in data, it's probably not.
  • When summing Poissons, scale the mean by the new interval length.
  • Use tables for \(P(X \le x)\) values; don't evaluate factorials if you can look them up.
  • In tests, always compare the cumulative probability with \(\alpha\) (not \(P(X = \text{obs})\) alone).
