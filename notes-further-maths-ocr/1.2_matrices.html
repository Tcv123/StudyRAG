1.2: Matrices

1. What Is a Matrix?

A matrix is a rectangular array of numbers arranged in rows and columns. A matrix with \(m\) rows and \(n\) columns has order \(m \times n\).

We write \(\mathbf{A} = (a_{ij})\) where \(a_{ij}\) is the entry in row \(i\), column \(j\). A square matrix has \(m = n\). A column vector is \(n \times 1\); a row vector is \(1 \times n\).

2. Matrix Arithmetic

Addition and Scalar Multiplication

Two matrices of the same order are added element-wise. Scalar multiplication multiplies every entry.

\((\mathbf{A} + \mathbf{B})_{ij} = a_{ij} + b_{ij}, \quad (k\mathbf{A})_{ij} = k\,a_{ij}\)

Matrix Multiplication

If \(\mathbf{A}\) is \(m \times n\) and \(\mathbf{B}\) is \(n \times p\), then the product \(\mathbf{AB}\) is \(m \times p\) with entries \[(\mathbf{AB})_{ij} = \sum_{k=1}^{n} a_{ik} b_{kj}.\] The inner dimensions must match.
Matrix multiplication is not commutative: in general \(\mathbf{AB} \neq \mathbf{BA}\). It is associative: \(\mathbf{(AB)C} = \mathbf{A(BC)}\).
\(\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 5 & 6 \\ 7 & 8 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1\cdot5+2\cdot7 & 1\cdot6+2\cdot8 \\ 3\cdot5+4\cdot7 & 3\cdot6+4\cdot8 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 19 & 22 \\ 43 & 50 \end{pmatrix}\).

Identity and Zero Matrices

The \(n \times n\) identity matrix \(\mathbf{I}\) has ones on the main diagonal and zeros elsewhere, satisfying \(\mathbf{AI} = \mathbf{IA} = \mathbf{A}\). The zero matrix \(\mathbf{0}\) has all entries zero.

3. Determinants

\(2\times2\) Determinant

\(\det \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix} = ad - bc\)

\(3\times3\) Determinant (Expansion Along the First Row)

\(\det \begin{pmatrix} a_1 & a_2 & a_3 \\ b_1 & b_2 & b_3 \\ c_1 & c_2 & c_3 \end{pmatrix} = a_1\!\begin{vmatrix} b_2 & b_3 \\ c_2 & c_3 \end{vmatrix} - a_2\!\begin{vmatrix} b_1 & b_3 \\ c_1 & c_3 \end{vmatrix} + a_3\!\begin{vmatrix} b_1 & b_2 \\ c_1 & c_2 \end{vmatrix}\)

Signs alternate \((+,-,+,\ldots)\) across the row. Expansion along any row or column gives the same answer — choose a row/column with zeros to make life easier.

Key Properties

  • \(\det(\mathbf{AB}) = \det(\mathbf{A})\det(\mathbf{B})\)
  • \(\det(\mathbf{A}^T) = \det(\mathbf{A})\)
  • \(\det(k\mathbf{A}) = k^n \det(\mathbf{A})\) for an \(n \times n\) matrix
  • \(\det(\mathbf{A}^{-1}) = 1/\det(\mathbf{A})\)
  • If \(\det(\mathbf{A}) = 0\), \(\mathbf{A}\) is singular (no inverse exists).

Geometric Meaning

\(|\det(\mathbf{A})|\) is the scale factor for areas (2D) or volumes (3D) under the linear transformation \(\mathbf{x} \mapsto \mathbf{A}\mathbf{x}\). Negative determinant signifies a reflection (reverses orientation).

4. Inverse Matrices

\(2\times2\) Inverse

\(\mathbf{A} = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix} \implies \mathbf{A}^{-1} = \dfrac{1}{ad - bc} \begin{pmatrix} d & -b \\ -c & a \end{pmatrix}\)  (provided \(ad - bc \ne 0\)).

\(3\times3\) Inverse (Adjugate Formula)

For a \(3\times3\) matrix \(\mathbf{A}\) with \(\det\mathbf{A} \ne 0\):

\(\mathbf{A}^{-1} = \dfrac{1}{\det\mathbf{A}}\, \operatorname{adj}(\mathbf{A})\)

The adjugate (adjoint) is the transpose of the cofactor matrix:

  1. Compute the cofactor \(C_{ij} = (-1)^{i+j} M_{ij}\), where \(M_{ij}\) is the determinant of the \(2\times2\) minor obtained by deleting row \(i\) and column \(j\).
  2. Form the cofactor matrix \(\mathbf{C} = (C_{ij})\).
  3. Transpose: \(\operatorname{adj}(\mathbf{A}) = \mathbf{C}^T\).

Inverse Properties

  • \((\mathbf{AB})^{-1} = \mathbf{B}^{-1}\mathbf{A}^{-1}\) — note the reversed order.
  • \((\mathbf{A}^{-1})^{-1} = \mathbf{A}\), \((\mathbf{A}^T)^{-1} = (\mathbf{A}^{-1})^T\).

5. Solving Simultaneous Equations with Matrices

A system \(a_{11}x + a_{12}y + a_{13}z = d_1\), etc., becomes \(\mathbf{A}\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{d}\), with solution

\(\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{A}^{-1}\mathbf{d}\)  (when \(\mathbf{A}\) is invertible).

6. Linear Transformations

A \(2\times2\) (or \(3\times3\)) matrix \(\mathbf{M}\) represents a linear transformation \(\mathbf{x} \mapsto \mathbf{M}\mathbf{x}\). Linear transformations fix the origin and preserve straight lines.

Standard 2D Transformations

TransformationMatrix
Rotation by \(\theta\) anticlockwise\(\begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{pmatrix}\)
Reflection in \(y = x\tan\theta\)\(\begin{pmatrix} \cos 2\theta & \sin 2\theta \\ \sin 2\theta & -\cos 2\theta \end{pmatrix}\)
Reflection in \(x\)-axis\(\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix}\)
Reflection in \(y\)-axis\(\begin{pmatrix} -1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}\)
Enlargement, scale factor \(k\)\(\begin{pmatrix} k & 0 \\ 0 & k \end{pmatrix}\)
Stretch parallel to \(x\)-axis, factor \(k\)\(\begin{pmatrix} k & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}\)
Shear parallel to \(x\)-axis by \(k\)\(\begin{pmatrix} 1 & k \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}\)

Composing Transformations

If you first apply \(\mathbf{P}\) then \(\mathbf{Q}\), the combined matrix is \(\mathbf{QP}\) — the second transformation is on the left.

Find the matrix for a rotation \(90°\) anticlockwise followed by reflection in the \(x\)-axis.
\(\mathbf{R} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}\), \(\mathbf{F} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix}\).
Combined: \(\mathbf{F}\mathbf{R} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ -1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}\).
This is reflection in the line \(y = -x\).

7. Invariant Points and Lines

Invariant point: a point \(\mathbf{p}\) with \(\mathbf{M}\mathbf{p} = \mathbf{p}\).
Invariant line: a line mapped to itself (as a set of points — individual points may move along the line).
Line of invariant points: a line on which every point is invariant.

Solve \((\mathbf{M} - \mathbf{I})\mathbf{p} = \mathbf{0}\) to find invariant points. For invariant lines, set up a generic point on the line, transform it, and require the image to lie on the line.

8. Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors

A non-zero vector \(\mathbf{v}\) is an eigenvector of \(\mathbf{A}\) with eigenvalue \(\lambda\) if \[\mathbf{A}\mathbf{v} = \lambda \mathbf{v}.\]

Finding Eigenvalues — the Characteristic Equation

\(\mathbf{Av} = \lambda\mathbf{v} \Leftrightarrow (\mathbf{A} - \lambda\mathbf{I})\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}\) with \(\mathbf{v} \ne \mathbf{0}\), which requires:

\(\det(\mathbf{A} - \lambda \mathbf{I}) = 0\)

For a \(2\times2\) matrix this is a quadratic in \(\lambda\); for \(3\times3\) it is a cubic.

Finding Eigenvectors

For each eigenvalue \(\lambda\), solve \((\mathbf{A} - \lambda\mathbf{I})\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}\) — you always get an infinite family (the solution is a line/plane). Pick a convenient non-zero representative.

Find eigenvalues and eigenvectors of \(\mathbf{A} = \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 1 \\ 2 & 3 \end{pmatrix}\).
\(\det(\mathbf{A} - \lambda\mathbf{I}) = (4 - \lambda)(3 - \lambda) - 2 = \lambda^2 - 7\lambda + 10 = 0\).
Eigenvalues: \(\lambda = 2, 5\).
\(\lambda = 5\): \(\begin{pmatrix} -1 & 1 \\ 2 & -2 \end{pmatrix}\mathbf{v} = 0 \Rightarrow \mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}\).
\(\lambda = 2\): \(\begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 2 & 1 \end{pmatrix}\mathbf{v} = 0 \Rightarrow \mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ -2 \end{pmatrix}\).

9. Diagonalisation

If an \(n \times n\) matrix \(\mathbf{A}\) has \(n\) linearly independent eigenvectors, it can be written as \[\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{P}\mathbf{D}\mathbf{P}^{-1}\] where \(\mathbf{D}\) is diagonal with eigenvalues on the diagonal, and \(\mathbf{P}\) has the corresponding eigenvectors as columns (in the same order).

Why It Matters

Powers and exponentials become easy:

\(\mathbf{A}^n = \mathbf{P}\mathbf{D}^n\mathbf{P}^{-1}\)

where \(\mathbf{D}^n\) just raises each diagonal entry to the \(n\)th power.

Using the previous example, compute \(\mathbf{A}^{10}\).
\(\mathbf{P} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & -2 \end{pmatrix}\), \(\mathbf{D} = \begin{pmatrix} 5 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix}\).
\(\det\mathbf{P} = -3\), \(\mathbf{P}^{-1} = -\tfrac{1}{3}\begin{pmatrix} -2 & -1 \\ -1 & 1 \end{pmatrix} = \tfrac{1}{3}\begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & -1 \end{pmatrix}\).
\(\mathbf{A}^{10} = \mathbf{P}\begin{pmatrix} 5^{10} & 0 \\ 0 & 2^{10} \end{pmatrix}\mathbf{P}^{-1}\).
OCR Exam Tip: Diagonalisation questions are usually structured: part (a) find eigenvalues and eigenvectors, part (b) hence find \(\mathbf{A}^n\) or solve a coupled recurrence. Lay out \(\mathbf{P}\), \(\mathbf{D}\), \(\mathbf{P}^{-1}\) clearly.

10. Cayley–Hamilton Theorem (Support)

Every square matrix satisfies its own characteristic equation. If \(\det(\mathbf{A} - \lambda\mathbf{I}) = \lambda^n + c_{n-1}\lambda^{n-1} + \cdots + c_0\), then

\(\mathbf{A}^n + c_{n-1}\mathbf{A}^{n-1} + \cdots + c_0\mathbf{I} = \mathbf{0}\).

Use this to express higher powers of \(\mathbf{A}\) in terms of lower ones, or to find \(\mathbf{A}^{-1}\).

11. Exam Style Strategy

  • Write determinants out carefully — sign errors in cofactor expansions are the single most common mistake.
  • For \(3\times 3\) systems, start with the determinant; only do elimination when \(\det = 0\).
  • For composite transformations, multiply in the order last first (the last transformation goes on the left).
  • Always verify an eigenvector by computing \(\mathbf{A}\mathbf{v}\) and checking it equals \(\lambda\mathbf{v}\).
