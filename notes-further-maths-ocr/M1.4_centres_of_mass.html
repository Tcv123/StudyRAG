M1.4: Centres of Mass

1. Definition and System of Particles

For a system of particles with masses \(m_i\) at position vectors \(\mathbf{r}_i\), the centre of mass is \[\bar{\mathbf{r}} = \dfrac{\sum_i m_i \mathbf{r}_i}{\sum_i m_i}.\]

For a uniform gravitational field, the centre of mass is also the centre of gravity.

2. Uniform Laminae (2D Plates)

For a uniform lamina, mass is proportional to area. The coordinates of the centre of mass are:

\(\bar{x} = \dfrac{\int x\,dA}{\int dA}, \qquad \bar{y} = \dfrac{\int y\,dA}{\int dA}.\)

For a region under \(y = f(x)\) between \(x = a\) and \(x = b\):

\(\bar{x} = \dfrac{\int_a^b x f(x)\,dx}{\int_a^b f(x)\,dx}, \quad \bar{y} = \dfrac{\tfrac{1}{2}\int_a^b [f(x)]^2\,dx}{\int_a^b f(x)\,dx}.\)

Standard Uniform Lamina CoMs

ShapeCentre of mass
RectangleIntersection of diagonals
TriangleCentroid: average of vertex coordinates, i.e. \(\tfrac{2}{3}\) along each median
Circular arc (radius \(r\), half-angle \(\alpha\))\(\bar{x} = \dfrac{r\sin\alpha}{\alpha}\) from centre (along the axis of symmetry)
Circular sector (radius \(r\), half-angle \(\alpha\))\(\bar{x} = \dfrac{2r\sin\alpha}{3\alpha}\)
Semicircle (radius \(r\))\(\bar{x} = \dfrac{4r}{3\pi}\) from the diameter

3. Solids of Revolution

A solid of revolution about the \(x\)-axis generated by \(y = f(x)\), \(a \le x \le b\):

\(\bar{x} = \dfrac{\int_a^b x \pi [f(x)]^2\,dx}{\int_a^b \pi [f(x)]^2\,dx} = \dfrac{\int_a^b x [f(x)]^2\,dx}{\int_a^b [f(x)]^2\,dx}.\)

4. Composite Bodies

For a body made of several simpler parts with individual centres of mass and masses (or areas, volumes):

\(\bar{\mathbf{r}} = \dfrac{\sum m_i \bar{\mathbf{r}}_i}{\sum m_i}.\)

Use signed masses (subtract "removed" parts) for bodies with holes.

A uniform square lamina of side 6 has a circular hole of radius 1 removed, centred at \((2, 2)\). Find the centre of mass of the remaining lamina (taking the bottom-left as origin).
Full square: area 36, CoM at \((3, 3)\).
Hole: area \(\pi\), CoM at \((2, 2)\).
Remaining area \(36 - \pi\). CoM:
\(\bar{x} = (36\cdot 3 - \pi\cdot 2)/(36 - \pi)\), \(\bar{y}\) similarly.
Numerically: \(\bar{x} = \bar{y} \approx (108 - 6.28)/32.86 \approx 3.096\).

5. Equilibrium and Toppling

A body on a horizontal surface is in equilibrium if the vertical through its centre of mass falls within the base of support.

A body on an inclined plane (angle \(\theta\)) topples if the vertical through the CoM falls outside the base — this occurs when

\(\tan\theta > \dfrac{a}{h}\)

where \(a\) is the half-width of the base and \(h\) the height of the CoM above the base. (If friction is insufficient, sliding occurs first.)

Sliding vs. Toppling

Compare the toppling angle (above) with the sliding angle \(\theta_s\) satisfying \(\tan\theta_s = \mu\):

  • If \(\mu < a/h\): slides first.
  • If \(\mu > a/h\): topples first.

6. Suspended Bodies

When a body hangs freely from a point, it rotates so that the centre of mass is vertically below the suspension point. The direction of hanging depends on the position of the CoM relative to the suspension point.

7. Exam Style Strategy

  • Use symmetry: the CoM of any uniform lamina/solid lies on every axis of symmetry.
  • For composites, make a table: part | mass (or area) | \(\bar{x}_i\) | \(\bar{y}_i\) | \(m_i\bar{x}_i\) | \(m_i\bar{y}_i\). Sum, then divide.
  • For equilibrium problems, draw the body tilted at the critical angle — geometry is usually the key.
  • Remember: CoG is just CoM in a uniform gravitational field — same point.
