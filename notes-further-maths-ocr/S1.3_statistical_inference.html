S1.3: Statistical Inference

1. Population, Sample, and Sampling Distribution

The sampling distribution of a statistic (e.g. the sample mean) is the probability distribution of that statistic across all possible samples of a given size.

Central Limit Theorem

If \(X_1, \ldots, X_n\) are IID with mean \(\mu\) and variance \(\sigma^2\), then for large \(n\), \[\bar{X} \approx N\!\left(\mu, \dfrac{\sigma^2}{n}\right).\] The approximation is good for \(n \ge 30\) (rule of thumb), regardless of the original distribution.

CLT is why so many statistical procedures assume approximate normality.

2. The Standard Error

The standard error of the sample mean is \[\operatorname{SE}(\bar{X}) = \dfrac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}.\] When \(\sigma\) is unknown, use the sample standard deviation \(s\) in its place.

3. Hypothesis Tests for a Population Mean

\(Z\)-Test (\(\sigma\) Known, or Large \(n\))

\(Z = \dfrac{\bar{x} - \mu_0}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}} \sim N(0, 1)\)  (under \(H_0\))

Critical values: \(\pm 1.96\) for 5% two-tailed; \(1.645\) for 5% one-tailed.

\(t\)-Test (\(\sigma\) Unknown, Sample Size Small)

\(t = \dfrac{\bar{x} - \mu_0}{s/\sqrt{n}} \sim t_{n-1}\)

See S1.5 (t-tests) for full procedure.

4. Confidence Intervals

CI for \(\mu\) with \(\sigma\) Known

\(\bar{x} \pm z_{\alpha/2}\cdot \dfrac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\)

CI for \(\mu\) with \(\sigma\) Unknown (Small Sample)

\(\bar{x} \pm t_{n-1, \alpha/2}\cdot \dfrac{s}{\sqrt{n}}\)
Sample of 36 students: \(\bar{x} = 72\), \(s = 8\). Find a 95% CI for \(\mu\).
Large sample, use \(z_{0.025} = 1.96\): margin \(= 1.96 \cdot 8/6 \approx 2.61\).
CI: \((69.39, 74.61)\).

5. Type I and Type II Errors

\(H_0\) true\(H_0\) false
Reject \(H_0\)Type I error (prob \(\alpha\))Correct (power = \(1 - \beta\))
Do not rejectCorrectType II error (prob \(\beta\))
  • \(\alpha\) = significance level = P(Type I) — we choose this in advance.
  • \(\beta\) = P(Type II) — depends on the true alternative value, sample size, and \(\alpha\).
  • Power of the test = \(1 - \beta\) = probability of correctly rejecting a false \(H_0\).

Computing \(\beta\)

For a specific alternative \(\mu = \mu_1\) and given \(\alpha\): find the critical region under \(H_0\), then compute the probability of the test statistic falling outside the critical region when \(\mu = \mu_1\).

One-tailed \(Z\)-test of \(H_0: \mu = 10\) vs. \(H_1: \mu > 10\), \(\sigma = 2\), \(n = 16\), \(\alpha = 0.05\). Find \(\beta\) at \(\mu = 11\).
Standard error \(= 2/4 = 0.5\). Critical value: \(\bar{x} > 10 + 1.645(0.5) = 10.823\).
Under \(\mu = 11\): \(P(\bar{X} \le 10.823) = P(Z \le (10.823 - 11)/0.5) = P(Z \le -0.354) \approx 0.362\).
So \(\beta \approx 0.362\); power \(\approx 0.638\).

6. Sample Size Determination

To achieve a margin of error \(E\) in a CI at confidence \(1 - \alpha\):

\(n \ge \left(\dfrac{z_{\alpha/2}\,\sigma}{E}\right)^2\)

7. Exam Style Strategy

  • Identify whether \(\sigma\) is known (use \(Z\)) or unknown (use \(t\)).
  • State null/alternative hypotheses symbolically.
  • For CIs, state confidence level explicitly.
  • For power/Type II calculations, draw the two overlapping distributions — it clarifies which tail you're computing.
