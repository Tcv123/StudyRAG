S1.5: t-Tests and Confidence Intervals

1. Sample Mean and Sample Variance

For a random sample \(x_1, \ldots, x_n\): \[\bar{x} = \dfrac{1}{n}\sum x_i, \qquad s^2 = \dfrac{1}{n-1}\sum (x_i - \bar{x})^2 = \dfrac{1}{n-1}\left(\sum x_i^2 - n\bar{x}^2\right).\]

The divisor \((n - 1)\) gives an unbiased estimator of the population variance \(\sigma^2\).

2. The \(t\)-Distribution

If \(X_1, \ldots, X_n\) are IID from \(N(\mu, \sigma^2)\) with \(\sigma\) unknown, then

\(T = \dfrac{\bar{X} - \mu}{s/\sqrt{n}} \sim t_{n-1}.\)

The \(t\)-distribution is symmetric and bell-shaped, with heavier tails than the normal. As \(n \to \infty\), \(t_{n-1} \to N(0, 1)\).

3. One-Sample \(t\)-Test

Test \(H_0: \mu = \mu_0\) using a sample of size \(n\) assumed normal (or from large \(n\) by CLT):

\(t = \dfrac{\bar{x} - \mu_0}{s/\sqrt{n}}, \quad \nu = n - 1.\)

Compare \(|t|\) with \(t_{\nu, \alpha/2}\) for two-tailed, or \(t\) with \(t_{\nu, \alpha}\) for one-tailed.

A machine is supposed to fill bottles with 500 mL. Sample of 10 gives \(\bar{x} = 497.2\), \(s = 3.5\). Test at 5% whether the mean has shifted.
\(H_0: \mu = 500\), \(H_1: \mu \ne 500\). Two-tailed, \(\alpha = 0.05\).
\(t = \dfrac{497.2 - 500}{3.5/\sqrt{10}} = \dfrac{-2.8}{1.107} = -2.53\).
\(\nu = 9\), critical value \(t_{9, 0.025} = 2.262\).
\(|{-2.53}| > 2.262\), so reject \(H_0\). Evidence that mean ≠ 500.

4. Two-Sample \(t\)-Test (Independent Samples)

For two independent samples with equal population variances \(\sigma^2\) (pooled):

\(s_p^2 = \dfrac{(n_1 - 1)s_1^2 + (n_2 - 1)s_2^2}{n_1 + n_2 - 2}\)
\(t = \dfrac{(\bar{x}_1 - \bar{x}_2) - (\mu_1 - \mu_2)_{H_0}}{s_p\sqrt{1/n_1 + 1/n_2}}, \quad \nu = n_1 + n_2 - 2.\)

Usually \(H_0: \mu_1 = \mu_2\), so the difference under \(H_0\) is 0.

5. Paired-Sample \(t\)-Test

When samples are paired (e.g. before/after on the same subject), work with the differences \(d_i = x_{2i} - x_{1i}\):

\(t = \dfrac{\bar{d} - \mu_{d, 0}}{s_d/\sqrt{n}}, \quad \nu = n - 1.\)

Usually \(H_0: \mu_d = 0\). Paired tests are more powerful than independent-sample tests when the pairing is meaningful — it removes between-subject variability.

Eight students' scores before and after tutoring:
Differences \(d_i\) (after − before) have \(\bar{d} = 4.5\), \(s_d = 3.0\).
Test at 5% whether tutoring improves scores.
\(H_0: \mu_d = 0\), \(H_1: \mu_d > 0\) (one-tailed).
\(t = 4.5 / (3.0/\sqrt{8}) = 4.24\). \(\nu = 7\), \(t_{7, 0.05} = 1.895\).
Since \(4.24 > 1.895\), reject \(H_0\). Evidence of improvement.

6. Confidence Interval for the Mean

A \((1 - \alpha) \times 100\%\) CI for \(\mu\) when \(\sigma\) is unknown and the sample is from a normal population:

\(\bar{x} \pm t_{n-1, \alpha/2}\cdot \dfrac{s}{\sqrt{n}}.\)
Sample of 15: \(\bar{x} = 68\), \(s = 4.2\). Find the 95% CI for \(\mu\).
\(t_{14, 0.025} = 2.145\). Margin: \(2.145 \cdot 4.2/\sqrt{15} = 2.326\).
CI: \((65.67,\; 70.33)\).

7. Confidence Interval for Difference of Means

Independent samples, pooled SD:

\((\bar{x}_1 - \bar{x}_2) \pm t_{n_1 + n_2 - 2, \alpha/2}\cdot s_p\sqrt{1/n_1 + 1/n_2}.\)

Paired samples:

\(\bar{d} \pm t_{n-1, \alpha/2}\cdot s_d/\sqrt{n}.\)

8. Link between Test and CI

A two-tailed test rejects \(H_0: \mu = \mu_0\) at significance level \(\alpha\) if and only if \(\mu_0\) is outside the \((1-\alpha)\) CI.

9. Assumptions and Checks

  • Data drawn from a normal population, or \(n\) is large enough for the CLT.
  • Samples are independent (or paired, for the paired test).
  • For two-sample pooled \(t\)-test: equal population variances (or sensible approximation).
OCR Exam Tip: State assumptions explicitly — e.g. "Assume the scores are normally distributed." Lost marks are often in the setup and conclusion, not the arithmetic.

10. Exam Style Strategy

  • Identify the test: one-sample, two-sample independent, or paired.
  • State hypotheses symbolically and verbally.
  • Compute \(t\), report \(\nu\), critical value, and decision.
  • Conclude in context — not just "reject \(H_0\)".
  • When asked for a CI, be clear about the confidence level and the endpoint calculations.
