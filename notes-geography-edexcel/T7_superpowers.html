Topic 7: Superpowers

7.1 What is a Superpower?

Superpower: A nation or group of nations with the ability to project dominating power and influence on a global scale, across military, economic, political, and cultural dimensions.
Emerging power: A nation that is rapidly increasing its global influence but has not yet achieved superpower status (e.g. China, India, Brazil). Sometimes referred to as a "rising power."

Types of Power

TypeDefinitionExamples
Hard powerThe use of military force, economic sanctions, or coercion to influence othersUS military interventions; Russian energy supply threats; trade embargoes
Soft powerThe ability to influence through attraction and persuasion — culture, values, diplomacyHollywood, US universities, BBC World Service, K-pop, Chinese Confucius Institutes
Smart powerA combination of hard and soft power used strategicallyUS foreign policy blending military capability with cultural influence and aid

Characteristics of Superpowers

  • Economic — large GDP; dominance of global trade and finance; control of key resources; home to major TNCs
  • Military — large, advanced armed forces; nuclear capability; global force projection (overseas bases, aircraft carriers); defence spending
  • Political/diplomatic — influence over international organisations (UN Security Council permanent seat); alliance leadership (NATO); ability to set global agendas
  • Cultural — global spread of language, media, values, and consumer culture; education and research institutions
  • Demographic — large population provides labour force, military personnel, and consumer market
  • Geographic — territorial size; access to resources; strategic location

7.2 Changing Patterns of Power

Historical Superpowers

EraSuperpower(s)Basis of Power
19th centuryBritish EmpireNaval dominance; largest empire in history (25% of world's land); industrial revolution; control of global trade routes; Sterling as reserve currency
Cold War (1947–1991)USA and USSR (bipolar world)USA: economic dominance (Bretton Woods, dollar), military (NATO), cultural (Hollywood). USSR: military (nuclear parity), ideological (communism), space programme
Post-Cold War (1991–~2010)USA (unipolar world)Sole superpower after USSR collapse; military supremacy; economic dominance; cultural hegemony; global institutions shaped by US interests
21st centuryUSA + China (emerging multipolar world)China's rise challenges US dominance; Russia reasserts itself; EU, India, Brazil grow in influence

Theories of Power

  • Unipolar — one superpower dominates (USA post-1991)
  • Bipolar — two superpowers compete (Cold War: USA vs USSR)
  • Multipolar — power is shared among several major states or blocs (the emerging 21st-century pattern)
  • Mackinder's Heartland Theory (1904) — whoever controls the Eurasian "heartland" (Central Asia/Russia) controls the world. Influenced Cold War containment strategy
  • Wallerstein's World Systems Theory — global economy divided into core (wealthy ACs controlling trade), semi-periphery (EDCs), and periphery (exploited LIDCs). Power flows from periphery to core

7.3 The Rise of China

Case Study: China as an Emerging Superpower
Economic power: World's 2nd largest economy (1st by PPP); largest exporter; $3+ trillion in foreign reserves; Belt and Road Initiative (BRI) investing $1+ trillion in infrastructure across 140+ countries; home to major TNCs (Huawei, Alibaba, Tencent, BYD).
Military: World's largest military by personnel (2+ million active); rapidly modernising navy; growing nuclear arsenal; military bases in Djibouti; territorial claims in South China Sea.
Political: UN Security Council permanent member (veto power); Shanghai Cooperation Organisation; BRICS founding member; growing influence in Africa through investment and loans; AIIB (Asian Infrastructure Investment Bank) as alternative to World Bank.
Cultural (soft power): Confucius Institutes (500+ worldwide promoting Chinese language/culture); state media expansion (CGTN); Mandarin as a growing global language; TikTok; however, soft power limited by human rights concerns, censorship, and authoritarian governance.
Challenges: Ageing population (one-child policy legacy); regional inequality; environmental degradation; debt concerns (BRI); tensions with USA (trade war, Taiwan, technology competition); lack of democratic soft power.

Case Study: The Belt and Road Initiative (BRI)
Context: Launched by Xi Jinping in 2013; a massive infrastructure investment programme spanning 140+ countries across Asia, Africa, Europe, and Latin America.
Scale: $1+ trillion invested in ports, railways, roads, power plants, digital infrastructure. Two routes: overland "Silk Road Economic Belt" (Central Asia to Europe) and maritime "21st Century Maritime Silk Road" (Southeast Asia, Indian Ocean, East Africa).
China's aims: Secure resource supply routes; open new markets; increase geopolitical influence; develop western China; establish yuan as international currency; create strategic dependencies.
Benefits for host countries: Infrastructure development; job creation; economic growth; connectivity.
Criticisms: "Debt-trap diplomacy" — countries unable to repay loans may cede strategic assets (e.g. Sri Lanka's Hambantota Port leased to China for 99 years); environmental damage; use of Chinese labour rather than local workers; lack of transparency; geopolitical strings attached; some projects economically unviable.

7.6 Development, the Environment, and Superpowers

Superpowers and Development

  • Superpowers shape global development through aid (bilateral/multilateral), trade rules (WTO agreements favouring ACs), investment (FDI by TNCs), and debt (IMF structural adjustment)
  • Neo-colonialism — former colonial powers (and new powers like China) maintain economic control over LIDCs through trade dependency, debt, and political influence, even after formal independence
  • Resource grabbing — superpowers secure access to strategic resources in LIDCs (oil, minerals, agricultural land) which may not benefit local populations

Superpowers and the Environment

  • USA and China are the world's two largest CO2 emitters (~28% and ~14% of global total respectively)
  • Superpowers have the greatest responsibility for climate change but also the greatest capacity to address it
  • Tensions in climate negotiations: historical emissions (ACs) vs current growth (EDCs); common but differentiated responsibilities
  • China is simultaneously the world's largest coal consumer AND largest investor in renewable energy
Exam Tip: For Superpowers essays, always consider multiple dimensions of power (economic, military, political, cultural) and use specific data. Evaluate whether the world is becoming more multipolar or whether US-China bipolarity is emerging. Consider the perspectives of both superpowers and those affected by superpower actions (LIDCs, small states). Use theories (Mackinder, Wallerstein) to support your analysis, but also critique them.
Case Study: BRICS as a Challenge to Western Dominance
Members: Originally Brazil, Russia, India, China, South Africa. Expanded in 2024 to include Egypt, Ethiopia, Iran, Saudi Arabia, UAE.
Significance: Expanded BRICS represents ~46% of world population and ~36% of global GDP (PPP). New Development Bank (NDB) offers alternative to World Bank; efforts to reduce dependence on US dollar; platform for coordinating emerging power interests.
Limitations: Members have very different political systems and interests; internal tensions (China-India border disputes); limited institutional cohesion compared to G7; some members are strategic rivals.
