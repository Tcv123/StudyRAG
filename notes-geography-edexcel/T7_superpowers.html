Topic 7: Superpowers

7.1 What is a Superpower?

Superpower: A nation or group of nations with the ability to project dominating power and influence on a global scale, across military, economic, political, and cultural dimensions. The term was first used during World War II to describe the USA, USSR, and British Empire. Today, the USA is often considered the sole superpower, with China as a rapidly rising challenger.
Emerging power: A nation rapidly increasing its global influence but not yet achieving superpower status. Often characterised by large population, fast economic growth, growing military capability, and increasing geopolitical assertiveness. Examples: China (often now classed as a superpower), India, Brazil, Russia (former superpower, diminished but still significant).

Types of Power

TypeDefinitionExamples
Hard powerCoercive power through military force, economic sanctions, or threatsUS military interventions (Iraq 2003, Afghanistan 2001–2021); Russian energy supply threats and military invasion of Ukraine (2022); Chinese military build-up in South China Sea; economic sanctions (Western sanctions on Russia, Iranian oil embargo)
Soft powerInfluence through attraction, persuasion, culture, values, and diplomacy rather than coercionHollywood and American media (Netflix: 260M subscribers globally); US universities (attract 1M+ international students); BBC World Service (492M weekly audience); K-pop (BTS, Blackpink); Chinese Confucius Institutes (500+ worldwide); French cuisine and fashion; Japanese anime/manga
Smart powerStrategic combination of hard and soft power tailored to specific situationsUS foreign policy blending military capability with cultural influence, aid, and diplomacy. China's Belt and Road Initiative combines infrastructure investment (hard) with cultural exchange programmes (soft).

Characteristics of Superpowers

  • Economic dominance — large GDP (USA: $27.4 trillion, China: $17.8 trillion in 2024); dominance of global trade and finance; home to major TNCs; reserve currency status (US dollar: 59% of global foreign exchange reserves); control of international financial institutions.
  • Military power — large, technologically advanced armed forces; nuclear capability (USA: 5,500 warheads, Russia: 6,257); global force projection through overseas bases (USA: 750+ bases in 80 countries), aircraft carriers (USA: 11, China: 3), and space/cyber capabilities; defence spending (USA: $886 billion/yr, 39% of world total).
  • Political/diplomatic influence — permanent seats on UN Security Council (USA, Russia, China, UK, France) with veto power; leadership of alliances (NATO, EU, BRICS); ability to set global agendas; influence over international organisations (IMF, World Bank, WTO).
  • Cultural/ideological influence — global spread of language (English: 1.5 billion speakers; Mandarin: 1.1 billion), media, values, education systems, consumer brands, and way of life.
  • Demographic weight — large population provides labour force, military personnel, consumer market, and innovation capacity. China: 1.4 billion; India: 1.44 billion (world's most populous since 2023); USA: 340 million.
  • Resource base — territorial size, access to natural resources (energy, minerals, food), strategic geographic location (controlling trade routes, choke points).

The Rise of China

China's transformation from a poor, agrarian economy to the world's second-largest economy is the defining geopolitical shift of the 21st century:

  • Economic growth — GDP grew from $306 billion (1980) to $17.8 trillion (2024), averaging 9.5%/yr for 40 years. 800 million people lifted from extreme poverty (1978–2020). China became the world's largest manufacturer (28% of global manufacturing output), largest exporter, and largest trading partner of 120+ countries.
  • Belt and Road Initiative (BRI) — launched 2013, a $1+ trillion infrastructure investment programme across 140+ countries in Asia, Africa, Europe, and Latin America. Includes railways, ports, highways, power plants, and digital infrastructure. Creates Chinese economic influence and political goodwill, but criticised for "debt-trap diplomacy" (Sri Lanka's Hambantota Port leased to China for 99 years after Sri Lanka couldn't repay loans) and environmental damage.
  • Military modernisation — China has the world's largest navy by vessel count (370+), 2 million active military personnel, nuclear capability (~350 warheads), space programme (Tiangong space station), and AI/cyber warfare investment. Military spending: $292 billion/yr (2nd largest globally). Assertive actions in South China Sea (building artificial islands with military bases).
  • Technology leadership — Huawei (5G infrastructure), TikTok (1.5 billion users), Alibaba (e-commerce), Tencent (WeChat), DJI (drones), BYD (EVs). China leads in patent applications, scientific publications, and AI research. However, US-China tech competition has led to export controls on advanced semiconductors.
  • Soft power — Confucius Institutes, international students, media expansion (CGTN, Xinhua), Chinese cuisine, traditional medicine, tourism. However, soft power undermined by authoritarian governance, human rights concerns (Uyghur detention in Xinjiang, Hong Kong crackdown, Tibet), and COVID-19 pandemic response.
Case Study: China's Belt and Road Initiative (BRI)
Launched by President Xi Jinping in 2013, the BRI is the largest infrastructure programme in history. Scale: $1+ trillion invested across 140+ countries, ~65% of world population. Projects include: Gwadar Port (Pakistan, $62 billion China-Pakistan Economic Corridor), Mombasa-Nairobi Railway (Kenya, $3.6 billion), Jakarta-Bandung High-Speed Railway (Indonesia), power plants across Southeast Asia and Africa. China's motivations: secure trade routes for exports and raw material imports, deploy excess industrial capacity (steel, cement), build geopolitical influence, establish yuan as international currency, create new markets for Chinese goods, challenge US-led global order. Benefits for host countries: infrastructure development (roads, railways, ports, power) that many countries desperately need but cannot finance domestically; job creation during construction; improved connectivity and trade capacity. Criticisms: "debt-trap diplomacy" (countries taking unsustainable Chinese loans, then forced to cede strategic assets — Hambantota Port, Sri Lanka); projects often use Chinese labour and materials rather than local; environmental damage (coal power plants, deforestation for infrastructure); lack of transparency; corruption concerns; some projects are white elephants (underutilised). Geopolitical significance: the BRI represents China's bid to reshape the global economic order, creating an alternative to US/Western-dominated institutions. The US, EU, Japan, and India have launched competing initiatives (G7 Partnership for Global Infrastructure and Investment: $600 billion) but on a smaller scale.

Emerging Groupings

  • BRICS (Brazil, Russia, India, China, South Africa + Egypt, Ethiopia, Iran, UAE, Saudi Arabia from 2024) — represents ~46% of world population and ~36% of global GDP (PPP). The New Development Bank ($100 billion capital) provides an alternative to the World Bank. BRICS challenges Western dominance of global governance but members have very different political systems and interests.
  • G7 (USA, UK, France, Germany, Italy, Canada, Japan + EU) — annual summit of the world's largest advanced economies. Coordinates economic policy, climate action, and security. Criticised as an unrepresentative "rich nations' club."
  • G20 — 19 countries + EU + AU, representing 85% of global GDP. More inclusive than G7 but criticised for informal, non-binding nature.
  • Shanghai Cooperation Organisation (SCO) — China, Russia, India, Pakistan, Central Asian states. Security and economic cooperation. Represents ~40% of world population.

7.4 Superpowers, Conflict, and the Environment

Geopolitical Tensions and Conflict

  • US-China rivalry — the defining geopolitical competition of the 21st century. Contested areas: trade (tariffs, technology restrictions), Taiwan (China claims sovereignty; US pledges to defend), South China Sea (territorial disputes, freedom of navigation), technology (AI, semiconductors, 5G), ideology (liberal democracy vs authoritarian capitalism).
  • Russia-West confrontation — Russia's invasion of Ukraine (2022) marked the most severe breach of European security since 1945. Consequences: NATO expansion (Finland, Sweden), Western sanctions, global energy and food price crisis, nuclear escalation risk, reshaping of European security architecture.
  • Middle East tensions — Iran-Saudi Arabia rivalry, Israel-Palestine conflict, competition for influence between USA, Russia, and China in the region.
  • Resource competition — control of energy resources (oil, gas, critical minerals) drives geopolitical competition. Arctic sovereignty disputes (Russia, USA, Canada, Denmark/Greenland, Norway) intensifying as ice melt opens new shipping routes and resource access.

Superpowers and the Global Commons

Global commons: Resources and spaces that lie beyond national jurisdiction, shared by all: the atmosphere, the high seas, outer space, and (partially) Antarctica and the deep seabed. Managing the global commons requires international cooperation, but superpower competition often undermines this.
  • Climate change — addressing climate change requires all major emitters to cooperate (Paris Agreement). But superpowers often prioritise economic growth: China emits 31% of global CO2; USA: 14%; India: 7%. The US withdrew from Paris Agreement under Trump (rejoined under Biden). Developing countries demand climate finance from historic emitters. COP process is slow and contested.
  • Oceans — overfishing (34% of fish stocks overfished, FAO), plastic pollution (~11 million tonnes/yr entering oceans), deep-sea mining disputes, South China Sea sovereignty claims. The UN High Seas Treaty (2023) aims to protect biodiversity in international waters.
  • Space — militarisation of space (anti-satellite weapons tested by USA, Russia, China, India), satellite congestion, space debris, resource extraction on the Moon and asteroids. Outer Space Treaty (1967) prohibits nuclear weapons in space but is increasingly outdated.
  • Arctic — melting sea ice opens the Northern Sea Route (40% shorter than Suez Canal for Europe-Asia shipping). Five Arctic states (Russia, USA, Canada, Denmark, Norway) and China compete for access to resources (oil, gas, minerals) and trade routes.
Case Study: The USA as a Superpower
The USA has been the world's dominant superpower since 1945 and remains so, though its relative power is declining. Economic: $27.4 trillion GDP (2024, world's largest at market exchange rates); home to 37% of the world's top 500 companies; US dollar is the global reserve currency (59% of global reserves); Wall Street and Silicon Valley dominate global finance and technology. Military: $886 billion defence budget (39% of world total); 750+ overseas military bases in 80 countries; 11 aircraft carriers; 5,500 nuclear warheads; global intelligence network (CIA, NSA); NATO leadership. Soft power: Hollywood ($42 billion global box office), American music, social media (Facebook, Instagram, X), US universities (8 of world's top 10), English language, NBA, NFL, cultural values of freedom and democracy. Challenges: rising national debt ($34+ trillion), domestic political polarisation, declining trust in institutions, relative economic decline vs China (China's GDP was 12% of US GDP in 2000; now 65%), Afghanistan withdrawal (2021) damaged credibility, growing anti-American sentiment in many regions, domestic inequality (Gini coefficient: 0.39, high for an HIC). Assessment: the USA remains the world's most powerful nation across all dimensions of power, but its dominance is no longer unchallenged. The 21st century is increasingly multipolar.
Case Study: India as an Emerging Power
India (population 1.44 billion, world's most populous country since 2023) is the world's fastest-growing major economy and an increasingly significant geopolitical player. Economic: $3.7 trillion GDP (5th largest); growth rate ~7%/yr; large, young workforce (median age 28); growing middle class (400 million); IT services hub (Bangalore, Hyderabad: $245 billion sector); "Make in India" manufacturing push. Military: 1.5 million active troops (4th largest); nuclear power (~170 warheads); growing naval capability (aircraft carrier, nuclear submarines); space programme (Chandrayaan-3 Moon landing 2023, Mangalyaan Mars orbiter 2014). Diplomatic: largest democracy; non-aligned tradition (balances relationships with USA, Russia, and China); G20 presidency 2023; growing voice in BRICS, QUAD (with USA, Japan, Australia), and UN reform advocacy. Soft power: Bollywood (1,500+ films/yr, largest global audience), yoga, cuisine, diaspora (18 million overseas Indians including 4.8 million in USA). Challenges: 228 million people still in extreme poverty; severe air pollution (14 of world's 20 most polluted cities); water stress; infrastructure gaps (roads, electricity); gender inequality; communal tensions; bureaucratic inefficiency. Assessment: India has the potential to become a superpower by mid-century (projected 3rd largest economy by 2030), but only if it can address poverty, infrastructure, education, and governance challenges. Its demographic dividend (large young workforce) is an opportunity that could become a liability without adequate job creation.
Exam Tip: Edexcel superpower questions often ask you to evaluate whether global power is becoming more or less multipolar. Structure your answer around evidence FOR multipolarity (China's rise, BRICS expansion, declining US share of global GDP, Russia's military assertiveness, EU as economic bloc) and evidence AGAINST (US military dominance remains unchallenged, dollar hegemony, US tech leadership, no country matches the USA across ALL dimensions of power). Use specific data to support your argument.
Exam Tip: When discussing superpowers and the global commons, always consider the tension between national interest and global cooperation. Climate change requires collective action but each superpower has incentives to free-ride. The Paris Agreement is voluntary precisely because mandatory targets were politically impossible. This tension between sovereignty and collective action is a recurring exam theme.
Exam Tip: Always use the concepts of hard power, soft power, and smart power in your answers. The most sophisticated responses recognise that power is multidimensional: China may have growing economic and military hard power but still lags behind the USA in soft power (cultural appeal, university prestige, media influence). Conversely, Japan has enormous soft power (anime, technology, cuisine) but limited hard power (constitutionally constrained military). These nuances demonstrate genuine understanding.
