Topic 5: Globalisation

5.1 What is Globalisation?

Globalisation: The increasing interconnection and interdependence of the world's economies, cultures, and populations, driven by trade in goods and services, flows of investment, technology, information, and migration.

Dimensions of Globalisation

  • Economic globalisation — growth of international trade, FDI, TNCs, and global financial markets
  • Political globalisation — increased cooperation through international organisations (UN, WTO, IMF, World Bank, EU)
  • Social/cultural globalisation — spread of ideas, values, languages, and cultural products (media, food, fashion)
  • Technological globalisation — rapid spread of communications technology, internet, social media

The KOF Globalisation Index

Measures globalisation across economic, social, and political dimensions for each country. Switzerland and the Netherlands consistently rank among the most globalised; many LIDCs in Sub-Saharan Africa rank lowest.

Trade Liberalisation

  • World Trade Organization (WTO) — promotes free trade by negotiating reductions in tariffs and trade barriers; 164 member states
  • Free Trade Agreements (FTAs) — bilateral or multilateral agreements reducing tariffs between countries (e.g. USMCA, EU Single Market)
  • Trade blocs — groups of countries with preferential trade arrangements (EU, ASEAN, NAFTA/USMCA, Mercosur)
  • Special Economic Zones (SEZs) — designated areas with relaxed regulations and tax incentives to attract FDI (e.g. Shenzhen, China)

5.3 Transnational Corporations (TNCs)

Transnational Corporation (TNC): A company that operates in at least two countries, with its headquarters typically in an AC and production/operations spread globally. TNCs are the primary agents of economic globalisation.

Why TNCs Locate Overseas

  • Lower labour costs — wages in LIDCs/EDCs can be a fraction of those in ACs
  • Access to raw materials — mining and extraction TNCs locate near resources
  • Avoiding trade barriers — manufacturing inside trade blocs avoids tariffs
  • Tax incentives — SEZs and low-tax jurisdictions offer favourable conditions
  • Access to new markets — growing consumer markets in EDCs (China, India, Brazil)
  • Relaxed regulations — weaker environmental and labour laws in some countries

Case Study: Apple — A Global TNC
HQ: Cupertino, California, USA. Revenue ~$383 billion (2023). Employs ~160,000 directly; millions more in supply chain.
Global operations: Products designed in USA; components sourced from 40+ countries (Samsung in South Korea for displays, TSMC in Taiwan for chips, rare earth minerals from China/DRC); assembled primarily by Foxconn in China and increasingly India and Vietnam.
Positive impacts: Millions of supply chain jobs; technology transfer; tax revenue in host countries; drives innovation.
Negative impacts: Foxconn criticised for harsh working conditions, excessive overtime, low wages; cobalt mining in DRC linked to child labour; environmental costs of mining; profit channelled through low-tax jurisdictions (Ireland); cultural homogenisation through dominance of iPhone globally.
Case Study: Unilever — TNC in LIDC/EDC Markets
Context: Anglo-Dutch consumer goods TNC; operates in 190+ countries; brands include Dove, Ben & Jerry's, Lipton, Omo. Revenue ~$60 billion.
EDC/LIDC strategy: Sells smaller, cheaper sachets of products in countries like India and Indonesia to reach low-income consumers. Sources raw materials (palm oil, tea) from LIDCs.
Positive: Employs thousands in developing countries; supports smallholder farmers; Sustainable Living Plan aims to reduce environmental footprint.
Negative: Palm oil sourcing linked to deforestation in Indonesia/Malaysia; sachet packaging creates plastic waste; market dominance undermines local producers.

5.4 The Global Shift

Global shift: The large-scale movement of manufacturing and, increasingly, service industries from ACs to EDCs/LIDCs, particularly in Asia. Also known as the New International Division of Labour (NIDL).

Causes of the Global Shift

  • Lower labour costs in Asia (China, Bangladesh, Vietnam, India)
  • Improved transport and communications (containerisation, internet)
  • Trade liberalisation and SEZs attracting FDI
  • Favourable government policies in host countries
  • TNCs seeking to maximise profit through global supply chains

Impacts of the Global Shift

On ACs (e.g. UK, USA)On EDCs (e.g. China, India)
EconomicDeindustrialisation; job losses in manufacturing; shift to service/knowledge economy; regional inequalityRapid economic growth; industrialisation; rising GDP; growing middle class; export earnings
SocialUnemployment in former industrial areas; social deprivation; need for retrainingUrbanisation; improved living standards; but also inequality, poor working conditions
EnvironmentalCleaner air (less heavy industry); but carbon emissions offshoredSevere pollution; environmental degradation; resource depletion
Case Study: China's Economic Transformation
Context: Since Deng Xiaoping's Open Door Policy (1978), China has transformed from a largely agrarian economy to the world's second-largest economy (and largest by PPP).
Key factors: SEZs (Shenzhen grew from a fishing village to a megacity of 17 million); massive FDI; vast labour force; export-oriented manufacturing; state investment in infrastructure.
Results: 800 million people lifted out of poverty; urbanisation rate rose from 18% to 65%; GDP grew ~10% per year for three decades; became the "factory of the world."
Challenges: Severe air and water pollution; inequality between coastal and interior regions; labour rights concerns; now faces rising wages and manufacturing moving to cheaper locations (Vietnam, Bangladesh).

Globalisation and Inequality

  • Winners: TNCs, consumers (cheaper goods), workers in EDCs gaining employment, countries attracting FDI, skilled/educated workers in ACs
  • Losers: Workers in deindustrialised AC regions, small-scale farmers undercut by global competition, marginalised communities bypassed by globalisation, environments exploited for resources
  • Global inequality is decreasing (between countries, as EDCs grow), but inequality within many countries is increasing (Gini coefficient rising in USA, UK, China)

5.6 Cultural Impacts of Globalisation

Cultural Globalisation

  • Westernisation/Americanisation — global spread of Western brands, media, food (McDonald's in 100+ countries), English language, Hollywood films
  • Cultural homogenisation — local cultures becoming more similar, traditional practices declining
  • Glocalisation — TNCs adapt global products to local tastes (e.g. McDonald's McSpicy Paneer in India, teriyaki burgers in Japan)
  • Cultural resistance — some communities and nations resist cultural globalisation (e.g. France's laws protecting French language; anti-globalisation movements)
  • Cultural hybridity — blending of cultures creates new forms (music, food, fashion)

5.7 Managing Globalisation

Challenges

  • Environmental degradation — global supply chains increase carbon emissions; resource extraction damages ecosystems
  • Labour exploitation — race to the bottom in wages and working conditions
  • Tax avoidance — TNCs exploit loopholes and tax havens (estimated $600 billion/year in profit shifting)
  • Dependency — LIDCs may become overly dependent on a single TNC or export commodity
  • Anti-globalisation sentiment — Brexit, Trump tariffs, rise of economic nationalism and protectionism

Ethical Consumption and Fair Trade

Fair trade: A trading partnership that seeks greater equity in international trade by ensuring producers in LIDCs receive a fair price, decent working conditions, and sustainable environmental practices. The Fairtrade mark guarantees minimum prices and a social premium.
Case Study: Fairtrade Coffee in Ethiopia
Context: Ethiopia is the birthplace of coffee and Africa's largest producer. Millions of smallholder farmers depend on coffee for income, but global commodity prices are volatile.
Impact of Fairtrade: Guaranteed minimum price ($1.40/lb, plus $0.20/lb social premium); cooperatives invest premiums in schools, health clinics, clean water; improved bargaining power; environmental standards promote shade-grown, organic farming.
Limitations: Only ~2.5% of global coffee is Fairtrade certified; certification costs can exclude the poorest farmers; market niche remains small; does not address structural causes of poverty.
Exam Tip: For globalisation essays, always present a balanced evaluation. Use the "winners and losers" framework, supported by specific examples and data. Show awareness that globalisation is not a single force but has economic, cultural, political, and environmental dimensions that affect places and people differently. Consider different scales: local, national, and global impacts.
