Health, Human Rights & Intervention (Edexcel Paper 2)

This topic explores the complex relationships between global health outcomes, human rights, and the role of geopolitical intervention. It examines how health varies globally, the causes and consequences of human rights abuses, the effectiveness and ethics of different forms of intervention by governments, IGOs, and NGOs, and how global governance structures shape health and rights outcomes across different scales.

1. Global Health Patterns

Health (WHO definition): A state of complete physical, mental, and social well-being, not merely the absence of disease or infirmity. This holistic definition highlights that health encompasses far more than the simple absence of illness — it includes psychological welfare, social functioning, and quality of life. Health outcomes are profoundly unequal globally, reflecting deep structural inequalities in wealth, governance, education, gender relations, and access to resources.

Measuring Health

No single indicator captures the full picture of a population's health. Geographers use multiple measures, each with strengths and limitations:

A top-level answer will explain that composite indicators (HALE, DALYs) are more useful than simple measures (IMR, LE) because they capture multiple dimensions of health, but they require more data and are harder to calculate accurately in data-poor countries.

Global Patterns of Health

Health outcomes show clear global patterns linked to development levels, though with significant exceptions and within-country variation:

  • High-income countries (HICs): Life expectancy typically 78–85+ years; IMR <5 per 1,000; disease burden dominated by non-communicable diseases (NCDs) such as cardiovascular disease, cancers, diabetes, and mental health conditions. Ageing populations create growing healthcare demand. Examples: Japan (LE 84), UK (LE 81), USA (LE 77 — lower than expected for its wealth due to inequality, gun violence, opioid crisis, and high obesity rates)
  • Middle-income countries (MICs): Rapidly improving but highly uneven outcomes. Face a dual burden of communicable AND non-communicable diseases simultaneously (e.g. HIV/TB alongside rising diabetes/heart disease). LE 65–78 years. Examples: India (LE 70, huge regional variation), Brazil (LE 76, significant racial disparities), China (LE 78, rapid improvement since 1980)
  • Low-income countries (LICs): LE often 50–65 years; IMR >40 per 1,000 in worst cases; disease burden dominated by communicable diseases (malaria, TB, HIV/AIDS, diarrhoeal diseases, respiratory infections). Maternal and child mortality remain extremely high. Examples: Chad (LE 54), Central African Republic (LE 54), Sierra Leone (LE 55)

2. The Epidemiological Transition Model

Epidemiological transition: The shift in the pattern of disease and causes of death that accompanies economic development and demographic change. First proposed by Abdel Omran (1971), it describes how populations transition from predominantly infectious/communicable disease mortality to chronic/degenerative non-communicable disease mortality as they develop, urbanise, and age. The model parallels the demographic transition model.

Exam Tip: The epidemiological transition model is useful but must be critiqued. Key criticisms: (1) it assumes a linear progression that all countries follow, ignoring the dual burden experienced by many MICs facing communicable AND non-communicable diseases simultaneously, (2) it was based primarily on Western European historical experience and may not apply to countries following different development paths, (3) it underestimates the persistence of infectious diseases in LICs and the emergence of new pandemics (COVID-19 challenged the assumption that infectious diseases only dominate in early stages), (4) it does not adequately account for within-country inequalities (e.g. Indigenous Australians have significantly lower LE than non-Indigenous Australians despite Australia being a HIC). Always evaluate models rather than simply describing them.

3. Communicable vs Non-Communicable Diseases

Communicable diseases (CDs): Diseases caused by pathogens (bacteria, viruses, parasites, fungi) that can be transmitted between individuals, directly (contact, droplets, bodily fluids) or indirectly (vectors, contaminated water/food, fomites). They are disproportionately concentrated in LICs and tropical regions due to poverty, poor sanitation, lack of healthcare access, and vector-friendly climates.
Non-communicable diseases (NCDs): Diseases that are not transmitted between people. They tend to be chronic (long-lasting), progressive, and linked to lifestyle factors (diet, exercise, smoking, alcohol), genetic predisposition, environmental pollution, and ageing. The four main NCDs — cardiovascular diseases, cancers, chronic respiratory diseases, and diabetes — account for over 70% of all deaths globally and are the leading causes of death in every world region including Africa.

Case Study — HIV/AIDS in Sub-Saharan Africa: Sub-Saharan Africa contains approximately 65% of all people living with HIV globally (~25 million people), despite having only ~17% of the world's population. Eswatini (formerly Swaziland) has the highest adult prevalence at approximately 27%. Contributing factors include: poverty (inability to access healthcare or condoms), gender inequality (women have limited power to negotiate safe sex), cultural stigma surrounding HIV testing and treatment, weak healthcare infrastructure, high rates of concurrent sexual partnerships, and historical denial by political leaders (e.g. Thabo Mbeki in South Africa in the early 2000s, who questioned the link between HIV and AIDS, delaying the rollout of antiretroviral therapy and resulting in an estimated 330,000 preventable deaths). Interventions: PEPFAR (US President's Emergency Plan for AIDS Relief, the largest single-disease programme in history, providing $100+ billion since 2003 for prevention, treatment, and care); the Global Fund to Fight AIDS, TB and Malaria; local NGO education campaigns; condom distribution. Success story: Botswana has achieved near-universal access to ART, raising life expectancy from 49 years (2002) to over 69 years (2023) — demonstrating that political will, international funding, and effective health systems can transform outcomes even in severely affected countries.
Case Study — NCDs in the UK: Cardiovascular disease and cancer are the leading causes of death in the UK, together responsible for over 50% of all deaths. Risk factors include: obesity (approximately 28% of UK adults are obese, one of the highest rates in Europe), physical inactivity (only 66% of adults meet recommended activity levels), smoking (13% of adults, down from 45% in the 1970s), alcohol consumption (over 7 million people drink at levels that increase health risks), and an ageing population (18.6% aged 65+). The NHS spends an estimated 70%+ of its budget managing long-term conditions. Government interventions: the Soft Drinks Industry Levy (sugar tax, 2018) — reduced sugar content in drinks by 44% before it even took effect as manufacturers reformulated; indoor smoking ban (2007) reduced heart attack admissions by 2.4%; Change4Life and 5-a-day campaigns; national cancer screening programmes (breast, cervical, bowel); minimum unit pricing for alcohol in Scotland.

4. The Health-Development Link

Health and development are intrinsically linked in a reinforcing cycle. Poor health constrains economic development (reduced workforce productivity, increased healthcare spending, lower educational attainment for sick children), while poverty perpetuates poor health (inability to afford healthcare, clean water, nutritious food, sanitation). The relationship between GNI per capita and life expectancy is logarithmic — health gains per dollar of income are greatest at lower income levels and diminish at higher incomes. This means that relatively small increases in income in LICs can produce dramatic health improvements, while large increases in spending in HICs yield only marginal gains.

Factors Affecting Global Health Patterns

  • Wealth and income: Higher GNI per capita enables better nutrition, sanitation infrastructure, healthcare spending, and disease prevention. The relationship is logarithmic — initial gains from escaping extreme poverty are enormous (clean water, basic vaccination), while additional spending in wealthy countries yields smaller marginal improvements. However, the USA demonstrates that wealth alone does not guarantee good health — inequality, lifestyle factors, and healthcare system design also matter greatly
  • Education (especially female education): One of the strongest predictors of child survival worldwide. Educated mothers are more likely to: seek prenatal care, deliver in a health facility with a skilled attendant, vaccinate their children, practice good hygiene and nutrition, use family planning, and earn income to invest in family health. UNESCO estimates that if all women completed secondary education, child mortality in Sub-Saharan Africa would fall by 49%
  • Governance and political stability: Stable, transparent, democratic governance enables effective health systems, disease surveillance, and resource allocation. Corruption diverts healthcare resources (the WHO estimates corruption accounts for $500 billion lost annually in health sectors globally). Armed conflict destroys health infrastructure, displaces populations, disrupts supply chains, and creates conditions for disease outbreaks
  • Climate and physical environment: Tropical climates harbour disease vectors (mosquitoes for malaria, dengue, Zika; tsetse flies for sleeping sickness). Water scarcity and poor sanitation increase waterborne diseases (cholera, typhoid). Climate change is expanding the range of vector-borne diseases into previously unaffected areas and increasing the frequency of extreme weather events that damage health infrastructure
  • Globalisation: A double-edged sword for health. Facilitates the rapid spread of infectious diseases across borders (pandemics travel via air routes). But also enables transfer of medical knowledge, pharmaceutical trade, international health cooperation, and economic growth that funds health improvements
  • Culture, gender, and social norms: Cultural practices can positively or negatively affect health-seeking behaviour. Gender inequality limits women's access to healthcare, education, and nutrition in many societies (e.g. female genital mutilation, child marriage, restricted mobility). Social stigma around mental illness, HIV, and disability prevents people from seeking treatment

5. Human Rights

Human rights: Universal, inalienable rights and freedoms to which all people are entitled, regardless of nationality, sex, ethnicity, religion, language, or other status. The foundational document is the Universal Declaration of Human Rights (UDHR), adopted by the UN General Assembly on 10 December 1948 (in response to the atrocities of World War II and the Holocaust). It sets out 30 fundamental rights, including the right to life, liberty, education, health, freedom from torture, and freedom of expression. While not legally binding itself, the UDHR has inspired legally binding international treaties including the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights (ICCPR) and the International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights (ICESCR).

Key Human Rights Issues in Geography

  • Right to health (Article 25 UDHR): Access to healthcare, clean water, adequate nutrition, and sanitation. Violated when governments fail to provide basic health services or when healthcare is prohibitively expensive for the poor
  • Right to education (Article 26): Free and compulsory primary education; equal access to higher education. Still denied to ~244 million children globally, disproportionately girls in LICs
  • Freedom from torture (Article 5): Prohibition of cruel, inhuman, or degrading treatment. Violated systematically in authoritarian states, conflict zones, and detention facilities
  • Freedom of expression (Article 19): Right to hold opinions and express them without interference. Restricted in authoritarian regimes through censorship, imprisonment of journalists, and internet shutdowns
  • Right to asylum (Article 14): Protection from persecution in other countries. Challenged by rising anti-immigration sentiment and border barriers in many HICs
  • Gender equality: Equal rights regardless of sex; freedom from discrimination. Violated through FGM (affecting 200+ million women globally), forced/child marriage, honour-based violence, and unequal legal rights
  • Children's rights (UN Convention on the Rights of the Child, 1989): Protection from exploitation, child labour (152 million children in child labour globally), trafficking, and recruitment as child soldiers (~250,000 worldwide)

Patterns of Human Rights Violations

Human rights abuses are not confined to any single region but are concentrated where conflict, authoritarian governance, poverty, and discrimination intersect:

  • Conflict zones: Syria (500,000+ deaths, 13 million displaced, chemical weapons use), Yemen (worst humanitarian crisis globally, 377,000+ deaths, 80% of population needing aid), Myanmar (Rohingya genocide), Ukraine (Russian invasion causing massive displacement and civilian casualties)
  • Authoritarian regimes: North Korea (political prison camps holding 80,000–120,000 people, forced labour, severe restrictions on all freedoms), Eritrea (indefinite military conscription amounting to forced labour, no press freedom, no elections), China (mass surveillance, social credit system, restrictions on religious and ethnic minorities)
  • Gender-based violations: Afghanistan (Taliban restrictions on women's education, employment, movement, and dress since 2021 — the only country banning girls from secondary school), FGM in parts of Africa and the Middle East, forced marriage affecting 12 million girls annually
  • Ethnic/religious persecution: Rohingya in Myanmar (ethnic cleansing since 2017), Uyghurs in China (mass detention, forced labour, cultural suppression in Xinjiang), caste-based discrimination in India and Nepal
Case Study — Rohingya Crisis, Myanmar: Since August 2017, over 740,000 Rohingya Muslims have fled Myanmar (Burma) to neighbouring Bangladesh following a military offensive described by the UN as "a textbook example of ethnic cleansing." The Rohingya have been denied citizenship since the 1982 Citizenship Law, facing restrictions on movement, education, marriage, and healthcare. The 2017 military operations involved mass killings, widespread sexual violence (used systematically as a weapon of war), and the burning of hundreds of villages (satellite imagery documented the destruction of over 350 villages). Over 1 million Rohingya now live in the world's largest refugee camp complex at Cox's Bazar, Bangladesh, in overcrowded conditions with limited access to education, healthcare, and livelihood opportunities. The crisis raises fundamental questions about: (1) state sovereignty vs the international Responsibility to Protect (R2P), (2) the effectiveness of the UN Security Council (vetoes by China and Russia have blocked binding resolutions against Myanmar), (3) the capacity and willingness of neighbouring countries to absorb large refugee populations, (4) the role of social media in inciting violence (Facebook was used to spread anti-Rohingya hate speech), and (5) the slow pace of international justice (the ICJ ordered Myanmar to prevent genocide in 2020 but enforcement remains minimal).

6. Geopolitical Intervention

Geopolitical intervention: Actions taken by external actors (states, military alliances, IGOs such as the UN) to influence the internal affairs of another sovereign state, ranging from diplomatic pressure at the soft end to military invasion at the hard end. Interventions may be motivated by humanitarian concerns (protecting civilians from atrocities), strategic interests (access to resources, containing rival powers), ideological goals (promoting democracy), or a combination. The legitimacy, effectiveness, and consequences of intervention are hotly debated.

The Responsibility to Protect (R2P)

Adopted by the UN World Summit in 2005, R2P establishes three pillars of responsibility:

  1. Pillar 1 (State responsibility): Every state has the primary responsibility to protect its own populations from genocide, war crimes, ethnic cleansing, and crimes against humanity
  2. Pillar 2 (International assistance): The international community should assist states in meeting this responsibility through capacity building, early warning, and diplomacy
  3. Pillar 3 (Collective action): If a state is manifestly failing to protect its population (or is itself the perpetrator), the international community should take collective action through the UN Security Council, which may include sanctions, peacekeeping, or, as a last resort, military intervention

R2P has been invoked in Libya (2011), Côte d'Ivoire (2011), and South Sudan (2013), but was notably NOT applied in Syria despite widespread atrocities. This selective application is a major criticism — R2P is only invoked when it aligns with the geopolitical interests of the P5 (permanent UN Security Council members: USA, UK, France, Russia, China). Russia and China's veto power has repeatedly blocked action in Syria and Myanmar.

Case Study — Libya (2011): NATO intervention in Libya was authorised by UN Security Council Resolution 1973 to protect civilians in Benghazi during the civil uprising against Colonel Muammar Gaddafi. A no-fly zone was imposed, followed by air strikes that supported rebel forces. Outcome: The immediate objective of protecting Benghazi was achieved, and Gaddafi was overthrown and killed. However, critics argue the intervention: (1) exceeded its mandate (regime change rather than civilian protection, undermining the legitimacy of R2P for future cases), (2) resulted in state collapse and a power vacuum, (3) enabled the rise of militia rule, with two rival governments and ongoing civil war, (4) contributed to a migrant crisis as Libya became a transit hub for people attempting to cross the Mediterranean to Europe (with horrific conditions in Libyan detention centres), and (5) the spread of weapons from Libyan arsenals destabilised the Sahel region (contributing to conflict in Mali, Niger, and Nigeria). Libya illustrates the law of unintended consequences and the critical importance of post-intervention planning.
Case Study — Syria (2011–present): The Syrian Civil War, which began with protests during the Arab Spring, has caused over 500,000 deaths and displaced 13+ million people (6.8 million internally, 6.8 million refugees). Despite widespread atrocities including documented chemical weapons attacks on civilians (Ghouta 2013, Khan Shaykhun 2017, Douma 2018) and the systematic targeting of hospitals and civilian infrastructure, direct military intervention to protect civilians was blocked by Russian and Chinese vetoes in the UN Security Council (Russia vetoed 16+ resolutions on Syria). Limited US-led air strikes targeted chemical weapons facilities (2017, 2018) but did not aim to change the conflict's trajectory. Russia and Iran provided military support to Assad's government, while the USA and allies supported some opposition groups. Syria demonstrates how geopolitical competition (Russian strategic interests in its naval base at Tartus, Western reluctance after the Iraq and Libya experiences) can prevent humanitarian intervention even in the face of the most severe human rights violations, effectively rendering R2P powerless when P5 interests conflict.

Exam Tip: Edexcel questions often require you to evaluate the effectiveness of interventions. Use specific case studies and consider multiple perspectives: (1) Was the intervention justified (legal basis, humanitarian need)? (2) Did it achieve its stated objectives? (3) Were there unintended consequences (state collapse, refugee flows, regional instability)? (4) Who benefited and who was harmed? (5) What does this tell us about the tension between state sovereignty and the international responsibility to protect? Always consider power dynamics: why are some crises intervened in (Libya) while others are not (Syria, Rohingya)? The answer usually lies in the geopolitical interests of the P5 powers.

8. Global Governance of Health

MDGs to SDGs

The Millennium Development Goals (MDGs, 2000–2015) included specific health targets. Progress was significant but uneven:

  • Child mortality (U5MR) fell by over 50% globally (from 90 to 43 per 1,000 live births)
  • New HIV infections declined by 40%; 15 million people accessing ART by 2015
  • Maternal mortality fell by 45%, short of the 75% target (Sub-Saharan Africa lagged furthest behind)
  • Malaria deaths fell by 60%, partly due to insecticide-treated bed nets (ITNs) funded by the Global Fund

The Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs, 2015–2030) include SDG 3 (Good Health and Well-being) with broader targets covering: maternal mortality (<70 per 100,000 by 2030), communicable diseases (end AIDS, TB, malaria epidemics), NCDs (reduce premature mortality by one-third), mental health, substance abuse, universal health coverage (UHC), and access to essential medicines and vaccines. Progress is currently off-track for most SDG 3 targets, exacerbated by COVID-19 disruption.

Pharmaceutical TNCs and Access to Medicines

Transnational pharmaceutical corporations play a crucial but deeply contested role in global health:

  • Patent protection (TRIPS Agreement): The WTO's TRIPS agreement protects pharmaceutical patents for 20 years, maintaining high prices and limiting access in LICs. A year's supply of ART for HIV cost $10,000+ per patient when first introduced, falling to ~$75 with generic competition. The tension between intellectual property rights (incentivising innovation) and access to essential medicines is a central debate in global health governance
  • Generic medicines: India is the "pharmacy of the developing world," producing ~70% of global generic medicines. Generic ARVs for HIV treatment in Africa cost a fraction of branded versions. The Doha Declaration (2001) affirmed that TRIPS should not prevent countries from protecting public health and allows compulsory licensing (governments can produce or import generics for public health emergencies)
  • Neglected tropical diseases (NTDs): Pharmaceutical companies invest less in diseases affecting mainly poor populations (sleeping sickness, Chagas disease, leishmaniasis, river blindness) because there is limited profit potential. This is the "90/10 gap" — historically, 90% of global health research spending targeted diseases affecting only 10% of the global disease burden (affluent populations)
  • Vaccine inequality (COVID-19): The pandemic starkly exposed global inequalities in access to health technologies. By mid-2021, wealthy nations had vaccinated over 50% of their populations while many African nations remained below 2%. Vaccine nationalism (wealthy countries securing bilateral deals directly with manufacturers, hoarding surplus doses) undermined multilateral efforts
Case Study — COVAX Initiative: COVAX (COVID-19 Vaccines Global Access) was established as a global mechanism to ensure equitable vaccine distribution, co-led by Gavi (the Vaccine Alliance), CEPI (Coalition for Epidemic Preparedness Innovations), and the WHO. It aimed to deliver 2 billion doses to LICs and MICs by end of 2021. However, COVAX fell far short of its targets due to: (1) vaccine nationalism — wealthy countries negotiated bilateral deals with manufacturers, securing enough doses for their own populations many times over, leaving COVAX unable to compete, (2) export bans by producer countries (e.g. India temporarily banned vaccine exports during its devastating second wave), (3) production shortages and supply chain bottlenecks, (4) intellectual property barriers — proposals by India and South Africa for a temporary TRIPS waiver on COVID vaccines were initially blocked by wealthy nations protecting pharmaceutical company interests. By end of 2021, COVAX had delivered only ~950 million of its 2 billion dose target. This experience highlighted the enduring challenge of translating global health governance principles into effective action when they conflict with national self-interest and corporate profit.
Exam Tip: When writing about global health governance, demonstrate synoptic thinking by linking this topic to other areas of your course: globalisation (TNCs, trade rules), development (aid, debt, governance), superpowers (geopolitical alliances shaping health policy), and migration (health of refugees and migrants). For example, discuss how TNC power, intellectual property rules, vaccine nationalism, and geopolitical alliances all intersect to shape who has access to medicines and health technologies. The best answers recognise that health is not merely a medical issue but a deeply political one, shaped by power, inequality, and governance at multiple scales.
