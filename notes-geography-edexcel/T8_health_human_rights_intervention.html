Health, Human Rights & Intervention (Edexcel Paper 2)

This topic explores the complex relationships between global health, human rights, and the role of geopolitical intervention. It examines how health outcomes vary globally, why human rights abuses occur, and the effectiveness and ethics of different forms of intervention by governments, IGOs, and NGOs.

1. Global Health Patterns

Health: A state of complete physical, mental, and social well-being, not merely the absence of disease or infirmity (WHO definition). Health outcomes are unevenly distributed globally, reflecting deep inequalities in wealth, governance, and access to resources.

Measuring Health

Indicator Description Strengths Limitations
Life expectancy Average number of years a newborn can expect to live Easy to compare; widely available Masks internal inequalities (gender, regional, ethnic)
Infant mortality rate Deaths of infants under 1 per 1,000 live births Sensitive indicator of healthcare quality Can be affected by different recording methods
Under-5 mortality rate Deaths of children under 5 per 1,000 live births Reflects broader child health and nutrition Does not reveal causes of death
Maternal mortality ratio Deaths of women during pregnancy/childbirth per 100,000 live births Strong indicator of healthcare access for women Under-reported in many developing nations
DALYs Disability-Adjusted Life Years lost to ill health, disability, or death Captures both fatal and non-fatal disease burden Complex to calculate; data gaps in LICs
HALE Healthy life expectancy (years lived in good health) More nuanced than basic life expectancy Relies on subjective health assessments

Global Patterns of Health

Health outcomes show a clear global pattern linked to development levels:

  • High-income countries (HICs): Life expectancy typically 75-85+ years; low infant mortality (<5 per 1,000); disease burden dominated by non-communicable diseases (NCDs) such as cancer, heart disease, and diabetes
  • Middle-income countries (MICs): Rapidly improving but uneven; dual burden of communicable and non-communicable diseases; life expectancy 65-78 years
  • Low-income countries (LICs): Life expectancy often 50-65 years; high infant and maternal mortality; disease burden dominated by communicable diseases (malaria, TB, HIV/AIDS, diarrhoeal diseases)

2. The Epidemiological Transition Model

Epidemiological transition: The shift in the pattern of disease and causes of death that accompanies economic development. First proposed by Abdel Omran (1971), it describes how populations move from predominantly infectious disease deaths to chronic/degenerative disease deaths as they develop.

Stages of the Epidemiological Transition

Stage Name Characteristics Examples
1 Age of Pestilence & Famine High mortality from infectious diseases, famine, and war. Life expectancy 20-40 years. Epidemics like plague, cholera, and smallpox are frequent. Pre-industrial societies; some parts of Sub-Saharan Africa still experience elements
2 Age of Receding Pandemics Improved nutrition, sanitation, and early medicine reduce infectious disease. Life expectancy rises to 50+ years. Mortality becomes more predictable. 19th-century Europe; present-day Bangladesh, Kenya
3 Age of Degenerative & Man-Made Diseases NCDs (heart disease, cancer, stroke, diabetes) become leading causes of death. Life expectancy 65-75+. Linked to ageing, lifestyle, and industrialisation. Present-day China, Brazil, many MICs
4 Age of Delayed Degenerative Diseases Medical technology delays NCD mortality. Rising obesity, mental health, and re-emerging infectious diseases. Life expectancy 75-85+. UK, Japan, USA, Western Europe
5 (proposed) Age of Re-emerging Infections Antimicrobial resistance, new pandemics (COVID-19), and lifestyle diseases create a complex health landscape. Some scholars propose this additional stage. Global (COVID-19 pandemic); drug-resistant TB
Exam Tip: The epidemiological transition model is a simplification. Be prepared to critique it: it assumes a linear progression, does not account for the "dual burden" experienced by many MICs (facing communicable AND non-communicable diseases simultaneously), and was based primarily on Western European experience. COVID-19 has challenged the assumption that infectious diseases only dominate in early stages.

3. Communicable vs Non-Communicable Diseases

Communicable diseases (CDs): Diseases caused by pathogens (bacteria, viruses, parasites, fungi) that can be transmitted between individuals directly or via vectors, water, food, or air.
Non-communicable diseases (NCDs): Diseases that are not transmitted between people. They tend to be chronic (long-lasting) and are often linked to lifestyle, genetics, and environmental factors. The four main NCDs are cardiovascular diseases, cancers, chronic respiratory diseases, and diabetes.
Feature Communicable Diseases Non-Communicable Diseases
Cause Pathogens (bacteria, viruses, parasites) Lifestyle, genetics, environmental factors
Transmission Person to person, vectors, contaminated water/food Not transmissible between people
Duration Usually acute (short-term), some chronic (HIV, TB) Usually chronic (long-term)
Prevention Vaccination, sanitation, vector control, hygiene Lifestyle changes, screening, regulation
Geographic pattern Higher burden in LICs and tropical regions Higher burden in HICs; rising rapidly in MICs
Key examples Malaria, HIV/AIDS, TB, cholera, Ebola Heart disease, cancer, diabetes, COPD
Case Study - HIV/AIDS in Sub-Saharan Africa: Sub-Saharan Africa accounts for approximately 65% of global HIV infections. Eswatini (formerly Swaziland) has the world's highest adult prevalence at around 27%. Contributing factors include poverty, gender inequality, limited access to antiretroviral therapy (ART), stigma, and weak healthcare infrastructure. The epidemic has reduced life expectancy, created millions of orphans, and devastated working-age populations. Interventions include PEPFAR (US-funded programme providing ART), the Global Fund, and local NGO education campaigns. Botswana is a success story where universal ART access has raised life expectancy from 49 (2002) to 69 (2023).
Case Study - NCDs in the UK: Cardiovascular disease and cancer are the leading causes of death in the UK, responsible for over 50% of all deaths. Risk factors include high rates of obesity (28% of adults), physical inactivity, smoking, and alcohol consumption. The NHS spends an estimated 70%+ of its budget on managing long-term conditions. Government interventions include the sugar tax (Soft Drinks Industry Levy, 2018), smoking bans, public health campaigns, and cancer screening programmes.

4. The Health-Development Link

Health and development are intrinsically linked in a reinforcing cycle. Poor health constrains economic development, while poverty perpetuates poor health outcomes.

Factors Affecting Global Health Patterns

  • Wealth and income: Higher GNI per capita enables better nutrition, sanitation, healthcare spending, and infrastructure. The relationship is logarithmic - gains are greatest at lower income levels.
  • Education: Female education is one of the strongest predictors of child survival. Educated mothers are more likely to seek prenatal care, vaccinate children, and practice good hygiene.
  • Governance: Stable, transparent governance enables effective health systems. Corruption diverts healthcare resources. Conflict destroys health infrastructure.
  • Climate and environment: Tropical climates harbour disease vectors (mosquitoes for malaria, dengue). Water scarcity and poor sanitation increase waterborne disease.
  • Globalisation: Facilitates spread of diseases (pandemics) but also enables medical knowledge transfer, pharmaceutical trade, and international health cooperation.
  • Culture and gender: Cultural practices can affect health-seeking behaviour. Gender inequality limits women's access to healthcare in many societies.

5. Human Rights

Human rights: Universal rights and freedoms to which all people are entitled, regardless of nationality, sex, ethnicity, religion, language, or other status. The Universal Declaration of Human Rights (UDHR, 1948) sets out 30 fundamental rights.

Key Human Rights Issues

  • Right to health: Access to healthcare, clean water, adequate nutrition, and sanitation
  • Right to education: Free and compulsory primary education; equal access to higher education
  • Freedom from torture: Prohibition of cruel, inhuman, or degrading treatment
  • Freedom of expression: Right to hold opinions and express them without interference
  • Right to asylum: Protection from persecution in other countries
  • Gender equality: Equal rights regardless of sex; freedom from discrimination
  • Children's rights: Protection from exploitation, child labour, trafficking, and recruitment as soldiers

Patterns of Human Rights Violations

Human rights abuses are not confined to any single region but are concentrated in areas affected by conflict, authoritarian governance, poverty, and discrimination. Key patterns include:

  • Conflict zones: Syria, Yemen, Myanmar - civilian casualties, displacement, use of chemical weapons, denial of humanitarian access
  • Authoritarian regimes: North Korea, Eritrea - suppression of political opposition, forced labour, severe restrictions on movement and expression
  • Gender-based violations: Afghanistan (Taliban restrictions on women), FGM in parts of Africa, forced marriage
  • Ethnic/religious persecution: Rohingya in Myanmar, Uyghurs in China, minorities in many regions
Case Study - Rohingya Crisis, Myanmar: Since 2017, over 700,000 Rohingya Muslims have fled Myanmar to Bangladesh following military operations described by the UN as ethnic cleansing. The Rohingya have been denied citizenship since 1982, face restrictions on movement, education, and healthcare, and have experienced mass killings, sexual violence, and village burnings. The crisis raises questions about state sovereignty vs. international responsibility to protect, the effectiveness of the UN Security Council (vetoes by China and Russia have blocked action), and the capacity of neighbouring countries to absorb refugees.

6. Geopolitical Intervention

Geopolitical intervention: Actions taken by external actors (states, IGOs, military alliances) to influence the internal affairs of another state, ranging from diplomatic pressure to military action. Interventions may be motivated by humanitarian concerns, strategic interests, or a combination.

Types of Intervention

The Responsibility to Protect (R2P)

Adopted by the UN World Summit in 2005, R2P establishes three pillars:

  1. Pillar 1: Every state has the responsibility to protect its populations from genocide, war crimes, ethnic cleansing, and crimes against humanity
  2. Pillar 2: The international community should assist states in meeting this responsibility
  3. Pillar 3: If a state fails to protect its population, the international community should take collective action through the UN Security Council
Case Study - Libya (2011): NATO intervention in Libya was authorised by UN Security Council Resolution 1973 to protect civilians during the civil uprising against Gaddafi. A no-fly zone was imposed, followed by air strikes. While the immediate objective of protecting Benghazi's population was achieved, critics argue the intervention exceeded its mandate (regime change rather than civilian protection), resulted in state collapse, and created a power vacuum that led to ongoing instability, militia rule, and a migrant crisis. Libya illustrates the unintended consequences of intervention and the challenge of post-intervention state-building.
Case Study - Syria (2011-present): The Syrian Civil War has caused over 500,000 deaths and displaced 13+ million people. Despite widespread atrocities including chemical weapons attacks, direct military intervention to protect civilians was blocked by Russian and Chinese vetoes in the UN Security Council. Limited US-led strikes targeted chemical weapons facilities (2017, 2018), but no comprehensive intervention occurred. Syria demonstrates how geopolitics (Russian support for Assad, Western reluctance after Iraq/Libya) can prevent intervention even in the face of severe human rights violations.

Exam Tip: Edexcel questions often require you to evaluate the effectiveness of interventions. Use specific case studies and consider multiple perspectives: (1) Was the intervention justified? (2) Did it achieve its objectives? (3) Were there unintended consequences? (4) Who benefited and who was harmed? Always consider the tension between state sovereignty and the international responsibility to protect.

8. Health and Development: Global Governance

Millennium Development Goals (MDGs) to Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs)

The MDGs (2000-2015) included specific health targets (reducing child mortality, combating HIV/AIDS, improving maternal health). Progress was mixed:

  • Child mortality fell by over 50% globally
  • New HIV infections declined by 40%
  • Maternal mortality fell by 45%, short of the 75% target
  • Progress was very uneven, with Sub-Saharan Africa lagging behind

The SDGs (2015-2030) include SDG 3 (Good Health and Well-being) with targets covering maternal mortality, communicable disease, NCDs, mental health, universal health coverage, and access to essential medicines.

Pharmaceutical TNCs and Access to Medicines

Transnational pharmaceutical companies play a crucial but controversial role in global health:

  • Patent protection: TRIPS agreement protects pharmaceutical patents for 20 years, keeping prices high and limiting access in LICs
  • Generic medicines: India is the world's largest producer of generic medicines, providing affordable alternatives (e.g., generic ARVs for HIV treatment in Africa)
  • Neglected tropical diseases: Pharmaceutical companies invest less in diseases affecting mainly poor populations due to limited profit potential
  • Vaccine inequality: COVID-19 highlighted stark global inequality in vaccine access - by mid-2021, wealthy nations had vaccinated over 50% of populations while many African nations remained below 2%
Case Study - COVAX Initiative: COVAX was established as a global mechanism to ensure equitable access to COVID-19 vaccines for all countries regardless of income. Led by Gavi, CEPI, and WHO, it aimed to deliver 2 billion doses to LICs by end of 2021. However, vaccine nationalism (wealthy countries securing bilateral deals directly with manufacturers), export bans, and production shortages meant COVAX fell far short of its targets. This highlighted the enduring challenge of translating global health governance principles into effective action against national self-interest.
Exam Tip: When writing about global health governance, demonstrate synoptic thinking by linking this topic to globalisation, development, superpowers, and geopolitics. For example, discuss how TNC power, trade rules, and geopolitical alliances shape access to medicines and health outcomes in different parts of the world.
