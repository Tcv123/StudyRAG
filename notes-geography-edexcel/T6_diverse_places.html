Topic 6: Diverse Places

6.1 What is a Place?

Place: A location with meaning. Places are given character by the people who live there, the activities that occur, and the physical environment. Place is a fundamental concept in geography that goes beyond simple location to include identity, lived experience, and meaning.
Sense of place: The subjective emotional attachment people have to a place, shaped by personal experiences, memories, and cultural connections. This varies between individuals and groups.

Insider and Outsider Perspectives

  • Insider perspective — the view of people who live in or have a strong connection to a place; shaped by everyday lived experience; often more nuanced and complex
  • Outsider perspective — the view of people who visit, observe, or study a place from outside; may rely on media representations, stereotypes, or limited visits
  • Both perspectives are valid but partial — a complete understanding requires both

Place Representations

How places are portrayed in media, art, literature, and official sources shapes perceptions:

  • Formal representations — census data, council reports, maps, textbooks, official statistics
  • Informal representations — films, novels, music, social media, photography, street art, personal narratives
  • Representations can be positive (promoting tourism, investment) or negative (reinforcing stereotypes, stigmatising communities)
  • Media representations often simplify complex realities and may not reflect lived experience
Exam Tip: You must study two contrasting places for this topic — one local place you can investigate through fieldwork, and one contrasting place (different region, different economic profile). Be prepared to compare them using specific data, qualitative evidence, and your own fieldwork findings.

6.2 How and Why Places Change

Factors Driving Change

Economic Change

  • Deindustrialisation — decline of manufacturing in former industrial areas (e.g. Sheffield steel, Detroit auto industry, South Wales coal); causes unemployment, depopulation, and social deprivation
  • Post-industrial economy — shift to service sector, creative industries, technology, retail, tourism; benefits some areas (London, Manchester) but not all
  • Regeneration — investment to revive declining areas through new housing, infrastructure, business incentives
  • Gentrification — wealthier people move into formerly deprived areas, renovating properties; raises house prices and can displace existing residents
  • Studentification — areas near universities dominated by student renters; impacts on community stability, house prices, noise
Gentrification: The process by which a previously low-income neighbourhood is transformed by an influx of wealthier residents and investment, leading to rising property values, improved amenities, but also potential displacement of original residents.

Demographic Change

  • International migration — increases ethnic and cultural diversity; migrants fill labour shortages; cultural contributions (food, festivals, religion); can cause social tensions
  • Internal migration — counterurbanisation (movement from cities to rural areas); rural depopulation; suburbanisation
  • Ageing population — places with high proportions of elderly residents (e.g. coastal retirement towns) have different service needs and economic profiles
  • Natural change — birth and death rates affect population structure and place character

Impact of Migration on UK Places

  • Economic — fills skills gaps (NHS, agriculture, construction); entrepreneurship; contributes more in tax than received in benefits (on average); consumer spending
  • Cultural — diverse cuisine, festivals, religious buildings, languages, music; enriches cultural landscape; creates distinct ethnic neighbourhoods (e.g. Brick Lane, Southall, Chinatown)
  • Social — pressure on housing and public services in some areas; language barriers; social tensions in some communities; but also community organisations and mutual support networks
  • Demographic — younger migrant population helps offset ageing; higher birth rates in some communities

6.4 Identity and Belonging

Factors Shaping Place Identity

  • Ethnicity and nationality — ethnic communities shape neighbourhood character; cultural institutions, religious buildings, shops, food
  • Socioeconomic status — wealthy areas vs deprived areas have very different identities; linked to housing type, services, health outcomes
  • Age — young people and elderly people may experience and identify with places very differently
  • Gender — women's experience of public spaces may differ from men's (safety, accessibility)
  • Length of residence — long-term residents have deeper attachment; newcomers bring change
  • Built environment — architecture, historical buildings, public spaces all contribute to place identity

Belonging and Exclusion

Place attachment: The emotional bond between a person and a particular place, developed through experience, memory, and social connections. Strong attachment contributes to wellbeing but can also create resistance to change.
  • Some groups may feel they belong to a place — deep roots, social networks, shared identity
  • Others may feel excluded — discrimination, poverty, language barriers, lack of representation
  • Territorial behaviour — some groups may try to control or define a place, excluding others (e.g. gang territories, gated communities, NIMBYism)

Index of Multiple Deprivation (IMD): A measure of relative deprivation in England, combining seven domains: income, employment, education, health, crime, barriers to housing and services, and living environment. Areas are ranked from 1 (most deprived) to 32,844 (least deprived).

6.6 Place Studies

Case Study: London Docklands / Tower Hamlets — Diverse Urban Place
Context: Tower Hamlets in East London is one of the UK's most diverse and contrasting boroughs. Historically a working-class area with large Bangladeshi community (Brick Lane). Canary Wharf financial district was built on former docklands (LDDC regeneration from 1981).
Diversity: 35%+ Bangladeshi heritage; significant Somali, Chinese, and Eastern European communities; 43% of residents born outside the UK; 90+ languages spoken. Simultaneously home to extremely wealthy Canary Wharf workers and some of the most deprived neighbourhoods in England.
Change: Massive gentrification since 2000s; new luxury apartments alongside council estates; property prices surged; tensions between established communities and newcomers; cultural richness from migration but also pressures on housing and services.
Inequality: Average salaries in Canary Wharf among highest in UK, yet child poverty rates in Tower Hamlets among the worst. Life expectancy varies by several years between wards within the same borough.
Case Study: Rural Place — The Cotswolds
Context: Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty in south-central England. Honey-coloured limestone villages. Tourism-dependent economy attracting ~38 million visitor days per year.
Demographics: Ageing population; limited ethnic diversity (~95% White British); outmigration of young people due to lack of affordable housing and employment; counterurbanisation by wealthy retirees and commuters.
Challenges: Average house prices 10–12x local salaries; second homes and holiday lets reduce housing stock; limited public transport; closure of rural services (post offices, pubs, shops); ageing workforce; seasonal tourism employment.
Identity: Strong sense of place linked to landscape, heritage, and tradition. Conservation vs development tensions. Place representation in media (TV shows, tourist brochures) creates an idealised image that may not reflect challenges faced by working residents.

6.7 Place-Making and Regeneration

Place-making: The process of creating quality places that people want to live, work, play, and learn in. It involves the planning, design, and management of public spaces to improve community wellbeing and sense of place.

Regeneration Strategies

Economic regenerationAttracting new businesses, creating enterprise zones, retraining workersSheffield Digital Campus (tech sector replacing steel)

Cultural regenerationUsing arts, heritage, and culture to revive an area's identityTate Modern transforming Bankside, London; Bilbao Guggenheim effect
Case Study: Stratford, East London — Olympic Regeneration
Before (pre-2005): One of the most deprived areas in England; contaminated industrial land; poor infrastructure; high unemployment; limited green space; negative image.
Regeneration: GBP 9 billion+ investment for 2012 Olympics; Queen Elizabeth Olympic Park (100 hectares of parkland); Westfield Stratford City shopping centre (largest urban shopping centre in Europe); new housing (East Village: 2,818 homes); improved transport (Crossrail/Elizabeth Line, DLR extensions); new schools and health centres.
Outcomes: 11,000+ new homes planned; 46,000 permanent jobs created in the area; Stratford property prices increased significantly; area transformed from deprived to aspirational.
Criticisms: Gentrification displaced some existing communities; affordable housing targets not fully met; legacy jobs are often low-paid service sector; some local residents feel excluded from benefits.
Exam Tip: For Diverse Places questions, always link to your fieldwork experience and the specific places you have studied. Use a mix of quantitative data (census, IMD, economic statistics) and qualitative evidence (interviews, observations, representations). Show awareness that different groups experience the same place differently, and critically evaluate whether regeneration benefits all residents or creates new inequalities.
