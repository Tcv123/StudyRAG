Topic 2: Landscape Systems, Processes & Change — Coastal Landscapes

2.1 The Coast as a System

Littoral zone: The area between the highest and lowest points affected by wave action, including the coast and nearshore zone. Coasts operate as open systems with inputs, outputs, stores, and transfers.

Systems Approach

ComponentExamples
InputsWave energy, tidal energy, wind energy, sediment (from rivers, cliff erosion, offshore), solar energy, precipitation
OutputsSediment lost offshore/alongshore, evaporation, wave energy dissipation
StoresBeaches, sand dunes, mudflats, marshes, cliffs
Transfers/FlowsLongshore drift, erosion, weathering, mass movement, deposition
Sediment cell: A self-contained stretch of coastline within which the movement of sediment is largely contained. England and Wales have 11 major sediment cells. They are a useful management unit for Shoreline Management Plans (SMPs).
Dynamic equilibrium: The state where the coast maintains a balance between inputs and outputs of energy and sediment. Disruption to one part of the system (e.g. building a groyne) creates feedback effects elsewhere.

2.2 Waves

Wave: An oscillation of water caused by wind blowing over the sea surface. Wave energy is determined by wind speed, wind duration, and fetch (the distance of open water over which the wind blows).

Wave Characteristics

  • Wave height — vertical distance from trough to crest
  • Wavelength — horizontal distance between successive crests
  • Wave frequency — number of waves per minute
  • Wave period — time between successive crests passing a fixed point
  • Fetch — distance of open water over which wind blows; longer fetch = larger, more powerful waves
  • Swash — the rush of water up the beach after a wave breaks
  • Backwash — the return flow of water back down the beach under gravity

Constructive vs Destructive Waves

FeatureConstructiveDestructive
Frequency6–8 per minute (low)10–14 per minute (high)
Wave heightLow (<1 m)High (>1 m)
WavelengthLongShort
Swash vs backwashStrong swash, weak backwashWeak swash, strong backwash
EffectDeposits sediment, builds beachesErodes beaches, removes sediment
Wave steepnessLow (spilling breakers)High (plunging breakers)

Wave Refraction

As waves approach an irregular coastline, they slow down in shallower water near headlands, causing wave energy to be concentrated on the headland. In bays, waves diverge and energy is spread out, leading to deposition. This differential erosion over time tends to straighten coastlines.

2.3 Coastal Processes

Weathering

  • Mechanical weathering — freeze-thaw (water in cracks expands by 9% on freezing), wetting/drying (expansion/contraction of clay minerals), salt crystallisation (salt crystals grow in rock pores)
  • Chemical weathering — carbonation (CO2 + rainwater forms weak carbonic acid, dissolves calcium carbonate in limestone/chalk), oxidation (iron-bearing minerals react with oxygen), hydrolysis
  • Biological weathering — root growth widens cracks; burrowing organisms; organic acids from decomposing vegetation

Erosion Processes

ProcessDescription
Hydraulic actionForce of water hitting the cliff compresses air in cracks, weakening the rock. Repeated compression and decompression causes rock to fracture.
Abrasion (corrasion)Waves hurl sediment at the cliff face, wearing it away like sandpaper. Most effective with large, angular material in high-energy conditions.
AttritionRocks and pebbles collide with each other in the water, becoming smaller and more rounded over time.
Corrosion (solution)Chemical dissolution of soluble rocks (limestone, chalk) by slightly acidic seawater.

Transport Processes

  • Traction — large boulders/cobbles rolled along the seabed by wave energy
  • Saltation — pebbles bounced along the seabed
  • Suspension — fine sand and silt carried within the water column
  • Solution — dissolved minerals carried invisibly in the water

Longshore Drift

Longshore drift (LSD): The movement of sediment along the coast by wave action. Waves approach at an angle determined by the prevailing wind, carrying sediment up the beach at that angle (swash), while backwash returns straight down the beach under gravity. This creates a zig-zag movement of sediment.

Deposition

Deposition occurs when wave energy is insufficient to transport sediment. This happens when:

  • Waves enter sheltered areas (bays, behind spits)
  • Reduced wind speed or shorter fetch
  • Constructive waves dominate
  • River enters the sea (reduced velocity)
  • Longshore drift meets an obstruction (natural or human)

2.4 Erosional Landforms

Headlands and Bays

Form on coastlines with alternating bands of resistant and less resistant rock (discordant coastline). Softer rock erodes faster to form bays; harder rock remains as headlands. Over time, wave refraction focuses energy on headlands and deposits sediment in bays.

Cliffs and Wave-Cut Platforms

  • Erosion concentrated at the base of the cliff between high and low tide marks (wave-cut notch)
  • Notch deepens until overhanging rock collapses
  • Cliff retreats, leaving a gently sloping wave-cut platform at the base
  • Platform grows wider over time; eventually limits further erosion as waves break further from the cliff

Caves, Arches, Stacks, and Stumps

  1. Cave — hydraulic action and abrasion exploit a line of weakness (fault or joint) in the headland, eroding it into a cave
  2. Arch — erosion penetrates through a headland from both sides, forming an arch
  3. Stack — roof of the arch collapses under gravity and weathering, leaving an isolated pillar (stack)
  4. Stump — continued erosion at the base of the stack causes it to collapse to a stump, visible at low tide
Case Study: Old Harry Rocks, Dorset
A classic example of the cave-arch-stack-stump sequence on the Jurassic Coast. Old Harry is a chalk stack at the eastern end of the Isle of Purbeck. Old Harry's Wife was a former stack that has collapsed to a stump. The headland here (Ballard Point) is made of resistant chalk, while Studland Bay to the north has formed in softer sands and clays.

2.5 Depositional Landforms

Beaches

  • Sandy beaches — wide, gentle gradient (1–5°); formed by constructive waves depositing fine material; often found in sheltered bays
  • Shingle beaches — steep gradient (10–20°); coarser material deposited by high-energy waves; berms (ridges) mark high-tide lines
  • Beach profiles vary with season: summer (wide, built up by constructive waves) vs winter (narrow, eroded by destructive storms)

Spits

Spit: A narrow finger of sand or shingle extending from the coastline into the sea, formed by longshore drift depositing sediment beyond a change in coastline direction (e.g. at a river mouth). The distal end often curves due to wave refraction.
Case Study: Spurn Head, East Yorkshire
A 5.5 km long spit extending into the Humber Estuary. Formed by longshore drift carrying material southward along the Holderness Coast. The spit has a recurved end and shelters mudflats and salt marshes on its landward side. It is very narrow (as little as 50 m wide) and breached during storms, making it dynamic and vulnerable.

Bars and Tombolos

  • Bar — a spit that extends across a bay, cutting off a lagoon behind it (e.g. Slapton Ley, Devon)
  • Tombolo — a bar connecting an island to the mainland (e.g. Chesil Beach connecting the Isle of Portland to Dorset)

Sand Dunes

Formed when wind blows dry sand inland from the beach. Vegetation (marram grass) traps sand, building up dunes. A succession (psammosere) develops from embryo dunes near the shore to mature, stable dunes further inland with increasing biodiversity and soil development.

2.6 Coastal Processes and Geology

Rock Type and Coastal Landforms

Rock TypeCharacteristicsCoastal Features
ChalkHard but permeable; jointedTall white cliffs, arches, stacks (e.g. Seven Sisters)
LimestoneHard, well-jointed, solubleCliffs with caves, arches (e.g. Lulworth Cove)
Clay/shaleSoft, impermeableLow cliffs, rapid erosion, slumping
GraniteVery hard, crystallineResistant headlands, high rugged cliffs
Sand/gravelUnconsolidated, easily erodedLow crumbling cliffs, rapid retreat (e.g. Holderness)

Concordant vs Discordant Coastlines

  • Discordant — rock types are perpendicular to the coast, creating alternating headlands and bays
  • Concordant — rock types run parallel to the coast; if the outer resistant layer is breached, rapid erosion of softer rock behind creates a cove (e.g. Lulworth Cove, Dorset)

2.7 Coastal Management

Shoreline Management Plan (SMP): A large-scale plan for the sustainable management of coastal erosion and flooding, based on sediment cells. Each section of coast is assigned one of four policy options.

SMP Policy Options

PolicyDescription
Hold the lineMaintain existing defences (hard or soft engineering)
Advance the lineBuild new defences seaward of current position
Managed retreatAllow controlled erosion/flooding; relocate people and activities inland
No active interventionLet natural processes take their course; no investment in defences

Case Study: Holderness Coast, East Yorkshire
Context: Europe's fastest eroding coastline (1–2 m per year average); made of soft glacial boulder clay (till); 4 km lost since Roman times; 30+ villages lost to the sea.
Management: Bridlington and Hornsea protected with sea walls and groynes (hold the line); Mappleton protected with two rock groynes and rock armour (GBP 2 million, 1991) — but sediment starvation downstream accelerated erosion at Cowden.
Conflicts: Farmers at Skipsea lose land but are not compensated; coastal communities argue over unequal protection; SMP recommends no active intervention for most of the coast, prioritising sediment supply to Spurn Head.
Case Study: Lulworth Cove & Jurassic Coast, Dorset — Concordant Coastline
Context: UNESCO World Heritage Site; classic concordant coast with limestone (Portland and Purbeck) forming the resistant outer layer, backed by softer clays and sands, then chalk inland.
Landforms: Lulworth Cove formed where the sea breached the limestone and rapidly eroded the softer clay behind, creating a near-circular cove. Stair Hole nearby shows an earlier stage of cove formation. Durdle Door is a natural limestone arch.
Management: National Trust manages visitor access; footpath erosion is a concern; no hard engineering to preserve natural character.
Exam Tip: For 12-mark and 20-mark questions on coastal management, always evaluate using a cost-benefit framework. Consider: economic value of the area protected, environmental impacts (sediment starvation, habitat loss), sustainability (is hard engineering a long-term solution?), and social equity (who benefits and who loses?). Reference ICZM (Integrated Coastal Zone Management) as the modern holistic approach.
