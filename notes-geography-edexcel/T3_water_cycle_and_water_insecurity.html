Topic 3: The Water Cycle & Water Insecurity

3.1 The Global Water Cycle

Hydrological cycle: The continuous movement of water between the atmosphere, land surface, and subsurface. It is a closed system at the global scale (no water enters or leaves the Earth system), but an open system at the drainage basin scale.

Global Water Stores

Store% of Total WaterType
Oceans96.5%Saltwater
Ice caps & glaciers1.7%Freshwater (frozen)
Groundwater1.7%Freshwater (mostly saline deep groundwater)
Surface freshwater (lakes, rivers)0.01%Freshwater
Atmosphere0.001%Water vapour
BiosphereTraceWater in living organisms

Only ~2.5% of Earth's water is freshwater, and only ~0.3% of that is readily accessible surface freshwater. The vast majority is locked in ice or deep groundwater.

Key Processes (Fluxes)

PrecipitationWater falls from clouds as rain, snow, sleet, or hail

InterceptionPrecipitation caught by vegetation before reaching the ground
StemflowWater running down plant stems/tree trunks to the ground

3.2 The Drainage Basin

Drainage basin: The area of land drained by a river and its tributaries. It is an open system at the local scale, bounded by a watershed (ridge of high land). Water enters as precipitation and leaves via the river channel or evapotranspiration.

Water Balance Equation

P = Q + E ± ΔS

Where: P = precipitation, Q = discharge (river flow), E = evapotranspiration, ΔS = change in storage (soil moisture, groundwater, snow/ice)

Seasonal Water Balance

  • Winter (UK) — P > E; soil moisture recharge; surplus water leads to high river flow and groundwater recharge
  • Summer (UK) — E > P; soil moisture utilisation then deficit; lower river levels; groundwater levels drop
  • Autumn — soil moisture recharge begins as temperatures fall and evapotranspiration decreases

Factors Affecting the Drainage Basin Cycle

FactorEffect on Water Cycle
Rainfall intensityIntense rainfall exceeds infiltration capacity, increasing surface runoff
TemperatureHigher temperatures increase evapotranspiration; snowmelt timing affects discharge
VegetationIntercepts rainfall, slows throughflow, increases transpiration; deforestation increases runoff
Soil typeSandy soils = high infiltration; clay soils = low infiltration, more runoff
Rock typePermeable rock (chalk, sandstone) = more percolation, less runoff; impermeable rock = more runoff
SlopeSteep slopes = faster runoff, less infiltration
UrbanisationImpermeable surfaces and drains increase speed of runoff; reduce infiltration
Land useAgriculture (compaction, removal of hedgerows) can increase runoff

3.3 The Storm Hydrograph

Storm hydrograph: A graph showing how river discharge at a specific point changes over time in response to a rainfall event. It shows the speed and volume of runoff reaching the river.

Key Hydrograph Features

  • Peak discharge — the maximum river flow recorded during the storm event
  • Lag time — the delay between peak rainfall and peak discharge (shorter lag time = flashier response)
  • Rising limb — the increase in discharge as stormwater reaches the river
  • Recession (falling) limb — the decline in discharge as water drains from the basin
  • Baseflow — the normal day-to-day discharge of the river, sustained by groundwater
  • Stormflow — the additional discharge caused by the rainfall event (above baseflow)

Flashy vs Subdued Hydrographs

FeatureFlashy (short lag, high peak)Subdued (long lag, low peak)
Drainage basinSmall, circular, steepLarge, elongated, gentle slopes
Rock/soilImpermeable clay/granitePermeable chalk/sandstone
Land useUrbanised, deforestedForested, rural
RainfallIntense, short durationGentle, prolonged
Drainage densityHigh (many tributaries)Low (few tributaries)
Flood riskHigherLower
Exam Tip: When interpreting a hydrograph, always identify lag time, peak discharge, and steepness of limbs. Link each feature to physical and human causes. A short lag time suggests impermeable surfaces, steep slopes, or urbanisation. Show this understanding in your extended writing.

3.4 Climate Change and the Water Cycle

Climate change is altering the water cycle in several ways:

  • Increased evaporation — warmer temperatures increase evaporation from oceans and land surfaces
  • Changed precipitation patterns — some regions get wetter (higher latitudes), others drier (subtropics); more intense rainfall events
  • Glacier and ice cap melting — transfers water from cryosphere to oceans; initially increases river flow, then reduces it as glaciers disappear
  • Sea level rise — from thermal expansion and ice melt; saltwater intrusion into coastal aquifers
  • Permafrost thaw — releases stored water and methane; changes drainage patterns in Arctic regions
  • More extreme events — more frequent and intense droughts and floods

3.5 Water Insecurity

Water security: The reliable availability of an acceptable quantity and quality of water for health, livelihoods, ecosystems, and production, coupled with an acceptable level of water-related risk (UN definition).
Water stress: When annual water supply per person falls below 1,700 m³. Water scarcity is below 1,000 m³ per person per year.

Causes of Water Insecurity

Physical CausesHuman Causes
Climate — arid/semi-arid regions receive low, unreliable rainfallPopulation growth — more people, more demand
Seasonal variation — monsoon climates have long dry seasonsEconomic development — industrialisation and agriculture increase demand
Drought — extended periods of below-average rainfallUrbanisation — concentrates demand; water-intensive lifestyles
Limited aquifer recharge in arid areasPollution — contaminates available freshwater supplies
Climate change — altering rainfall and increasing evaporationPoor governance — lack of infrastructure, corruption, mismanagement

Consequences of Water Insecurity

  • Health — waterborne diseases (cholera, typhoid); 2 billion people lack safe drinking water globally
  • Food production — agriculture uses ~70% of global freshwater; water scarcity reduces crop yields
  • Economic development — industry requires water; drought damages economies
  • Conflict — transboundary water disputes (e.g. Nile, Jordan, Tigris-Euphrates); water as a weapon in conflicts
  • Environmental — over-abstraction causes wetland loss, river drying, ecosystem collapse (e.g. Aral Sea)
  • Gender inequality — women and girls disproportionately affected by water collection duties in LIDCs

3.6 Water Management Strategies

Supply-Side Strategies (Increasing Supply)

StrategyDescriptionAdvantagesDisadvantages
Dams & reservoirsStore water for regulated release; multipurpose (HEP, flood control, irrigation)Reliable supply; HEP generation; flood controlExpensive; displaces communities; sediment trapping; ecosystem disruption
DesalinationRemoving salt from seawater (reverse osmosis or distillation)Unlimited raw material; reliable in arid coastal areasExtremely energy-intensive; expensive; brine waste disposal; high carbon footprint
Water transfer schemesMoving water from surplus to deficit areas via pipes/canalsAddresses spatial imbalanceVery expensive; environmental impacts; political disputes
Groundwater extractionPumping water from aquifers via boreholesLarge stores available; relatively cheapOver-abstraction leads to depletion, subsidence; saltwater intrusion

Demand-Side Strategies (Reducing Demand)

  • Water metering — charging per unit of water used; reduces waste
  • Water-efficient technology — low-flow toilets and showers, drip irrigation, grey water recycling
  • Leakage reduction — fixing ageing pipe networks (UK loses ~20% of treated water to leakage)
  • Education and awareness — campaigns to reduce consumption
  • Agricultural efficiency — drip irrigation instead of flood irrigation; drought-resistant crops; mulching
  • Pricing reform — removing subsidies that encourage wasteful irrigation
Case Study: China's South-North Water Transfer Project
Context: Northern China has ~20% of China's water but 46% of its population and 64% of farmland. The project diverts water from the Yangtze River basin to the Yellow and Hai River basins.
Scale: Three routes (Eastern, Central, Western) totalling ~4,350 km of canals; Central route operational since 2014, supplying Beijing.
Benefits: Supplies 95 million people; reduces groundwater depletion; supports agriculture and industry in the north.
Concerns: USD 62+ billion cost; environmental disruption to Yangtze ecosystems; displacement of 345,000+ people; pollution in transfer canals; does not address underlying demand issues.
Case Study: The Aral Sea — Water Management Disaster
Context: Soviet-era diversion of the Amu Darya and Syr Darya rivers for cotton irrigation in Uzbekistan and Kazakhstan caused the Aral Sea to shrink by ~90% since the 1960s.
Impacts: Exposed seabed created toxic dust storms (salt and pesticides); fishing industry collapsed; local climate became more extreme; health problems from contaminated water and air; biodiversity loss.
Response: Kok-Aral Dam (World Bank funded, 2005) partially restored the North Aral Sea, increasing water levels and fish stocks. The South Aral Sea remains largely dry.

3.7 Sustainable Water Management

Integrated Water Resources Management (IWRM): A process that promotes the coordinated development and management of water, land, and related resources to maximise economic and social welfare without compromising the sustainability of ecosystems.

Approaches to Sustainable Water Management

  • Rainwater harvesting — collecting and storing rainwater from rooftops for domestic use or irrigation; appropriate technology for LIDCs
  • Wastewater recycling — treating and reusing greywater/sewage for non-potable uses (irrigation, industrial cooling); Singapore's NEWater produces 40% of demand
  • Restoration of wetlands — natural water storage and filtration; reduces flood risk
  • Community-based management — local ownership of water resources; e.g. WaterAid projects providing wells and boreholes in LIDCs
  • Virtual water trade — importing water-intensive products from water-rich countries rather than growing them domestically
Case Study: WaterAid in Ethiopia
Context: 57 million Ethiopians lacked access to clean water (2015). Rural communities relied on contaminated surface water.
Approach: WaterAid works with local partners to install protected springs, hand-dug wells, and gravity-fed systems. Communities trained to maintain systems. Hygiene education (WASH programmes) reduces waterborne disease.
Impact: Improved health outcomes; girls attend school instead of collecting water; economic productivity increases; community empowerment through local management committees.
Exam Tip: For water management questions, always evaluate solutions by considering: sustainability (does it address root causes?), scale (local vs national), cost-effectiveness, environmental impact, and equity (who benefits?). Compare top-down engineering solutions (dams, desalination) with bottom-up approaches (rainwater harvesting, community wells). The best answers integrate both supply-side and demand-side strategies.
