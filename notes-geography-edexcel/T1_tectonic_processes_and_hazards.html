Topic 1: Tectonic Processes & Hazards

1.1 The Structure of the Earth

Plate tectonics: The theory that the Earth's lithosphere is divided into large rigid plates that move relative to each other, driven by convection currents in the mantle.

The Earth has a layered internal structure:

  • Inner core — solid iron and nickel, ~5,500°C, radius ~1,220 km
  • Outer core — liquid iron and nickel, generates Earth's magnetic field, ~2,200 km thick
  • Mantle — semi-molten silicate rock (asthenosphere is the upper, plastic layer), ~2,900 km thick
  • Crust — thin outer shell; continental (30–70 km, granitic, less dense) and oceanic (6–10 km, basaltic, denser)
  • Lithosphere — rigid outer layer (crust + upper mantle), broken into tectonic plates

Evidence for Plate Tectonics

  • Continental fit — jigsaw-like fit of continents (e.g. South America and Africa)
  • Geological evidence — matching rock types, mountain chains, and fossil distributions across continents (e.g. Glossopteris fern found on multiple southern continents)
  • Palaeomagnetism — symmetrical patterns of magnetic striping on the ocean floor either side of mid-ocean ridges, showing periodic magnetic field reversals
  • Sea-floor spreading — younger rocks near ridges, older rocks further away (Harry Hess, 1960s)
  • GPS measurements — direct measurement of plate movement (typically 1–15 cm/year)

Driving Mechanisms of Plate Movement

  • Convection currents — heat from the core causes mantle material to rise, spread, cool, and sink, dragging plates along
  • Slab pull — the weight of a subducting plate pulls the rest of the plate behind it (considered the dominant mechanism)
  • Ridge push — elevated position of new crust at mid-ocean ridges causes it to slide downhill under gravity
  • Mantle plumes — localised upwellings of hot mantle material creating hotspots (e.g. Hawaii)

1.2 Plate Boundaries

Divergent (Constructive) Boundaries

Divergent boundary: Where two tectonic plates move apart, allowing magma to rise and create new crust (e.g. Mid-Atlantic Ridge).
  • Magma rises through the gap as plates separate
  • Forms mid-ocean ridges (submarine mountain chains) and rift valleys (on land, e.g. East African Rift)
  • Volcanic activity — typically effusive eruptions with basaltic lava (low viscosity)
  • Shallow-focus earthquakes only (<70 km depth)
  • Rate of spreading: slow (1 cm/yr, Mid-Atlantic Ridge) to fast (15 cm/yr, East Pacific Rise)

Convergent (Destructive) Boundaries

Three sub-types depending on plate composition:

TypeProcessFeaturesExample
Oceanic-continentalDenser oceanic plate subducts beneath continental plateDeep ocean trench, fold mountains, explosive volcanic arc, Benioff zone earthquakesNazca & South American Plates (Andes)
Oceanic-oceanicDenser/older oceanic plate subductsDeep ocean trench, island arc volcanoesPacific & Philippine Plates (Mariana Trench)
Continental-continentalNeither plate subducts; collision and compressionFold mountains, no volcanism, powerful shallow earthquakesIndian & Eurasian Plates (Himalayas)

Conservative (Transform) Boundaries

Conservative boundary: Where two plates slide laterally past each other. Crust is neither created nor destroyed, but friction generates powerful earthquakes.
  • No volcanic activity (no magma generation)
  • Shallow-focus, high-magnitude earthquakes when built-up stress is released
  • Example: San Andreas Fault (Pacific and North American Plates)

1.3 Volcanic Hazards

Volcano: An opening in the Earth's crust through which magma, volcanic ash, and gases escape to the surface.

Types of Volcano

TypeShapeEruption StyleLava TypeExample
ShieldBroad, gentle slopesEffusive (gentle)Basaltic (low viscosity)Mauna Loa, Hawaii
Composite (stratovolcano)Steep, conical, layeredExplosive & effusiveAndesitic (intermediate)Mt Pinatubo, Philippines
CalderaCollapsed craterSupervolcanic (rare, catastrophic)Rhyolitic (very viscous)Yellowstone, USA

Volcanic Hazards

  • Lava flows — rivers of molten rock; destructive but usually slow enough to evacuate from
  • Pyroclastic flows — superheated gas and rock fragments travelling at 100–700 km/h; the most deadly volcanic hazard
  • Tephra and ash falls — airborne volcanic debris; collapses roofs, disrupts aviation, damages crops
  • Lahars — volcanic mudflows (water + ash + debris); travel rapidly down valleys
  • Toxic gases — SO2, CO2, H2S can be lethal near vents
  • Volcanic tsunamis — caused by flank collapse or caldera formation displacing water
Case Study: Mt Pinatubo, Philippines (1991) — Volcanic Hazard in an AC
Context: Composite volcano on Luzon island; convergent boundary (Philippine & Eurasian Plates). VEI 6 eruption, the largest of the 20th century.
Impacts: 847 deaths (mostly from lahars); 1.2 million people displaced; 8,000 homes destroyed; 20 million tonnes of SO2 injected into stratosphere, lowering global temperatures by ~0.5°C for 2 years; agricultural land buried under ash; US Clark Air Base destroyed.
Response: PHIVOLCS monitoring and early warning led to evacuation of 58,000 people; international aid; long-term resettlement programmes.
Case Study: Montserrat (1995–present) — Volcanic Hazard in a SIDS
Context: Soufriere Hills volcano, Caribbean island; destructive boundary. Dormant for 350 years before reactivating.
Impacts: 19 deaths from pyroclastic flows (1997); capital Plymouth buried; southern 2/3 of island designated exclusion zone; population fell from 11,000 to <5,000; economy devastated.
Response: UK aid (GBP 400 million+); new capital built at Little Bay in the north; volcano observatory established; ongoing monitoring.

1.4 Seismic Hazards

Earthquake: A sudden release of energy in the Earth's crust or upper mantle that produces seismic waves. The point of origin underground is the focus (hypocentre); the point directly above on the surface is the epicentre.

Earthquake Measurement

ScaleWhat it MeasuresDetails
Moment Magnitude Scale (Mw)Energy released (magnitude)Logarithmic; each whole number = ~32x more energy. Most scientifically accurate.
Modified Mercalli Intensity (MMI)Effects/damage experiencedI–XII scale based on observed impacts. Varies with distance from epicentre.

Earthquake Hazards

  • Ground shaking — primary hazard; intensity depends on magnitude, depth, distance, and local geology
  • Liquefaction — saturated, loose sediments behave like liquid during shaking (e.g. Christchurch 2011)
  • Landslides — triggered by shaking on steep slopes
  • Tsunamis — generated by submarine earthquakes displacing the seabed
  • Secondary fires — caused by ruptured gas mains (e.g. San Francisco 1906)
Case Study: Nepal Earthquake (2015) — Seismic Hazard in an EDC/LIDC
Context: Mw 7.8, shallow focus (15 km), convergent boundary (Indian & Eurasian Plates).
Impacts: 8,900+ deaths; 22,000 injured; 600,000+ homes destroyed; historical temples in Kathmandu collapsed; landslides blocked roads; avalanche on Mt Everest killed 18; GDP loss ~50% of national budget.
Response: Slow government response due to poor infrastructure; international aid ($4.1 billion pledged); problems reaching remote villages; long-term rebuilding still ongoing.
Case Study: L'Aquila, Italy (2009) — Seismic Hazard in an AC
Context: Mw 6.3, shallow focus (8.8 km), normal faulting related to the Apennine mountain belt.
Impacts: 309 deaths; 1,500 injured; 67,000 homeless; historic medieval centre destroyed; university buildings collapsed; estimated EUR 10 billion in damage.
Response: Rapid military/civil protection deployment; temporary housing provided; controversy over scientists convicted (later acquitted) for inadequate risk communication; C.A.S.E. project built new earthquake-resistant housing.

1.5 Tsunami Hazards

Tsunami: A series of ocean waves caused by a large-scale displacement of water, typically triggered by submarine earthquakes, volcanic eruptions, or underwater landslides.

Characteristics of tsunamis:

  • Travel at 500–950 km/h in deep ocean (speed of a jet aircraft)
  • Wavelength up to 200 km in open ocean; wave height <1 m (barely noticeable)
  • As waves reach shallow coastal waters, they slow down and pile up to heights of 10–30+ metres
  • Multiple waves may arrive over several hours; the first wave is not always the largest
Case Study: Indian Ocean Tsunami (2004)
Context: Triggered by Mw 9.1 earthquake off Sumatra (convergent: Indo-Australian & Eurasian Plates). ~1,600 km of fault ruptured.
Impacts: ~230,000 deaths across 14 countries; Indonesia, Sri Lanka, India, and Thailand worst affected; 1.7 million displaced; fishing communities devastated; USD 9.9 billion in damage; coral reefs and mangroves destroyed.
Response: Massive international aid; Indian Ocean Tsunami Warning System (IOTWS) established 2006; DART buoys deployed; coastal early warning sirens installed; mangrove replanting programmes.

1.6 Hazard Management

The Hazard Management Cycle

Four phases: Mitigation → Preparedness → Response → Recovery

Prediction & Forecasting

HazardCan it be predicted?Monitoring Methods
VolcanoesReasonably well (days to weeks warning)Seismometers, gas monitoring (SO2), ground deformation (tiltmeters, GPS, InSAR), thermal imaging
EarthquakesCannot be reliably predictedSeismic gap theory, historical recurrence intervals, foreshock patterns (unreliable)
TsunamisAfter earthquake detected, yesDART buoys, tide gauges, seismometer networks, satellite monitoring

Protection & Adaptation

  • Earthquake-resistant buildings — base isolation, cross-bracing, reinforced concrete, tuned mass dampers
  • Land-use planning — hazard mapping and zoning to prevent development in high-risk areas
  • Sea walls and tsunami barriers — Japan's coastal defences (e.g. Kamaishi breakwater, 63 m deep)
  • Education and drills — Japan's annual Disaster Prevention Day; school tsunami drills
  • Early warning systems — Pacific Tsunami Warning Centre; Japan Meteorological Agency earthquake alerts (seconds of warning)

Factors Affecting Disaster Impact

Disaster Risk Equation: Risk = (Hazard × Vulnerability) / Capacity to Cope
  • Level of development — ACs have better infrastructure, emergency services, building codes, insurance
  • Governance — effective governments can enforce building codes, fund monitoring, coordinate responses
  • Population density — higher density = more people exposed
  • Urbanisation — poorly built informal settlements in LIDCs are especially vulnerable
  • Time of day — affects whether people are in buildings, asleep, or able to receive warnings
Exam Tip: When comparing tectonic disasters in ACs vs LIDCs/EDCs, always consider the Pressure and Release (PAR) model: root causes (governance, economics) → dynamic pressures (urbanisation, deforestation) → unsafe conditions (poor buildings, dangerous locations) → disaster. Higher vulnerability does not mean higher hazard magnitude.

1.7 Theoretical Frameworks

The Hazard Profile

A method of comparing hazards across multiple dimensions: magnitude, speed of onset, duration, areal extent, spatial predictability, and frequency. Useful for comparing different types of tectonic hazard.

The Park Model (Response Curve)

Shows how a community's quality of life changes before, during, and after a disaster event:

  • Pre-disaster — normal quality of life
  • Hazard event — sharp decline in quality of life
  • Relief/rescue — stabilisation at lowest point
  • Rehabilitation — gradual recovery begins
  • Reconstruction — quality of life returns to or exceeds pre-disaster levels
Exam Tip: Use the Park Model to compare disaster recovery in ACs vs LIDCs. ACs typically show a shallower dip and faster recovery (V-shaped curve), while LIDCs may show a deeper dip and slower, incomplete recovery (L-shaped curve). Always support with named examples.
