Topic 4: The Carbon Cycle & Energy Security

4.1 The Carbon Cycle as a System

Carbon cycle: The biogeochemical cycle by which carbon is exchanged between the Earth's biosphere, atmosphere, hydrosphere, pedosphere (soils), and lithosphere. It operates as a closed system at the global scale.

Carbon Fluxes

DecompositionBacteria and fungi break down dead organic matter, releasing CO2 and CH4Variable

Fast vs Slow Carbon Cycle

  • Fast (biological) carbon cycle — operates over years to decades; involves photosynthesis, respiration, decomposition, ocean-atmosphere exchange. Carbon cycles relatively quickly between the atmosphere, biosphere, soils, and surface ocean.
  • Slow (geological) carbon cycle — operates over millions of years; involves rock weathering, sedimentation, fossilisation, and volcanic degassing. Transfers carbon between the lithosphere and other stores.
Exam Tip: Human activities (fossil fuel combustion, deforestation) are transferring carbon from slow geological stores to the fast biological cycle, increasing atmospheric CO2 at a rate far exceeding natural fluxes. This is the fundamental cause of enhanced greenhouse warming.

4.2 Human Impacts on the Carbon Cycle

Fossil Fuel Combustion

Burning coal, oil, and natural gas releases ~9.5 GtC/yr into the atmosphere, transferring carbon stored over millions of years into the atmospheric store in decades. This is the largest anthropogenic contribution to rising CO2 levels.

Deforestation

  • Removes a major carbon sink (forests absorb ~2.6 GtC/yr through photosynthesis)
  • Burning or decomposing felled trees releases stored carbon
  • Exposes soil to erosion, releasing soil carbon
  • Tropical deforestation accounts for ~10% of global CO2 emissions

Agriculture

  • Rice paddies and livestock produce methane (CH4 — 28x more potent greenhouse gas than CO2 over 100 years)
  • Ploughing exposes soil organic carbon to oxidation
  • Fertiliser use releases nitrous oxide (N2O — 265x more potent than CO2)
  • Drainage of peatlands releases vast quantities of stored carbon

The Enhanced Greenhouse Effect

Enhanced greenhouse effect: The additional warming of the Earth's atmosphere caused by increased concentrations of greenhouse gases (CO2, CH4, N2O) resulting from human activities. Atmospheric CO2 has risen from ~280 ppm (pre-industrial) to ~420 ppm (2024).

Positive Feedback Loops

  • Ice-albedo feedback — warming melts ice, reducing reflectivity (albedo), so more solar energy is absorbed, causing more warming and more ice melt
  • Permafrost-methane feedback — warming thaws permafrost, releasing stored CH4 and CO2, which causes further warming
  • Water vapour feedback — warming increases evaporation, adding more water vapour (a greenhouse gas) to the atmosphere
  • Ocean warming feedback — warmer oceans absorb less CO2 (reduced solubility), leaving more in the atmosphere
  • Wildfire feedback — warmer, drier conditions increase wildfire frequency, releasing stored carbon and destroying carbon sinks

Negative Feedback Loops

  • CO2 fertilisation effect — higher atmospheric CO2 may increase plant growth and photosynthesis, absorbing more carbon (limited by nutrient and water availability)
  • Enhanced weathering — warmer, wetter conditions may accelerate chemical weathering, drawing down atmospheric CO2 (very slow)

Types of Fossil Fuel

FuelFormationCarbon IntensityKey Producers

Unconventional Fossil Fuels

  • Tar sands (oil sands) — bitumen mixed with sand/clay; energy-intensive extraction; major deposits in Alberta, Canada
  • Shale gas/oil — extracted by hydraulic fracturing (fracking); USA shale revolution transformed global energy markets
  • Deepwater drilling — extracting oil from beneath deep ocean (e.g. Deepwater Horizon disaster, Gulf of Mexico, 2010)
  • Arctic drilling — controversial extraction in fragile Arctic environments as ice melts

4.4 Energy Security

Energy security: The uninterrupted availability of energy sources at an affordable price. A country is energy secure when it can meet current and future demand reliably and at reasonable cost.

Factors Affecting Energy Security

FactorDetail
Physical availabilityNot all countries have fossil fuel reserves; renewables depend on climate/geography
Cost and affordabilityOil price volatility; fuel poverty in LIDCs
TechnologyExtraction technology (fracking); renewable energy innovation; grid infrastructure
GeopoliticsResource nationalism; OPEC control; trade embargoes; pipeline disputes (e.g. Russia-Europe gas)
Environmental concernsClimate change policies restrict fossil fuel use; renewable targets
DemandPopulation growth, industrialisation, and rising living standards increase demand
Energy mix: The combination of different energy sources used by a country. This reflects resource availability, economic development, government policy, and environmental priorities.
Case Study: Russia-Europe Gas Dependency
Context: Europe historically imported ~40% of its natural gas from Russia, mainly via pipelines through Ukraine and Belarus. Russia used energy exports as a geopolitical tool.
2022 Energy Crisis: Following Russia's invasion of Ukraine, Russia cut gas supplies to Europe. European gas prices surged 400%+. Energy bills doubled for households.
Response: EU rapidly diversified imports (LNG from USA, Qatar, Norway); accelerated renewable energy deployment; energy rationing measures; Germany reactivated coal plants short-term; Nord Stream pipelines sabotaged. This demonstrated the risks of over-reliance on a single energy supplier.

Nuclear Energy

  • Low-carbon electricity generation (no CO2 during operation)
  • Reliable baseload power (runs continuously)
  • Very high energy density (small amount of uranium produces enormous energy)
  • Concerns: radioactive waste disposal, safety risks (Chernobyl 1986, Fukushima 2011), decommissioning costs, weapon proliferation, extremely high construction costs and long build times
Case Study: Iceland — Renewable Energy Pioneer
Context: Iceland generates virtually 100% of its electricity from renewable sources: ~73% geothermal and ~27% hydroelectric. Its location on the Mid-Atlantic Ridge provides abundant geothermal energy.
Benefits: Energy independence; very low electricity costs; zero-carbon electricity; geothermal heating for 90% of homes; attracts energy-intensive industries (aluminium smelting).
Limitations: Transport still relies on imported fossil fuels; not replicable in countries without geothermal resources; small population (370,000) makes per-capita costs manageable.

4.6 Managing the Carbon Cycle

Mitigation Strategies

  • Carbon capture and storage (CCS) — capturing CO2 from power stations/industry and storing it underground in depleted oil/gas fields or saline aquifers; promising but expensive and unproven at scale
  • Afforestation and reforestation — planting trees to increase the terrestrial carbon sink; REDD+ programme pays developing countries to preserve forests
  • Peatland restoration — rewetting drained peatlands prevents oxidation of stored carbon; UK peatlands store ~3.2 billion tonnes of carbon
  • Carbon trading (emissions trading schemes) — cap-and-trade systems set a maximum emissions level and allow companies to buy/sell emission permits (e.g. EU ETS)
  • Carbon tax — a price per tonne of CO2 emitted, incentivising emission reductions
  • International agreements — Paris Agreement (2015): limit warming to 1.5°C above pre-industrial levels; Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs)
Case Study: REDD+ in Brazil
Context: REDD+ (Reducing Emissions from Deforestation and Forest Degradation) provides financial incentives to developing countries to preserve forests as carbon sinks.
Brazil's Amazon: Contains ~150–200 GtC in vegetation and soils. Deforestation rates fell ~80% between 2004–2012 under monitoring programmes and enforcement, but rose again after 2019 before declining.
Challenges: Monitoring compliance; ensuring payments reach indigenous communities; addressing drivers of deforestation (cattle ranching, soya); balancing economic development with conservation; sovereignty concerns.
Exam Tip: For carbon cycle questions, always link stores and fluxes explicitly. Show understanding of both natural and anthropogenic fluxes, and explain how human activity has disrupted the natural equilibrium. Use specific data (e.g. 280 ppm → 420 ppm CO2; ~9.5 GtC/yr from fossil fuels). For energy security essays, evaluate multiple perspectives: economic, environmental, geopolitical, and social.

TermDefinition
