B6.2 How does evolution take place?

OCR B GCSE Biology (J257) Twenty First Century Science — Topic B6: Life on Earth — past, present and future

1. Sources of variation

SourceDetail
Sexual reproductionMixing of alleles from two parents → unique combination in each offspring.
MutationRandom change in DNA base sequence; the only way new alleles arise.
EnvironmentModifies phenotype but is not inherited (e.g. tan).

2. Natural selection — step by step

  1. Variation exists in a population due to genetic differences.
  2. A selection pressure (predation, disease, food, climate) means not all offspring survive.
  3. Individuals with characteristics best suited to the environment are more likely to survive and reproduce.
  4. They pass on their alleles to the next generation.
  5. Over many generations, the frequency of advantageous alleles increases. The population evolves.
Common error: do NOT say "the giraffe stretched its neck so its offspring had longer necks" — that's Lamarck's incorrect idea. Always frame natural selection in terms of inherited variation already present in the population.

3. Worked examples

Antibiotic-resistant bacteria (MRSA). Some bacteria randomly mutate to become resistant to an antibiotic. When the antibiotic is used, susceptible bacteria die but resistant bacteria survive and reproduce. Over generations the resistant strain dominates. Plasmids can transfer resistance genes to other bacteria, accelerating spread.
Peppered moths (Industrial revolution). Pre-1850, light moths camouflaged on lichen-covered trees; rare dark mutants were eaten by birds. As soot from factories blackened tree trunks (1850–1920), dark moths became better camouflaged → light moths eaten more often → dark form dominated. After clean-air laws, lichen returned and the light form recovered.
Darwin's finches. Different islands had different food sources (seeds, insects, cactus). Over generations, finch beak shapes diverged to suit each food, eventually producing distinct species.

4. Speciation

Speciation is the formation of a new species. It happens when two populations of one species become reproductively isolated for long enough that they no longer interbreed to produce fertile offspring.

Steps

  1. A barrier (geographic, behavioural) separates two populations.
  2. Different environments cause different selection pressures on each.
  3. Over many generations, allele frequencies in each population diverge.
  4. Eventually individuals from the two populations can no longer interbreed → new species.

5. Selective breeding (artificial selection)

Humans choose parents with desired traits. Examples: high-yield wheat, woolly sheep, dog breeds. Mechanism is similar to natural selection but the "selection pressure" is human choice. Drawback: reduced genetic variation makes populations vulnerable to new diseases.

6. Genetic engineering

Faster than selective breeding; transfers specific gene(s) directly. Covered in B1.3.

7. Classification of organisms

SystemDetail
Linnaean (1750s)Kingdom → Phylum → Class → Order → Family → Genus → Species. Based on observable features.
Three-domain (Woese, 1990)Archaea, Bacteria, Eukarya — based on genetic and biochemical differences.

Evolutionary trees

Show how species are related. Closer branching = more recent common ancestor. Modern trees are based on DNA evidence and are more accurate than those based on shape alone.
