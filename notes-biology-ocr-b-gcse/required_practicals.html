Required Practical Activities (PAGs)

OCR B GCSE Biology (J257) Twenty First Century Science — Practical Activity Groups

OCR B GCSE Biology requires students to carry out a number of practical activities (PAGs). Examiners test your understanding of these in the written papers. Each practical below shows the aim, key apparatus, method, expected results and how to handle the data and errors.

When asked about a practical in the exam, always: (1) state the independent and dependent variables and at least 2 control variables; (2) describe how the dependent variable is measured; (3) explain how repeats and means improve precision; (4) suggest one source of error and how to minimise it.

PAG 1 — Microscopy of plant and animal cells

Aim: Use a light microscope to observe and draw plant and/or animal cells; calculate magnification.

Apparatus: Light microscope, slides, coverslips, onion epidermis (or cheek cells), iodine or methylene blue stain, mounted needle, ruler.

Method:

  1. Peel a thin layer of onion epidermis (or scrape cells gently from inside the cheek).
  2. Place flat on a slide with a drop of stain.
  3. Lower a coverslip with a mounted needle to avoid air bubbles.
  4. Place slide on the microscope stage; secure with clips.
  5. Focus at the lowest power first; use coarse focus to bring stage close to lens, then move stage away while looking through eyepiece.
  6. Switch to higher magnification; use only the fine focus.
  7. Draw cells using sharp pencil; include a scale bar and total magnification.

Calculations: Total magnification = eyepiece × objective. Magnification = image size ÷ real size (use the same units throughout).

Hazards: sharp coverslip and slide edges; iodine stains skin and clothes — wear goggles and wash hands.

PAG 2 — Effect of antiseptics or antibiotics on bacterial growth

Aim: Investigate the effect of an antiseptic or antibiotic on bacterial growth using a clear zone of inhibition.

Apparatus: Sterile agar plates, broth culture of safe bacterium (e.g. Bacillus subtilis or Micrococcus luteus), sterile cotton swabs, paper discs, antiseptic/antibiotic solutions, control disc soaked in distilled water, forceps, Bunsen burner, ruler.

Method:

  1. Use aseptic technique throughout: pass forceps and inoculating loop through a Bunsen flame; lift agar lid only briefly.
  2. Spread bacterial culture evenly over the agar with a sterile swab.
  3. Place paper discs (different antiseptics + control) onto the agar with sterile forceps.
  4. Tape the lid (do not seal completely — risk of anaerobic growth).
  5. Incubate at 25 °C for 48 hours (UK schools should not exceed 25 °C to avoid growing pathogens).
  6. Measure the diameter of the clear "zone of inhibition" around each disc with a ruler.

Calculations: Zone area = π × (radius)² ; larger area = more effective inhibition.

Hazards: microbiological hazard — work aseptically; dispose of plates by autoclaving or heating to 121 °C.

PAG 4 — Effect of pH (or temperature) on enzyme activity

Aim: Investigate how pH affects the activity of amylase using starch and iodine.

Apparatus: Amylase solution, 1% starch solution, buffers pH 3–9, iodine solution in spotting tile, water bath at 30 °C, syringes, stopwatch.

Variables: IV — pH; DV — time for starch to be fully digested (no blue-black with iodine); CVs — temperature, enzyme concentration, starch concentration, volume.

Method:

  1. Add 2 cm³ amylase + 1 cm³ buffer + 2 cm³ starch into a boiling tube; mix.
  2. Place in 30 °C water bath; start stopwatch.
  3. Every 30 s remove a drop and place on iodine in a spotting tile.
  4. Stop the clock when iodine no longer turns blue-black.
  5. Repeat for each pH; calculate mean.

Calculations: Rate = 1 ÷ time (s⁻¹). Plot rate vs pH; expect a peak at the optimum pH (≈ 7 for amylase).

Hazards: Iodine is irritant — wash off skin; buffers may be acidic/alkaline — handle with care.

PAG 5 — Photosynthesis: rate vs light intensity

Aim: Investigate the effect of light intensity on the rate of photosynthesis using pondweed (Cabomba or Elodea).

Apparatus: Pondweed, sodium hydrogen carbonate solution (CO₂ source), boiling tube, lamp, ruler, stopwatch.

Variables: IV — light intensity (1/d² where d = distance from lamp); DV — bubbles per minute (or volume of O₂ collected); CVs — temperature (use beaker of water as heat sink), CO₂ concentration, species/length of pondweed.

Method:

  1. Cut a 5 cm piece of pondweed; place in tube with NaHCO₃ solution.
  2. Set lamp at distance d; allow 2 min to acclimatise.
  3. Count bubbles released in 1 min; repeat 3 times and take mean.
  4. Repeat at five different distances.

Calculations: Light intensity ∝ 1/d² (inverse-square law). Plot mean bubbles min⁻¹ vs 1/d².

Hazards: hot lamp — do not touch; risk of electric shock if water spilled near plug.

PAG 6 — Osmosis in plant tissue

Aim: Investigate the effect of sucrose concentration on the mass of potato tissue (osmosis).

Apparatus: Cork borer, potato, scalpel, balance, sucrose solutions 0.0–1.0 mol dm⁻³, boiling tubes, paper towels.

Method:

  1. Cut potato cylinders of equal length; blot dry; record initial mass.
  2. Place one cylinder in each sucrose concentration.
  3. Leave for 30 min, then remove, blot, and record final mass.
  4. Calculate % change in mass for each concentration.

Calculations: % change = (final − initial) / initial × 100. Plot % change vs concentration; the line crosses 0 % at the concentration equal to the cell sap (isotonic point).

Hazards: sharp scalpel; clean up spillages.

PAG 7 — Investigating reaction times

Aim: Measure reaction time using the dropped-ruler test and investigate the effect of caffeine (or a distraction).

Apparatus: 30 cm ruler, partner, calculator, optional caffeine drink (with informed consent).

Method:

  1. Subject sits with hand at the table edge, thumb and index finger 5 cm apart.
  2. Tester holds the ruler vertically with 0 cm at the bottom of subject's fingers.
  3. Tester drops the ruler without warning; subject catches it as quickly as possible.
  4. Record the distance fallen (cm) above the thumb.
  5. Repeat 5 times; calculate mean.
  6. Use a conversion table or t = √(2d/g) to convert distance into time.

Calculations: Reaction time t = √(2d/g) where g = 9.81 m s⁻². Convert d to metres.

Hazards: Caffeine — ensure informed consent and no relevant medical issues.

PAG 8 — Field investigation of distribution: quadrats and transects

Aim: Investigate how the distribution of an organism (e.g. daisies) varies along an environmental gradient (e.g. light intensity from shaded to open ground).

Apparatus: Tape measure, quadrats (0.25 m²), light meter, soil moisture probe, identification key.

Method:

  1. Lay a tape from shaded area into open ground.
  2. At each marked interval (e.g. every 2 m) place the quadrat; record number of daisies and abiotic factors.
  3. Repeat for several lines (transects) to improve reliability.

Calculations: Mean count per quadrat × area of habitat ÷ area of quadrat = total estimated population. Plot abundance vs distance / abiotic factor.

Hazards: Slips/trips; sun exposure — wear suitable clothing.

PAG 9 — Investigating the rate of decay (decomposition)

Aim: Investigate factors affecting the decay of milk or food using pH change as a proxy for bacterial activity.

Apparatus: Milk, lipase solution, sodium carbonate (alkali), phenolphthalein indicator, water baths.

Variables: IV — temperature; DV — time taken for indicator to lose its pink colour (acid produced as fats are broken down to fatty acids); CVs — milk volume, lipase concentration, indicator volume.

Method:

  1. Mix milk + sodium carbonate + phenolphthalein in a tube — solution turns pink (alkali).
  2. Add lipase; start stopwatch.
  3. Stop the clock when pink colour disappears (pH falls below 8.3).
  4. Repeat at 10 °C, 20 °C, 30 °C, 40 °C, 50 °C.

Calculations: Rate = 1 / time. Plot rate vs temperature; expect a peak at the lipase's optimum (≈ 35 °C) and denaturation above.

PAG 10 — Aerobic respiration (effect of temperature on yeast)

Aim: Investigate how temperature affects the rate of respiration in yeast.

Apparatus: Yeast suspension, glucose solution, methylene blue indicator (or limewater for CO₂), water baths.

Variables: IV — temperature; DV — time for methylene blue to decolourise (yeast removes oxygen) OR rate of CO₂ production; CVs — yeast and glucose concentration.

Method:

  1. Mix yeast + glucose in a tube; add a few drops of methylene blue.
  2. Place in a water bath at the set temperature; do not shake.
  3. Time how long for the indicator to lose its blue colour.
  4. Repeat at 5 temperatures (10–55 °C).

Calculations: Rate = 1/time. Plot rate vs temperature; expect a peak around 35–40 °C.

Practical skills you must master

SkillTip
VariablesAlways state IV, DV and at least 2 control variables.
Repeats and meansRepeats expose anomalies; means smooth random error.
Reliability vs accuracyReliable = same result if repeated; accurate = close to true value.
AnomaliesIdentify, ignore in mean calculation, suggest cause.
ErrorsRandom (variation in counts) vs systematic (mis-zeroed balance) — give an example.
GraphsIV on x-axis, DV on y-axis. Use sensible scales; line/curve of best fit; label axes with units.
ConclusionState trend, support with figures from data, refer back to the hypothesis.
When asked "how would you improve this experiment?" — give a SPECIFIC suggestion (e.g. "use a colorimeter instead of judging colour by eye to remove subjective error"), not generic ones like "use better equipment".
