B2.1 What are the causes of disease?

OCR B GCSE Biology (J257) Twenty First Century Science — Topic B2: Keeping healthy

1. Health and disease

The WHO defines health as a state of complete physical, mental and social well-being — not merely the absence of disease. A disease is any condition that disrupts the normal functioning of the body or mind.

2. Two types of disease

TypeCauseExamples
Communicable (infectious)Caused by a pathogen and can be spread from one person to another.Tuberculosis (TB), measles, influenza, cholera, malaria, HIV/AIDS, athlete's foot.
Non-communicableCannot be spread; caused by lifestyle, genetics, or environment.Cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, lung cancer, obesity-related disease.

3. Pathogens

A pathogen is a microorganism that causes infectious disease. There are four main groups: bacteria, viruses, fungi and protists.
Pathogen groupDescriptionExample diseaseTreatment
BacteriaSingle-celled prokaryotes; multiply rapidly; some release toxins.TB, salmonella food poisoning, gonorrhoeaAntibiotics
VirusesNot cells; reproduce only inside host cells, bursting them open.Influenza, HIV, measles, COVID-19, tobacco mosaic virusAntiviral drugs (limited); vaccines for prevention
FungiEukaryotic; many produce spores; some grow as hyphae.Athlete's foot, rose black spotAntifungals
ProtistsSingle-celled eukaryotes; often need a vector.Malaria (Plasmodium, spread by Anopheles mosquito)Antimalarials

4. How pathogens are spread

RouteExamples
Air (droplets)Influenza, TB, common cold — coughs and sneezes.
Direct contactAthlete's foot, HIV (via body fluids), some STIs.
Water / foodCholera (contaminated water), salmonella (undercooked food).
VectorsMosquitoes (malaria), ticks (Lyme disease).
Contaminated surfacesNorovirus on door handles; many bacteria.

5. Non-communicable causes

  • Lifestyle — smoking (lung cancer, COPD), excessive alcohol (liver disease), poor diet (CVD, type 2 diabetes), lack of exercise.
  • Genetic — inherited alleles (e.g. familial hypercholesterolaemia, sickle-cell anaemia).
  • Environmental — UV exposure (skin cancer), air pollution (asthma, lung cancer), occupational hazards (asbestos and mesothelioma).

6. Interactions between diseases

Diseases often interact: a virus can damage the immune system (HIV → AIDS), allowing other infections to take hold. Long-term physical illness can also cause mental ill-health (depression, anxiety). Severe stress weakens immunity to communicable disease.

When asked "explain how a pathogen makes you ill", give two routes: (1) damage to cells/tissues directly (e.g. viruses bursting cells); (2) producing toxins (e.g. cholera toxin in gut).

7. Summary checklist

  • Define communicable and non-communicable disease and give examples.
  • Name the four pathogen groups and a disease caused by each.
  • Describe four routes of pathogen transmission.
  • Explain how lifestyle, genetics and environment cause non-communicable disease.
  • Explain how diseases can interact (e.g. HIV → other infections).
