B1.2 How is genetic information inherited?

OCR B GCSE Biology (J257) Twenty First Century Science — Topic B1: You and your genes

1. Sexual reproduction, gametes and chromosomes

In sexual reproduction, two specialised sex cells — gametes — fuse to form a zygote. Gametes are haploid (one set of chromosomes); body cells are diploid (two sets). Fertilisation joining two haploid gametes restores the diploid number, ensuring that the chromosome number stays constant from one generation to the next.

2. Alleles, genotype and phenotype

TermMeaning
AlleleA version of a gene (e.g. B = brown, b = blue).
HomozygousHas two identical alleles for a gene (BB or bb).
HeterozygousHas two different alleles (Bb).
GenotypeThe combination of alleles for a gene.
PhenotypeThe observable characteristic produced by the genotype (and the environment).
Dominant allele (capital letter)One copy is enough to produce the phenotype (B in Bb still gives brown).
Recessive allele (lower-case letter)Phenotype only appears when both alleles are recessive (bb).

3. Predicting offspring — Punnett squares

Punnett squares show every possible combination of gametes. Write the gametes from one parent along the top, the gametes from the other parent down the side, and fill the boxes with the resulting offspring genotypes.

Worked example: Bb × Bb (heterozygous parents)

B (½)b (½)
B (½)BBBb
b (½)Bbbb
  • Genotype ratio = 1 BB : 2 Bb : 1 bb
  • Phenotype ratio = 3 dominant (B_) : 1 recessive (bb)
  • Probability that a single child is bb = 1/4 (25%).
A 3:1 ratio in F2 is a strong clue for a single dominant/recessive gene. A 1:1 ratio means the unknown parent must be heterozygous (test cross). Always show the Punnett square — examiners give marks for clear gametes and offspring boxes.

4. Sex inheritance

Of the 23 pairs of human chromosomes, the 23rd pair are the sex chromosomes. Females are XX, males are XY.

  • All eggs carry an X chromosome.
  • Half the sperm carry an X (→ daughter) and half carry a Y (→ son).
  • So the father's sperm determines the sex of the child.

5. Variation

TypeDescriptionExample
ContinuousA range of values forming a normal (bell-shaped) distribution. Usually polygenic and influenced by environment.Height, mass, leaf length
DiscontinuousDistinct categories with no intermediates. Usually controlled by one or a few genes.Blood group, ability to roll the tongue, eye colour

Variation can be caused by genetic factors (different alleles), environmental factors (e.g. UV exposure, diet, language) or both. Even identical twins, who share the same genome, often show different phenotypes when raised in different environments.

6. Inherited disorders

An inherited disorder is a disease caused by a faulty allele or chromosomal abnormality. Some are recessive (Cystic fibrosis, Sickle-cell anaemia) — both alleles must be the faulty form. Some are dominant (Huntington's disease) — only one faulty allele is needed. Some are X-linked (Haemophilia, red-green colour blindness) and affect mostly males.

DisorderInheritanceEffect
Cystic fibrosisAutosomal recessive (cc)Thick, sticky mucus in lungs and digestive system; recurrent infections.

7. Genetic testing and embryo screening

MethodWhat it does
Carrier testingAdult blood test to find out if a person carries an allele for a recessive disorder.
Pre-natal testing (CVS, amniocentesis)Test cells from the developing fetus — small risk of miscarriage.
Pre-implantation genetic diagnosis (PGD)IVF embryos screened before implantation; only unaffected embryos used.
Newborn screening (heel-prick)Detects disorders such as PKU and CF early so treatment can start.
When asked about embryo screening, balance benefits (avoiding suffering, informed choice) with concerns (selective abortion of disabled fetuses, "designer baby" worries, cost and equity, possible discrimination by insurers). Higher-mark answers always discuss BOTH sides.
