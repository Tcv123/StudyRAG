B2.2 How do organisms protect themselves against pathogens?

OCR B GCSE Biology (J257) Twenty First Century Science — Topic B2: Keeping healthy

1. Non-specific human defences

BarrierHow it works
SkinTough physical barrier; produces antimicrobial sebum.
Mucus + cilia (airways)Mucus traps pathogens; cilia waft them up to the throat to be swallowed.
Stomach acidHydrochloric acid (pH ~2) kills most pathogens in food and mucus.
Tears, salivaContain lysozyme enzyme that destroys bacterial cell walls.
Blood clottingPlatelets form a scab to seal cuts and prevent entry.

2. The immune system — specific defence

If pathogens get past the barriers, white blood cells (lymphocytes and phagocytes) fight them. The immune response is specific to the pathogen because each pathogen has unique antigens (chemical markers) on its surface.
White blood cellAction
PhagocytesEngulf and digest pathogens (phagocytosis). Non-specific.
Lymphocytes (B-cells)Produce antibodies with shapes complementary to specific antigens. Antibodies clump pathogens together (agglutination) so phagocytes can engulf them.
Lymphocytes (T-cells / memory cells)Some destroy infected body cells; memory cells remain after the infection so the response is faster on re-exposure.

Antibody specificity

An antibody has a region that is complementary in shape to one specific antigen — like a lock and key. Once it has been produced, the body remembers how to make more of it. This is why we usually only get diseases like measles or chickenpox once.

3. Vaccination

A vaccine contains a small amount of dead, weakened or harmless parts of a pathogen. When injected, white blood cells respond, producing antibodies and memory cells. If the real pathogen is later met, the response is faster and stronger so symptoms are reduced or prevented.

In a "describe how a vaccine works" question always mention: (1) introduces a small amount of inactive pathogen; (2) lymphocytes produce antibodies; (3) memory cells remain; (4) on re-exposure, antibodies produced rapidly so person doesn't get ill.

Herd immunity

If enough people in a population are vaccinated, the pathogen has too few susceptible hosts to spread, so even unvaccinated people are protected. This is called herd immunity. It is especially important for those who cannot be vaccinated (very young, immunocompromised).

4. Plant defences

DefenceHow it works
Cellulose cell wallsPhysical barrier preventing pathogens entering cells.
Tough waxy cuticle on leavesStops pathogens reaching cells underneath.

Antibacterial chemicalsSome plants produce mint, witch hazel etc.

MimicrySome plants mimic poisonous species to deter feeding.
