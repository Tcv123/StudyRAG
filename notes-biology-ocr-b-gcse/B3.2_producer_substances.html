B3.2 How do producers get the substances they need?

OCR B GCSE Biology (J257) Twenty First Century Science — Topic B3: Living together — food and ecosystems

1. Plants as producers

A producer makes its own food by photosynthesis. Plants need: water, carbon dioxide, light, and several mineral ions. Without these, growth slows or stops and characteristic deficiency symptoms appear.

2. Mineral ions

IonUse in plantDeficiency symptom
Nitrate (NO₃⁻)Used to make amino acids → proteins.Stunted growth; older leaves yellow (chlorosis).
Phosphate (PO₄³⁻)Used in DNA and cell membranes.Poor root growth; discoloured young leaves.
Potassium (K⁺)Helps enzymes in photosynthesis & respiration.Yellow leaves with dead spots.
Magnesium (Mg²⁺)Component of chlorophyll molecule.Yellow leaves between green veins.

3. Root hair cells

Specialised epidermal cells in the root absorb water and mineral ions.

AdaptationFunction
Long projectionIncreases surface area for absorption.
Thin cell wallShort diffusion path for water.
Many mitochondriaProvide ATP for active transport of mineral ions.
Permanent vacuole with cell sapMaintains water-potential gradient for osmosis.

4. How substances move into plants

SubstanceMethod
WaterOsmosis — soil water has higher water potential than cell sap, so water moves in down a gradient through a partially permeable membrane.
Mineral ionsActive transport — ions are at low concentration in soil; ATP from respiration moves them into the cell against the gradient.
CO₂ at leafDiffusion through stomata down a concentration gradient.

6. Transpiration

Water evaporates from leaf surfaces (mainly through stomata). This creates the transpiration stream — a continuous flow of water from roots, up the xylem, and out of the leaves.

Factors affecting rate

FactorEffect
Light intensity ↑Stomata open more → rate ↑
Temperature ↑Faster evaporation; faster diffusion → rate ↑
Wind speed ↑Removes water vapour from leaf surface → steeper gradient → rate ↑
Humidity ↑Less of a gradient → rate ↓

Measuring transpiration: the potometer

A potometer measures water uptake by tracking an air bubble moving along a capillary. Distance moved per unit time is proportional to water uptake (a proxy for transpiration).

In transpiration calculations: rate = distance ÷ time. Always include units (mm s⁻¹). When changing one factor, keep all others constant.
