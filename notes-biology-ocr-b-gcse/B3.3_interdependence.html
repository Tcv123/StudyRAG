B3.3 How are organisms in an ecosystem interdependent?

2. Food chains and food webs

A food chain shows how energy passes between organisms; arrows point from food to feeder.

A food web connects multiple food chains. Many organisms have multiple feeding relationships.

Trophic levels

  • L1 — Producers (plants/algae make food via photosynthesis).
  • L2 — Primary consumers (herbivores).
  • L3 — Secondary consumers (carnivores eating herbivores).
  • L4 — Tertiary / apex consumers.
  • Decomposers (bacteria, fungi) return nutrients to the soil.

3. Energy flow and biomass

Only ~10% of energy is transferred between trophic levels. The rest is lost as heat (respiration), in waste, or as inedible parts. This is why food chains rarely have more than 4–5 levels.

PyramidWhat it shows
Pyramid of numbersNumber of organisms at each level — can be inverted (e.g. one tree feeding many caterpillars).
Pyramid of biomassMass of living material at each level — usually a strict pyramid.
Pyramid of energyEnergy flow per unit time — always strict pyramid.

4. Decomposition and nutrient cycles

Decomposers (bacteria and fungi) break down dead organisms and waste, releasing minerals back into the soil/atmosphere. Decay rate depends on temperature, moisture, oxygen and microbe numbers.

Carbon cycle

  • CO₂ removed from atmosphere by photosynthesis.
  • Returned by respiration of plants and animals.
  • Returned by combustion of fossil fuels.
  • Returned by decomposers respiring on dead material.

Water cycle

  • Evaporation from oceans/lakes; transpiration from plants.
  • Condenses to form clouds → precipitation (rain/snow).
  • Runs off into rivers / soaks into the ground → returns to oceans.

Nitrogen cycle (key bacteria)

BacteriaRole
Nitrogen-fixing (in root nodules of legumes)N₂ → ammonia/nitrate.
DecomposersProteins/urea → ammonia.
NitrifyingAmmonia → nitrites → nitrates (used by plants).
DenitrifyingNitrates → N₂ (lost back to atmosphere).
"Why are decomposers important?" — they recycle minerals (especially nitrogen, phosphorus) so plants can re-absorb them; without them, dead material would build up and nutrients would be locked away.
