B2.5 How can lifestyle, genes and the environment affect health?

OCR B GCSE Biology (J257) Twenty First Century Science — Topic B2: Keeping healthy

1. Risk factors

A risk factor is a feature of a person's lifestyle, environment or genome that increases their probability of developing a particular disease.
Risk factorDiseases linked to it
SmokingLung cancer, COPD, cardiovascular disease, low-birth-weight babies.
Excessive alcoholCirrhosis, liver cancer, brain damage, fetal alcohol syndrome.
Poor diet (high fat / sugar / salt)Obesity, type 2 diabetes, CVD, hypertension.
Lack of exerciseObesity, CVD, type 2 diabetes, depression.
Obesity (BMI > 30)Type 2 diabetes, CVD, osteoarthritis, some cancers.
UV radiation / sunlightSkin cancer (melanoma).
Carcinogens (e.g. asbestos, tobacco tar)Cancers (mesothelioma, lung).
Genetic factorsBRCA1/2 → breast & ovarian cancer; familial hypercholesterolaemia → CVD.

2. Cardiovascular disease (CVD)

The leading cause of death in the UK. Includes coronary heart disease (blocked coronary arteries), heart attack, stroke and heart failure. Caused mainly by atherosclerosis — fatty plaque builds up in arteries, narrows them, restricts blood flow.

CVD risk factorWhy it increases risk
High saturated fat / cholesterol intakeMore LDL cholesterol → fatty plaque formation in arteries.
High salt intakeRaises blood pressure → damages artery walls.
SmokingNicotine raises BP; CO reduces oxygen carrying.
InactivityWeight gain, insulin resistance, raised cholesterol.
Genetics (family history)Inherited high cholesterol or BP.

3. Cancer

Cancer is a group of diseases caused by uncontrolled cell division producing a tumour. Benign tumours stay in one place; malignant tumours invade neighbouring tissue and can spread (metastasis) via blood or lymph.
CancerLifestyle/environmental factor
Lung cancerSmoking, radon, air pollution.
Skin cancer (melanoma)UV exposure, fair skin (genetic).
Liver cancerAlcohol, hepatitis B/C virus, aflatoxins.
Breast cancerGenetics (BRCA1/2), age, hormonal factors.
Bowel cancerLow fibre, processed meat, lack of exercise.

4. Mental health

Mental health is part of overall health. Depression, anxiety and schizophrenia are influenced by genes, life events, brain chemistry and lifestyle (sleep, exercise, social contact). Long-term physical illness raises risk; conversely, depression worsens recovery from physical disease.

5. Sampling and correlation

Population health data is gathered by surveys and registries. A positive correlation between two factors (e.g. smoking and lung cancer) does not by itself prove that one causes the other, but a strong, dose-dependent and biologically plausible link is good evidence.

Use the BradFord-Hill style "evidence" terms when evaluating data: dose–response, consistency across studies, biological plausibility, temporality (cause precedes effect).
