B5.3 How do hormones control responses in the human body?

OCR B GCSE Biology (J257) Twenty First Century Science — Topic B5: The human body — staying alive

1. The endocrine system

The endocrine system is a network of glands that release hormones directly into the blood. Hormones are chemical messengers that travel to target organs to bring about a response.

3. Blood glucose control

Two pancreatic hormones regulate glucose:

  • Insulin — released when blood glucose is too high; causes liver and muscle cells to take in glucose and store it as glycogen.
  • Glucagon — released when blood glucose is too low; causes the liver to break glycogen back into glucose, releasing it to the blood.

Diabetes

Don't confuse glucose (sugar in blood), glycogen (storage form in liver/muscle) and glucagon (hormone). Carefully spell each.

6. Treating infertility

  • Fertility drugs — FSH and LH stimulate egg production.
  • IVF (In-vitro fertilisation) — eggs collected, fertilised in lab, embryos transferred to uterus.
  • Pros: helps couples have biological children. Cons: low success rate per cycle (~25-30%); expensive; emotionally and physically demanding; multiple births more risky.

7. Adrenaline and thyroxine

  • Adrenaline — released in stressful or exciting situations; increases heart and breathing rates and prepares body for action.
  • Thyroxine — sets the basal metabolic rate. Its level is controlled by negative feedback: too much thyroxine inhibits TSH from the pituitary so thyroxine production drops.

8. Plant hormones (briefly)

Plants use hormones too: auxin controls phototropism (growth toward light) and gravitropism. Synthetic hormones are used as weed killers (selectively kill broad-leaf weeds), rooting powders and to ripen fruit (ethene).
