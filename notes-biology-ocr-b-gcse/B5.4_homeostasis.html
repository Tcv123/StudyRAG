B5.4 Why do we need to maintain a constant internal environment?

OCR B GCSE Biology (J257) Twenty First Century Science — Topic B5: The human body — staying alive

1. Homeostasis

Homeostasis is the regulation of internal conditions (in cells and the body) to maintain optimal conditions for cell function and enzyme activity. Conditions controlled include body temperature, blood glucose and water balance.

Negative feedback loop

  1. A change in the internal environment is detected by receptors.
  2. Information passes via the nervous and/or endocrine system to a coordination centre (often brain or pancreas).
  3. The coordination centre triggers effectors that produce a response opposing the change.
  4. Conditions return towards the set point — and the response stops.

2. Temperature regulation (thermoregulation)

Body core temperature is held at ~37 °C — optimum for enzymes. The hypothalamus monitors blood temperature and skin receptors detect external temperature.

3. Water and salt balance — kidneys

The kidneys filter the blood and adjust water/salt balance. Substances excreted: urea (from breakdown of excess amino acids in the liver), excess water and ions.

How a kidney nephron works (overview)

  1. Ultrafiltration — blood at high pressure forces small molecules (water, ions, glucose, urea) into the nephron.
  2. Selective reabsorption — useful substances (all glucose, some water and ions) are reabsorbed into the blood by active transport / osmosis.
  3. The remaining liquid is urine: water + ions + urea. Stored in the bladder.

ADH and water balance

  • If blood is too concentrated (too little water), the pituitary releases more ADH.
  • ADH makes the collecting duct more permeable → more water reabsorbed → small volume of concentrated urine.
  • If blood has too much water, less ADH is released → less water reabsorbed → large volume of dilute urine.
