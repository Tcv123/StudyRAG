B1.3 How can and should gene technology be used?

OCR B GCSE Biology (J257) Twenty First Century Science — Topic B1: You and your genes

1. Selective breeding

Selective breeding (artificial selection) is the process by which humans choose parents with desirable traits and breed them together so that the offspring inherit those traits. Repeated over many generations, it has produced our modern crops, livestock and pets.
Examples of selective breedingTrait selected for
Dairy cowsHigh milk yield
WheatLarge grain size and disease resistance
SheepLong fine wool / lots of meat
DogsBehavioural traits (e.g. obedient sheepdogs) and appearance

Process: (1) identify individuals with the desired trait, (2) breed them together, (3) from the offspring choose those that show the trait most strongly, (4) continue across many generations until the trait is "fixed" in the line.

A common drawback question: explain inbreeding. Repeated breeding of close relatives reduces genetic variation; harmful recessive alleles come together more often, increasing genetic disorders and making the population vulnerable to new diseases.

2. Genetic engineering

Genetic engineering is deliberately modifying the DNA of an organism by adding, removing or editing one or more genes. Organisms whose genes have been changed in this way are called genetically modified organisms (GMOs).

Tools used in genetic engineering

ToolFunction
Restriction enzymesCut DNA at specific recognition sequences, producing single-stranded "sticky ends".
DNA ligaseJoins two fragments of DNA together by sealing the sugar–phosphate backbone.
Vector (e.g. plasmid or virus)Carries the inserted gene into the host cell.
Marker genee.g. antibiotic resistance — used to confirm successful insertion.

Producing human insulin in bacteria

  1. Restriction enzymes cut the human DNA at the start and end of the insulin gene, leaving sticky ends.
  2. The same restriction enzyme cuts open a bacterial plasmid, leaving complementary sticky ends.
  3. The sticky ends pair up by complementary base pairing; DNA ligase seals the sugar–phosphate backbone, producing a recombinant plasmid.
  4. The recombinant plasmid is taken up by a bacterium (transformation).
  5. The bacterium is grown in a fermenter under controlled conditions; it transcribes the gene and produces large quantities of human insulin which is then purified.

In 1996 Bt cotton was introduced in the USA. Pesticide use fell sharply; yields rose. By 2010 some pest populations had evolved resistance, prompting "refuge" planting strategies and gene-stacking (multiple resistance genes) to slow further resistance evolution.

5. Stem cells

Stem cells are unspecialised cells able to divide and differentiate into different cell types. Embryonic stem cells can become any cell type. Adult stem cells (e.g. in bone marrow) can become a limited range of cell types. Induced pluripotent stem (iPS) cells are adult cells reprogrammed into a stem-like state.
UseExample
Replace damaged tissueBone-marrow transplant for leukaemia; trials in spinal-cord injury, type 1 diabetes.
Test new drugsDrug screened on heart cells made from iPS cells.
Study diseaseMake iPS cells from a patient with motor-neurone disease and watch how it develops.

Ethical issues

  • Embryonic stem cells involve using human embryos — religious and moral objections to destroying potential human life.
  • iPS cells are an alternative because they are made from adult cells, but technology is still being refined.
  • Donor stem cells may be rejected by the immune system; iPS cells from the patient avoid this.

6. Gene therapy

Gene therapy aims to treat genetic disorders by inserting a working copy of the affected gene into a patient's cells using a vector (often a modified virus).
  • Currently most effective for single-gene recessive disorders (e.g. SCID — "bubble baby" disease).
  • Long-term success limited because targeted cells are eventually replaced by uncorrected ones.
  • Risks include immune reactions to the vector and accidental disruption of other genes.
  • CRISPR-Cas9 is a newer, more precise tool for editing specific DNA sequences directly. Trials underway for sickle-cell anaemia and beta-thalassaemia.

Germline vs somatic editing

TypeWhat changesInherited?Ethics
SomaticBody cells of one patientNoGenerally accepted for treating disease

7. DNA fingerprinting

Different people have different non-coding regions of DNA with variable repeats. Cutting DNA into fragments and separating them by gel electrophoresis produces a unique banding pattern called a DNA profile. Profiles are used to:

  • Identify suspects from DNA left at a crime scene.
  • Confirm paternity / family relationships.
  • Identify victims of mass disasters.
  • Identify endangered species or trace traded animal/plant material.
When asked about DNA fingerprinting in court, mention both the very small chance of a coincidental match (less than 1 in a billion) AND the limitations: contamination, sample degradation, identical twins, and the fact that DNA at a scene doesn't prove how it got there.

8. Should we do all this? Ethical considerations

Gene technology raises important questions. Use a balanced framework when answering:

StakeholderLikely concerns
Patients & familiesAccess to treatment; informed consent; psychological impact of test results.
SocietyEquity (only the wealthy have access?); risk of "designer babies"; disability rights.
Religious groupsSanctity of life; status of the embryo; "playing God".
Insurers / employersGenetic discrimination if test results are shared.
EnvironmentGene flow from GM crops; biodiversity; ecological balance.
Scientific communityLong-term safety; off-target edits; international regulation.
