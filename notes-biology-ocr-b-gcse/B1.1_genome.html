B1.1 What is the genome and what does it do?

OCR B GCSE Biology (J257) Twenty First Century Science — Topic B1: You and your genes

1. DNA, genes and chromosomes

DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid) is the molecule that stores the genetic information of all living organisms. A short section of DNA that codes for a particular protein is called a gene. The complete set of DNA in an organism is its genome.

In a eukaryotic cell DNA is packaged with proteins (histones) into chromosomes, which are stored inside the nucleus. A normal human body cell has 46 chromosomes (23 pairs); one of each pair is inherited from each parent.

TermDefinition
DNAA double-stranded polymer made of nucleotides; stores genetic information.
NucleotideThe repeating unit of DNA: a phosphate group, a deoxyribose sugar and a nitrogenous base.
GeneA section of DNA that codes for a specific sequence of amino acids in a protein.
AlleleA different version of the same gene (e.g. blue-eye allele vs brown-eye allele).
ChromosomeA long DNA molecule wrapped around proteins; humans have 23 pairs in body cells.
GenomeThe entire set of genetic material of an organism, including all genes and non-coding DNA.

2. The structure of DNA

DNA is a polymer of nucleotides, arranged in a double helix first described by Watson and Crick in 1953 using X-ray diffraction images from Rosalind Franklin and Maurice Wilkins, plus Chargaff's base ratios.

  • DNA has two strands that twist around each other (a double helix).
  • Each strand has a sugar–phosphate backbone with bases attached.
  • The four bases are Adenine (A), Thymine (T), Cytosine (C) and Guanine (G).
  • Bases pair across the two strands by complementary base pairing: A pairs with T and C pairs with G, held by hydrogen bonds.
  • The two strands are antiparallel — they run in opposite directions.
Common exam ask: "Describe the structure of DNA". A 4-mark answer needs: (1) two strands forming a double helix, (2) sugar–phosphate backbone, (3) bases attached and pairing A–T / C–G, (4) hydrogen bonds hold the strands together.

3. From gene to protein

The order of bases in a gene determines the order of amino acids in the protein it codes for. Three bases (a triplet or codon) code for one amino acid. The genetic code is therefore read in groups of three — and is virtually the same in all organisms.

The genetic code is the set of rules by which a sequence of bases in DNA is translated into a sequence of amino acids in a protein. It is read in triplets (3 bases = 1 amino acid). Different proteins have different shapes and so different functions in the cell.

Worked example

A gene contains 90 bases. What is the maximum number of amino acids it can code for?

  1. 3 bases code for 1 amino acid.
  2. 90 ÷ 3 = 30 amino acids.

4. The human genome

The Human Genome Project (1990–2003) sequenced the ~3 billion base pairs of human DNA and identified the ~20 000 protein-coding genes. Most of human DNA is non-coding — it does not directly code for amino acids in proteins, but parts of it regulate when and where genes are switched on.

When the exam asks for benefits of genome sequencing always combine with one limitation — privacy, cost, ethical concerns — for higher-mark answers.

5. Mutations

A mutation is a random change in the base sequence of DNA. Mutations occur naturally during cell division and can be increased by certain factors (mutagens) — UV light, ionising radiation (X-rays, gamma rays) and some chemicals (e.g. tobacco tar).
Type of mutationEffect on protein
Silent (synonymous)New triplet codes for the same amino acid → no change.
MissenseChanges one amino acid → may slightly alter the protein.
NonsenseCodes for an early "stop" → produces a short, non-functional protein.
Insertion / deletion (frameshift)Shifts the reading frame for every triplet that follows → most/all amino acids changed.

Most mutations have no observable effect on the organism because they occur in non-coding DNA, are silent, or affect a protein in a way that does not alter its function.

Occasionally a mutation has a large effect: it may produce a non-functional protein causing disease (e.g. cystic fibrosis), produce a beneficial change that natural selection favours, or trigger uncontrolled cell division leading to cancer.

6. Variation between individuals

Although humans share ~99.9% of their DNA, the small differences (single base changes, called SNPs) account for variation between individuals. Variation may be:

  • Genetic — caused by different alleles inherited from parents (e.g. blood group, eye colour).
  • Environmental — caused by external factors (e.g. tan from sunlight, scar tissue, language spoken).
  • Both — most traits, e.g. height (genes set the range, diet/exercise determine the actual value).

7. Summary checklist

  • State what a gene, allele, chromosome and genome are — and how they relate.
  • Describe the structure of DNA (double helix, four bases, A–T and C–G pairing).
  • Explain how the order of bases codes for proteins (triplet code, 3 bases = 1 amino acid).
  • Recall how many chromosomes are in a body cell (46) and a gamete (23).
  • Define mutation; explain why most mutations have no effect.
  • Suggest uses of, and concerns about, genome sequencing.
