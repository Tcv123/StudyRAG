B5.1 How do substances get into, out of, and around our bodies?

OCR B GCSE Biology (J257) Twenty First Century Science — Topic B5: The human body — staying alive

1. Movement of substances across membranes

ProcessDescriptionATP needed?
DiffusionNet movement of particles down a concentration gradient.No
OsmosisNet movement of water molecules across a partially-permeable membrane from a higher water potential to lower.No
Active transportMovement of particles against the gradient using carrier proteins and ATP.Yes

Surface area to volume ratio

As an organism gets bigger, its volume increases faster than its surface area. So large organisms need specialised exchange surfaces (lungs, gills, gut, leaves) and a transport system (blood, xylem) to deliver substances quickly enough.

2. The human heart and circulation

Humans have a double circulation: blood passes through the heart twice for each complete circuit. The right side pumps deoxygenated blood to the lungs (pulmonary), the left side pumps oxygenated blood to the body (systemic). Higher pressure to the body comes from the thicker left ventricle wall.

The heart structure

  • Four chambers: left/right atrium (top) and left/right ventricle (bottom).
  • Valves prevent backflow: AV valves between atria and ventricles; semilunar valves between ventricles and arteries.
  • Coronary arteries supply the heart muscle with O₂ and glucose.
  • Heart rate is set by the SAN (sino-atrial node) in the right atrium — a natural pacemaker.

5. Gas exchange in lungs

Air enters via trachea → bronchi → bronchioles → alveoli (millions of tiny air sacs). Adaptations:

  • Huge surface area (~70 m² in adults).
  • Walls one cell thick — short diffusion path.
  • Moist surface for gases to dissolve.
  • Rich blood supply (capillaries) maintains a steep concentration gradient.
  • Ventilation by ribs/diaphragm renews air constantly.

6. Digestion and absorption

Food is broken down by enzymes:

EnzymeSubstrate → productSite
AmylaseStarch → maltoseSaliva, pancreas, small intestine
Protease (e.g. pepsin)Proteins → amino acidsStomach, pancreas, small intestine
LipaseLipids → fatty acids + glycerolPancreas, small intestine

Bile from the liver/gall bladder emulsifies fat (large droplets → small ones, increasing surface area for lipase) and neutralises stomach acid.

Products of digestion are absorbed in the small intestine, with adaptations: long; villi and microvilli give huge surface area; thin walls; rich capillary network.
