B2.3 How can we prevent the spread of infections?

OCR B GCSE Biology (J257) Twenty First Century Science — Topic B2: Keeping healthy

1. Public-health measures

MeasureHow it reduces spread
Hand washing with soapRemoves pathogens from hands; soap disrupts viral/bacterial membranes.
Covering mouth when coughing/sneezingStops droplets travelling 2 m or more.
Safe disposal of wastePrevents bacteria growing on rotten food/sewage.
Clean drinking water and treated sewagePrevents waterborne disease (e.g. cholera).
Quarantine / isolationReduces contact between infected and susceptible people.
Vector control (e.g. mosquito nets, draining standing water)Breaks the malaria/dengue transmission cycle.
Food hygiene / cooking thoroughlyHeat kills salmonella, E. coli, Campylobacter.
Safer sex (condoms)Prevents transmission of STIs (HIV, gonorrhoea, chlamydia).

2. Vaccination programmes

National immunisation schedules (e.g. UK MMR, HPV, COVID-19) prevent epidemics. Herd immunity protects those too young or too immunocompromised to be vaccinated. Eradication of smallpox (1980) was achieved through global vaccination.

3. Antibiotics and antibiotic resistance

Antibiotics kill bacteria or stop them growing — they do not work against viruses. Different antibiotics treat different bacteria and choice depends on the species and tissue infected.

How does antibiotic resistance evolve?

  1. Genetic mutation produces a few bacteria with resistance to the antibiotic.
  2. Antibiotic kills non-resistant bacteria.
  3. Resistant bacteria survive and reproduce; their offspring inherit the resistance gene.
  4. Within generations the resistant strain dominates the population.
  5. Bacteria can also share resistance via plasmids — quickly spreading the gene.

What slows resistance?

  • Doctors only prescribe antibiotics when needed; never for viral infections.
  • Patients finish the full course so all bacteria are killed.
  • Restricted use of antibiotics in agriculture.
  • Hospital hygiene (washing hands, isolating MRSA patients).
  • Developing new antibiotics — slow and expensive.
"Why don't antibiotics treat the flu?" — viruses live inside host cells and have no cell walls or bacterial enzymes, so antibiotics have no target. Antiviral drugs are needed instead.

4. Drug development for infectious disease

StageWhat happens
1. DiscoveryComputer modelling and lab screening identify candidate molecules.
2. Pre-clinical (cells & animals)Tested on cell cultures and animals for toxicity, dose and effectiveness.
3. Phase 1 trialsSmall group of healthy volunteers; safety and side-effects.
4. Phase 2/3 trialsLarger groups of patients with the disease; efficacy & dose.
5. Approval and monitoringRegulator (e.g. MHRA) approves; ongoing safety monitoring.

Double-blind trials with a placebo are the gold standard. Neither the patient nor the doctor knows whether real drug or placebo is given, removing bias.
