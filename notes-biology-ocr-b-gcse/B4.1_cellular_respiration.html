B4.1 What happens during cellular respiration?

OCR B GCSE Biology (J257) Twenty First Century Science — Topic B4: Using food and controlling growth

1. What is respiration?

Cellular respiration releases energy from glucose for use in cell processes. It is an exothermic reaction (releases energy). It happens continuously in every living cell.

2. Aerobic respiration

glucose + oxygen → carbon dioxide + water (+ energy)
C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O

Mostly in mitochondria. Releases the most energy per glucose molecule.

3. Anaerobic respiration

In animal cells (e.g. muscles)In yeast (fermentation)
glucose → lactic acid (+ small amount of energy)glucose → ethanol + carbon dioxide (+ small amount of energy)

Used when there is too little O₂ for aerobic. Releases less energy per glucose because glucose is not fully oxidised.

4. Energy uses

  • Building larger molecules (proteins, lipids).
  • Movement / muscle contraction.
  • Maintaining body temperature in mammals/birds.
  • Active transport.
  • Cell division.

5. Exercise and respiration

During exercise:

  • Heart rate ↑ and breathing rate ↑ to deliver more O₂ and remove CO₂.
  • Muscles contract more — need more energy.
  • If supply of O₂ cannot keep up, anaerobic respiration kicks in producing lactic acid.
  • Oxygen debt — extra O₂ required after exercise to oxidise the lactic acid (in the liver).
"Why do we breathe deeply after exercise?" — to repay the oxygen debt: extra O₂ converts lactic acid back to glucose/CO₂ and water in the liver.

6. Comparing aerobic and anaerobic

7. Metabolism

Metabolism is the sum of all chemical reactions in cells. The energy from respiration is used to drive other reactions (e.g. building proteins from amino acids, converting glucose to glycogen for storage).

Examples of metabolic reactions

  • Conversion of glucose to starch / glycogen (storage).
  • Formation of lipid molecules from glycerol and fatty acids.
  • Use of glucose + nitrate → amino acids → proteins.
  • Breakdown of excess proteins to form urea (excreted).

8. Investigating respiration

Yeast in glucose solution at different temperatures: rate of CO₂ production indicates respiration rate. Higher temperature → faster rate up to optimum (~35–40 °C); above this enzymes denature.
