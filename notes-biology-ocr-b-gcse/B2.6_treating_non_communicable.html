1. Treating cardiovascular disease (CVD)

TreatmentHow it worksLimitations
Lifestyle changeStop smoking, healthier diet, exercise — reduces plaque progression.Requires sustained patient effort; may not reverse advanced disease.
StatinsReduce LDL cholesterol made by the liver.Side-effects: muscle aches, raised liver enzymes; lifelong daily medication.
Blood-pressure drugse.g. ACE inhibitors lower BP, reducing artery damage.Side-effects: dizziness, dry cough.
Aspirin / antiplateletsReduce blood clotting, lowering risk of further heart attack.Risk of bleeding.
StentsMesh tube inserted to keep narrowed coronary artery open.Surgical risk; stents can re-narrow.
Coronary artery bypass graftHealthy vessel grafted around blockage.Major surgery; recovery weeks.
Heart transplantFor end-stage heart failure.Donor shortage; lifelong immunosuppression.

Mechanical / artificial replacements

  • Pacemakers — electrical device controls heart rhythm.
  • Mechanical heart valves — replace damaged valves.
  • Ventricular assist devices — bridge to transplant.

2. Treating cancer

TreatmentHow it worksSide-effects
SurgeryRemove tumour and surrounding tissue.Risks of any major surgery; cannot remove metastases.
RadiotherapyTargeted radiation kills rapidly dividing cells.Damage to nearby healthy tissue; tiredness; nausea.
ChemotherapyDrugs that kill or slow rapidly dividing cells throughout the body.Hair loss, low immunity, nausea — affects all dividing cells.
Hormone therapyBlocks hormones that drive certain cancers (e.g. tamoxifen for ER+ breast cancer).Menopausal-like effects; long-term use needed.
Immunotherapy / mAbsActivate immune system to attack cancer cells; targeted antibodies (e.g. trastuzumab).Autoimmune side-effects.
Targeted gene therapiesNewer; correct or block specific cancer-driver genes.Limited availability; expensive.

3. Treating type 2 diabetes

  • Lifestyle — weight loss, healthy diet, exercise. Often most effective.
  • Metformin — increases insulin sensitivity; first-line drug.
  • Other drugs — sulphonylureas, GLP-1 agonists, SGLT2 inhibitors.
  • Insulin injections — only when other treatments fail.

4. Organ transplants

Donor organ replaces a failed organ. Issues:

  • Donor shortage — demand exceeds supply (kidneys, hearts, livers).
  • Tissue typing — closer match reduces rejection.
  • Immunosuppression — lifelong drugs prevent rejection but increase infection risk.
  • Ethical issues — opt-in vs opt-out donor systems; living donors; ability to pay.

5. Public-health vs personal-health interventions

Public-healthPersonal-health
Sugar tax, smoking ban in public, exercise programmes, vaccination schedules.Diet plans, gym membership, drug prescription.
Affects whole populations; cheaper per person.Tailored to individual risk and genetic profile.
"Evaluate" treatments — always weigh effectiveness vs cost, side-effects, ethics and availability. Higher-mark answers compare two options.
