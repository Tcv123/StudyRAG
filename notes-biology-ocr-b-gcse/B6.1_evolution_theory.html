B6.1 How was the theory of evolution developed?

OCR B GCSE Biology (J257) Twenty First Century Science — Topic B6: Life on Earth — past, present and future

1. What is evolution?

Evolution is the change in inherited characteristics of a population of organisms over many generations, leading to new species over time. Evolution acts by natural selection on inherited variation.

2. Charles Darwin and Alfred Wallace

YearEvent
1831–1836Darwin's voyage on HMS Beagle. Observed variation, especially in finches on the Galápagos Islands.
1858Alfred Russel Wallace independently developed similar ideas. Joint paper with Darwin.
1859Darwin published On the Origin of Species.

Darwin's reasoning

  1. Individuals in a species show variation.
  2. More offspring are born than can survive — a struggle for resources.
  3. Individuals with characteristics best suited to the environment are more likely to survive and reproduce.
  4. They pass these characteristics to offspring.
  5. Over many generations, beneficial alleles spread; the species changes.

3. Why was Darwin's theory slow to be accepted?

  • It contradicted religious views that all species were created in their current form.
  • Mechanism of inheritance unknown — Mendel's work on genes wasn't widely known until later.
  • Insufficient fossil evidence at the time to support links between species.
  • Society in Victorian Britain was reluctant to accept that humans share ancestors with other apes.

4. Evidence for evolution

EvidenceDetail
FossilsShow changes in body form over time (e.g. horse evolution from small five-toed forest dweller to large single-hoofed grazer; whale evolution from land mammals).
Anatomical similaritiesPentadactyl limb in mammals, birds and amphibians shows shared ancestry.
EmbryologyVertebrate embryos look very similar in early development.
DNA evidenceClosely related species share more DNA (~98% human–chimp). Cytochrome c sequences confirm phylogenetic trees.
Direct observationAntibiotic resistance in bacteria; peppered moths; Darwin's finches.

5. Fossils — the record of life

A fossil is the preserved remains, impression or trace of an organism from millions of years ago.
How fossils formDetail
Hard parts (bones, shells, teeth)Replaced by minerals over time.
Casts and mouldsOrganism dissolves; cavity fills with sediment forming a cast.
PreservationInsects in amber; mammoths in ice; conditions stop decay (anoxic / acidic peat bogs).
Trace fossilsFootprints, burrows, droppings (coprolites).

Limitations of the fossil record

  • Soft-bodied organisms rarely fossilise.
  • Many fossils have been destroyed by geological activity (e.g. volcanism, plate tectonics).
  • Some fossils have not yet been found.
  • Conditions for fossil formation are rare.

6. Extinction

A species is extinct when no living members remain. Causes:

  • New predators, diseases or competitors.
  • Environmental change (climate, habitat loss).
  • Single catastrophic event (e.g. asteroid impact 65 mya — dinosaurs).
  • Human activity (over-hunting dodo, habitat destruction).
When given fossil data, always tell the story chronologically — from oldest layers up. State a trend (e.g. "leg bones gradually elongated") and link it to a likely environmental change.
