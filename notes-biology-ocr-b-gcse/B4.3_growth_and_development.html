B4.3 How do organisms grow and develop?

OCR B GCSE Biology (J257) Twenty First Century Science — Topic B4: Using food and controlling growth

1. The cell cycle and mitosis

Mitosis is cell division producing two genetically identical diploid daughter cells. It is essential for growth, repair and asexual reproduction.

Stages

  1. Interphase (G1, S, G2) — cell grows; DNA replicates so each chromosome becomes two sister chromatids; organelles (mitochondria, ribosomes) double.
  2. Prophase — chromosomes condense.
  3. Metaphase — chromosomes align in the middle.
  4. Anaphase — sister chromatids pulled to opposite poles.
  5. Telophase + Cytokinesis — nuclei reform; cytoplasm divides → two daughter cells.

2. Differentiation and specialised cells

After cell division, daughter cells often differentiate — they switch on different genes to take on a specialised role (e.g. nerve cell, muscle cell). Most animal cells lose the ability to differentiate as they specialise; many plant cells retain it (which is why plants regrow more easily).

Therapeutic uses

  • Bone-marrow transplants (leukaemia).
  • Spinal-cord injury, type 1 diabetes (trials).
  • Test new drugs.
  • Repair damaged heart muscle after a heart attack.

Ethical issues

  • Embryonic — destroys embryos; objections from some religious/moral viewpoints.
  • Therapeutic cloning — embryo created from patient's own cell to avoid rejection.
  • Risk of tumour formation (cells divide uncontrollably) and rejection.

4. Plant growth

Plants keep growing throughout life. Cell division happens in meristems:

  • Root tip — increases length downward.
  • Shoot tip — increases length upward.
  • Lateral meristems — increase girth (in woody plants).

Percentile growth charts

Babies' length, mass and head circumference are plotted on percentile charts to track growth. The 50th percentile is the median; falling well below or above could indicate disease, malnutrition, or genetic conditions.

When asked "explain how a percentile chart helps doctors", say: it shows whether a baby's growth is unusual compared to peers; a sudden drop across percentiles suggests a problem requiring investigation.

6. Cancer — uncontrolled cell division

Cancer is caused by mutations in genes that control the cell cycle (e.g. tumour-suppressor genes). Cells divide uncontrollably forming a tumour.
