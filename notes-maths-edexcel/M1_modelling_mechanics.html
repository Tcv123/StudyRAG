M1: Modelling in Mechanics

1. Mathematical Models in Mechanics

Mechanics uses mathematical models to simplify real-world situations. A model makes assumptions to make the mathematics tractable, then solutions are interpreted in context.

A model is an idealised mathematical representation of a physical situation. Models involve assumptions that may be stated and their reasonableness assessed.

Common Assumptions and What They Mean

4. Newton's Three Laws of Motion

Newton's First Law: An object remains at rest, or moves with constant velocity, unless a resultant (net) external force acts on it.
Newton's Second Law: The resultant force on an object equals its mass times its acceleration: $$F = ma$$ where \(F\) is the resultant force in N, \(m\) is mass in kg, and \(a\) is acceleration in m s⁻².
Newton's Third Law: For every action force, there is an equal and opposite reaction force. If object A exerts a force on object B, then B exerts an equal force in the opposite direction on A.
Exam tip: Newton's Third Law pairs must act on different objects. The normal reaction on a book from a table and the book's weight are NOT a Newton's Third Law pair — they happen to be equal only because the book is in equilibrium.

5. Forces Acting on Objects

Weight

Every object with mass experiences a downward gravitational force:

$$W = mg, \quad g = 9.8 \text{ m s}^{-2} \text{ (use unless told otherwise)}$$

Normal Reaction \(R\)

When an object rests on a surface, the surface exerts a normal reaction force perpendicular to the surface, preventing the object from passing through it.

Friction \(F\)

On a rough surface, friction opposes the direction of motion (or tendency to move). For a sliding object:

$$F = \mu R$$

where \(\mu\) is the coefficient of friction. If the object is in equilibrium (or on the verge of moving): \(F \leq \mu R\).

Tension \(T\)

A string under tension pulls its endpoints towards the centre of the string. For a light inextensible string, T is the same throughout.

Thrust

A rod under compression exerts a thrust (push) at its ends, in contrast to tension which pulls.

6. Free Body Diagrams

A free body diagram (FBD) shows all forces acting on a single object, drawn as arrows from or at the object's centre. It is essential for applying Newton's Second Law.

Resolving Forces on a Slope

Resolve forces parallel and perpendicular to the slope (taking up the slope as positive):

$$\text{Perpendicular: } R - mg\cos\theta = 0 \Rightarrow R = mg\cos\theta$$ $$\text{Parallel (no acceleration): } F - mg\sin\theta = 0 \Rightarrow F = mg\sin\theta$$
Example: A 5 kg particle rests on a smooth slope inclined at 30° to the horizontal. Find the normal reaction.

Resolving perpendicular to slope:
\(R = mg\cos 30° = 5 \times 9.8 \times \cos 30° = 49 \times \dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \approx 42.4 \text{ N}\)

7. Applying Newton's Second Law

Example: A 3 kg box is pushed along a rough horizontal floor by a force of 20 N. The coefficient of friction is 0.4. Find the acceleration.

Normal reaction: \(R = mg = 3 \times 9.8 = 29.4 \text{ N}\)
Friction: \(F = \mu R = 0.4 \times 29.4 = 11.76 \text{ N}\)
Net force: \(F_{\text{net}} = 20 - 11.76 = 8.24 \text{ N}\)
Newton's Second Law: \(F = ma \Rightarrow a = \dfrac{8.24}{3} \approx 2.75 \text{ m s}^{-2}\)
