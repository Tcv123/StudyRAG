M4: Moments

1. Moment of a Force

The moment of a force about a point is the turning effect of that force about the point.

$$M = F \times d$$ where \(F\) is the magnitude of the force and \(d\) is the perpendicular distance from the point to the line of action of the force.

Units: Newton-metres (N·m)

Direction matters: moments are clockwise or anticlockwise. By convention, anticlockwise is often taken as positive, but you can choose either — be consistent.

Key: Always use the perpendicular distance from the pivot to the line of action of the force. If the force is not perpendicular to the rod, use \(M = F \times d\sin\theta\) where \(\theta\) is the angle between the force and the rod.

2. Principle of Moments

Principle of Moments: For a body in equilibrium,
$$\text{Sum of clockwise moments} = \text{Sum of anticlockwise moments}$$ about any point.

This holds about any chosen point — choose the pivot wisely to eliminate unknown forces from the equation (take moments about a point where an unknown acts to remove it).

3. Equilibrium Conditions

For a rigid body in equilibrium, both conditions must hold:

$$\sum F = 0 \qquad \text{(resultant force = 0)}$$ $$\sum M = 0 \qquad \text{(resultant moment = 0 about any point)}$$
  • \(\sum F = 0\) means the body does not accelerate (translate).
  • \(\sum M = 0\) means the body does not rotate.

4. Uniform Rod — Weight at Midpoint

A uniform rod of length \(L\) has its weight \(W = mg\) acting at its midpoint (centre of mass at \(L/2\) from either end).

For a non-uniform rod, the centre of mass is at some unknown position \(x\) from one end. Use the principle of moments to find \(x\).

5. Solving Beam Problems

  1. Draw a diagram with all forces (weights, reactions, applied forces).
  2. Take moments about one support to find the reaction at the other (unknown eliminates itself).
  3. Use \(\sum F = 0\) (upward = downward) to find the remaining reaction.
  4. Check: both reactions should be positive (if not, recheck assumptions).
Example: A uniform beam AB, length 4 m, mass 20 kg, rests on supports at A and C where C is 3 m from A. A load of 50 N hangs at B. Find reactions at A and C.

Weight of beam: \(20 \times 9.8 = 196\ \text{N}\) at 2 m from A.
Take moments about A: \(R_C \times 3 = 196 \times 2 + 50 \times 4\)
\(3R_C = 392 + 200 = 592 \Rightarrow R_C = 197.3\ \text{N}\)
Resolve vertically: \(R_A + R_C = 196 + 50 = 246\)
\(R_A = 246 - 197.3 = 48.7\ \text{N}\)

6. Non-Uniform Rods — Finding Centre of Mass

If a rod AB of mass \(m\) has its centre of mass at distance \(x\) from A (unknown), use two different experimental configurations (e.g., different pivot positions) and apply the principle of moments to find \(x\).

Setup: Non-uniform rod AB, length 5 m, mass 8 kg. Balanced on pivot 2 m from A. A 4 kg particle is placed at A to achieve balance. Find the distance of the centre of mass from A.

Taking moments about pivot (2 m from A):
Anticlockwise: \(4g \times 2 = 8g\) ...(load at A, distance 2 m)
Clockwise: \(8g \times (x - 2)\) ...(weight at CoM, distance \(x-2\) from pivot)
\(4g \times 2 = 8g(x-2)\)
\(8 = 8(x-2) \Rightarrow x - 2 = 1 \Rightarrow x = 3\ \text{m from A}\)

7. Tilting

A body is about to tilt about a support when the contact force (reaction) at the other support becomes zero. At this point, the body is on the verge of rotating about the tilting support.

Tilting: body rotates about right pivot; reaction at left support = 0

To find the load required to cause tilting: set the reaction at the non-tilting support to zero and take moments about the tilting support.

8. Ladder Problems

A uniform ladder of mass \(m\), length \(2L\), leans against a smooth vertical wall at angle \(\theta\) to the horizontal, with its base on rough ground.

  • Forces: Weight \(mg\) at midpoint, Normal reaction from wall \(S\) (horizontal, smooth wall), Normal reaction from ground \(R\) (vertical), Friction from ground \(F\) (horizontal, toward wall).
  • Resolve horizontally: \(F = S\)
  • Resolve vertically: \(R = mg\)
  • Take moments about base: \(S \times 2L\sin\theta = mg \times L\cos\theta\)
  • So: \(S = \dfrac{mg\cos\theta}{2\sin\theta} = \dfrac{mg}{2\tan\theta}\)
  • For ladder not to slip: \(F \leq \mu R\), so \(S \leq \mu mg\), giving \(\tan\theta \geq \dfrac{1}{2\mu}\)

9. Key Formulae Summary

FormulaUse
\(M = F \times d\)Moment of force about a point
\(\sum \text{CW moments} = \sum \text{ACW moments}\)Principle of moments (equilibrium)
\(\sum F = 0\) and \(\sum M = 0\)Full equilibrium conditions
Weight at midpointUniform rod/beam
\(R = 0\) at one supportCondition for tilting
Strategy for Moments Problems:
  • Always take moments about a point where an unknown force acts — this eliminates it from the equation.
  • For a beam on two supports, take moments about each support in turn to find both reactions directly.
  • For tilting questions, explicitly state "R = 0 at [support]" before taking moments.
  • For non-uniform rods, set up two equations (resolve vertically + take moments) to find two unknowns.
