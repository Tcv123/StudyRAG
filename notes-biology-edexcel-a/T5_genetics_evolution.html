5.1 DNA Structure and Replication

DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid): A double-stranded polynucleotide that carries the hereditary genetic information. Each nucleotide consists of deoxyribose sugar, a phosphate group, and one of four nitrogenous bases (A, T, C, G).

DNA Structure

  • Two antiparallel polynucleotide strands form a double helix
  • Sugar-phosphate backbone on the outside (covalent phosphodiester bonds)
  • Bases point inward and pair by complementary base pairing: A–T (2 hydrogen bonds), C–G (3 hydrogen bonds)
  • Strands are antiparallel: one runs 5'→3', the other 3'→5'

Semi-Conservative Replication

  1. Helicase unwinds the double helix and breaks hydrogen bonds between base pairs, separating the two strands
  2. Each strand acts as a template
  3. DNA polymerase adds free nucleotides to the exposed bases by complementary base pairing (A with T, C with G)
  4. DNA polymerase works in the 5'→3' direction only
  5. Leading strand is synthesised continuously; lagging strand is synthesised in short Okazaki fragments (joined by DNA ligase)
  6. Result: two identical DNA molecules, each with one original strand and one new strand (semi-conservative)
The Meselson-Stahl experiment (using heavy nitrogen 15N and density gradient centrifugation) provided evidence for semi-conservative replication. After one generation in 14N, all DNA was intermediate density. After two generations, half was intermediate and half was light.

5.2 Protein Synthesis

Transcription (Nucleus)

  1. RNA polymerase binds to the promoter region of the gene on the template (antisense) strand
  2. Hydrogen bonds between DNA base pairs are broken; DNA strands separate
  3. RNA polymerase moves along the template strand (3'→5'), assembling a complementary mRNA molecule (5'→3')
  4. Free RNA nucleotides are added by complementary base pairing (U pairs with A in DNA)
  5. RNA polymerase reaches a terminator sequence and detaches; mRNA is released
  6. In eukaryotes: pre-mRNA undergoes splicing (introns removed, exons joined) before leaving the nucleus through nuclear pores

Translation (Ribosomes in Cytoplasm)

  1. mRNA binds to the small ribosomal subunit; start codon (AUG) is positioned at the P site
  2. tRNA molecules carry specific amino acids; each has an anticodon complementary to an mRNA codon
  3. First tRNA (carrying methionine) binds to AUG at the P site
  4. Second tRNA binds at the A site (anticodon matches next mRNA codon)
  5. A peptide bond forms between adjacent amino acids (catalysed by ribosome/rRNA)
  6. Ribosome translocates one codon along mRNA; first tRNA released
  7. Process repeats until a stop codon (UAA, UAG, UGA) is reached
  8. Polypeptide is released and folds into its functional protein shape
The genetic code: A triplet code (three bases = one codon = one amino acid). It is degenerate (most amino acids coded by more than one codon), universal (same in almost all organisms) and non-overlapping.

5.3 Gene Mutations

Gene mutation: A change in the nucleotide sequence of DNA. Mutations are the ultimate source of genetic variation and can be spontaneous or induced by mutagens (UV radiation, chemicals, ionising radiation).

Types of Point Mutation

TypeChangeEffect on Protein
Substitution (silent/synonymous)One base replaced by anotherNo change — degenerate code means same amino acid coded
Substitution (missense)One base replacedDifferent amino acid incorporated — may alter protein function (e.g., sickle cell: GAG → GTG, Glu → Val)
Substitution (nonsense)One base replacedCreates a premature stop codon — truncated, non-functional protein
InsertionOne or more bases addedFrameshift — all codons downstream are altered; usually non-functional protein
DeletionOne or more bases removedFrameshift — same severe effect as insertion
Frameshift mutations (insertions and deletions) are generally more damaging than substitutions because they alter EVERY codon after the mutation point. A substitution typically only affects one amino acid.

5.4 Inheritance

Key Terminology

TermDefinition
GeneA section of DNA that codes for a particular polypeptide or functional RNA
AlleleAn alternative form of a gene (different nucleotide sequence at the same locus)
LocusThe specific position of a gene on a chromosome
GenotypeThe combination of alleles an organism possesses for a particular gene
PhenotypeThe observable characteristics resulting from genotype and environment
HomozygousTwo identical alleles at a locus (e.g., AA or aa)
HeterozygousTwo different alleles at a locus (e.g., Aa)
DominantAllele expressed in both homozygous and heterozygous individuals
RecessiveAllele only expressed in the homozygous condition

Monohybrid Inheritance

Involves one gene with two alleles. Use Punnett squares to predict offspring ratios. A cross between two heterozygotes (Aa × Aa) gives a 3:1 phenotypic ratio (3 dominant : 1 recessive) and a 1:2:1 genotypic ratio.

Dihybrid Inheritance

Involves two genes on different chromosomes (independently assorting). A cross between two double heterozygotes (AaBb × AaBb) gives a 9:3:3:1 phenotypic ratio.

Codominance

Both alleles are equally expressed in the heterozygote (neither is dominant). Example: ABO blood groups where IA and IB are codominant (IAIB gives blood group AB), while both are dominant over i (recessive, gives group O).

Sex Linkage

Genes located on the X chromosome (X-linked). Males (XY) are hemizygous — only one allele, so recessive X-linked conditions are more common in males. Examples: haemophilia, red-green colour blindness.

Carrier females (XHXh) are phenotypically normal but can pass the recessive allele to sons.

Autosomal Linkage

Genes on the same chromosome tend to be inherited together (do not independently assort). Linked genes produce fewer recombinant offspring than expected. Crossing over during meiosis can break linkage, producing recombinants.

Epistasis

One gene masks or modifies the expression of another gene at a different locus. This produces modified dihybrid ratios (e.g., 9:3:4 for recessive epistasis, 12:3:1 for dominant epistasis).

In genetics problems: (1) Define your symbols clearly, (2) State the parental genotypes, (3) Show gametes, (4) Draw a Punnett square, (5) State offspring ratios. For sex linkage, always use XHXh notation showing the alleles as superscripts on the X chromosome.

5.5 Hardy-Weinberg Principle

Hardy-Weinberg principle: In a large, randomly mating population with no selection, mutation, migration or genetic drift, allele frequencies remain constant from generation to generation.
p + q = 1   (where p = frequency of dominant allele, q = frequency of recessive allele)
p2 + 2pq + q2 = 1   (p2 = freq of AA, 2pq = freq of Aa, q2 = freq of aa)

Using Hardy-Weinberg

  1. Identify the number/frequency of homozygous recessive individuals (q2)
  2. Take the square root to find q
  3. Calculate p = 1 − q
  4. Calculate carrier frequency: 2pq

Conditions for Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium (all must be met):

  • Large population size (no genetic drift)
  • Random mating (no sexual selection)
  • No natural selection (all genotypes equally fit)
  • No mutation
  • No migration (gene flow)

5.6 Evolution and Speciation

Evolution: A change in allele frequency in a population over successive generations, driven by natural selection, genetic drift, mutation and gene flow.

Genetic Drift

Random changes in allele frequency due to chance events, particularly significant in small populations. Can lead to fixation (allele frequency reaches 1.0) or loss (frequency reaches 0) regardless of selective advantage. Two types:

  • Bottleneck effect: Dramatic population reduction (disaster) randomly eliminates alleles
  • Founder effect: Small group colonises new area; limited gene pool differs from parent population

Speciation

Speciation is the formation of new species. A species is a group of organisms that can interbreed to produce fertile offspring and are reproductively isolated from other groups.

TypeMechanismExample
AllopatricGeographic isolation → different selection pressures → genetic divergence → reproductive isolationDarwin's finches on Galapagos Islands
SympatricReproductive isolation without geographic barrier (polyploidy, behavioural, temporal, ecological isolation)Polyploidy in plants (e.g., wheat)

Reproductive Isolation Mechanisms

  • Pre-zygotic: temporal (different breeding times), mechanical (incompatible genitalia), behavioural (different courtship), gametic (gametes incompatible)
  • Post-zygotic: hybrid inviability (offspring do not survive), hybrid sterility (offspring infertile, e.g., mules)
When explaining speciation, follow this sequence: (1) populations separated/isolated, (2) different selection pressures/environments, (3) different mutations arise, (4) natural selection favours different alleles, (5) allele frequencies diverge, (6) eventually reproductively isolated = new species.
