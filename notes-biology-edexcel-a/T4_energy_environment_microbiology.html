4.1 Photosynthesis

Photosynthesis: The process by which light energy is used to convert carbon dioxide and water into glucose and oxygen. It occurs in chloroplasts of plant cells and algae.
6CO2 + 6H2O → C6H12O6 + 6O2 (light energy required)

Chloroplast Structure

Chloroplasts have a double outer membrane (envelope), an internal system of thylakoid membranes arranged in stacks called grana (connected by intergranal lamellae), and a fluid-filled stroma containing enzymes, DNA, ribosomes and starch grains. Photosynthetic pigments (chlorophylls and accessory pigments) are located in the thylakoid membranes.

Light-Dependent Reactions (Thylakoid Membranes)

  1. Photoexcitation: Light energy is absorbed by chlorophyll a in photosystem II (PSII, P680). Electrons are excited to a higher energy level and pass to an electron acceptor
  2. Photolysis of water: Water is split to replace lost electrons: 2H2O → 4H+ + 4e− + O2
  3. Electron transport chain: Excited electrons pass along a chain of carriers (including plastoquinone and cytochrome complexes), releasing energy used to pump H+ into the thylakoid lumen (chemiosmosis)
  4. ATP synthesis: H+ ions flow back through ATP synthase (down concentration gradient) — photophosphorylation produces ATP
  5. Photosystem I (PSI, P700): Light re-excites electrons; they are passed to NADP+ reductase
  6. NADP reduction: Electrons combine with H+ and NADP+ to form reduced NADP (NADPH)

Products: ATP, reduced NADP (NADPH) and O2 (waste product from photolysis)

Light-Independent Reactions — The Calvin Cycle (Stroma)

  1. Carbon fixation: CO2 combines with ribulose bisphosphate (RuBP, 5C) catalysed by RuBisCO, forming two molecules of glycerate-3-phosphate (GP, 3C)
  2. Reduction: GP is reduced to triose phosphate (TP/G3P, 3C) using ATP and reduced NADP from light-dependent reactions
  3. Regeneration of RuBP: Most TP (5 out of every 6 molecules) is used to regenerate RuBP using ATP
  4. Product: 1 in 6 TP molecules exits the cycle to form glucose (and other organic molecules)
3CO2 + 9ATP + 6NADPH → G3P + 9ADP + 8Pi + 6NADP+ (one turn produces one TP; six turns for one glucose)
Know what happens when light is suddenly removed: light-dependent reactions stop, so no ATP or reduced NADP is produced. GP accumulates (cannot be reduced to TP), TP and RuBP decrease. Conversely, if CO2 drops: RuBP accumulates, GP decreases.

Limiting Factors

  • Light intensity: Rate increases proportionally at low light; plateaus at saturation point
  • CO2 concentration: Increases rate until another factor is limiting; RuBisCO saturates at ~0.1%
  • Temperature: Affects enzyme activity; optimum ~25–30°C; above this, enzyme denaturation

4.2 Cellular Respiration

Respiration: The metabolic process in which organic molecules (usually glucose) are broken down to release energy, which is used to synthesise ATP. It occurs in all living cells continuously.

Overview of Aerobic Respiration

C6H12O6 + 6O2 → 6CO2 + 6H2O + energy (ATP)

Stage 1: Glycolysis (Cytoplasm)

  1. Glucose (6C) is phosphorylated using 2 ATP → fructose-1,6-bisphosphate (6C)
  2. Split into 2 molecules of triose phosphate (3C)
  3. Each TP is oxidised (hydrogen removed to NAD+) and converted to pyruvate (3C)
  4. Net yield per glucose: 2 ATP (substrate-level phosphorylation), 2 reduced NAD, 2 pyruvate

Glycolysis is anaerobic (does not require oxygen) and occurs in all living cells.

The Link Reaction (Mitochondrial Matrix)

Pyruvate enters the mitochondrial matrix via active transport. It is:

  • Oxidised (hydrogen removed → reduced NAD)
  • Decarboxylated (CO2 removed)
  • Combined with coenzyme A to form acetyl CoA (2C)
Pyruvate (3C) + NAD+ + CoA → Acetyl CoA (2C) + CO2 + reduced NAD (per pyruvate)

Stage 2: Krebs Cycle (Mitochondrial Matrix)

  1. Acetyl CoA (2C) combines with oxaloacetate (4C) to form citrate (6C); CoA is released
  2. Citrate is decarboxylated and oxidised through a series of reactions, regenerating oxaloacetate
  3. Per turn: 2CO2, 3 reduced NAD, 1 reduced FAD, 1 ATP (via GTP)
  4. Per glucose (2 turns): 4CO2, 6 reduced NAD, 2 reduced FAD, 2 ATP

Stage 3: Oxidative Phosphorylation (Inner Mitochondrial Membrane)

  1. Reduced NAD and reduced FAD donate electrons to the electron transport chain (series of protein complexes on cristae)
  2. Electrons pass along the chain, releasing energy at each step
  3. Energy used to pump H+ from matrix into intermembrane space (creating proton gradient)
  4. H+ flows back through ATP synthase (chemiosmosis) — drives ATP synthesis
  5. Oxygen is the final electron acceptor: combines with H+ and electrons to form water
Theoretical maximum ATP yield per glucose: ~30–32 ATP (actual yield varies)
If oxygen is absent, the ETC stops, reduced NAD/FAD cannot be reoxidised, Krebs cycle stops, link reaction stops. Only glycolysis continues (using anaerobic pathways to regenerate NAD). In animals: pyruvate → lactate. In yeast: pyruvate → ethanol + CO2.

4.3 Ecosystems and Energy Flow

Ecosystem: A community of organisms (biotic) interacting with each other and their physical environment (abiotic factors) within a defined area. Ecosystems involve energy flow and nutrient cycling.

Energy Transfer Through Trophic Levels

Energy enters ecosystems as light and is fixed by producers (photosynthesis). Only about 1–3% of light energy is converted to chemical energy in plants. Energy is transferred through food chains, but only approximately 10% passes between trophic levels. Energy is lost at each level through:

  • Respiration (heat lost to surroundings)
  • Excretion (urine, faeces contain chemical energy)
  • Parts not eaten (roots, bones)
Net primary productivity (NPP) = Gross primary productivity (GPP) − Respiration (R)
Efficiency of transfer = (energy at trophic level n+1 / energy at trophic level n) × 100%

Nutrient Cycling

Unlike energy (which flows through ecosystems), nutrients are recycled. Key cycles include carbon and nitrogen cycles.

The Carbon Cycle

  • CO2 fixed by photosynthesis into organic compounds
  • Returned to atmosphere by respiration, combustion, decomposition
  • Carbon locked in fossil fuels (coal, oil, gas) and limestone
  • Human activities (burning fossil fuels, deforestation) increasing atmospheric CO2

4.4 Decomposition

Decomposition: The breakdown of dead organic matter by saprobionts (decomposers such as bacteria and fungi) that secrete extracellular enzymes and absorb the soluble products.

Factors affecting rate of decomposition:

  • Temperature — affects enzyme activity of decomposers
  • Water availability — needed as a solvent and for enzyme function
  • Oxygen availability — aerobic decomposition is faster than anaerobic
  • pH — affects enzyme activity
  • Type of material — lignin decomposes more slowly than simple sugars

4.5 Microbiology Techniques

Aseptic Technique

Essential to prevent contamination of cultures and protect the worker:

  • Sterilise equipment (autoclave at 121°C, 15 min)
  • Flame inoculating loops until red hot before and after use
  • Work near a Bunsen burner (updraught reduces airborne contamination)
  • Flame necks of bottles when opened
  • Seal Petri dishes with tape (not fully sealed — allow aerobic conditions)
  • Incubate at ≤25°C in school labs (prevents growth of human pathogens)

Measuring Microbial Growth

Bacterial growth follows a characteristic curve with four phases:

  1. Lag phase — bacteria adapting; producing enzymes; no increase in number
  2. Log (exponential) phase — rapid binary fission; unlimited nutrients; no competition
  3. Stationary phase — growth rate = death rate; nutrients limiting; waste accumulates
  4. Death (decline) phase — death rate > growth rate; nutrients exhausted; toxic waste

Serial Dilution and Plating

To count viable bacteria: perform serial dilutions (e.g., 10-fold), plate known volumes on agar, incubate, count colonies. Each colony arose from one bacterium.

Number of bacteria per cm3 = colonies counted × dilution factor × (1 / volume plated)
In practical questions on microbiology, always explain WHY each aseptic step is necessary. Also, remember that turbidity (measured with a colorimeter/spectrophotometer) measures total cell count (including dead cells), while colony counting (viable count) only measures living cells.
