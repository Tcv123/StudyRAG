Homeostasis and Response Edexcel A A-Level Biology — Topic 7

7.1 Principles of Homeostasis

Homeostasis: The maintenance of a constant internal environment within restricted limits, despite changes in the external environment. It involves negative feedback mechanisms with receptors, a coordination centre and effectors.

Negative Feedback

A mechanism that detects a deviation from the set point (norm) and activates responses that return the variable to its normal level. The response opposes the change. Most homeostatic mechanisms use negative feedback.

  1. Stimulus — change in internal environment detected
  2. Receptor — detects the change (sensory cells)
  3. Coordination centre — processes information (brain, pancreas)
  4. Effector — carries out response (muscle or gland)
  5. Response — counteracts the original change, returning to set point

Positive Feedback

A mechanism where the response amplifies the original change, moving further from the set point. Examples: oxytocin during childbirth (contractions stimulate more oxytocin release), blood clotting cascade, depolarisation during an action potential. Positive feedback is rare in homeostasis but important in specific biological events.

7.2 The Nervous System

Nervous system: A rapid, short-lived communication system using electrical impulses transmitted along neurones. It coordinates responses to stimuli and consists of the central nervous system (CNS: brain and spinal cord) and peripheral nervous system (PNS).

Neurone Structure

Resting Potential

At rest, the neurone membrane is polarised at approximately −70 mV (inside negative relative to outside). This is maintained by:

  • Na+/K+ ATPase pump: Actively pumps 3 Na+ out for every 2 K+ in (using ATP)
  • Potassium leak channels: K+ diffuses out down concentration gradient (more permeable to K+ at rest)
  • Large organic anions trapped inside the cell

Action Potential

  1. Depolarisation: Stimulus causes Na+ channels to open; Na+ floods in; membrane depolarises (becomes positive inside, ~+40 mV)
  2. Threshold: If depolarisation reaches ~−55 mV, an action potential fires (all-or-nothing)
  3. Repolarisation: Na+ channels close; K+ channels open; K+ flows out; membrane repolarises
  4. Hyperpolarisation: Slight overshoot below −70 mV (refractory period)
  5. Resting potential restored by Na+/K+ pump

Transmission Speed

Factors increasing speed of transmission:

  • Myelination: Schwann cells wrap around axon; impulse jumps between nodes of Ranvier (saltatory conduction) — much faster
  • Axon diameter: Larger diameter = less resistance = faster conduction
  • Temperature: Higher temperature increases rate (up to a point)
The refractory period ensures: (1) impulses travel in one direction only (cannot re-stimulate the section just depolarised), (2) impulses are discrete events (cannot merge), (3) limits the maximum frequency of impulses.

7.3 Synapses

Synapse: The junction between two neurones (or a neurone and an effector) across which a nerve impulse is transmitted, usually by chemical neurotransmitters (e.g., acetylcholine) diffusing across the synaptic cleft.

Cholinergic Synapse Transmission

  1. Action potential arrives at presynaptic knob
  2. Voltage-gated Ca2+ channels open; Ca2+ floods in
  3. Ca2+ causes synaptic vesicles (containing acetylcholine, ACh) to fuse with presynaptic membrane
  4. ACh released into synaptic cleft by exocytosis
  5. ACh diffuses across cleft and binds to specific receptors on postsynaptic membrane
  6. Na+ channels open on postsynaptic membrane; Na+ influx; depolarisation
  7. If threshold reached, new action potential generated in postsynaptic neurone
  8. Acetylcholinesterase (AChE) in cleft hydrolyses ACh into choline + acetyl; recycled back into presynaptic knob

Summation

  • Temporal summation: Multiple impulses from the same presynaptic neurone in rapid succession; cumulative neurotransmitter release reaches threshold
  • Spatial summation: Multiple presynaptic neurones release neurotransmitter simultaneously at one postsynaptic neurone; combined effect reaches threshold

7.4 Hormonal Coordination

Endocrine vs Nervous System

FeatureNervous SystemEndocrine System
CommunicationElectrical impulses along neuronesChemical hormones via blood
SpeedVery fast (milliseconds)Slower (seconds to hours)
DurationShort-livedLonger-lasting
TargetSpecific (single effector)Widespread (all cells with specific receptor)

Hormones: Peptide vs Steroid

  • Peptide hormones (e.g., insulin, ADH): water-soluble; cannot cross cell membrane; bind to cell-surface receptors; trigger secondary messenger cascade (e.g., cAMP)
  • Steroid hormones (e.g., oestrogen, testosterone): lipid-soluble; cross membrane; bind to intracellular receptors; hormone-receptor complex acts as transcription factor in nucleus

7.5 Blood Glucose Regulation

Blood glucose homeostasis: The maintenance of blood glucose concentration at approximately 4–6 mmol/L, primarily controlled by the hormones insulin and glucagon secreted by the islets of Langerhans in the pancreas.

After a Meal (High Blood Glucose)

  1. Beta cells of islets of Langerhans detect high blood glucose
  2. Beta cells secrete insulin
  3. Insulin binds to receptors on liver and muscle cells
  4. Effects: increased glucose uptake (GLUT4 transporters inserted into membrane); increased glycogenesis (glucose → glycogen); increased cell respiration; increased conversion to fat
  5. Blood glucose falls back to normal

Between Meals (Low Blood Glucose)

  1. Alpha cells detect low blood glucose
  2. Alpha cells secrete glucagon
  3. Glucagon binds to receptors on liver cells (hepatocytes)
  4. Effects: glycogenolysis (glycogen → glucose); gluconeogenesis (amino acids/glycerol → glucose); decreased glycogenesis
  5. Blood glucose rises back to normal

7.6 Thermoregulation

Core body temperature is maintained at approximately 37°C. The hypothalamus acts as the thermoregulatory centre, receiving information from thermoreceptors in the skin (peripheral) and in the blood flowing through the hypothalamus itself (central).

Response to Cold (Below Set Point)

  • Vasoconstriction: Arterioles supplying skin capillaries constrict; less blood near surface; less heat radiated
  • Shivering: Involuntary contraction of skeletal muscles; generates heat from respiration
  • Piloerection: Erector pili muscles contract; hairs stand up (traps insulating air layer — more effective in furry animals)
  • Increased metabolic rate: Thyroid hormones increase cellular respiration
  • Behavioural: Adding layers, moving to warmer area, curling up

Response to Heat (Above Set Point)

  • Vasodilation: Arterioles dilate; more blood flows near skin surface; more heat radiated/conducted to environment
  • Sweating: Sweat glands secrete sweat; evaporation absorbs latent heat from skin (cooling)
  • Hairs lie flat: Erector pili muscles relax; less insulation
  • Behavioural: Removing clothing, seeking shade, reducing activity

7.7 The Kidney and Osmoregulation

Osmoregulation: The control of water potential (and ion concentration) of the blood and body fluids. In mammals, this is primarily carried out by the kidneys under the control of ADH (antidiuretic hormone).

Kidney Structure and Nephron Function

RegionProcessDetails
Glomerulus & Bowman's capsuleUltrafiltrationHigh blood pressure forces small molecules (water, glucose, ions, urea) through basement membrane into capsule. Blood cells, platelets and proteins too large to pass.
Proximal convoluted tubule (PCT)Selective reabsorption~85% of filtrate reabsorbed. All glucose and amino acids reabsorbed (active transport + co-transport). Na+ actively pumped out; water follows by osmosis. Microvilli increase surface area.

ADH and Osmoregulation

  1. Blood water potential decreases (too concentrated/dehydrated)
  2. Osmoreceptors in hypothalamus detect change (cells shrink)
  3. Posterior pituitary releases more ADH
  4. ADH travels in blood to kidneys; binds to receptors on collecting duct cells
  5. Aquaporins (water channels) inserted into collecting duct membrane
  6. More water reabsorbed from collecting duct by osmosis (into hypertonic medulla)
  7. Small volume of concentrated urine produced
  8. Blood water potential returns to normal → less ADH released (negative feedback)
Longer loops of Henle (e.g., in desert animals like kangaroo rats) create a steeper medullary gradient, allowing more water to be reabsorbed and more concentrated urine to be produced. This is an adaptation to arid environments.

7.8 Muscle Contraction

Sliding filament theory: Muscle contraction occurs when actin (thin) filaments slide over myosin (thick) filaments, shortening the sarcomere. The filaments themselves do not shorten; they overlap more.

Structure of Skeletal Muscle

Muscle → fascicles (bundles) → muscle fibres (cells, multinucleate) → myofibrils → sarcomeres (functional units). Each sarcomere is bounded by Z lines and contains:

  • A band (dark) — length of myosin filaments (does NOT change during contraction)
  • I band (light) — actin only; shortens during contraction
  • H zone — myosin only (centre of A band); shortens during contraction

Mechanism of Contraction

  1. Nerve impulse arrives at neuromuscular junction; ACh released; muscle fibre depolarised
  2. Depolarisation spreads along sarcolemma and down T-tubules
  3. Sarcoplasmic reticulum (SR) releases Ca2+ ions into sarcoplasm
  4. Ca2+ binds to troponin on actin filaments; causes conformational change
  5. Tropomyosin moves away from myosin-binding sites on actin (sites exposed)
  6. Myosin heads (energised by ATP hydrolysis) bind to actin → cross-bridge forms
  7. Power stroke: Myosin head pivots, pulling actin towards centre of sarcomere; ADP + Pi released
  8. New ATP binds to myosin head → cross-bridge detaches
  9. ATP hydrolysed → myosin head re-cocked (ready for next cycle)
  10. Cycle repeats as long as Ca2+ and ATP are present

Relaxation

When nerve impulses stop: Ca2+ is actively pumped back into SR; troponin returns to original shape; tropomyosin re-covers binding sites; no cross-bridges form; muscle relaxes (returns to resting length by antagonistic muscle contraction).

During contraction: I bands and H zones get SHORTER (more overlap); A bands stay the SAME (myosin length unchanged); sarcomere gets shorter (Z lines move closer). Remember this for electron micrograph interpretation questions.
