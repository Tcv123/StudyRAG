2.1 Cell Structure

Cell: The basic structural and functional unit of all living organisms. All cells are bounded by a cell surface membrane and contain genetic material (DNA). The cell theory states: (1) all living things are made of cells, (2) cells are the basic unit of structure and function, (3) new cells arise from pre-existing cells by division.

Eukaryotic Cell Organelles

Eukaryotic cells (animals, plants, fungi, protists) are characterised by membrane-bound organelles, a true nucleus with a nuclear envelope, and linear chromosomes. Compartmentalisation allows different metabolic reactions to occur simultaneously in optimal conditions within the same cell.

Prokaryotic Cells

Prokaryotes (bacteria and archaea) are smaller (typically 0.5–5 μm) and structurally simpler than eukaryotes. They lack membrane-bound organelles and have no true nucleus. Despite their simplicity, they are enormously successful and found in virtually every habitat on Earth.

Understanding prokaryotic cell structure is critical for medicine. Penicillin works by inhibiting transpeptidase, preventing peptidoglycan cross-linking during cell wall synthesis — the wall weakens and the bacterium lyses due to osmotic pressure. This does not affect human cells because we lack cell walls. Gram staining (distinguishing cell wall types) helps clinicians choose appropriate antibiotics. Plasmid-mediated antibiotic resistance is a major public health threat (e.g., MRSA, ESBL-producing bacteria).

2.2 Cell Division

The Cell Cycle

The cell cycle is the ordered sequence of events between one cell division and the next. It consists of interphase (the longest period, where the cell grows and prepares) and M phase (mitosis + cytokinesis). The entire cycle is regulated by cyclins and cyclin-dependent kinases (CDKs) at key checkpoints (G1/S, G2/M, and the metaphase/spindle assembly checkpoint).

Interphase has three sub-phases:

  • G1 (Gap 1): Cell grows; synthesises proteins, RNA, and organelles; prepares for DNA replication. The cell checks for DNA damage at the G1/S checkpoint (the "restriction point"). If conditions are not met, the cell enters G0 (quiescent state — non-dividing, e.g., mature neurones)
  • S (Synthesis): DNA is replicated semi-conservatively. Each chromosome becomes two sister chromatids joined at the centromere. Histones are also synthesised and assembled onto new DNA. Centrioles replicate
  • G2 (Gap 2): Further cell growth; synthesis of proteins needed for mitosis (e.g., tubulin for spindle fibres). The G2/M checkpoint verifies DNA replication is complete and checks for damage before committing to mitosis
Cancer results from uncontrolled cell division when cell cycle checkpoints fail. Mutations in proto-oncogenes (which promote cell division) can convert them to oncogenes that are permanently active, driving excessive proliferation. Mutations in tumour suppressor genes (e.g., p53, Rb) remove the "brakes" on the cell cycle. p53 normally halts the cell cycle at G1 to allow DNA repair, or triggers apoptosis if damage is too severe. Loss of p53 function (found in ~50% of cancers) allows damaged cells to continue dividing, accumulating further mutations. Chemotherapy drugs often target rapidly dividing cells by disrupting DNA replication (S phase) or spindle formation (M phase).

Mitosis

Mitosis: Nuclear division producing two genetically identical daughter nuclei, each with the same number of chromosomes as the parent cell (maintaining the diploid number, 2n). Used for growth, repair of damaged tissues, replacement of dead cells, and asexual reproduction. Followed by cytokinesis (division of the cytoplasm).

Cytokinesis differs between animal and plant cells:

  • Animal cells: A cleavage furrow forms as a contractile ring of actin and myosin filaments pinches the cell membrane inward, eventually dividing the cell in two
  • Plant cells: A cell plate forms at the equator from vesicles derived from the Golgi apparatus. These vesicles fuse to create a new cell membrane and cell wall (middle lamella) between the two daughter cells
To remember the order: PMAT (Prophase, Metaphase, Anaphase, Telophase). In root tip squash practicals (CP), you can identify each stage: prophase cells have visible chromosomes but no clear arrangement; metaphase cells have chromosomes at the equator; anaphase cells show chromosomes at opposite ends; telophase cells show two groups with a dividing line. Count cells in each stage to calculate the mitotic index = (number of cells in mitosis / total cells counted) × 100%. A high mitotic index may indicate rapid growth or cancerous tissue.

Meiosis

Meiosis: Two successive nuclear divisions (meiosis I and meiosis II) producing four genetically different haploid daughter cells from one diploid parent cell. It is essential for sexual reproduction: it halves the chromosome number (maintaining the species chromosome number across generations) and introduces genetic variation through crossing over and independent assortment.

Meiosis I (the reduction division — homologous chromosomes are separated):

  • Prophase I: Homologous chromosomes pair up (synapsis), forming bivalents (tetrads). Crossing over occurs: non-sister chromatids of homologous chromosomes exchange corresponding segments of DNA at chiasmata (points of crossover). This recombines alleles on the same chromosome, creating new allele combinations. Chromosomes condense; nuclear envelope breaks down; spindle forms
  • Metaphase I: Bivalents line up at the equator with random orientation (independent assortment) — which homologue faces which pole is entirely random for each bivalent, creating 2n possible arrangements (in humans, 223 = ~8.4 million combinations)
  • Anaphase I: Homologous chromosomes are pulled to opposite poles (NOT sister chromatids — centromeres do NOT divide). Each pole receives one chromosome from each homologous pair
  • Telophase I and cytokinesis: Two haploid cells form, each containing one chromosome from each homologous pair (but each chromosome still consists of two sister chromatids)

Meiosis II is similar to mitosis:

  • No further DNA replication occurs between meiosis I and II
  • Chromosomes line up at the equator; centromeres divide; sister chromatids are pulled to opposite poles
  • Result: four haploid cells, each genetically unique

Key differences between mitosis and meiosis: Mitosis produces 2 identical diploid cells; meiosis produces 4 different haploid cells. Mitosis has one division; meiosis has two. Crossing over and independent assortment occur in meiosis I only. Homologous chromosomes pair in meiosis I (not in mitosis). In anaphase I, homologous chromosomes separate; in anaphase II (and mitotic anaphase), sister chromatids separate.

2.3 Cell Differentiation and Stem Cells

Stem cell: An undifferentiated cell capable of dividing by mitosis to produce either more stem cells (self-renewal) or cells that can differentiate into specialised cell types. The range of cell types a stem cell can produce defines its potency.

Induced Pluripotent Stem Cells (iPSCs)

Shinya Yamanaka (2006, Nobel Prize 2012) showed that adult somatic cells (e.g., skin fibroblasts) can be reprogrammed to a pluripotent state by introducing four specific transcription factors (Oct4, Sox2, Klf4, c-Myc — the "Yamanaka factors"). These iPSCs behave similarly to embryonic stem cells and can differentiate into any cell type. Advantages: avoids the ethical issues of destroying embryos; allows patient-specific cells (reducing immune rejection risk). Challenges: risk of tumour formation (especially from c-Myc, an oncogene); reprogramming efficiency is low; epigenetic "memory" of the original cell type.

Cell Differentiation

Differentiation is the process by which an unspecialised cell becomes specialised for a particular function. It involves the selective expression of specific genes while other genes are switched off (silenced). All body cells contain the same genome (same DNA), but only transcribe the genes appropriate for their function. This differential gene expression is controlled by:

  • Transcription factors — proteins that bind to DNA promoter/enhancer regions to activate or repress specific genes
  • Signalling molecules — hormones, growth factors, and cell-surface signals from neighbouring cells
  • Epigenetic modifications — DNA methylation (silences genes) and histone modification (acetylation opens chromatin, deacetylation closes it) without changing the DNA sequence

In animals, most differentiated cells are terminally differentiated and cannot revert to a stem cell state (notable exceptions: iPSCs, liver regeneration). In plants, many differentiated cells retain totipotency — they can dedifferentiate and produce a whole new organism (the basis of plant tissue culture and cloning from cuttings).

Stem cell therapies hold enormous medical potential: bone marrow transplants (haematopoietic stem cells) already treat leukaemia and sickle cell disease. Research is ongoing for treating Parkinson's disease (dopamine-producing neurones from stem cells), type 1 diabetes (insulin-producing beta cells), spinal cord injuries, and heart failure. Ethical considerations include: the moral status of the embryo (pluripotent ES cells require embryo destruction), informed consent for donated embryos, safety concerns (tumour risk), and the distinction between therapeutic use and enhancement.

2.4 Biodiversity

Biodiversity: The variety of living organisms in an area, considered at three levels: (1) genetic diversity — the range of alleles/genotypes within a species, (2) species diversity — the number and relative abundance of different species in a community, (3) ecosystem diversity — the range of different habitats and communities in an area.

Measuring Biodiversity

Species richness is simply the number of different species present in an area. It is easy to measure but does not account for relative abundance (a community with 100 species where one species dominates at 99% of individuals is less diverse than one with 100 equally abundant species).

Species diversity takes into account both the number of species AND the evenness of their distribution (relative abundance). It is measured using Simpson's Diversity Index:

D = 1 − Σ(n/N)2

Where n = number of individuals of each species, N = total number of all individuals. Values range from 0 (no diversity — only one species) to approaching 1 (high diversity — many equally abundant species). A higher D value indicates a more diverse, and generally more stable and resilient, ecosystem.

When calculating Simpson's Index: (1) find n/N for each species, (2) square each value, (3) sum all the squared values, (4) subtract the sum from 1. Common mistake: forgetting to subtract from 1, which gives the dominance index instead. Also note: some versions use D = 1 − (Σn(n−1))/(N(N−1)) for finite populations. Check which formula is given in the exam question.

Biodiversity conservation is critically important. Ecosystem services provided by biodiversity include: pollination of crops (worth billions of pounds annually), water purification, carbon sequestration, soil formation, flood regulation, and natural pest control. Loss of biodiversity (through habitat destruction, pollution, overexploitation, invasive species, and climate change) threatens food security, medicine (many drugs derived from natural products), and ecosystem stability. The Convention on Biological Diversity aims to conserve biodiversity, ensure sustainable use, and share benefits equitably.

2.5 Classification and Taxonomy

Taxonomic Hierarchy

Organisms are classified into nested groups (taxa) based on shared characteristics. Classification helps to organise biological knowledge, predict characteristics of poorly studied species, and reflect evolutionary relationships. The hierarchy (from largest/most inclusive to smallest/most specific) is:

Domain → Kingdom → Phylum → Class → Order → Family → Genus → Species

The binomial naming system (Linnaeus) gives each species a two-part Latin name: Genus species (e.g., Homo sapiens, Escherichia coli). The genus is capitalised, the species is not, and both are italicised (or underlined if handwritten). This system provides a universal, unambiguous name for every species.

Three Domain System (Carl Woese, 1990)

Based on analysis of ribosomal RNA (rRNA) gene sequences, Woese proposed dividing life into three domains — a level above kingdom:

Phylogenetics and Molecular Evidence

Modern classification increasingly uses molecular phylogenetics — comparing DNA base sequences, amino acid sequences, or rRNA sequences between organisms. The principle is that organisms that share more similar sequences diverged more recently from a common ancestor. This approach:

  • Provides quantitative, objective data (unlike morphological classification which can be subjective)
  • Can compare organisms that look very different morphologically but are closely related genetically (and vice versa)
  • Led to the three-domain system (Archaea were previously grouped with Bacteria)
  • Revealed that classification based on physical appearance alone can be misleading (e.g., convergent evolution produces similar features in unrelated organisms)
  • Allows construction of phylogenetic trees (cladograms) showing evolutionary relationships. On these trees, branch points (nodes) represent speciation events, and species that share a more recent common ancestor are more closely related
When interpreting phylogenetic trees: the closer two species are on the tree (i.e., the more recently they share a common ancestor), the more closely related they are. The length of branches on some trees represents evolutionary time or the degree of genetic difference. Do NOT assume that species at the "top" of a tree are more "evolved" — all living species are equally evolved; they have all been evolving for the same amount of time since the origin of life.

2.6 Natural Selection and Evolution

Natural selection: The process by which organisms with heritable phenotypic characteristics that are better adapted to their environment are more likely to survive, reproduce, and pass on their advantageous alleles to the next generation. Over time, this leads to changes in allele frequency in a population — this is evolution.

Mechanism of Natural Selection (Darwin's Theory)

  1. Variation exists within a population due to genetic differences (caused by mutation, meiosis, and sexual reproduction). Individuals have different alleles for many genes, producing different phenotypes
  2. Overproduction of offspring — organisms produce more offspring than the environment can support, leading to a struggle for existence (competition for limited resources: food, mates, territory, light)
  3. Selection pressure — environmental factors (predation, disease, competition, climate) act as selective pressures. Individuals with phenotypes better suited to the environment have a selective advantage
  4. Differential survival and reproduction — individuals with advantageous traits are more likely to survive to reproductive age and produce more offspring (survival of the fittest = greatest reproductive success, NOT just physical strength)
  5. Inheritance — the alleles conferring the advantage are passed to the next generation. Over many generations, the frequency of these advantageous alleles increases in the population

Speciation

Speciation is the formation of new, distinct species that are reproductively isolated from each other (they can no longer interbreed to produce fertile offspring).

Allopatric speciation (geographic speciation):

  1. A population is divided by a geographic barrier (mountain range, river, island colonisation, continental drift)
  2. The separated populations experience different selection pressures and genetic drift (random changes in allele frequency, especially important in small populations)
  3. Over time, the populations diverge genetically and develop different adaptations
  4. Eventually, they become so different that they can no longer interbreed even if the barrier is removed — they are now reproductively isolated and constitute separate species

Sympatric speciation (speciation without geographic isolation):

  • Polyploidy (common in plants) — errors in meiosis produce individuals with 3n, 4n or more chromosome sets. They can self-fertilise or breed with other polyploids but not with the diploid parent population. Instant speciation. Examples: bread wheat (hexaploid, 6n = 42); many crop plants are polyploid
  • Behavioural isolation — different courtship behaviours or mating calls prevent interbreeding
  • Temporal isolation — different breeding seasons or times of day
  • Ecological isolation — populations exploit different niches within the same geographic area

Evidence for Evolution

  • Fossil record — shows progressive changes in organisms over geological time; transitional fossils (e.g., Archaeopteryx — dinosaur/bird; Tiktaalik — fish/tetrapod)
  • Comparative anatomy — homologous structures (same evolutionary origin, different function, e.g., pentadactyl limb in whale flipper, bat wing, human arm) indicate common ancestry. Analogous structures (different origin, similar function, e.g., insect wing vs bird wing) indicate convergent evolution
  • Molecular evidence — DNA and protein sequence similarities; more similar sequences = more closely related. Molecular clocks (rate of neutral mutations) estimate divergence times. Universal genetic code and conserved genes (e.g., Hox genes) across all life suggest common origin
  • Biogeography — distribution patterns of species across continents and islands (e.g., Darwin's finches on Galapagos, marsupials in Australia)
  • Observed natural selection — antibiotic resistance in bacteria, pesticide resistance in insects, industrial melanism in peppered moths, changes in beak size in Galapagos finches during droughts
Antibiotic resistance is the most commonly examined example of evolution by natural selection. The correct explanation is: (1) random mutation produces a resistance allele before exposure to the antibiotic; (2) the antibiotic acts as a selection pressure, killing susceptible bacteria; (3) resistant bacteria survive and reproduce; (4) the resistance allele increases in frequency in the population. NEVER say bacteria "develop" or "become" resistant in response to antibiotics — this is Lamarckism (inheritance of acquired characteristics), which is WRONG. Resistance arises from random, pre-existing mutations that are selected for.
Antibiotic resistance is one of the biggest threats to global public health. MRSA (methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus) causes serious hospital-acquired infections. Strategies to combat resistance include: completing prescribed courses of antibiotics, not using antibiotics for viral infections, reducing agricultural antibiotic use, developing new antibiotics and alternative treatments (e.g., bacteriophage therapy), and improved infection control in hospitals. Horizontal gene transfer (conjugation) via plasmids can spread resistance genes between different bacterial species, accelerating the problem.
