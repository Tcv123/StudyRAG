2.2 Cell Division

The Cell Cycle

The cell cycle consists of interphase (G1, S, G2) and mitosis (M phase). Most of the cell's life is spent in interphase, where it grows, replicates DNA (S phase) and prepares for division.

Mitosis

Mitosis: Nuclear division producing two genetically identical daughter nuclei, each with the same number of chromosomes as the parent cell (diploid). Used for growth, repair and asexual reproduction.
StageKey Events
ProphaseChromosomes condense (visible as sister chromatids joined at centromere); centrioles move to poles; nuclear envelope breaks down; spindle fibres form
MetaphaseChromosomes line up at the cell equator (metaphase plate); spindle fibres attach to centromeres
AnaphaseCentromeres split; sister chromatids pulled to opposite poles by shortening spindle fibres
TelophaseChromatids reach poles; nuclear envelopes reform; chromosomes decondense; cytokinesis begins

Meiosis

Meiosis: Two successive nuclear divisions (meiosis I and II) producing four genetically different haploid daughter cells. It introduces genetic variation and halves the chromosome number for sexual reproduction.

Meiosis I (reduction division):

  • Homologous pairs synapse (bivalents form) during prophase I
  • Crossing over occurs between non-sister chromatids at chiasmata
  • Independent assortment — random orientation of bivalents at metaphase I
  • Homologous chromosomes separated (chromosome number halved)

Meiosis II is similar to mitosis: sister chromatids are separated, producing four haploid cells.

Sources of genetic variation in meiosis: (1) crossing over in prophase I, (2) independent assortment in metaphase I, and (3) random fertilisation. Be able to calculate the number of possible chromosome combinations: 2n where n = haploid number.

2.3 Cell Differentiation and Stem Cells

Stem cell: An undifferentiated cell capable of dividing by mitosis to produce either more stem cells (self-renewal) or cells that can differentiate into specialised cell types.

Types of Stem Cells

TypePotencySource
TotipotentCan differentiate into any cell type including extraembryonic tissuesZygote and early embryo (up to ~8 cells)
PluripotentCan differentiate into any cell type except extraembryonic tissuesEmbryonic stem cells (inner cell mass of blastocyst)
MultipotentCan differentiate into a limited range of related cell typesAdult stem cells (e.g., haematopoietic stem cells in bone marrow)

Induced Pluripotent Stem Cells (iPS cells)

Adult somatic cells can be reprogrammed to a pluripotent state by introducing specific transcription factors. This avoids the ethical issues of using embryonic stem cells while potentially allowing patient-specific therapies (reducing rejection risk).

Cell Differentiation

Differentiation involves the expression of specific genes while other genes are switched off. All body cells contain the same genome but only transcribe the genes appropriate for their function. Differentiation is controlled by transcription factors and signalling molecules. Once differentiated, most animal cells cannot de-differentiate (unlike plant cells, which retain totipotency).

2.4 Biodiversity

Biodiversity: The variety of living organisms in an area, measured at three levels: genetic diversity (within species), species diversity (between species) and ecosystem diversity.

Measuring Biodiversity

Species richness is simply the number of different species in an area. However, species diversity takes into account both the number of species AND the evenness of distribution (abundance). It is measured using Simpson's Diversity Index:

D = 1 − Σ(n/N)2

Where n = number of individuals of each species, N = total number of individuals. Values range from 0 (no diversity) to 1 (infinite diversity). A higher value indicates a more stable, diverse ecosystem.

Sampling Techniques

  • Quadrats — placed randomly (using random number tables for coordinates); record frequency, percentage cover or density
  • Transects — line or belt transect to study distribution along an environmental gradient
  • Mark-release-recapture — estimates mobile animal population size using Lincoln Index
Population estimate = (n1 × n2) / nm

Where n1 = number captured first time, n2 = number captured second time, nm = number marked in second sample.

2.5 Classification and Taxonomy

Taxonomic Hierarchy

Organisms are classified into groups based on shared characteristics. The hierarchy (from largest to smallest) is:

Domain → Kingdom → Phylum → Class → Order → Family → Genus → Species

Three Domain System (Woese)

DomainKey FeaturesExamples
BacteriaProkaryotic; peptidoglycan cell walls; 70S ribosomesE. coli, Streptococcus
ArchaeaProkaryotic; no peptidoglycan; unique membrane lipids; extremophilesMethanogens, thermophiles
EukaryaEukaryotic; 80S ribosomes; membrane-bound organellesAnimals, plants, fungi, protists

Phylogenetics and Molecular Evidence

Modern classification increasingly uses molecular phylogenetics — comparing DNA base sequences, amino acid sequences or ribosomal RNA sequences. Organisms with more similar sequences are more closely related (diverged more recently). This evidence led to the reclassification of organisms and the three-domain system.

Phylogenetic trees (cladograms) show evolutionary relationships. The closer two species are on the tree, the more recently they shared a common ancestor. Branch points (nodes) represent speciation events.

2.6 Natural Selection and Evolution

Natural selection: The process by which organisms with heritable characteristics better suited to their environment are more likely to survive and reproduce, passing on their advantageous alleles to the next generation.

Mechanism of Natural Selection

  1. Within a population, there is genetic variation (caused by mutation, meiosis, sexual reproduction)
  2. Organisms produce more offspring than can survive (overproduction)
  3. There is a struggle for existence (competition for resources)
  4. Individuals with advantageous phenotypes have a selective advantage (higher survival and reproduction rate)
  5. These individuals pass on their alleles — allele frequency changes over generations

Types of Selection

TypeEffectExample
Directional selectionFavours one extreme phenotype; shifts meanAntibiotic resistance in bacteria; peppered moth
Stabilising selectionFavours average phenotype; reduces variationHuman birth weight
Disruptive selectionFavours both extremes; can lead to speciationBeak size in seed-eating birds with two seed sizes

Speciation

Allopatric speciation: Geographic isolation separates populations; different selection pressures and genetic drift cause divergence; eventually populations become reproductively isolated (different species).

Sympatric speciation: Speciation without geographic isolation, often through polyploidy (in plants), behavioural isolation or temporal isolation.

Evidence for Evolution

  • Fossil record — shows progressive changes over time
  • Comparative anatomy — homologous structures (same origin, different function)
  • Molecular evidence — DNA/protein sequence similarities; molecular clocks
  • Biogeography — distribution of species on islands and continents
  • Observed selection — antibiotic resistance, pesticide resistance
Antibiotic resistance is a key example of evolution by natural selection.
