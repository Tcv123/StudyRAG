⚗️ CP1: Effect of Caffeine on Daphnia Heart Rate

Apparatus

  • Daphnia (water fleas), cavity slides, cotton wool strands
  • Microscope (low power), pipette
  • Caffeine solutions at range of concentrations (0%, 0.1%, 0.3%, 0.5%)
  • Pond water (for control and recovery), stopwatch

Method

  1. Place a Daphnia on a cavity slide in a small drop of pond water. Use cotton wool strands to restrict movement.
  2. View under low-power microscope. Locate the heart (visible through transparent body).
  3. Count heartbeats for 20 seconds. Multiply by 3 for beats per minute. Repeat 3 times and average.
  4. Add a few drops of caffeine solution to the slide. Wait 2 minutes for it to take effect.
  5. Count heartbeats again (3 counts of 20 s). Record.
  6. Use a fresh Daphnia for each concentration. Return all to pond water after use.

Safety

  • Ethical treatment of Daphnia — minimise time on slide; return to pond water promptly.
  • Use low-power light to reduce heating under microscope.
  • Caffeine solutions are low concentration — low hazard.

Analysis

  • Calculate mean heart rate (bpm) for each caffeine concentration.
  • Plot mean heart rate (y-axis) against caffeine concentration (x-axis).
  • Caffeine is a stimulant that increases heart rate by inhibiting phosphodiesterase.

Sources of Error

ErrorEffectImprovement
Heart beats too fast to countUnderestimate rateTap pencil in time; use dot method on paper
Heat from microscope lampIncreases heart rate independentlyUse LED light; limit time under lamp
Stress from handlingElevated baseline rateAllow 2 min acclimatisation before counting
Same Daphnia reusedCumulative effect of caffeineUse a fresh Daphnia for each concentration
Exam Tip: Discuss ethical considerations: use the minimum number of organisms, use low concentrations of caffeine, allow recovery time, and return to habitat. Explain why a fresh Daphnia is used each time (to avoid cumulative drug effects).

⚗️ CP2: Vitamin C Content (DCPIP Titration)

Apparatus

  • DCPIP solution (0.1%), test tubes
  • Fruit juice samples (fresh, pasteurised, etc.)
  • Graduated pipette or burette, syringe
  • Known concentration of vitamin C (ascorbic acid) for calibration

Method

  1. Add 1 cm³ of DCPIP solution to a test tube.
  2. Using a graduated pipette, add the juice drop by drop, shaking after each addition.
  3. Record the volume of juice needed to decolourise the DCPIP (blue to colourless).
  4. Repeat with each juice sample. Repeat 3 times per sample.
  5. Use known vitamin C concentrations to create a calibration curve.

Safety

  • DCPIP stains skin and clothing — wear gloves and lab coat.
  • Eye protection when handling solutions.

Analysis

  • Less juice needed to decolourise = higher vitamin C content.
  • Use calibration curve (volume of known ascorbic acid vs concentration) to determine vitamin C concentration of unknown samples.
  • Compare different juice types or storage methods.

Sources of Error

ErrorEffectImprovement
Judging end-point colourSubjective; inconsistent resultsUse colorimeter for objective measurement
Juice added too quicklyOvershoot end-pointAdd drop by drop; swirl after each drop
Vitamin C degrades in light/airLower readings than actualTest immediately; keep samples covered
Pulp in juiceObscures colour changeFilter juice before testing
Exam Tip: Vitamin C (ascorbic acid) is an antioxidant that reduces DCPIP. More vitamin C means less volume of juice needed. Always state the end-point: blue to colourless.

⚗️ CP3: Membrane Permeability (Beetroot Temperature)

Apparatus

  • Fresh beetroot, cork borer, scalpel, white tile
  • Test tubes and rack, distilled water
  • Water baths at various temperatures (20–70 °C)
  • Colorimeter and cuvettes, thermometer

Method

  1. Cut uniform beetroot discs with a cork borer. Rinse thoroughly in cold distilled water.
  2. Place one disc in each test tube with 5 cm³ distilled water.
  3. Incubate in water baths at 20, 30, 40, 50, 60, 70 °C for 20 minutes.
  4. Remove beetroot. Measure absorbance of each solution using colorimeter.
  5. Repeat 3 times per temperature. Calculate mean absorbance.

Safety

  • Sharp cutting tools — cut on a white tile away from body.
  • Hot water baths — use tongs for test tubes.

Analysis

  • Plot mean absorbance (y-axis) against temperature (x-axis).
  • Higher temperatures denature membrane proteins and increase phospholipid fluidity, increasing permeability.
  • Sharp increase expected above ~40 °C.

Sources of Error

ErrorEffectImprovement
Unequal disc sizesDifferent surface areasUse same cork borer and cut to same thickness
Insufficient rinsingSurface pigment inflates readingsRinse until water runs clear
Timing inconsistencyDifferent exposure timesUse stopwatch; stagger starts at 1-min intervals
Exam Tip: Explain the increase in permeability in terms of the fluid mosaic model: membrane proteins denature and phospholipid bilayer becomes more fluid at high temperatures, allowing anthocyanin to leak out.

⚗️ CP4: Enzyme Activity (Substrate Concentration)

Apparatus

  • Catalase source (potato discs or liver extract)
  • Hydrogen peroxide at various concentrations (0.5–8%)
  • Conical flask, bung with delivery tube, gas syringe
  • Water bath (25 °C constant), stopwatch

Method

  1. Place catalase source in conical flask. Add H₂O₂ solution and immediately fit bung.
  2. Record volume of O₂ collected every 30 seconds for 5 minutes.
  3. Repeat with each H₂O₂ concentration. Repeat 3 times per concentration.
  4. Calculate initial rate of reaction for each concentration.

Safety

  • H₂O₂ is an irritant — wear eye protection and gloves.
  • Avoid high concentrations of H₂O₂ (use ≤8%).

Analysis

  • Plot volume of O₂ against time for each concentration. Calculate initial rate from tangent.
  • Plot rate against substrate concentration: expect curve that plateaus (Vmax) due to enzyme saturation.

Sources of Error

ErrorEffectImprovement
Gas escapes before bung fittedLower recorded volumePractise fast fitting; use side-arm flask
Variable enzyme amountUnreliable rate comparisonUse same mass/number of potato discs
Temperature variationAffects enzyme activityUse thermostatically controlled water bath
Exam Tip: At low substrate concentrations, rate increases proportionally (first order). At high concentrations, all active sites are occupied and rate plateaus (zero order). This is Vmax — where every enzyme molecule is forming an enzyme-substrate complex.

⚗️ CP5: Dissection of Gas Exchange Surfaces

Apparatus

  • Fresh fish head, dissecting board, pins
  • Scissors, forceps, scalpel, mounted needle
  • Magnifying glass or dissecting microscope
  • Gloves, eye protection

Method

  1. Place fish head on board. Lift the operculum to expose the gills.
  2. Cut away the operculum with scissors.
  3. Identify and examine the 4 gill arches. Remove one arch carefully.
  4. Place in water and examine under magnification. Identify filaments and lamellae.
  5. Produce a labelled biological drawing. Relate structures to gas exchange function.

Safety

  • Wear gloves when handling biological specimens.
  • Use sharp instruments carefully; cut away from body.
  • Dispose of tissue in designated biological waste.

Analysis

  • Identify adaptations: large surface area (many filaments and lamellae), thin epithelium, rich blood supply, counter-current flow.
  • Produce a clear biological drawing with labels and annotations linking structure to function.

Sources of Error

ErrorEffectImprovement
Structures damaged during cuttingHard to identify featuresUse fine scissors and gentle technique
Tissue dries outStructures shrinkKeep specimen moist with water
Exam Tip: In any dissection question, always link structure to function. For gills: large surface area, thin walls, rich blood supply, and counter-current mechanism all maximise the rate of diffusion of gases.

⚗️ CP6: Rate of Water Uptake (Potometer)

Apparatus

  • Potometer (bubble or mass type), leafy plant shoot
  • Rubber tubing, clamp stand, capillary tube with scale
  • Water reservoir, stopwatch
  • Fan, lamp, plastic bags (to vary conditions)

Method

  1. Cut the plant shoot underwater to prevent air entering the xylem. Assemble potometer underwater or under running water.
  2. Seal all joints with Vaseline. Allow to equilibrate for 5 minutes.
  3. Introduce an air bubble by briefly lifting the capillary tube end out of water.
  4. Record the distance the air bubble moves along the capillary tube every minute.
  5. Repeat under different conditions: with a fan (windy), with a lamp (increased light), in a plastic bag (increased humidity).
  6. Reset the bubble using the reservoir tap between readings.

Safety

  • Use care with sharp cutting tools when preparing the shoot.
  • Ensure electrical equipment (fan, lamp) is kept away from water.

Analysis

  • Rate of water uptake = distance moved by bubble / time.
  • This measures water uptake, which is approximately equal to transpiration rate.
  • Windy / warm / bright conditions increase transpiration rate; humid conditions decrease it.

Sources of Error

ErrorEffectImprovement
Air lock in apparatusBubble does not moveAssemble underwater; check all seals
Shoot not cut underwaterAir enters xylem, blocking water flowAlways cut stem under water
Leaks at jointsWater loss not from transpirationApply Vaseline to all connections
Temperature fluctuationsAffects gas expansion, not just transpirationControl temperature; equilibrate first
Exam Tip: A potometer measures water uptake, not transpiration directly. Some water is used for photosynthesis and cell expansion, but the vast majority is lost by transpiration so it is a good estimate. Always state this distinction.

⚗️ CP7: Antimicrobial Substances (Zones of Inhibition)

Apparatus

  • Pre-poured nutrient agar plates, bacterial broth culture
  • Antibiotic multidiscs or individual discs / plant extract discs
  • Sterile spreader, Bunsen burner, forceps
  • Marker pen, adhesive tape, ruler

Method

  1. Work near a Bunsen flame. Spread bacteria evenly across agar using sterile spreader.
  2. Place antibiotic/antimicrobial discs using flamed forceps. Space evenly.
  3. Seal with two pieces of tape (not fully sealed). Label. Incubate upside down at ≤25 °C.
  4. After 24–48 hours, measure the diameter of each zone of inhibition.

Safety

  • Maximum 25 °C incubation in schools.
  • Do not open plates after incubation. Autoclave before disposal.
  • Full aseptic technique to prevent contamination.

Analysis

  • Calculate zone area: A = πr² (use radius = diameter / 2).
  • Larger area indicates more effective antimicrobial agent.

Sources of Error

ErrorEffectImprovement
ContaminationExtra colonies obscure zonesStrict aseptic technique
Uneven bacterial lawnInconsistent zonesSpread evenly; use sterile spreader
Discs not in contact with agarAntibiotic does not diffusePress gently with forceps
Exam Tip: Always calculate πr² for the zone area. Larger zone = more effective antibiotic. Explain why ≤25 °C is used in schools: to prevent growth of human pathogens that thrive at 37 °C.

⚗️ CP8: Factors Affecting Photosynthesis Rate

Apparatus

  • Elodea (pondweed), beaker, sodium hydrogen carbonate solution
  • Bench lamp (variable distance), ruler
  • Stopwatch, thermometer
  • Optional: gas syringe for more accurate O₂ measurement

Method

  1. Place Elodea in a beaker of NaHCO₃ solution (provides CO₂). Cut the stem at an angle.
  2. Position a lamp at a set distance. Allow 2 minutes to equilibrate.
  3. Count the number of bubbles released per minute (repeat 3 times).
  4. Move the lamp to different distances (e.g. 5, 10, 15, 20, 25 cm).
  5. Record bubble count at each distance. Light intensity ∝ 1/d².

Safety

  • Do not let the lamp get wet — electrical hazard.
  • Place a heat shield (beaker of water) between lamp and plant to reduce heating.

Analysis

  • Plot rate of bubble production against 1/d² (light intensity).
  • Initially rate increases proportionally (light is the limiting factor), then plateaus (another factor becomes limiting, e.g. CO₂ or temperature).

Sources of Error

ErrorEffectImprovement
Bubbles vary in sizeInaccurate measure of O₂ volumeUse a gas syringe to measure volume directly
Heat from lampTemperature increases at close distancesUse heat shield; monitor temperature
Background lightAdditional photosynthesis at all distancesWork in darkened room
Air bubbles not O₂Overestimate rateEquilibrate plant first; discard first 2 min
Exam Tip: Light intensity is inversely proportional to distance squared: I ∝ 1/d². NaHCO₃ is added to provide a constant supply of CO₂ so that it does not become a limiting factor. Always state this.

⚗️ CP9: Factors Affecting Respiration Rate

Apparatus

  • Simple respirometer, germinating seeds (e.g. mung beans)
  • Glass beads (for control), soda lime, cotton wool
  • Capillary tube with coloured fluid, scale
  • Water bath, thermometer, stopwatch

Method

  1. Place germinating seeds on soda lime (separated by cotton wool) in one tube. Place equal volume of glass beads on soda lime in the control.
  2. Seal and connect to capillary tube. Place in water bath. Equilibrate for 5 minutes.
  3. Introduce coloured fluid drop. Record distance moved per minute for 10 minutes.
  4. Repeat at different temperatures.

Safety

  • Soda lime is corrosive — wear gloves and eye protection.
  • Do not allow soda lime to contact organisms.

Analysis

  • Rate = distance moved / time.
  • Plot rate against temperature. Expect Q₂₀ effect (rate roughly doubles for every 10 °C rise) up to a point, then decreases as enzymes denature.

Sources of Error

ErrorEffectImprovement
Leaky apparatusNo movement of fluidSeal with Vaseline; check connections
Seeds at different stagesVariable respiration ratesUse seeds germinated for same time
No control tubeCannot account for pressure changesAlways include glass bead control
Exam Tip: The soda lime absorbs CO₂ so that only O₂ uptake causes the volume change. The glass bead control accounts for changes in atmospheric pressure and temperature. State Q₂₀ = rate at (T+10) / rate at T.

⚗️ CP10: Effects of Exercise on Ventilation

Apparatus

  • Spirometer or peak flow meter, clean mouthpieces
  • Stopwatch, step or exercise bike
  • Nose clip (if using spirometer)

Method

  1. Measure resting breathing rate (breaths per minute) and tidal volume (using spirometer or peak flow meter).
  2. Subject performs exercise (e.g. step-ups for 5 minutes).
  3. Immediately after, measure breathing rate and tidal volume.
  4. Record at intervals during recovery (every minute for 5 minutes).
  5. Calculate minute ventilation = tidal volume × breathing rate.

Safety

  • Screen participants for respiratory or cardiac conditions.
  • Use clean, individual mouthpieces (hygiene).
  • Stop exercise if participant feels dizzy or unwell.

Analysis

  • Plot breathing rate and tidal volume before, during, and after exercise.
  • Both increase during exercise to deliver more O₂ and remove more CO₂.
  • Recovery time to baseline can be measured.

Sources of Error

ErrorEffectImprovement
Inconsistent exercise intensityVariable resultsUse metronome for step-ups; standardise duration
Nose not clipped during spirometryAir escapes through noseUse nose clip
Individual fitness variationDifficult to compare between subjectsCompare within the same individual; use large sample
Exam Tip: Minute ventilation = tidal volume × breathing rate. During exercise, CO₂ concentration rises, which is detected by chemoreceptors in the medulla and carotid/aortic bodies, triggering increased ventilation.

⚗️ CP11: Effect of Gibberellin on Germination

Apparatus

  • Seeds (e.g. wheat or barley), Petri dishes, cotton wool
  • Gibberellic acid (GA₃) solution at various concentrations
  • Distilled water (control), ruler, labels

Method

  1. Place 10 seeds on damp cotton wool in each Petri dish.
  2. Add water to control dishes and GA₃ solution to treatment dishes.
  3. Incubate in the same conditions (temperature, light).
  4. Record germination percentage and shoot/stem length daily for 7–14 days.
  5. Use multiple concentrations of GA₃ (e.g. 0, 10, 100, 1000 ppm).

Safety

  • GA₃ is low hazard at concentrations used. Wash hands after handling.
  • Avoid inhaling seed dust.

Analysis

  • Plot germination % and mean shoot length against time for each treatment.
  • Gibberellin stimulates production of amylase in the aleurone layer, which breaks down starch into maltose for the embryo.

Sources of Error

ErrorEffectImprovement
Variable seed qualityDifferent germination ratesUse seeds from same batch
Unequal volumes of solutionDifferent water availabilityMeasure exact volumes with pipette
Temperature variationAffects germination rateKeep all dishes in same incubator
Exam Tip: Gibberellin (GA₃) stimulates the aleurone layer of seeds to produce α-amylase, which hydrolyses starch into maltose. This provides the energy source for the growing embryo. This is a key example of a plant growth regulator.

⚗️ CP12: Tensile Strength of Plant Fibres

Apparatus

  • Plant fibres (e.g. celery strings, nettle fibres, sclerenchyma)
  • Clamp stand, boss, clamp
  • Masses (10 g, 20 g, 50 g), hook or S-clip
  • Ruler, micrometer or microscope for measuring diameter

Method

  1. Extract a single fibre from the plant. Measure its diameter using a micrometer or microscope with graticule.
  2. Clamp one end securely in the clamp stand. Attach a hook to the lower end.
  3. Add masses one at a time (e.g. 10 g increments). Record each addition.
  4. Continue until the fibre snaps. Record the total mass at the point of breaking.
  5. Repeat for at least 10 fibres. Calculate mean breaking force.
  6. Calculate tensile stress = force (N) / cross-sectional area (m²).

Safety

  • Place a cushion or container below the masses in case they fall.
  • Wear eye protection in case fibre snaps suddenly.

Analysis

  • Convert mass to force: F = m × g (9.81 N/kg).
  • Calculate cross-sectional area: A = πr².
  • Tensile stress = F / A. Higher stress = stronger fibre.
  • Compare fibres from different species or different parts of the same plant.

Sources of Error

ErrorEffectImprovement
Fibre damaged during extractionWeakened; breaks soonerExtract carefully with forceps
Variable fibre diameterInconsistent stress calculationsMeasure diameter at multiple points; take mean
Fibre slips from clampApparent break at lower forceWrap fibre around clamp securely
Small sample sizeUnreliable meanTest at least 10 fibres per type
Exam Tip: Tensile strength is due to cellulose microfibrils in the cell wall, which are arranged in parallel and cross-linked. Sclerenchyma fibres are particularly strong due to secondary thickening with lignin.

⚗️ CP13: DNA Extraction from a Plant

Apparatus

  • Strawberries (or kiwi, onion, peas), zip-lock bag
  • Extraction buffer: salt + washing-up liquid + warm water
  • Muslin cloth or tea strainer, beaker, test tube
  • Ice-cold ethanol (>95%), glass rod

Method

  1. Mash strawberries in a zip-lock bag with extraction buffer (warm water + salt + detergent) for 3 minutes.
  2. Filter through muslin cloth into a beaker. Transfer filtrate to a test tube (one-third full).
  3. Gently layer ice-cold ethanol down the side of the test tube (equal volume).
  4. Wait 2–3 minutes. White, stringy DNA precipitates at the boundary.
  5. Spool the DNA onto a glass rod by gently twisting.

Safety

  • Ethanol is flammable — no naked flames nearby.
  • Detergent is an irritant — wash hands if contact occurs.

Analysis

  • The detergent dissolves cell and nuclear membranes (phospholipid bilayer).
  • Salt causes DNA to clump (neutralises negative charges on phosphate groups).
  • DNA precipitates in cold ethanol because it is insoluble in alcohol.

Sources of Error

ErrorEffectImprovement
Ethanol not cold enoughLess DNA precipitatesKeep ethanol in freezer before use
Mixing layers too vigorouslyDNA fragments and is hard to spoolLayer gently; do not shake
Insufficient mashingFewer cells lysed, less DNAMash thoroughly for 3+ minutes
Exam Tip: Know the role of each chemical: detergent lyses membranes, salt makes DNA precipitate by neutralising the phosphate charges, and cold ethanol causes the DNA to come out of solution. Strawberries are often used because they are octoploid (8n) so contain lots of DNA.

⚗️ CP14: Effect of Temperature on Enzyme Rate

Apparatus

  • Enzyme (e.g. amylase or catalase), substrate (starch or H₂O₂)
  • Water baths at 20, 30, 40, 50, 60 °C
  • Test tubes, stopwatch, thermometer
  • Iodine solution (for amylase) or gas syringe (for catalase)

Method

  1. Place enzyme and substrate in separate test tubes in a water bath. Equilibrate for 5 minutes.
  2. Mix enzyme and substrate. Start the stopwatch.
  3. For amylase: sample every 30 s into iodine on a spotting tile. Record time for iodine to stop turning blue-black (starch fully broken down).
  4. For catalase: measure volume of O₂ collected in the first 60 seconds.
  5. Repeat at each temperature (3 repeats per temperature). Calculate initial rate.

Safety

  • Care with hot water baths. Use tongs for test tubes.
  • Iodine stains skin — wear gloves.

Analysis

  • Rate = 1/time (for amylase) or volume/time (for catalase).
  • Plot rate against temperature: expect a bell-shaped curve.
  • Optimum temperature is the peak. Above this, enzyme denatures (active site changes shape permanently).

Sources of Error

ErrorEffectImprovement
Not equilibrating firstTemperature not at set value when mixedEquilibrate both tubes for 5 min before mixing
Delay between mixing and timingMisses initial reactionStart stopwatch immediately on mixing
Subjective colour judgementInconsistent end-pointUse colorimeter for objective measurement
Exam Tip: Always equilibrate enzyme and substrate separately at the target temperature before mixing. This ensures the reaction starts at the correct temperature. Use rate = 1/t to calculate initial rate from time-based experiments.

⚗️ CP15: Populations in a Habitat

Apparatus

  • Quadrats, tape measure, random number table
  • Pitfall traps or sweep nets (for mark-release-recapture)
  • Non-toxic marker (paint or correction fluid for marking animals)
  • Identification keys, recording sheets, calculator

Method

  1. Quadrat sampling: Use random coordinates to place quadrats. Count species in each quadrat. Calculate mean density and % cover.
  2. Mark-release-recapture: Capture a sample (M), mark them (e.g. dot of paint), release. After time, recapture a second sample (n). Count marked individuals (m). Estimate population: N = Mn/m.
  3. Calculate Simpson’s Diversity Index for the habitat.

Safety

  • Fieldwork risk assessment (weather, terrain, allergies).
  • Handle organisms gently; return to habitat.
  • Wash hands after soil or animal contact.

Analysis

  • Lincoln index assumptions: marks do not affect survival or predation, population is closed (no migration), enough time between samples for mixing.
  • Simpson’s D = 1 − Σ(n/N)². Higher D = greater diversity.

Sources of Error

ErrorEffectImprovement
Marks wear offUnderestimates recaptured marked (m), overestimates NUse permanent, non-toxic markers
Marked animals more visible to predatorsFewer marked in recapture; overestimates NUse small, inconspicuous marks
Immigration/emigrationPopulation not closed; estimate inaccurateSample within short time frame
Non-random samplingBiased species countsUse random number table for coordinates
Exam Tip: The Lincoln index formula is N = Mn/m. Always state the assumptions: no births/deaths/migration between samples, marks do not affect survival, and there is random mixing. State the formula for Simpson’s D and know that higher values mean greater biodiversity.

⚗️ CP16: Environmental Factor on Plant Growth

Apparatus

  • Seedlings (e.g. cress or oat coleoptiles), pots with soil or cotton wool
  • Cardboard box with hole (for unidirectional light) or clinostat
  • Light source, ruler, protractor
  • Labels, camera (for recording curvature)

Method

  1. Grow seedlings in identical conditions until a few cm tall.
  2. Phototropism: Place seedlings in a box with a single light source from one side. Record the angle and direction of bending daily.
  3. Gravitropism: Place a seedling on its side. Record the direction of root and shoot growth.
  4. Control: clinostat (rotating slowly) to eliminate gravity as a directional stimulus.
  5. Measure shoot length and angle of curvature. Repeat with multiple seedlings.

Safety

  • Wash hands after handling soil or compost.
  • Keep electrical equipment away from water.

Analysis

  • Positive phototropism: shoots grow towards light. Auxin redistributes to the shaded side, causing greater elongation.
  • Positive gravitropism: roots grow downwards. Auxin accumulates on the lower side — inhibits root cell elongation, stimulates shoot cell elongation.
  • Plot mean angle of curvature against time.

Sources of Error

ErrorEffectImprovement
Light leaking into boxNot truly unidirectionalSeal box thoroughly; check for gaps
Variable seedling agesDifferent growth responsesUse seedlings germinated at the same time
Temperature differencesAffects growth rateKeep all conditions at same temperature
Measuring angle subjectivelyInaccurate dataUse protractor; take photographs for comparison
Exam Tip: Auxin (IAA) is produced in the shoot tip and moves to the shaded side. It stimulates cell elongation in shoots (positive phototropism) but inhibits elongation in roots (positive gravitropism). The clinostat eliminates gravity as a variable by continuous slow rotation.
