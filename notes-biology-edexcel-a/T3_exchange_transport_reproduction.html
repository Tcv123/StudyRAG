3.1 Gas Exchange Surfaces

Surface area to volume ratio (SA:V): As organisms increase in size, their SA:V ratio decreases, meaning simple diffusion across the body surface is insufficient. Larger organisms require specialised gas exchange surfaces and transport systems. A cube of side length 1 cm has a SA:V of 6:1, but a cube of side 6 cm has a SA:V of only 1:1.

All gas exchange surfaces share common features that maximise the rate of diffusion according to Fick's law:

Rate of diffusion ∝ (surface area × concentration difference) / thickness of exchange surface

These features are:

  • Large surface area — increases the area over which diffusion can occur
  • Thin barrier (short diffusion distance) — often just one or two cells thick
  • Good blood supply / ventilation — maintains a steep concentration gradient
  • Moist surface — gases dissolve before diffusing across respiratory surfaces

Gas Exchange in Single-Celled Organisms

Organisms such as Amoeba have a sufficiently large SA:V ratio that gases (O2 and CO2) can diffuse directly across the cell surface membrane. No specialised structures are needed. The short diffusion distance means the entire cell can be supplied by diffusion alone.

Gas Exchange in Insects

Insects use a tracheal system. Air enters through spiracles (openings on the body surface that can open and close to reduce water loss) and passes through a network of tracheae (reinforced with chitin rings to prevent collapse). These branch into smaller tracheoles that penetrate tissues directly, delivering O2 to respiring cells by diffusion.

  • No respiratory pigment needed — gas delivered directly to cells
  • Ventilation by abdominal pumping (muscular contractions of the abdomen) and flight muscle contraction
  • Water at ends of tracheoles is drawn back into cells during exercise (due to increased osmotic pressure from lactate accumulation), increasing the gas exchange surface area in direct contact with cells
  • Spiracles can close via valve-like structures to reduce water loss — a critical adaptation for terrestrial life

Gas Exchange in Fish

Fish use gills with a countercurrent flow mechanism. Water flows over the gill lamellae in the opposite direction to blood flow. This maintains a concentration gradient along the entire length of the lamella, achieving approximately 80% oxygen extraction (far more efficient than parallel flow, which would only achieve ~50%).

  • Each gill arch has many gill filaments (primary lamellae)
  • Each filament has many secondary lamellae with thin epithelium (one cell thick, ~2–5 μm) and dense capillary network
  • Water enters through mouth, passes over gills, exits through operculum (bony fish) — this is a unidirectional ventilation mechanism
  • The operculum creates a buccal-opercular pump: lowering the floor of the mouth draws water in; closing the mouth and raising the floor forces water over gills; the operculum movement creates a pressure differential for continuous flow
In countercurrent flow diagrams, always show that a diffusion gradient is maintained at EVERY point along the lamella. In parallel flow, equilibrium would be reached halfway along, and no further exchange would occur. Label blood and water flow directions with arrows and show partial pressure values at each end.

Gas Exchange in the Mammalian Lung

The lungs provide a large surface area (~70 m2 — roughly half a tennis court) through approximately 480 million alveoli. Each alveolus has walls one cell thick (squamous epithelium), surrounded by a dense capillary network. Ventilation (breathing) maintains a steep concentration gradient for O2 and CO2.

Adaptations of the Alveoli

  • Enormous total surface area (~70 m2) due to huge number of alveoli
  • Very thin walls — squamous epithelium of alveolus + capillary endothelium = total diffusion distance of only ~0.5 μm
  • Dense capillary network around each alveolus — maintains steep concentration gradient as blood is constantly flowing
  • Surfactant — secreted by type II pneumocytes, reduces surface tension and prevents alveoli from collapsing during expiration
  • Elastic fibres in alveolar walls — allow stretch during inspiration and recoil during expiration (passive process)
  • Ventilation — continuous supply of fresh air maintains a steep O2 gradient (high in alveolus, low in blood) and CO2 gradient (high in blood, low in alveolus)
When explaining why the lungs are efficient at gas exchange, always refer to Fick's law and explain each adaptation in terms of which factor it optimises: large SA (increases area), thin barrier (decreases distance), ventilation and blood flow (maintains concentration gradient).

3.2 Transport in Plants

Xylem — Water Transport

Transpiration: The loss of water vapour from the aerial parts of a plant, primarily through stomata on leaf surfaces. This creates a transpiration pull (tension) that draws water up through the xylem. Transpiration is an inevitable consequence of gas exchange — stomata must open for CO2 uptake in photosynthesis, but water vapour is also lost.

Water movement from roots to leaves involves:

  1. Root uptake: Water enters root hair cells by osmosis (soil water has higher Ψ than root cells). Root hair cells have a large surface area due to their extensions into the soil.
  2. Movement across root:
    • Apoplast pathway — water moves through cell walls and intercellular spaces (no membranes crossed, fastest route, accounts for most water movement)
    • Symplast pathway — water moves through cytoplasm via plasmodesmata (crosses membranes, slower)
    • Vacuolar pathway — water moves from vacuole to vacuole by osmosis (slowest)
  3. Casparian strip: A waterproof band of suberin in the endodermis cell walls blocks the apoplast pathway, forcing water into the symplast. This gives the plant control over which minerals enter the xylem. Endodermis cells actively transport mineral ions into the xylem, lowering Ψ and drawing water in by osmosis.
  4. Cohesion-tension theory:
    • Transpiration creates negative pressure (tension) at the top of the plant
    • Cohesion between water molecules (hydrogen bonds) pulls a continuous column of water upward through the xylem
    • Adhesion of water molecules to lignified xylem walls helps prevent the column from breaking
    • This generates a transpiration stream from roots to leaves

Xylem Structure

Xylem vessels are dead, hollow tubes with lignified walls. They have no end walls (continuous lumen), no cytoplasm (no resistance to flow) and lignin provides strength, waterproofing, and prevents the vessel from collapsing under tension. Bordered pits allow lateral water movement between adjacent vessels. Xylem also provides structural support to the plant — wood is largely composed of secondary xylem.

Stomata and Guard Cells

Stomata (singular: stoma) are pores in the leaf epidermis, mainly on the lower surface (reduces water loss from direct sunlight). Each stoma is flanked by two guard cells that control opening and closing:

  • Opening: In light, guard cells photosynthesise and actively pump K+ ions in → water potential decreases → water enters by osmosis → guard cells become turgid → because of uneven thickening (inner wall thicker), they curve apart, opening the pore
  • Closing: In darkness or when water-stressed, K+ ions leave → water follows by osmosis → guard cells become flaccid → pore closes
  • Abscisic acid (ABA) — produced during water stress, causes rapid stomatal closure by promoting K+ efflux from guard cells

Xerophyte Adaptations

Plants adapted to dry environments (xerophytes) have evolved features to reduce transpiration:

  • Thick waxy cuticle — reduces evaporation from the leaf surface
  • Sunken stomata — creates a pocket of humid air, reducing the water potential gradient (e.g., marram grass)
  • Rolled leaves — traps moist air inside, reducing water loss from the inner surface
  • Reduced leaf surface area — needles (conifers) or spines (cacti) reduce the area for transpiration
  • Dense hairs (trichomes) — trap a layer of still, moist air near the leaf surface
  • CAM metabolism — stomata open at night when it is cooler and more humid (e.g., cacti, pineapple)

A potometer measures the rate of water uptake by a cut shoot (approximation of transpiration rate, since ~99% of water absorbed is lost through transpiration). A bubble potometer tracks movement of an air-water meniscus along a calibrated capillary tube.

A potometer measures water uptake, NOT transpiration directly. A small amount of water taken up is used in photosynthesis and to keep cells turgid, but this is negligible. Always state this caveat in exam answers. When setting up, ensure the shoot is cut underwater to prevent air locks in the xylem.

Phloem — Translocation

Translocation: The transport of dissolved organic solutes (mainly sucrose and amino acids) through phloem sieve tubes from sources (where produced, e.g., photosynthesising leaves, germinating seeds, storage organs releasing stores) to sinks (where used or stored, e.g., roots, growing points, developing fruits, storage organs receiving stores). Note that the same organ can be a source or sink at different times — a potato tuber is a sink when growing but a source when sprouting.

Mass Flow Hypothesis (Munch)

  1. At the source, sucrose is actively loaded into sieve tube elements via companion cells. Companion cells use ATP to pump H+ out by active transport, creating a proton gradient. Sucrose is then co-transported into the companion cell (and then into sieve tube via plasmodesmata) using this proton gradient.
  2. This lowers water potential (Ψ) in the sieve tube; water enters by osmosis from nearby xylem
  3. Hydrostatic pressure increases at the source end
  4. At the sink, sucrose is actively removed from sieve tubes (used in respiration, converted to starch for storage, used for cellulose synthesis in growing cells, etc.)
  5. Water potential rises in sieve tube at sink; water leaves by osmosis (often returning to xylem)
  6. Pressure difference between source (high pressure) and sink (low pressure) drives mass flow of phloem sap along the pressure gradient

Phloem Structure

Sieve tube elements are living cells with no nucleus, very little cytoplasm (reduced organelles to minimise resistance to flow), and sieve plates (perforated end walls) to allow flow of phloem sap. Companion cells are metabolically active with many mitochondria, a dense cytoplasm, and a nucleus; they load sucrose into sieve tubes via the proton co-transport mechanism and support sieve tube function through plasmodesmata connections.

Evidence For and Against Mass Flow

Evidence for mass flow: aphid stylet experiments show sieve tubes are under pressure; radioactive tracers show directional movement; ringing experiments cause sugar accumulation above the ring. Always explain the evidence, not just list it.

3.3 Transport in Animals

Why Do Mammals Need a Transport System?

Mammals are large, active organisms with a high metabolic rate. Their small SA:V ratio means diffusion alone is insufficient to supply all cells with oxygen and nutrients or remove waste products quickly enough. A closed circulatory system with a pump (heart) and blood vessels provides rapid bulk transport to all tissues.

Double Circulatory System

Mammals have a double circulatory system: blood passes through the heart twice per circuit:

  • Pulmonary circulation: heart → lungs → heart (deoxygenated blood is oxygenated)
  • Systemic circulation: heart → body → heart (oxygenated blood delivers O2 to tissues)

This maintains high blood pressure in the systemic circuit (blood pressure drops as blood passes through the lung capillaries, so returning it to the heart for a second pump ensures adequate pressure for efficient delivery of oxygen and nutrients to body tissues). It also allows pulmonary and systemic pressures to be maintained independently.

Heart Structure and Function

The heart has four chambers: right and left atria (receive blood) and right and left ventricles (pump blood out). Key features:

  • Septum separates oxygenated and deoxygenated blood, preventing mixing
  • Atrioventricular valves (tricuspid on right, bicuspid/mitral on left) prevent backflow from ventricles to atria — they are anchored by chordae tendinae ("heart strings") to prevent them inverting
  • Semilunar valves in the aorta and pulmonary artery prevent backflow into ventricles during diastole
  • Left ventricle wall is 2–3 times thicker than right ventricle wall (generates higher pressure for systemic circulation to the entire body; right ventricle only pumps to the nearby lungs)
  • Coronary arteries branch from the aorta and supply heart muscle (myocardium) with oxygenated blood — blockage causes myocardial infarction (heart attack)

The Cardiac Cycle

The cardiac cycle describes the events of one complete heartbeat, lasting approximately 0.8 seconds at rest (75 bpm). It has three phases:

  1. Atrial systole (~0.1 s): Atria contract, pushing remaining blood into ventricles (ventricles are already ~80% filled passively). Atrioventricular (AV) valves are open; semilunar valves are closed.
  2. Ventricular systole (~0.3 s): Ventricles contract from the apex upward. Pressure in ventricles rises above pressure in atria → AV valves snap shut (first heart sound, "lub"). Pressure rises above pressure in arteries → semilunar valves open → blood ejected into pulmonary artery and aorta.
  3. Diastole (~0.4 s): All chambers relax. Ventricular pressure drops below arterial pressure → semilunar valves close (second heart sound, "dub"). Blood flows from veins into atria passively. Ventricles fill passively through open AV valves.

Cardiac Conduction System

The heart is myogenic (generates its own rhythmic contractions without external nervous stimulation):

  1. SAN (sinoatrial node) in the right atrium wall initiates the impulse — this is the natural pacemaker, setting the heart rate at ~60–100 bpm
  2. Wave of depolarisation (electrical impulse) spreads across both atria via gap junctions in the intercalated discs → atrial systole
  3. AVN (atrioventricular node) at the junction of atria and ventricles delays the impulse for ~0.1 s, allowing atria to fully empty before ventricles contract
  4. Impulse passes down the bundle of His (atrioventricular bundle) in the interventricular septum
  5. Purkyne fibres (Purkinje fibres) spread the impulse from the apex upward through the ventricular walls → ventricular systole (contraction from bottom up ensures blood is squeezed upward into the arteries)

Haemoglobin and Oxygen Transport

Haemoglobin: A quaternary protein (globular, conjugated) with four polypeptide subunits (2 alpha, 2 beta chains), each containing a prosthetic haem group with an iron (Fe2+) ion that reversibly binds one molecule of oxygen. Each haemoglobin can carry up to four O2 molecules, forming oxyhaemoglobin: Hb + 4O2 ↔ HbO8 (Hb(O2)4).

The oxygen dissociation curve is sigmoid (S-shaped) due to cooperative binding: when the first O2 binds, it causes a conformational change in the haemoglobin that makes it easier for subsequent O2 molecules to bind (positive cooperativity). Conversely, when one O2 is released, the others are released more readily. This produces a very efficient loading and unloading pattern:

  • At the lungs (high pO2, ~13 kPa): haemoglobin is ~98% saturated (plateau region)
  • At resting tissues (lower pO2, ~5 kPa): haemoglobin is ~75% saturated — releases ~25% of its O2
  • At active tissues (very low pO2, ~2 kPa): haemoglobin may be only ~25% saturated — releases ~75% of its O2

The Bohr Effect

In actively respiring tissues, CO2 concentration is high. CO2 lowers blood pH (combines with water to form carbonic acid, catalysed by carbonic anhydrase in red blood cells). Lower pH (higher H+ concentration) causes haemoglobin to have a lower affinity for oxygen, so it releases O2 more readily to the respiring tissues that need it most. This shifts the dissociation curve to the right — the Bohr shift.

CO2 + H2O ↔ H2CO3 ↔ H+ + HCO3− (carbonic anhydrase in RBCs catalyses the first step)

The bicarbonate ions (HCO3−) diffuse out of the red blood cell into the plasma (the main way CO2 is transported in blood, ~85%). Chloride ions (Cl−) move into the RBC to maintain electrical neutrality — this is the chloride shift.

Fetal Haemoglobin

Fetal haemoglobin (HbF) has a higher affinity for oxygen than adult haemoglobin (HbA) because it has a different quaternary structure (two gamma chains instead of two beta chains). Its dissociation curve is to the left of adult haemoglobin. This ensures oxygen transfers from maternal blood to fetal blood across the placenta, where the partial pressure of O2 is relatively low. At any given pO2, fetal Hb will be more saturated than maternal Hb, enabling O2 to pass from mother to fetus.

Other Respiratory Pigments

  • Myoglobin — found in muscle tissue; has a very high affinity for O2 (curve far to the left); acts as an oxygen store, only releasing O2 when muscle pO2 drops very low during intense exercise. Gives red meat its colour.
  • Haemoglobin in organisms at high altitude (e.g., llamas) or in low-O2 environments has a higher O2 affinity (curve shifted left), enabling efficient O2 loading despite lower atmospheric pO2.
When interpreting dissociation curves: a shift LEFT means higher affinity (picks up O2 more easily, e.g., fetal Hb, myoglobin, high altitude species). A shift RIGHT means lower affinity (releases O2 more easily, e.g., Bohr effect in active tissues). Always label your axes correctly: x-axis = partial pressure of O2 (pO2), y-axis = percentage saturation of haemoglobin.

Tissue Fluid Formation and Return

Tissue fluid is formed at the arterial end of capillary beds and returned at the venous end:

  1. Arterial end: Hydrostatic pressure of blood (from ventricular contraction) forces water, dissolved gases, glucose, amino acids, and ions out through gaps between capillary endothelial cells. Plasma proteins and blood cells are too large to pass through — this is ultrafiltration.
  2. Tissue fluid bathes the cells, supplying nutrients and removing waste by diffusion.
  3. Venous end: Hydrostatic pressure has dropped. The osmotic effect of plasma proteins (oncotic pressure) draws water back into the capillary by osmosis. Most tissue fluid is reabsorbed this way.
  4. Lymphatic system: Excess tissue fluid (~10%) drains into blind-ended lymph capillaries, becoming lymph. Lymph vessels have valves and rely on skeletal muscle contraction for flow. Lymph is returned to the blood via the thoracic duct into the subclavian vein. Lymph nodes filter the lymph and contain immune cells.

3.4 Sexual and Asexual Reproduction

Sexual Reproduction

Sexual reproduction: The fusion of two haploid gametes (fertilisation) to produce a diploid zygote. It involves meiosis (to produce gametes) and results in genetically variable offspring due to independent assortment, crossing over, and random fertilisation.

Advantages: genetic variation provides raw material for natural selection, enabling adaptation to changing environments, resistance to new diseases, and avoiding accumulation of harmful mutations. Disadvantages: requires two parents, slower than asexual reproduction, energy cost of finding a mate and producing gametes, only 50% of genes passed to each offspring.

Asexual Reproduction

Asexual reproduction: Production of new organisms from a single parent without fusion of gametes, involving only mitosis. Offspring are genetically identical clones of the parent (barring rare mutations).

Advantages: rapid population growth, no mate needed (advantageous in isolated or stable environments), successful genotype preserved and all genes passed to offspring (100% genetic contribution). Disadvantages: no genetic variation, population vulnerable to environmental change or new diseases (all individuals equally susceptible), accumulation of harmful mutations over time (Muller's ratchet).

Reproductive Strategies in Plants

  • Sexual — pollination (wind-pollinated flowers: small petals, no scent, large feathery stigma, copious lightweight pollen; insect-pollinated: large colourful petals, nectar, scent, sticky pollen), fertilisation in ovule, seed and fruit dispersal (wind, animal, water, explosion)
  • Asexual (vegetative propagation) — runners (strawberry), bulbs (daffodil), rhizomes (iris), tubers (potato). These allow rapid colonisation of suitable habitats. Many plants can reproduce both sexually and asexually depending on conditions.

Meiosis — Producing Gametes

Meiosis is a two-division process that produces four haploid cells from one diploid cell. It is essential for sexual reproduction and introduces genetic variation through:

  • Crossing over (prophase I) — homologous chromosomes pair up (bivalents) and exchange segments of DNA at chiasmata, creating new combinations of alleles on each chromatid
  • Independent assortment (metaphase I) — the orientation of each bivalent at the equator is random, so maternal and paternal chromosomes are distributed randomly into daughter cells. With 23 pairs, there are 223 (~8.4 million) possible combinations
  • Random fertilisation — any sperm can fuse with any egg, further increasing variation

Gametogenesis

Spermatogenesis (in testes) produces four small, motile sperm from each primary spermatocyte. It is continuous from puberty, producing millions of sperm daily. Sperm have: a head with an acrosome (enzymes for zona pellucida digestion) and a haploid nucleus, a midpiece packed with mitochondria (ATP for swimming), and a tail (flagellum) for locomotion.

Oogenesis (in ovaries) produces one large ovum and two or three small polar bodies from each primary oocyte. The ovum retains most of the cytoplasm (nutrient reserves for early embryo development). Oogenesis begins before birth, but primary oocytes arrest in prophase I until puberty. One oocyte is typically released per menstrual cycle (ovulation).

Fertilisation in Mammals

  1. Sperm undergoes capacitation in the female reproductive tract — removal of cholesterol from the sperm membrane, enabling the acrosome reaction. This takes several hours and ensures sperm are ready to fertilise at the right time and place.
  2. Acrosome reaction: On contact with the zona pellucida (glycoprotein layer around the egg), the acrosome releases digestive enzymes (e.g., acrosin) that break down the zona, allowing the sperm to reach the egg membrane
  3. Sperm membrane fuses with egg membrane — the sperm nucleus, centriole, and mitochondria enter the egg cytoplasm
  4. Cortical reaction: Cortical granules in the egg release their contents by exocytosis, causing the zona pellucida to harden and change structure. This is the block to polyspermy — prevents more than one sperm from fertilising the egg (which would create a non-viable polyploid zygote)
  5. Nuclei of sperm and egg fuse (karyogamy), restoring the diploid number (2n = 46 in humans) and forming a zygote
Remember the distinction: sexual reproduction introduces genetic variation through meiosis (crossing over + independent assortment) and random fertilisation. Asexual reproduction produces clones (only source of variation is mutation). In exam answers about the advantages of sexual reproduction, always link genetic variation to natural selection and adaptation to changing environments.
