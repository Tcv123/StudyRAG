3.1 Gas Exchange Surfaces

Surface area to volume ratio (SA:V): As organisms increase in size, their SA:V ratio decreases, meaning simple diffusion across the body surface is insufficient. Larger organisms require specialised gas exchange surfaces and transport systems.

Gas Exchange in Insects

Insects use a tracheal system. Air enters through spiracles (openings on the body surface that can open and close to reduce water loss) and passes through a network of tracheae (reinforced with chitin rings). These branch into smaller tracheoles that penetrate tissues directly, delivering O2 to respiring cells by diffusion.

  • No respiratory pigment needed — gas delivered directly to cells
  • Ventilation by abdominal pumping and flight muscle contraction
  • Water at ends of tracheoles is drawn back during exercise, increasing gas exchange surface

Gas Exchange in Fish

Fish use gills with a countercurrent flow mechanism. Water flows over the gill lamellae in the opposite direction to blood flow. This maintains a concentration gradient along the entire length of the lamella, achieving approximately 80% oxygen extraction (far more efficient than parallel flow).

  • Each gill arch has many gill filaments (primary lamellae)
  • Each filament has many secondary lamellae with thin epithelium (one cell thick) and dense capillary network
  • Water enters through mouth, passes over gills, exits through operculum
In countercurrent flow diagrams, always show that a diffusion gradient is maintained at EVERY point along the lamella. In parallel flow, equilibrium would be reached halfway along, and no further exchange would occur.

Gas Exchange in the Mammalian Lung

The lungs provide a large surface area (~70 m2) through millions of alveoli. Each alveolus has walls one cell thick (squamous epithelium), surrounded by a dense capillary network. Ventilation maintains a steep concentration gradient for O2 and CO2.

3.2 Transport in Plants

Xylem — Water Transport

Transpiration: The loss of water vapour from the aerial parts of a plant, primarily through stomata on leaf surfaces. This creates a transpiration pull that draws water up through the xylem.

Water movement from roots to leaves involves:

  1. Root uptake: Water enters root hair cells by osmosis (soil water has higher Ψ than root cells)
  2. Movement across root: Apoplast pathway (through cell walls), symplast pathway (through cytoplasm via plasmodesmata), or vacuolar pathway
  3. Casparian strip: Endodermis forces water into the symplast pathway; minerals actively transported into xylem (lowers Ψ, drawing water in by osmosis)
  4. Cohesion-tension theory: Transpiration creates negative pressure (tension) at the top; cohesion between water molecules (hydrogen bonds) pulls a continuous column of water upward; adhesion to xylem walls prevents column breaking

Xylem Structure

Xylem vessels are dead, hollow tubes with lignified walls. They have no end walls (continuous lumen), no cytoplasm (no resistance to flow) and lignin provides strength and waterproofing. Bordered pits allow lateral water movement between vessels.

A potometer measures the rate of water uptake by a cut shoot (approximation of transpiration rate). A bubble potometer tracks movement of an air-water meniscus.

Phloem — Translocation

Translocation: The transport of dissolved organic solutes (mainly sucrose and amino acids) through phloem sieve tubes from sources (where produced, e.g., photosynthesising leaves) to sinks (where used or stored, e.g., roots, growing points, fruits).

Mass Flow Hypothesis (Munch)

  1. At the source, sucrose is actively loaded into sieve tube elements (companion cells use ATP to pump H+ out; sucrose co-transported in via cotransporters)
  2. This lowers water potential in the sieve tube; water enters by osmosis from nearby xylem
  3. Hydrostatic pressure increases at the source end
  4. At the sink, sucrose is actively removed from sieve tubes (used in respiration, converted to starch, etc.)
  5. Water potential rises in sieve tube at sink; water leaves by osmosis
  6. Pressure difference between source and sink drives mass flow of phloem sap

Phloem Structure

Sieve tube elements are living cells with no nucleus, very little cytoplasm, and sieve plates (perforated end walls) to allow flow. Companion cells are metabolically active with many mitochondria; they load sucrose into sieve tubes and support their function.

Evidence for mass flow: aphid stylet experiments show sieve tubes are under pressure; radioactive tracers show bidirectional movement in different sieve tubes; ringing experiments (removing phloem) cause sugar accumulation above the ring.

3.3 Transport in Animals

Double Circulatory System

Mammals have a double circulatory system: blood passes through the heart twice per circuit (pulmonary circulation: heart → lungs → heart; systemic circulation: heart → body → heart). This maintains high blood pressure in the systemic circuit, ensuring efficient delivery of oxygen and nutrients.

Heart Structure and Function

The heart has four chambers: right and left atria (receive blood) and right and left ventricles (pump blood out). Key features:

  • Septum separates oxygenated and deoxygenated blood
  • Atrioventricular valves (tricuspid right, bicuspid/mitral left) prevent backflow from ventricles to atria
  • Semilunar valves in aorta and pulmonary artery prevent backflow into ventricles
  • Left ventricle wall is thicker (generates higher pressure for systemic circulation)
  • Coronary arteries supply heart muscle with oxygenated blood

Cardiac Conduction System

The heart is myogenic (contracts without external nervous stimulation):

  1. SAN (sinoatrial node) in right atrium wall initiates impulse (pacemaker)
  2. Wave of depolarisation spreads across both atria → atrial systole
  3. AVN (atrioventricular node) delays impulse (~0.1 s) allowing atria to empty before ventricles contract
  4. Impulse passes down bundle of His in septum
  5. Purkyne fibres spread impulse from apex upward through ventricular walls → ventricular systole (from bottom up)

Haemoglobin and Oxygen Transport

Haemoglobin: A quaternary protein with four polypeptide subunits, each containing a haem group with an iron (Fe2+) ion that reversibly binds one molecule of oxygen. Each haemoglobin can carry up to four O2 molecules.

The oxygen dissociation curve is sigmoid (S-shaped) due to cooperative binding: when one O2 binds, the haemoglobin changes shape making it easier for subsequent O2 molecules to bind (positive cooperativity).

The Bohr Effect

In actively respiring tissues, CO2 concentration is high. CO2 lowers blood pH (combines with water to form carbonic acid). Lower pH causes haemoglobin to have a lower affinity for oxygen, so it releases O2 more readily to respiring tissues. This shifts the dissociation curve to the right.

CO2 + H2O ↔ H2CO3 ↔ H+ + HCO3− (carbonic anhydrase in RBCs)

Fetal Haemoglobin

Fetal haemoglobin (HbF) has a higher affinity for oxygen than adult haemoglobin (HbA). Its dissociation curve is to the left of adult haemoglobin. This ensures oxygen transfers from maternal blood to fetal blood across the placenta.

When interpreting dissociation curves: a shift LEFT means higher affinity (picks up O2 more easily); a shift RIGHT means lower affinity (releases O2 more easily). The Bohr effect shifts the curve RIGHT in active tissues.

3.4 Sexual and Asexual Reproduction

Sexual Reproduction

Sexual reproduction: The fusion of two haploid gametes (fertilisation) to produce a diploid zygote. It involves meiosis and results in genetically variable offspring.

Advantages: genetic variation (adaptation to changing environments, resistance to disease). Disadvantages: requires two parents, slower, energy cost of finding mate.

Asexual Reproduction

Asexual reproduction: Production of new organisms from a single parent without fusion of gametes, involving only mitosis. Offspring are genetically identical clones of the parent.

Advantages: rapid, no mate needed, successful genotype preserved. Disadvantages: no genetic variation, population vulnerable to environmental change or disease.

Reproductive Strategies in Plants

  • Sexual — pollination (wind or insect), fertilisation in ovule, seed dispersal
  • Asexual (vegetative propagation) — runners (strawberry), bulbs (daffodil), rhizomes (iris), tubers (potato)

Gametogenesis

Spermatogenesis produces four small, motile sperm from each primary spermatocyte (continuous from puberty). Oogenesis produces one large ovum (and polar bodies) from each primary oocyte (limited supply, cyclical release).

Fertilisation in Mammals

  1. Sperm undergoes capacitation in female reproductive tract
  2. Acrosome reaction: enzymes digest zona pellucida of egg
  3. Sperm membrane fuses with egg membrane
  4. Cortical reaction: cortical granules released, zona pellucida hardens (prevents polyspermy)
  5. Nuclei of sperm and egg fuse, restoring diploid number
Remember the distinction: sexual reproduction introduces genetic variation through meiosis (crossing over + independent assortment) and random fertilisation. Asexual reproduction produces clones (only source of variation is mutation).
