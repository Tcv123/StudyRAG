8.1 Control of Gene Expression

Gene expression: The process by which information from a gene is used to synthesise a functional gene product (usually a protein). Not all genes are expressed in all cells; differential gene expression produces different cell types from the same genome.

Levels of Control

LevelMechanismExample
TranscriptionalTranscription factors bind to promoter/enhancer regions; control whether RNA polymerase can transcribe a geneLac operon in bacteria; hormone-receptor complexes in eukaryotes
Post-transcriptionalAlternative splicing of pre-mRNA (different combinations of exons retained); mRNA stabilityOne gene can produce multiple proteins by including different exons
TranslationalRegulation of translation initiation; mRNA degradation rate; microRNAs (miRNA) that silence mRNAIron response elements control ferritin translation
Post-translationalProtein modification (phosphorylation, glycosylation); activation/deactivation; targeted degradationInsulin processed from proinsulin by cleavage

Transcription Factors

Proteins that bind to specific DNA sequences (promoters, enhancers, silencers) to regulate transcription. Activators increase transcription by helping RNA polymerase bind to the promoter. Repressors decrease transcription by blocking RNA polymerase binding or recruitment.

In eukaryotes, transcription factors often work in combinations, allowing precise control of gene expression in different cell types and at different developmental stages.

The Lac Operon (Prokaryotic Gene Regulation)

In E. coli, the lac operon demonstrates inducible gene expression:

  • Without lactose: Repressor protein (produced by regulatory gene) binds to operator; blocks RNA polymerase; structural genes (lacZ, lacY, lacA) NOT transcribed
  • With lactose: Lactose (inducer) binds to repressor, changing its shape; repressor cannot bind operator; RNA polymerase transcribes structural genes; enzymes for lactose metabolism produced
The lac operon is an example of negative inducible control. The default state is OFF (repressor blocks transcription). The inducer (lactose) switches it ON. This is an efficient use of resources — enzymes only produced when the substrate is present.

8.2 Epigenetics

Epigenetics: Heritable changes in gene expression that do NOT involve changes to the DNA base sequence. Epigenetic modifications affect whether genes are switched on or off without altering the genetic code itself.

Mechanisms of Epigenetic Modification

MechanismHow It WorksEffect on Gene Expression
DNA methylationMethyl groups (CH3) added to cytosine bases (usually CpG islands near promoters)Silences gene (prevents transcription factor binding; recruits proteins that condense chromatin)
Histone modification (acetylation)Acetyl groups added to histone tails by histone acetyltransferases (HATs)Activates gene (loosens DNA-histone interaction; chromatin becomes euchromatin; accessible to transcription machinery)
Histone deacetylationAcetyl groups removed by histone deacetylases (HDACs)Silences gene (tighter DNA wrapping; heterochromatin formed)

Significance of Epigenetics

  • Explains cell differentiation — same genome, different gene expression patterns
  • Can be influenced by environment (diet, stress, toxins, age)
  • Some epigenetic marks are heritable (passed to offspring)
  • Involved in diseases: abnormal methylation patterns in cancer (tumour suppressor genes silenced); imprinting disorders
  • Monozygotic twins accumulate epigenetic differences over time, explaining phenotypic divergence
Key distinction: mutations change the DNA sequence (permanent, irreversible); epigenetic changes modify gene expression WITHOUT changing the sequence (potentially reversible). Both can be heritable and both can contribute to disease.

8.3 Gene Technology Tools

Restriction Enzymes (Endonucleases)

Restriction enzyme: A bacterial enzyme that cuts DNA at specific palindromic recognition sequences (4–8 base pairs). They produce either sticky ends (short single-stranded overhangs) or blunt ends.

Sticky ends are preferred in genetic engineering because complementary sticky ends from different DNA sources can base-pair and be joined by DNA ligase, allowing recombinant DNA to be formed.

DNA Ligase

An enzyme that joins (ligates) DNA fragments by forming phosphodiester bonds between the sugar-phosphate backbones. Used to seal the gene of interest into a vector after restriction enzyme cutting.

Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR)

PCR: An in vitro technique for amplifying (copying) a specific DNA sequence millions of times in a few hours. It requires: template DNA, primers (short complementary DNA sequences), Taq DNA polymerase (thermostable), and free nucleotides (dNTPs).
  1. Denaturation (94–98°C): Heat breaks hydrogen bonds between complementary strands; DNA separates into single strands
  2. Annealing (50–65°C): Temperature lowered; primers bind (anneal) to complementary sequences on template strands
  3. Extension/Elongation (72°C): Taq polymerase extends primers by adding complementary nucleotides in 5'→3' direction
  4. Cycle repeated 25–35 times; DNA doubles each cycle (2n copies after n cycles)
Number of DNA copies = 2n (where n = number of cycles)

Gel Electrophoresis

Separates DNA (or protein) fragments by size and charge:

  1. DNA loaded into wells in agarose gel
  2. Electric current applied; DNA moves to positive electrode (DNA is negatively charged due to phosphate groups)
  3. Smaller fragments move faster/further through gel pores
  4. Bands visualised with UV + ethidium bromide stain (or fluorescent markers)
  5. Fragment sizes determined by comparison with a DNA ladder (known size markers)

8.4 Genetic Engineering

Genetic engineering (recombinant DNA technology): The deliberate modification of an organism's genome by introducing a gene from another organism (often a different species), producing a transgenic/genetically modified organism (GMO).

Steps in Genetic Engineering

  1. Identify and isolate the gene of interest: Use restriction enzymes to cut it from source DNA, or use reverse transcriptase to make cDNA from mRNA, or synthesise chemically
  2. Insert gene into a vector: Plasmid (bacterial), virus, or liposome. Cut vector with same restriction enzyme (compatible sticky ends); insert gene; seal with DNA ligase
  3. Transform the host cell: Introduce recombinant vector into host (e.g., heat shock for bacteria, electroporation, viral infection, gene gun for plants)
  4. Identify transformed cells: Use marker genes (e.g., antibiotic resistance, fluorescent protein) to select cells that have taken up the recombinant DNA
  5. Clone and express: Grow transformed cells; gene expressed to produce desired protein

Applications of Genetic Engineering

ApplicationExampleBenefit
MedicineHuman insulin produced by GM bacteria (E. coli or yeast)Unlimited supply; no allergic reactions; pure; consistent
AgricultureBt crops (gene from Bacillus thuringiensis produces insecticidal toxin)Reduced pesticide use; increased yield
AgricultureGolden Rice (genes for beta-carotene synthesis added)Addresses vitamin A deficiency in developing countries
ResearchGFP (green fluorescent protein) used as reporter geneTracks gene expression; visualises protein localisation

Cloning Organisms

Reproductive cloning (somatic cell nuclear transfer, SCNT): Remove nucleus from egg cell; insert nucleus from adult somatic cell; stimulate division; implant embryo in surrogate. Example: Dolly the sheep.

Therapeutic cloning: Same nuclear transfer technique but embryo used to harvest embryonic stem cells (not implanted); stem cells can differentiate into any tissue for treating disease.

When discussing genetic engineering in exams, consider both benefits AND risks/ethical concerns: gene transfer to wild populations, loss of biodiversity, unknown long-term effects, ethical concerns about 'playing God', patent issues, and the precautionary principle.

8.5 Biotechnology and Fermentation

Biotechnology: The use of living organisms (or their components) in industrial processes to produce useful substances or provide services. Applications include fermentation, enzyme technology, gene therapy and bioremediation.

Fermentation and Bioreactors

Industrial fermenters (bioreactors) are large vessels that maintain optimal conditions for microbial growth and product formation:

Condition ControlledHowWhy
TemperatureWater jacket / cooling coilsOptimum for enzyme activity; microbial metabolism generates heat
pHBuffers; acid/alkali additionEnzyme optimum pH; metabolites can change pH
OxygenSterile air supply; agitator (stirrer)Aerobic respiration for maximum growth; even distribution
NutrientsSterile nutrient mediumCarbon source, nitrogen source, minerals for growth
AsepsisSterilisation of vessel, medium, air; sealed systemPrevent contamination by unwanted microorganisms

Batch vs Continuous Culture

FeatureBatch CultureContinuous Culture
ProcessFixed amount of nutrients added at start; organism grows; products harvested at endNutrients continuously added; products continuously removed; steady state maintained
Growth phase usedAll phases (lag, log, stationary)Maintained in log phase
Contamination riskLower (closed system, short runs)Higher (open system, long runs)
ExamplesPenicillin production, beer/wine makingSingle-cell protein (Quorn/mycoprotein), yoghurt on industrial scale

8.6 Gene Therapy

Gene therapy: The introduction of a normal (functional) copy of a gene into cells to treat or prevent disease caused by a defective or missing gene. It aims to correct genetic disorders at the molecular level.

Types of Gene Therapy

TypeTargetHeritable?Status
Somatic cell gene therapyBody cells of affected individualNo (not passed to offspring)In clinical use for some conditions
Germ line gene therapyGametes or early embryoYes (passed to all future generations)Currently banned in humans (ethical concerns)

Vectors for Gene Therapy

  • Viral vectors (e.g., adenovirus, retrovirus, lentivirus): Efficient at entering cells; virus modified to be non-pathogenic; gene inserted into viral genome
  • Liposomes: Lipid vesicles that fuse with cell membrane; deliver DNA directly; less immunogenic but less efficient
  • Direct injection: Naked DNA or via gene gun (biolistics)

Challenges of Gene Therapy

  • Difficulty targeting the correct cells
  • Immune response against vector (especially viral vectors)
  • Risk of insertional mutagenesis (gene inserts into wrong location; may disrupt tumour suppressor gene → cancer)
  • Temporary effect (somatic cells divide; transgene may be lost)
  • Only effective for single-gene disorders (most diseases are polygenic)

8.7 Genomics and Bioinformatics

Genomics: The study of the entire genome of an organism, including the mapping, sequencing and analysis of all genes and their interactions. The Human Genome Project (completed 2003) sequenced all ~3 billion base pairs of human DNA.

DNA Sequencing

Modern sequencing (next-generation sequencing, NGS) allows rapid, high-throughput determination of DNA base sequences. The original Sanger method (chain termination) used dideoxynucleotides (ddNTPs) that terminate DNA synthesis at specific bases, producing fragments of different lengths separated by electrophoresis.

Bioinformatics

The use of computer science and statistics to store, analyse and interpret biological data (especially DNA and protein sequences). Applications include:

  • Sequence comparison: BLAST searches to identify similar genes across species
  • Gene annotation: Identifying gene locations and functions from raw sequence data
  • Phylogenetics: Constructing evolutionary relationships from sequence data
  • Protein structure prediction: Predicting 3D protein structure from amino acid sequence
  • Personalised medicine: Pharmacogenomics (tailoring drugs to individual genotype)

Applications of Genomics

ApplicationDescription
Personalised/precision medicineDrug selection based on individual genetic profile; minimise adverse effects
Gene discoveryIdentifying genes associated with diseases (GWAS studies)
Comparative genomicsUnderstanding evolution and gene function by comparing genomes across species
MetagenomicsSequencing DNA directly from environmental samples (studying unculturable microbes)
Synthetic biologyDesigning and constructing new biological systems or organisms

CRISPR-Cas9 Gene Editing

A revolutionary gene editing tool that allows precise modification of DNA sequences in living organisms:

  1. A guide RNA (gRNA) is designed to be complementary to the target DNA sequence
  2. The gRNA directs the Cas9 endonuclease to the target site in the genome
  3. Cas9 creates a double-strand break in the DNA at the precise location
  4. Cell's repair mechanisms either: disrupt the gene (non-homologous end joining, NHEJ) or insert a new sequence (homology-directed repair, HDR) if a template is provided

Advantages over traditional genetic engineering: more precise, faster, cheaper, can edit genes in situ (no need to remove and reinsert). Applications: correcting genetic diseases, creating disease-resistant crops, studying gene function, potential to eliminate vector-borne diseases (gene drives).

For any biotechnology question, be prepared to discuss ethical, social and ecological implications. Key points: informed consent, access and inequality, unintended consequences, environmental release of GMOs, designer babies, and the distinction between treating disease vs enhancement.
