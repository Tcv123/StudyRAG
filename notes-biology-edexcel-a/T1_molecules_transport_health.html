1.1 Water

Water: A polar inorganic molecule (H2O) that acts as the universal solvent in biological systems, essential for metabolic reactions and transport.

Properties of Water

Water molecules are polar due to the uneven distribution of charge — oxygen is more electronegative than hydrogen, creating a dipole. This polarity allows hydrogen bonds to form between adjacent water molecules.

SolventPolarity attracts ions and polar moleculesMetabolic reactions occur in aqueous solution; transport medium in blood and xylem

When explaining water's properties, always link the molecular structure (polarity, hydrogen bonds) to the biological significance. A common mistake is describing properties without explaining the mechanism.

1.2 Carbohydrates

Carbohydrate: An organic molecule containing carbon, hydrogen and oxygen in the ratio Cn(H2O)n. They function as energy sources, energy stores and structural components.

Monosaccharides

The simplest sugars with the general formula (CH2O)n. They are classified by the number of carbon atoms:

  • Trioses (3C) — e.g., glyceraldehyde (intermediate in respiration)
  • Pentoses (5C) — e.g., ribose (in RNA, ATP), deoxyribose (in DNA)
  • Hexoses (6C) — e.g., glucose (alpha and beta forms), fructose, galactose

Alpha-glucose and beta-glucose differ in the position of the hydroxyl group on carbon-1. Alpha-glucose has the –OH group below the ring; beta-glucose has it above.

Disaccharides and Glycosidic Bonds

Two monosaccharides join via a condensation reaction (releasing water) to form a disaccharide linked by a glycosidic bond. This bond is broken by hydrolysis (adding water).

Polysaccharides

Long chains of monosaccharides linked by glycosidic bonds. Their structure determines their function:

Remember that starch and glycogen are insoluble (do not affect water potential) and compact, making them ideal storage molecules. Cellulose has high tensile strength due to hydrogen bonds between parallel chains.

1.3 Lipids

Lipid: A diverse group of hydrophobic organic molecules including triglycerides and phospholipids, containing carbon, hydrogen and oxygen (with a higher H:O ratio than carbohydrates).

Triglycerides

Formed from one molecule of glycerol and three fatty acid chains joined by ester bonds through condensation reactions. They are the main energy storage molecules in animals and plants (oils in seeds).

Fatty acids can be:

  • Saturated — no C=C double bonds; straight chains pack tightly; solid at room temperature (animal fats)
  • Unsaturated — one or more C=C double bonds; kinked chains; liquid at room temperature (plant oils)
Glycerol + 3 Fatty acids → Triglyceride + 3H2O (condensation)

Phospholipids

Similar to triglycerides but with one fatty acid replaced by a phosphate group. This gives them an amphipathic nature: a hydrophilic phosphate head and two hydrophobic fatty acid tails. This is crucial for forming the phospholipid bilayer of cell membranes.

Cholesterol

A lipid with a four-ring hydrocarbon structure. In membranes, cholesterol sits between phospholipid molecules, regulating membrane fluidity: at high temperatures it reduces fluidity; at low temperatures it prevents the membrane from becoming too rigid.

1.4 Proteins

Protein: A polymer of amino acids joined by peptide bonds. Proteins have diverse functions including enzymes, antibodies, transport proteins, structural proteins and hormones.

Amino Acid Structure

All amino acids share a common structure: a central carbon atom bonded to an amino group (–NH2), a carboxyl group (–COOH), a hydrogen atom and a variable R group (side chain). There are 20 different amino acids, distinguished by their R groups.

Peptide Bonds and Polypeptides

Amino acids are linked by peptide bonds formed through condensation reactions between the amino group of one amino acid and the carboxyl group of another. A chain of many amino acids is a polypeptide.

Globular proteins (e.g., enzymes, haemoglobin) are soluble and have metabolic roles. Fibrous proteins (e.g., collagen, keratin) are insoluble with structural roles. Know specific examples for each.

1.5 Enzymes

Enzyme: A biological catalyst (usually a globular protein) that lowers the activation energy of a metabolic reaction, increasing the rate of reaction without being used up.

Lock and Key Model vs Induced Fit Model

The lock and key model proposes that the substrate is exactly complementary to the enzyme's active site. However, the induced fit model (more accurate) suggests that the active site changes shape slightly upon substrate binding, moulding around the substrate for a tighter fit. This places strain on bonds in the substrate, lowering activation energy.

Factors Affecting Enzyme Activity

  • Temperature: Increasing temperature increases kinetic energy and collisions; above optimum, hydrogen bonds break, active site denatures, rate drops to zero
  • pH: Each enzyme has an optimum pH; extreme pH changes break ionic and hydrogen bonds, denaturing the enzyme
  • Substrate concentration: Rate increases until all active sites are occupied (Vmax); further increases have no effect
  • Enzyme concentration: More active sites available; rate increases proportionally (if substrate is in excess)

Inhibition

CompetitiveInhibitor competes with substrate for the active site (similar shape)Unchanged (overcome with excess substrate)Increased (apparent lower affinity)

1.6 Cell Membranes

Fluid mosaic model: The accepted model of membrane structure describing a phospholipid bilayer with proteins, cholesterol and glycolipids/glycoproteins distributed throughout like a mosaic, with components free to move laterally (fluid).

Membrane Components and Functions

ComponentFunction
Phospholipid bilayerBarrier to water-soluble substances; provides fluidity
Channel proteinsAllow specific ions/polar molecules through (passive)
Carrier proteinsTransport molecules via facilitated diffusion or active transport
GlycoproteinsCell signalling, cell recognition, immune response
GlycolipidsCell recognition, stability
CholesterolRegulates fluidity and permeability

Factors Affecting Membrane Permeability

Temperature increases cause greater phospholipid movement and increased permeability. Very high temperatures denature proteins and cause gaps. Organic solvents (e.g., ethanol) dissolve lipids, increasing permeability. This can be investigated using beetroot discs and measuring anthocyanin leakage with a colorimeter.

Water potential (Ψ): The tendency of water molecules to move from one region to another. Pure water has the highest water potential of 0 kPa. Adding solute lowers (makes more negative) water potential.
Ψ = Ψs + Ψp (water potential = solute potential + pressure potential)
Osmosis questions: water always moves from LESS negative (higher) water potential to MORE negative (lower) water potential. In animal cells, this can cause lysis (hypotonic) or crenation (hypertonic). In plant cells, turgid (hypotonic) or plasmolysed (hypertonic).

The Heart

The heart is a double pump with four chambers. The right side pumps deoxygenated blood to the lungs (pulmonary circulation) and the left side pumps oxygenated blood to the body (systemic circulation). The left ventricle has a thicker wall because it must generate higher pressure for systemic circulation.

Cardiac Cycle

  1. Atrial systole: Atria contract, forcing blood through atrioventricular (AV) valves into ventricles
  2. Ventricular systole: Ventricles contract, AV valves close (first heart sound), blood forced through semilunar valves into arteries
  3. Diastole: Heart relaxes, semilunar valves close (second heart sound), blood flows from veins into atria

Atherosclerosis and Cardiovascular Disease

Atherosclerosis is the build-up of fatty deposits (atheroma) in arterial walls, leading to plaque formation. This narrows the lumen, reduces blood flow, increases blood pressure and can lead to thrombosis (blood clot formation). If a coronary artery is blocked, the result is a myocardial infarction (heart attack).

Risk factors include: high blood cholesterol (especially LDL), smoking, high blood pressure, obesity, lack of exercise, genetic predisposition, diabetes and high-salt diets.

1.9 Gas Exchange

Gas exchange surface: A specialised area where respiratory gases (O2 and CO2) are exchanged between an organism and its environment by diffusion.

Features of Efficient Gas Exchange Surfaces

  • Large surface area — alveoli provide approximately 70 m2
  • Thin barrier — alveolar and capillary walls are one cell thick (short diffusion distance)
  • Good blood supply — maintains steep concentration gradient
  • Ventilation — breathing maintains concentration gradient
  • Moist surface — gases dissolve before diffusing

Lung Structure

Air passes through: trachea → bronchi → bronchioles → alveoli. The trachea and bronchi are supported by C-shaped cartilage rings to prevent collapse. Goblet cells secrete mucus to trap pathogens, and ciliated epithelial cells beat to move mucus towards the throat.

Pulmonary Ventilation

Pulmonary ventilation rate = tidal volume × breathing rate

Inspiration: External intercostal muscles and diaphragm contract, increasing thoracic volume, decreasing pressure, air drawn in.

Expiration (at rest): Muscles relax, elastic recoil of lungs decreases volume, increases pressure, air forced out. Forced expiration involves internal intercostal muscles and abdominal muscles.

Fick's law describes the rate of diffusion: Rate ∝ (surface area × concentration difference) / thickness. Always relate lung adaptations back to these three factors when answering questions about gas exchange efficiency.
