P4.1 Wave Basics

A wave transfers energy (and information) from one place to another without transferring matter. The particles of the medium only oscillate about a fixed position — they don't travel with the wave.

Transverse vs Longitudinal Waves

TypeOscillation directionExamples
TransversePerpendicular to energy transferLight, all EM waves, water ripples, string vibrations, S-waves in earthquakes
LongitudinalParallel to energy transfer (compressions and rarefactions)Sound, P-waves in earthquakes, ultrasound

Wave Quantities

  • Wavelength (λ) — distance between two equivalent points (e.g. crest to crest). Unit: m.
  • Amplitude (A) — maximum displacement from the rest position. Unit: m. Bigger amplitude = more energy.
  • Frequency (f) — number of complete waves passing a point per second. Unit: hertz (Hz).
  • Period (T) — time for one complete wave to pass. T = 1 / f. Unit: s.
v = f × λ  (m/s) = (Hz) × (m) — the wave equation
T = 1 / f  (s)
A sound wave has frequency 500 Hz and wavelength 0.66 m. Speed?
v = f × λ = 500 × 0.66 = 330 m/s (speed of sound in air at room temperature).

P4.2 Reflection, Refraction and Diffraction

Reflection

  • Specular reflection — from a smooth surface (mirror). Parallel rays stay parallel; clear image.
  • Diffuse reflection — from a rough surface (paper, wall). Rays scatter in many directions; no image.

Refraction

When a wave crosses a boundary between materials of different densities, its speed changes. If the wave hits the boundary at an angle, it also changes direction — this is refraction. Wavelength changes but frequency stays the same.

When learning refraction directions: SLOWER = TOWARDS normal. A light ray entering a denser medium slows down and bends towards the normal; leaving it speeds up and bends away.

Diffraction

When waves pass through a gap or around an obstacle they spread out — this is diffraction. The amount of diffraction is largest when the gap is similar in size to the wavelength.

P4.3 The Electromagnetic (EM) Spectrum

The electromagnetic spectrum is a continuous range of transverse waves that all travel at the same speed in a vacuum — 3 × 10⁸ m/s (the speed of light). They differ only in wavelength and frequency.

Dangers increase with frequency. UV, X-rays and gamma rays are ionising — they carry enough energy per photon to knock electrons out of atoms, which damages DNA and can cause cancer.

Absorbed / Reflected / Transmitted

When an EM wave hits a material, it can be reflected, absorbed, or transmitted. Which happens depends on the material and the wave's frequency:

  • A black surface absorbs visible light & IR — that's why it gets hot in the sun.
  • A silver surface reflects IR — hence foil survival blankets reflect body heat back.
  • Glass transmits visible light but absorbs UV and IR — so a greenhouse warms up.

P4.4 Sound and Ultrasound

Sound waves are longitudinal and need a medium (solid, liquid, gas) — they cannot travel through a vacuum. In air at room temperature, sound travels at about 330 m/s; faster in liquids (~1500 m/s in water); fastest in solids (5000 m/s in steel).

Hearing Range

  • Humans hear roughly 20 Hz – 20 000 Hz (20 kHz).
  • Ultrasound = above 20 kHz. Used for pre-natal scanning, cleaning jewellery, echo-location, non-destructive testing (finding cracks in metal).
  • Infrasound = below 20 Hz. Used for monitoring earthquakes, volcanoes, whale calls.

Ultrasound Imaging

Ultrasound pulses partially reflect at each boundary between different tissues. By timing the echoes and using d = v × t (halved — there and back), the depth of each boundary is found. Safer than X-rays for pregnancy scans because it is non-ionising.

P4.5 The Atomic Model

History of the Atom

ModelWhoKey idea
Solid-sphereDalton (1803)Atoms are tiny indestructible solid spheres, one type per element.
Plum puddingThomson (1897)Positively-charged "pudding" with electrons ("plums") embedded in it.
NuclearRutherford (1911)Gold-foil experiment: most α-particles passed straight through, a few deflected. Most of the atom is empty space; mass is concentrated in a tiny, positive nucleus.
PlanetaryBohr (1913)Electrons orbit the nucleus at fixed energy levels (shells).
Nuclear (with neutrons)Chadwick (1932)Discovered the neutron — explained why atoms were heavier than their proton count suggested.

Conclusions from Rutherford's experiment: atoms are mostly empty space; they have a small, dense, positively-charged nucleus; electrons orbit that nucleus.

  • Atomic (proton) number Z — number of protons. Defines the element.
  • Mass number A — protons + neutrons.
  • Isotopes — atoms of the same element (same Z) but different numbers of neutrons (different A). Example: carbon-12 and carbon-14.

Size scale: atom ≈ 10⁻¹⁰ m; nucleus ≈ 10⁻¹⁴ m. The nucleus is about 10 000 times smaller than the atom, yet contains >99.9% of the mass.

P4.6 Radioactive Decay

An unstable nucleus can lose energy and become more stable by emitting radiation. This is radioactive decay, and it is a random process — we cannot predict when any particular atom will decay.

Nuclear Equations

In a decay equation: the mass number (top) and atomic number (bottom) must balance on both sides.

α decay: AZX → A−4Z−2Y + 42He
Example: 23892U → 23490Th + 42He
β⁻ decay: a neutron turns into a proton + electron. AZX → AZ+1Y + 0−1e
Example: 146C → 147N + 0−1e

Gamma emission is different — a γ ray carries off excess energy without changing proton or neutron count.

Half-Life

The half-life is the average time taken for the number of undecayed nuclei (or the activity) of a radioactive sample to halve. It is constant for any given isotope.
A sample has an activity of 800 Bq and a half-life of 15 minutes. What will its activity be after 1 hour?
1 hour = 4 × 15 minutes = 4 half-lives.
800 → 400 → 200 → 100 → 50 Bq.

Background Radiation

We are all exposed to low-level background radiation from natural and artificial sources.

Uses of Radioactivity

  • Medical tracers — short-half-life γ-emitters (e.g. Tc-99m) injected and tracked to image organs.
  • Radiotherapy — high-dose γ or X-rays aimed at tumour cells.
  • Sterilising medical equipment and food — γ rays kill microbes without physical contact.
  • Smoke alarms — small α source ionises air; smoke disrupts the current, triggering alarm.
  • Industrial thickness control — β source + detector give consistent paper/metal thickness.
  • Radiocarbon dating — C-14 ratio in organic material tells you how long ago it died.
