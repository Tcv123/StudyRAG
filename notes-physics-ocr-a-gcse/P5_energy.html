P5.1 Energy Stores and Transfers

Energy is never created or destroyed — it is only transferred from one store to another (or from one object to another). This is the principle of conservation of energy.

The Eight Energy Stores

StoreWhere it isExample
KineticIn anything movingA moving car, a falling apple
Gravitational potentialIn objects at heightA raised weight, water in a dam
Elastic potentialIn stretched / compressed springsA drawn bow, a compressed spring in a toy car
Thermal (internal)In warm objects — random motion of particlesA hot cup of tea
ChemicalIn bonds between atomsFuel, food, a battery
MagneticIn separated magnetic polesTwo magnets held apart
ElectrostaticIn separated electric chargesA charged balloon near a wall
NuclearIn the nuclei of atomsUranium fuel in a reactor

Energy Transfer Pathways

Energy is moved between stores by four pathways:

  1. Mechanically — a force acts through a distance (work done).
  2. Electrically — charge moves through a pd.
  3. By heating — temperature difference drives energy flow.
  4. By radiation — waves (light, sound, IR) carry energy.
A kettle: chemical store (gas / electricity source) → electrical pathway → thermal store of water.
A falling ball: gravitational store → kinetic store (as it falls) → thermal store (of ground + ball when it hits).
A torch: chemical store (battery) → electrical pathway → thermal + light radiation stores (bulb).

P5.2 Calculating Energy

Kinetic Energy

Ek = ½ × m × v²  (J) = (kg) × (m/s)²
Note: doubling speed quadruples kinetic energy (because of v²).
A 1 200 kg car travels at 20 m/s. Kinetic energy?
Ek = ½ × 1200 × 20² = ½ × 1200 × 400 = 240 000 J = 240 kJ.
At 40 m/s (double): Ek = ½ × 1200 × 40² = 960 kJ — four times as much.

Gravitational Potential Energy

Ep = m × g × h  (J) = (kg) × (N/kg) × (m)
g ≈ 9.8 N/kg on Earth (often rounded to 10).
A 70 kg climber ascends a cliff 150 m high. GPE gained?
Ep = m × g × h = 70 × 9.8 × 150 = 102 900 J ≈ 103 kJ. This is the minimum work the climber must do against gravity.

Energy Stored in a Spring (Elastic Potential)

Ee = ½ × k × e²  (J) = (N/m) × (m)²
k = spring constant, e = extension.

Energy for Heating — Specific Heat Capacity

ΔE = m × c × Δθ  (J) = (kg) × (J/kg °C) × (°C)

Covered in detail in P1; worth re-reading as it often appears in energy-efficiency questions.

P5.3 Energy Transfers in a Closed System

In a closed, frictionless system, as one energy store goes down another goes up by exactly the same amount (conservation of energy). The total is constant.

A 0.5 kg pendulum bob is released from a height of 0.8 m above its lowest point. Speed at the bottom (no friction)?
Energy conservation: all GPE → KE.
m g h = ½ m v²  →  v = √(2 g h) = √(2 × 9.8 × 0.8) = √15.68 = 3.96 m/s.

P5.4 Efficiency and Power

Efficiency is how good a device is at transferring energy to useful output. It is a ratio (0 to 1) or a percentage.
Efficiency = useful output / total input × 100%
η = Euseful / Etotal × 100%  or  η = Puseful / Ptotal × 100%
A motor uses 500 J of electrical energy and lifts an object giving it 320 J of GPE. Efficiency?
η = 320 / 500 × 100% = 64%. The rest (180 J) is wasted — mostly as heat in the motor.

Power

Power is the rate of energy transfer — how much energy per second. 1 watt = 1 joule per second.
P = E / t  (W) = (J) / (s)   or   P = W / t (power = work done per unit time)
A crane lifts a 2 000 kg load through 15 m in 25 s. Output power?
Work done = m g h = 2000 × 9.8 × 15 = 294 000 J.
P = 294 000 / 25 = 11 760 W ≈ 11.8 kW.

P5.5 Reducing Unwanted Energy Transfers

In every real process some energy is transferred to less useful stores (usually thermal), often because of friction or air resistance. Good engineering minimises the fraction lost.

Device / situationWasted energy goes toWays to reduce waste
Car engineThermal (friction in engine & tyres, air resistance, exhaust heat)Lubrication, low-friction bearings, aerodynamic shape, regenerative braking
Old filament bulbThermal (heat from hot filament)Replace with LED — far higher efficiency
Home heatingThermal loss through walls, roof, windowsLoft insulation, cavity wall insulation, double glazing, draught proofing
SpeakerThermal (coil heating)Better magnet materials, efficient amplifier

Insulation & Thermal Conductivity

The rate of thermal conduction through a wall depends on the thermal conductivity of the material and its thickness. A low-conductivity material (good insulator) resists heat flow and keeps a house warm.

Double glazing: two panes of glass with a small air or argon gap. Air has very low thermal conductivity, so heat conducts very slowly across the gap, reducing loss through windows by about half compared with single glazing.

P5.6 National & Global Energy Resources

A renewable energy resource is one that is replenished as fast as it is used — it will not run out on a human timescale.
A non-renewable resource is finite — burning it uses it up faster than natural processes can form more (e.g. fossil fuels took hundreds of millions of years to form).

The UK Energy Mix & Why It's Changing

The UK has been moving away from coal (virtually zero in 2025 compared with over 40% in the 1990s). Reasons:

  • Climate change — commitments under the Paris Agreement and UK Net Zero target for 2050 require cutting CO₂ emissions.
  • Air quality — coal emits SO₂, NOx, particulates, all harmful to health.
  • Falling renewable costs — wind and solar are now often cheaper per kWh than new-build gas.
  • Energy security — domestic renewables reduce reliance on imported fuel.

Balancing Supply and Demand

Demand varies daily (peaks in the evening) and seasonally (higher in winter). Grid operators need a mix of:

  • Base-load — runs 24/7 (nuclear, biomass, some gas).
  • Dispatchable — can be turned up or down quickly (gas, hydro).
  • Variable renewables — when available (wind, solar).
  • Storage — pumped-storage hydroelectric and battery farms absorb surplus and release it when demand peaks.
Dinorwig pumped-storage station (Wales) — off-peak surplus electricity pumps water uphill into a reservoir (GPE stored). At times of peak demand, water flows back down through turbines (GPE → electrical) in just seconds. Useful as a grid "battery" with rapid response.

P5.7 Forces and Springs (Work Done on Springs)

When a force stretches or compresses a spring, work is done, and energy is stored in the elastic potential store.

F = k × e (Hooke's law)
F = force (N), k = spring constant (N/m), e = extension (m). Valid below the limit of proportionality.

Up to the limit of proportionality, extension is directly proportional to force (straight line through origin). Beyond this, the spring deforms permanently (plastic deformation).

A spring extends 6.0 cm (0.06 m) when a 3.0 N weight is hung on it. Spring constant?
k = F / e = 3.0 / 0.06 = 50 N/m.
Elastic PE stored: Ee = ½ k e² = ½ × 50 × 0.06² = 0.09 J.
