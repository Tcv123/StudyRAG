P1.1 The Particle Model

The particle model states that all matter is made of tiny particles (atoms or molecules) that are in constant motion. The arrangement, spacing and motion of these particles determines whether a substance is a solid, liquid or gas.

We cannot see atoms directly, but the model explains a huge range of observations — from why liquids flow to why balloons deflate. You need to know the typical particle arrangement in each state and how the particles behave.

A common trap: gases fill their container, they do not just "take its shape". Because there are almost no intermolecular forces between gas particles, they spread out to occupy all the space available to them.

P1.2 Changes of State

A change of state is a physical change — the chemical composition does not alter, so the change is reversible. Energy must be transferred to or from the substance to break or form the forces between particles.

Mass is always conserved in a change of state — the same number of particles exists before and after, they are just rearranged. A gas condensing back to a liquid gives back exactly the same mass of liquid.

Internal Energy

The internal energy of a system is the total energy stored by the particles. It is the sum of the kinetic energy of the particles (from their motion) and the potential energy stored in the bonds/intermolecular forces between them.

Heating a substance transfers energy to its particles. This energy either:

  • Increases the kinetic energy of the particles — they move faster and the temperature rises, OR
  • Increases the potential energy of the particles — bonds/forces between particles are overcome, causing a change of state (temperature stays constant during the change).

Specific Heat Capacity

The specific heat capacity (c) of a substance is the amount of energy required to raise the temperature of 1 kg of the substance by 1 °C. Units: J/kg °C.
Energy transferred (ΔE) = mass (m) × specific heat capacity (c) × temperature change (Δθ)
ΔE = m × c × Δθ   (J) = (kg) × (J/kg °C) × (°C)
A 2 kg block of copper (c = 385 J/kg °C) is heated from 20 °C to 70 °C. Energy transferred?
ΔE = m × c × Δθ = 2 × 385 × (70 − 20) = 2 × 385 × 50 = 38 500 J = 38.5 kJ.

Water has an unusually high specific heat capacity (c ≈ 4200 J/kg °C). That is why it is used in car radiators and central-heating systems — it can absorb and release lots of energy without a huge temperature change.

Specific Latent Heat

The specific latent heat (L) is the energy needed to change the state of 1 kg of a substance without changing its temperature. Units: J/kg.
  • Latent heat of fusion — for melting / freezing.
  • Latent heat of vaporisation — for boiling / condensing.
Energy for change of state = mass × specific latent heat
E = m × L   (J) = (kg) × (J/kg)
How much energy is needed to melt 0.5 kg of ice (L = 334 000 J/kg)?
E = m × L = 0.5 × 334 000 = 167 000 J = 167 kJ.
Note: this is the energy used only during melting — raising the temperature of the resulting water is a separate calculation using c.
If a question mixes warming and a change of state (e.g. ice at −10 °C → steam at 110 °C), split it into stages and use c for temperature-change stages and L for change-of-state stages. Add the answers together.

P1.3 Density

Density (ρ) is the mass per unit volume of a substance. It tells you how "heavy" a given volume of a material is.
ρ = m / V   (kg/m³) = (kg) / (m³)
Also common: g/cm³. 1 g/cm³ = 1000 kg/m³.

Measuring Density

The method depends on whether the object is regular, irregular, or a liquid:

  • Regular solid (cube, cylinder): measure mass with a balance. Calculate volume from dimensions with a ruler / vernier calliper (V = l × w × h for a cuboid, V = πr²h for a cylinder).
  • Irregular solid: measure mass with a balance. Measure volume by displacement — lower the object into a partially-full measuring cylinder or eureka can. Volume displaced = volume of object.
  • Liquid: place an empty measuring cylinder on a balance, zero it, pour in a known volume (e.g. 50 cm³), record mass. ρ = m / V.
A stone has mass 135 g and displaces 50 cm³ of water. Its density?
ρ = m / V = 135 / 50 = 2.7 g/cm³ (= 2700 kg/m³). That's the density of aluminium / granite.

Why Density Varies Between States

The same substance in different states usually has very different densities because the spacing between particles changes. Solids and liquids of the same substance have similar densities because the particles are still touching. A gas has a much lower density because the particles are far apart.

Water is unusual — its solid form (ice) is less dense than its liquid form, so ice floats. This is because water molecules form an open hexagonal lattice when they freeze.

P1.4 Pressure

Pressure is force per unit area — how much force is pushing on a given area. Unit: pascal (Pa) = 1 N/m².
p = F / A   (Pa) = (N) / (m²)

The same force produces a much higher pressure if applied over a smaller area. That's why a sharp knife cuts easily (force concentrated on a tiny area) but a snowshoe stops you sinking in snow (force spread over a large area).

A 600 N person stands on ice. With boots, their boots cover 0.015 m². With snowshoes, they cover 0.30 m². Compare the pressure they exert.
• Boots: p = 600 / 0.015 = 40 000 Pa.
• Snowshoes: p = 600 / 0.30 = 2 000 Pa.
The snowshoes reduce the pressure by a factor of 20 — enough to stop sinking.

Pressure in Gases

Gas particles move randomly at high speed and collide with the walls of their container. Each collision exerts a tiny force. The total force per unit area from all these collisions is the gas pressure.

Effect of Temperature on Gas Pressure

When you increase the temperature of a gas (at constant volume):

  1. Particles gain kinetic energy — they move faster.
  2. They hit the walls more often.
  3. They hit the walls harder (bigger change of momentum per collision).
  4. Net result: pressure increases.

Doubling the absolute temperature (in kelvin) doubles the pressure, if volume is constant. This is why an aerosol can explodes if heated — the pressure inside rises above what the can can contain.

Gas Pressure and Volume (Boyle's Law)

At constant temperature, compressing a gas into a smaller volume increases its pressure. Halving the volume doubles the pressure, because the particles hit the walls twice as often.

p × V = constant   (at constant temperature)
So: p₁V₁ = p₂V₂
A gas has volume 2.0 m³ at pressure 100 kPa. It is compressed to 0.5 m³ (temperature unchanged). New pressure?
p₁V₁ = p₂V₂   ⇒   100 × 2.0 = p₂ × 0.5   ⇒   p₂ = 200 / 0.5 = 400 kPa.

Atmospheric Pressure

The Earth's atmosphere has weight. The column of air pressing down on you creates atmospheric pressure — about 101 kPa (≈ 101 000 Pa) at sea level. Pressure decreases with altitude because there is less air above you.

Pressure in Liquids

p = h × ρ × g   (Pa)
h = height of liquid column (m)   ρ = density of liquid (kg/m³)   g = 9.8 N/kg

Pressure in a liquid acts in all directions, and it increases with depth because a deeper point has more liquid above it pressing down. Dams are built thicker at the base than the top for this reason.

Water is 3.0 m deep (ρ = 1000 kg/m³). Pressure at the bottom due to the water?
p = h × ρ × g = 3.0 × 1000 × 9.8 = 29 400 Pa ≈ 29 kPa. (Total pressure at bottom = this + atmospheric.)

Upthrust — Why Things Float

Because pressure increases with depth, the pressure on the bottom of a submerged object is greater than on the top. This difference produces a net upward force called upthrust, equal to the weight of fluid the object displaces (Archimedes' principle).

  • If upthrust > weight: object floats and the object rises until enough is above the surface that upthrust = weight.
  • If upthrust = weight: object floats in equilibrium at the current position.
  • If upthrust < weight: object sinks.

A steel ship floats because its shape displaces a huge volume of water — enough that the weight of the displaced water exceeds the weight of the ship. A solid lump of steel of the same mass would not displace enough water and would sink.

Know the three equations for pressure and when each applies:
• p = F/A — solid on a surface.
• p = hρg — liquid pressure at depth.
• p₁V₁ = p₂V₂ — gas at constant temperature.
Check units carefully: volume in m³ not cm³, density in kg/m³ not g/cm³.
