Geography — Eduqas A-Level (A110QS) — Component 2: Synoptic Theme

1. Synopticity — What Topic 2.5 Is and How It Is Examined

Topic 2.5 is the synoptic theme of the Eduqas A-level (A110QS) specification. It is not a new body of content but rather a lens through which candidates are expected to integrate material from across the specification — and especially from the Component 2 Global Systems and Global Governance topics (2.1 water, 2.2 carbon & energy, 2.3 migration, 2.4 oceans) — to evaluate how the major geographical challenges of the twenty-first century are produced, distributed and contested. Synopticity rewards candidates who connect physical and human geography, who move fluently between local, regional, national and global scales, and who use named examples across multiple regions of the world rather than relying on a single favoured case study.

The Eduqas examination style for 2.5 emphasises extended evaluative writing. Typical questions take the form: “To what extent is climate change the most serious 21st-century challenge?” or “Assess the effectiveness of global governance in managing contemporary challenges.” A 20-mark essay is expected to produce a clear thesis, weigh multiple positions with located evidence, introduce counter-arguments and alternative interpretations, and reach a defended conclusion. The Welsh context is important — Eduqas is the WJEC awarding body — so Welsh/UK-specific angles on climate leadership, post-Brexit trade, or sustainable development (the Well-being of Future Generations (Wales) Act 2015 is a rare legislative example of the SDGs being embedded in national law) are rewarded where relevant.

Synopticity: The explicit drawing together of material from different parts of a specification so that candidates can demonstrate they understand geography as an integrated discipline — linking physical processes, human systems, scales and case studies — rather than as a set of isolated topics.
Exam tip — synopticity in practice: In any 20-mark Topic 2.5 answer, signpost the synoptic links explicitly. Use phrases such as “linking to Topic 2.1”, “as seen in the 2.4 ocean governance material” or “the 2.3 migration case of Syria illustrates this”. Examiners cannot award synoptic credit for connections they cannot see you making.

2. Globalisation and Interdependence

The defining context of 21st-century geography is globalisation: the deepening of economic, political, social, environmental and technological connections between places. Globalisation is not new — the Silk Roads, the Atlantic slave trade and the British Empire were all globalising systems — but its velocity, density and depth since the 1990s are qualitatively new. A 2008 crisis in US sub-prime mortgages collapsed Icelandic banks; a 2020 virus in Wuhan halted factories in northern Italy; a 2022 war in Ukraine drove wheat prices in Egypt and petrol prices in Pembrokeshire. Interdependence is no longer a theory; it is daily experience.

Globalisation: The process by which national economies, cultures, institutions and populations have become increasingly interconnected through cross-border flows of goods, services, capital, labour, technology, ideas and environmental impacts, producing a degree of interdependence such that events in one place materially shape conditions in others.

Waves and phases of globalisation

Economic historians commonly identify three waves:

  • First wave (c. 1870–1914): driven by steam shipping, the telegraph, the gold standard and liberal trade; ended by WWI and the inter-war collapse.
  • Second wave (c. 1945–1990): Bretton Woods, GATT tariff cuts, containerisation (1956 onwards — reducing port handling costs ~20×), jet aviation. Still largely an OECD-to-OECD phenomenon.
  • Third wave (c. 1990–2008): hyperglobalisation; collapse of the USSR; China’s WTO accession (2001); ICT revolution. Global trade grew ~2× faster than GDP.
  • Post-2008 “slowbalisation” or fourth phase: global trade-to-GDP has plateaued; geopolitical rivalry, “friendshoring” and industrial policy are reshaping supply chains. The IMF documented rising trade restrictions, with ~3,200 new measures in 2023 alone.

Dimensions of globalisation

  • Economic: global supply chains (an iPhone is designed in Cupertino, components drawn from 43 countries, assembled in Zhengzhou, sold globally), foreign direct investment (FDI), transnational corporations (TNCs). Walmart (revenue ~$650 bn), Amazon, Apple (market capitalisation peaking >$3 trillion in 2024), Toyota and Saudi Aramco are examples whose revenues exceed the GDP of most states.
  • Financial: global capital flows (~$7 trillion daily on foreign-exchange markets), currency interdependence, the 2008 Global Financial Crisis which began in US mortgage-backed securities and caused recessions across the OECD.
  • Political: multilateral institutions — United Nations, World Trade Organization (WTO), International Monetary Fund (IMF), World Bank — and regional blocs (EU, ASEAN, African Union, Mercosur).
  • Social / cultural: migration (Topic 2.3), global media, diaspora communities, the spread of English, hybridisation of food, music, dress.
  • Environmental: the shared atmosphere, oceans (Topic 2.4) and biodiversity produce global externalities — climate change is the defining case.
  • Technological: the internet (~5.4 bn users by 2024), undersea fibre-optic cables carrying ~99% of intercontinental data, satellites, AI foundation models released as global products.

The Brandt Line and its critique

Willy Brandt’s 1980 Commission divided the world schematically into a wealthy global North and a poor global South, with the line running south of the USA and Europe, north of most of Latin America, Africa and South Asia, but dipping below Australia and New Zealand. The line still has pedagogical value but has been repeatedly critiqued. China’s GDP per capita rose from ~$195 in 1980 to ~$12,600 in 2023; South Korea, Singapore and the UAE are indisputably “developed”; meanwhile Detroit, eastern Kentucky and south Wales contain pockets of deprivation comparable to parts of the global South. The 21st-century map is patchwork, not banded.

Alternative framings include Hans Rosling’s “four levels” (dollar-a-day, $4/day, $16/day, $64/day) which emphasise that most of humanity now lives in the middle two levels rather than in either extreme; the World Bank’s classification into Low-, Lower-Middle-, Upper-Middle- and High-Income economies; and Wallerstein’s world-systems model of core, semi-periphery and periphery. Each has analytical strengths: Rosling’s emphasises the shape of the global income distribution rather than a binary, the World Bank’s is operationally tied to lending terms, Wallerstein’s foregrounds historical and structural relations rather than income snapshots. For Eduqas 2.5 essays, the key move is to choose a framing self-consciously rather than inheriting one.

Brandt Line (1980): A schematic North–South division of the world proposed by the Independent Commission on International Development Issues (chaired by former West German Chancellor Willy Brandt). Useful as a teaching device but increasingly criticised for obscuring the rise of emerging economies and intra-national inequality.
Exam tip — always name examples: Questions on globalisation will be marked down for vague “developing countries” or “big companies”. Name Vietnam’s garment sector, name TSMC’s Taiwan fabs, name the Port of Rotterdam. A named example is the minimum bar; located, dated and quantified detail is what lifts an answer into the higher bands.

3. Superpowers and Shifting Geopolitics

Twentieth-century geopolitics was structured by a bipolar Cold War (USA vs USSR, 1947–1991) followed by a brief unipolar moment of American primacy. The early 21st century is increasingly multipolar: a world of several centres of economic and political power, with shifting alignments rather than fixed blocs.

Superpower: A state possessing overwhelming capability across multiple dimensions — military, economic, political, cultural, technological — allowing it to project influence globally. Joseph Nye distinguishes hard power (coercion, military, economic sanctions) from soft power (attraction, culture, values) and smart power (their combination).
Multipolar world: A geopolitical configuration in which three or more states or state groupings possess comparable power and no single hegemon dominates. Contrasted with unipolar (single hegemon) and bipolar (two rival superpowers) systems.
Geopolitics: The study of how geography (location, resources, territory) shapes the political behaviour of states, and how power politics shape geographical outcomes. Classical geopolitics (Mackinder, Spykman) emphasised land versus sea power; contemporary geopolitics adds cyberspace, supply-chain chokepoints, and resource geopolitics (rare earths, lithium, semiconductors).

Power is measured in several dimensions simultaneously. Hard power captures military and economic coercion. Soft power (Nye, 1990) captures attraction and agenda-setting through culture, values and institutions — Hollywood, K-pop, the English Premier League, Oxbridge, the Confucius Institutes. Sharp power (Walker and Ludwig, 2017) captures coercive influence operations short of war — disinformation, bribery, cyberattack. Structural power (Strange, 1988) captures the ability to shape the systems within which others operate — the dollar-denominated global financial system, the SWIFT messaging network, the US-dominated internet stack, the standards baked into the International Maritime Organization. Twenty-first-century geopolitics is increasingly contested on structural-power terrain: the question is not just who has the most tanks but who sets the operating system of the world.

The main poles in 2026

  • United States: still the largest economy in nominal GDP (~$28 tn, 2024), the world reserve currency (US dollar used in ~88% of FX transactions), the largest military spender (~$877 bn, 2023 — ~39% of the global total), and the dominant power in AI, finance (Wall Street), and culture (Hollywood, Silicon Valley platforms). Leadership in “Big Tech” (Alphabet, Apple, Meta, Microsoft, Amazon, Nvidia).
  • China: second-largest economy nominally (~$18 tn) and largest on purchasing-power parity (PPP) terms; the “workshop of the world” responsible for ~28% of global manufacturing output. Belt and Road Initiative since 2013 (see case study). Technology leadership in 5G (Huawei), EVs (BYD outsold Tesla globally in Q4 2023), solar PV and batteries. People’s Liberation Army modernising rapidly.
  • European Union: 27 member states, single market of ~450 million people, collectively the second- or third-largest economy depending on measure. Leader in regulatory power (“Brussels effect”: GDPR, DSA, AI Act).
  • India: most populous country since 2023 (~1.43 bn), fastest-growing large economy, demographic dividend (median age ~28), strong service sector (IT offshoring to Bangalore, Hyderabad). Fifth-largest economy nominally, projected third by ~2030.
  • Russia: fossil-fuel power, the world’s second military by strength and first by nuclear stockpile, but economy smaller than Italy’s. Declining demographically. Post-2022 pariah in the West but enduring partnerships with China, India, Iran.
  • Others: Japan, the UK, Brazil, South Africa, Saudi Arabia, Turkey, Indonesia form a second tier; BRICS bloc (Brazil, Russia, India, China, South Africa, joined 2024 by UAE, Iran, Egypt, Ethiopia) explicitly positions itself as a non-Western counterweight.
BRICS: Originally an acronym coined by Jim O’Neill (Goldman Sachs, 2001) for Brazil, Russia, India, China; later institutionalised with South Africa (2010) and expanded in 2024 to include the UAE, Iran, Egypt and Ethiopia (“BRICS+”). Represents ~45% of the world’s population and ~35% of global GDP (PPP). Positioned as an alternative to G7-dominated global governance.

4. Development and Inequality

Development is itself a contested concept. Modernisation theorists (Rostow, 1960) saw it as a linear path from traditional to high-mass-consumption society; dependency theorists (Frank, Wallerstein) argued that “underdevelopment” is produced by the relations between core and periphery rather than being a stage that simply “comes later”. Amartya Sen reframed development as the expansion of capabilities and freedoms, a move institutionalised in the UNDP’s Human Development Index.

Measures of development

Human Development Index (HDI): A composite index published by UNDP since 1990 combining life expectancy at birth, mean and expected years of schooling, and logged GNI per capita (PPP). Ranges 0–1; classified Very High (>0.8), High, Medium, Low. Norway, Switzerland and Iceland consistently rank at the top; South Sudan, Somalia, Chad at the bottom.
Gini coefficient: A statistical measure of income (or wealth) inequality ranging from 0 (perfect equality) to 1 (one household owns everything). Typical values: Scandinavia ~0.25–0.30, UK ~0.35, USA ~0.40, South Africa ~0.63 (highest among large economies). Geographers also use a Lorenz curve to visualise the distribution.

Global inequality

Between-country inequality has narrowed slightly (thanks largely to China’s catch-up) but within-country inequality has widened almost everywhere. Credit Suisse’s 2022 Global Wealth Report estimated that the top 1% of adults owned ~43% of global household wealth, while the bottom 50% owned ~1%. Oxfam reported in January 2024 that the five richest men had more than doubled their fortunes since 2020, while the bottom five billion had grown poorer. Inequality matters for governance because it shapes political legitimacy, populism and migration pressures.

The elephant graph (Lakner and Milanovic, 2013) captures this striking pattern: between 1988 and 2008, the biggest gains in real income went to the global middle class (largely the emerging middle class in China) and to the very top of the distribution (the global 1%), while the upper-middle deciles of rich countries — the industrial working classes of the US and Europe — experienced near-stagnation. This “twin peaks” pattern has been interpreted as a structural cause of political backlash in advanced economies (Brexit, Trump, populism in France, Germany, Italy). The geography of inequality also has a spatial dimension: cities and regions diverge within countries. London’s GDP per capita is ~2.7× that of north-east England; San Francisco’s is ~2× that of Mississippi. The “levelling up” agenda in the UK and the Biden administration’s “place-based” industrial policy (CHIPS Act, Inflation Reduction Act) are responses to this geography.

Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs): Seventeen goals adopted by all UN member states in September 2015 (Agenda 2030), succeeding the Millennium Development Goals. Cover poverty (SDG1), hunger (SDG2), health (SDG3), education (SDG4), gender (SDG5), water (SDG6), energy (SDG7), climate (SDG13), oceans (SDG14), life on land (SDG15), peace (SDG16) and partnerships (SDG17). Monitored through 231 indicators; progress seriously off-track in 2024 (UN estimate: only ~15% of targets on track).
Exam tip — SDGs as synoptic hinge: The SDGs are a gift for synoptic answers because each goal maps to a topic: SDG6 water (Topic 2.1), SDG7 energy (2.2), SDG10 reduced inequalities (migration 2.3), SDG14 oceans (2.4). Use them as a structural device for essays that ask about “21st-century challenges” generally.

5. Migration in the 21st Century (link to Topic 2.3)

As Topic 2.3 makes clear, human mobility has reached unprecedented levels — UN DESA estimated ~281 million international migrants in 2020 (~3.6% of global population), plus ~108 million forcibly displaced by mid-2023 (UNHCR). Migration is simultaneously a symptom (of war, climate, inequality) and a driver (of labour markets, demographic balance, cultural change) of 21st-century challenges.

  • Labour mobility and remittances: remittances to low- and middle-income countries reached ~$656 bn in 2023 (World Bank) — more than FDI plus aid combined. For Tonga, remittances are ~41% of GDP; Nepal ~23%; Tajikistan ~48%.
  • Brain drain / brain gain: Zambia loses ~50% of its trained doctors to OECD economies; conversely the Indian diaspora in the USA (~5 million) reinvests in Bangalore’s IT cluster.
  • Refugees and asylum seekers are indicators of political instability. Syria, Venezuela, Ukraine, Afghanistan and Sudan together produced >50% of the world’s refugees by 2024.
  • Ageing in the global North — Japan’s median age is 49.5, Germany’s 46, Italy’s 48. These societies face shrinking workforces and rising dependency ratios, which create structural demand for immigration even where politics resists it.
  • Population growth in Africa — Nigeria is projected to reach ~400 million by 2050; the Democratic Republic of Congo and Tanzania also triple-digit growth. UN medium projection: global population peaks near 10.4 billion around 2086.

6. Climate Change as the Defining Global Challenge

Anthropogenic climate change is the single most integrative 21st-century challenge. It is physical (forced by greenhouse-gas emissions from Topic 2.2 fossil combustion), biological (biodiversity loss), economic (trillions in stranded assets and adaptation cost), political (demands cooperation between >190 states), and ethical (those least responsible are most exposed). Global mean surface temperature has risen ~1.2 °C above the 1850–1900 baseline (IPCC AR6, updated WMO 2024); 2024 provisionally became the first calendar year with mean temperature exceeding 1.5 °C (Copernicus), though the Paris threshold refers to a multi-decadal average rather than a single year.

Paris Agreement (2015): Legally binding international treaty adopted at COP21 in December 2015, in force since November 2016. Commits parties (196 + the EU) to hold the increase in global mean temperature to “well below 2 °C” above pre-industrial levels and to pursue efforts to limit warming to 1.5 °C, through Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs) updated every five years, supported by climate finance (a $100 bn/year developed-country goal) and a periodic global stocktake.
Loss and damage: The residual harms from climate change that exceed the adaptive capacity of countries and communities — for example a Bangladeshi village permanently submerged by sea-level rise, or a Pacific atoll rendered uninhabitable. The term appears in Article 8 of the Paris Agreement; a dedicated Loss and Damage Fund was agreed in principle at COP27 (Sharm el-Sheikh, 2022) and operationalised at COP28 (Dubai, 2023), though pledges (~$700 million by early 2024) fall far short of estimated need (hundreds of billions of dollars annually).

COP summits — recent milestones

  • COP21 Paris (2015): adopted the Paris Agreement.
  • COP26 Glasgow (2021): Glasgow Climate Pact — first explicit reference to “phase down” (not out) of unabated coal; methane pledge (>100 countries, 30% reduction by 2030).
  • COP27 Sharm el-Sheikh (2022): agreement in principle on the Loss and Damage Fund; limited progress on mitigation.
  • COP28 Dubai (2023): operationalised Loss and Damage Fund; first Global Stocktake found the world far off-track; “UAE Consensus” called for “transitioning away from fossil fuels” — the first COP text to name fossil fuels directly, although language (no “phase out”) was contested.
  • COP29 Baku (2024): the “climate finance COP”; agreed a new collective quantified goal of $300 bn/year by 2035 from developed countries — criticised as insufficient by the G77 and Climate Vulnerable Forum.
Exam tip — keep COPs current: Eduqas examiners are impressed by candidates who know the latest COP. Memorise one-sentence summaries of COP26, 27, 28 and 29 and use them to punctuate evaluations of global governance effectiveness.

Unequal impacts

Climate impacts are profoundly uneven. Small Island Developing States (SIDS) — Tuvalu, Kiribati, Marshall Islands, Maldives — face existential risk from sea-level rise (sea level has risen ~21 cm since 1900; rate now ~4.5 mm/yr). The Sahel (Mali, Burkina Faso, Niger, Chad) faces compounding heat, drought and conflict. Least Developed Countries (LDCs) contribute ~1% of cumulative emissions but bear disproportionate impacts. By contrast, Russia gains longer growing seasons and navigable Arctic routes in the short term. This climate injustice is the moral spine of the loss and damage debate.

Climate feedbacks and non-linearity

A central reason climate change is hazardous is its non-linearity. Positive feedbacks amplify warming beyond the direct forcing from CO2: as Arctic sea ice retreats, a dark ocean replaces reflective white ice (the ice-albedo feedback), absorbing ~90% of incoming shortwave radiation rather than reflecting ~80%. Thawing permafrost (~15 million km², storing ~1,500 GtC — roughly twice the carbon currently in the atmosphere) releases CO2 and methane; the methane budget update by the Global Carbon Project (2023) showed atmospheric methane rising at the fastest rate on record. Boreal forest dieback, wildfire release of stored carbon (Canada’s 2023 fire season released an estimated ~480 Mt CO2 — more than Canadian annual fossil emissions), and potential Amazon tipping toward savanna represent further feedbacks. The Earth system is not a linear machine whose response is proportional to input; it is a set of coupled non-linear systems with tipping points.

7. Food Security

The FAO’s 2023 State of Food Security and Nutrition in the World estimated that 735 million people were undernourished in 2022 — roughly 122 million more than in 2019. Drivers are what FAO calls the three Cs: conflict (Ukraine, Yemen, Sudan), climate (Horn of Africa drought), and cost-of-living (2022 energy and fertiliser price spike). Food insecurity is a synoptic issue par excellence — it depends on Topic 2.1 (water for irrigation), Topic 2.2 (energy for fertilisers and transport), Topic 2.3 (migration triggered by hunger), and global governance capacity.

Debates over sustainable intensification (produce more on existing land through precision agriculture, drought-tolerant varieties, drip irrigation) versus land sparing (intensify on small areas so that wider land is left to nature) versus agroecology (mixed systems with biodiversity at their core) are central to the 21st-century food policy agenda.

Food security is also a trade question. Just six countries (USA, Russia, Ukraine, Canada, Australia, Argentina) supply ~60% of global wheat exports; ten countries supply ~80% of maize. Concentration creates systemic vulnerability: when one producer fails (climate) or is cut off (war, export bans), the shock is global. The 2007–08 food-price crisis was exacerbated by export bans from India, Vietnam and Argentina; the 2022 crisis by Indian wheat and rice export restrictions. Food-system resilience therefore requires diversified production, strategic reserves (China holds ~50% of global wheat reserves), and rules-based trade — all areas where the WTO’s Agreement on Agriculture is under strain.

The EAT-Lancet planetary health diet (2019) proposed a reference diet that could feed 10 billion within planetary boundaries: roughly halving global red-meat and sugar consumption while doubling fruit, vegetable, legume and nut intake. Its implementation has been politically fraught; shifting diets intersects with culture, income and inequality. Food security in the 21st century therefore overlaps with public-health governance, climate policy, biodiversity conservation and trade law simultaneously — the classic synoptic tangle.

8. Water Security (link to Topic 2.1)

Water security is covered substantively in Topic 2.1 (water cycle and water insecurity) and is a synoptic case here. Key 2.5-relevant framings: ~2 billion people lack safely managed drinking water; ~2.3 billion live in water-stressed countries (UN-Water, 2023); 153 countries share 286 transboundary rivers and aquifers, requiring cooperation rather than conflict. “Water wars” are rarer than the media suggest; shared basins have produced >3,600 treaties since 805 CE (Oregon State TFDD). The Grand Ethiopian Renaissance Dam (GERD) dispute with Egypt and Sudan on the Nile, however, shows how tensions can escalate.

9. Energy Security and Transition (link to Topic 2.2)

Topic 2.2 covers the carbon cycle and the transition from fossil fuels. From a 2.5 perspective three points dominate:

  • Energy geopolitics: Russia’s 2022 invasion of Ukraine (see case study) weaponised gas exports, triggering an energy crisis in Europe; the EU reduced reliance on Russian gas from ~40% to ~15% of imports by the end of 2023.
  • Critical minerals: the clean-energy transition is mineral-intensive. An electric vehicle contains ~6× more minerals than an internal-combustion car. The Democratic Republic of Congo mines ~70% of global cobalt; China refines ~60% of lithium and ~80% of rare earths. “Green geopolitics” is producing new dependencies.
  • Transition pace: renewables added ~510 GW of capacity in 2023 (~50% above 2022; IEA); solar PV alone added more capacity than all fossil fuels combined. Yet fossil fuels still supplied ~80% of primary energy demand.

10. Ocean Governance (link to Topic 2.4)

Topic 2.4 covers UNCLOS, the International Seabed Authority, Regional Fisheries Management Organisations and the 2023 BBNJ “High Seas Treaty”. The synoptic relevance for 2.5 is that oceans exemplify the challenge of governing global commons: resources that nobody owns and everybody can over-exploit. Ocean governance rehearses the same problems — commitment, compliance, enforcement, finance — that beset climate, biodiversity and atmosphere governance.

Transition is also politically uneven. At one extreme, Denmark sourced >55% of electricity from wind in 2023; Norway’s new car sales are ~90% EV. At the other, coal consumption reached an all-time high globally in 2023 (~8.7 billion tonnes), driven by Asia. The International Energy Agency estimates that limiting warming to 1.5 °C requires tripling renewable capacity and doubling energy-efficiency improvements by 2030 (a commitment made at COP28) — a target widely viewed as physically feasible but politically very demanding. The geography of the energy transition therefore maps onto the geography of capital, technology, state capacity and political will — a synoptic tangle linking 2.2 directly to 2.5.

11. Health and Pandemics

COVID-19 (2020–2023) was the defining health event of the century so far. The World Health Organization reported ~7 million confirmed deaths; excess-mortality estimates are 2–3× higher. The pandemic demonstrated the velocity of interdependence: the SARS-CoV-2 virus reached every inhabited continent within ~10 weeks of the first reported cluster. Vaccine development at unprecedented speed (<1 year for mRNA vaccines) was an extraordinary scientific achievement; vaccine distribution was a governance failure — by mid-2021 ~80% of doses had gone to high- and upper-middle-income countries, despite the COVAX facility. New pandemic risks — bird flu H5N1 spillover, antimicrobial resistance, Ebola, Marburg — compound with biodiversity loss (zoonotic spillover correlated with habitat disturbance).

Beyond pandemics, global health challenges include the “double burden” of communicable and non-communicable disease facing middle-income countries (Mexico, Brazil, South Africa — combining HIV/TB and diabetes/obesity); maternal mortality (still ~287,000 deaths in 2020, WHO, concentrated in sub-Saharan Africa); and antimicrobial resistance (AMR), projected by the O’Neill review (2016) to kill ~10 million people annually by 2050 if unchecked. Universal Health Coverage (SDG3.8) is one of the most off-track SDGs. Climate change is also a health issue: heat deaths, expanded range of vector-borne disease (dengue now present in southern Europe), and disruption to health systems from extreme events are all intensifying. The 2023 Lancet Countdown tracked deterioration in 10 of 15 indicators of climate-health.

12. Technology and the Digital Divide

The ITU’s 2023 estimate is that ~67% of the world’s population uses the internet — meaning ~2.6 billion people (~33%) remain offline. The divide is geographically concentrated: ~63% of Africa is offline versus ~11% of Europe. Within countries there is also a digital divide by income, age, gender and rurality. Beyond access, the 21st-century technology agenda includes:

  • AI disruption: large-language-model deployment from 2022 (ChatGPT) to 2025 (frontier models in medicine, code, science); labour-market effects; governance through the EU AI Act (2024), the US Executive Order on AI (2023) and the Bletchley Declaration (2023).
  • Cybersecurity: ransomware, critical-infrastructure attacks (Colonial Pipeline 2021), state-sponsored hacking.
  • Data governance and surveillance: GDPR (EU, 2018), China’s social-credit experiments, Five Eyes intelligence sharing.
  • Platform power: five US “Big Tech” firms + Chinese counterparts (Tencent, Alibaba, ByteDance) structure the informational environment of billions of people.
  • Digital geography: submarine fibre-optic cables (~500 active, ~1.4 million km) carry ~99% of intercontinental data; their chokepoints (Red Sea, Strait of Malacca, Luzon Strait) are new geopolitical pressure points (Houthi cable cuts in the Red Sea, February 2024).

The digital divide is itself a synoptic issue. Lack of connectivity in rural Africa and South Asia constrains participation in the global economy, access to education (stark during COVID school closures), remittance flows, climate-adaptation information and civic participation. Conversely, surveillance capitalism (Zuboff) and platform concentration raise questions about too much digital exposure in affluent societies. The governance response is fragmented: the EU is the clear global regulator via the “Brussels effect” (GDPR 2018, DMA/DSA 2022, AI Act 2024); the US relies on markets plus sectoral regulation; China maintains a separate sovereign internet with the Great Firewall and the “Cyberspace Administration”. This splinternet pattern is itself a 21st-century governance feature.

13. Future Scenarios — the SSP Framework

The IPCC’s Shared Socioeconomic Pathways (SSPs) are not forecasts but structured narratives used for climate, land, water and economic modelling. They pair socioeconomic storylines (SSP1–5) with radiative-forcing pathways (e.g. “1.9”, “2.6” W/m² by 2100) to generate scenarios used throughout AR6.

The SSPs help examiners test candidates’ ability to reason about futures. They also underpin the concept of tipping points: non-linear, potentially irreversible transitions in the Earth system (Amazon dieback, Greenland ice-sheet collapse, AMOC shutdown, permafrost thaw, coral reef die-off) whose probability rises with each fraction of a degree of warming.

Tipping points are not only ecological. Social tipping points — the rapid normalisation of a previously fringe behaviour once it passes a threshold share of a population — may also shape 21st-century trajectories, for good (the collapse of apartheid, the rapid rise of LGBTQ+ recognition in many societies, the sudden normalisation of EVs in Norway and China) or ill (the collapse of democratic norms under populist regimes). Political tipping points include state failure and the cascading effect on neighbours (Somalia, Syria, Venezuela). Raworth’s Doughnut Economics (2017) reframes 21st-century development as a question of staying within planetary boundaries (the “ecological ceiling”) while meeting social thresholds (the “social foundation”) — an explicit departure from GDP-growth-centric models.

14. Global Governance Architecture

The “governance architecture” of the 21st century consists of overlapping, sometimes competing institutions at multiple scales.

UN system: The United Nations Organization (founded 1945, HQ New York) has 193 member states and comprises six principal organs: General Assembly, Security Council (5 permanent members with veto — P5 — plus 10 elected), ECOSOC, Secretariat, International Court of Justice, and the now-dormant Trusteeship Council. Specialised agencies (WHO, FAO, UNESCO, ILO), funds and programmes (UNDP, UNICEF, UNHCR, WFP) and related bodies (WTO, IAEA) constitute the wider “UN system”.

16. Required Case Studies

Case Study 1 — COVID-19 pandemic (2020–2023): First cases reported in Wuhan, Hubei, China in December 2019; WHO declared a Public Health Emergency of International Concern (PHEIC) on 30 January 2020 and a pandemic on 11 March 2020. By mid-2024, the WHO recorded ~7.0 million confirmed deaths (true toll, including excess mortality, estimated 15–25 million). Global GDP contracted ~3.1% in 2020 — the sharpest peacetime contraction since the 1930s. Vaccine development was historically fast: Pfizer-BioNTech and Moderna mRNA vaccines received emergency use authorisation in December 2020, <12 months after genome sequencing. Yet distribution was starkly unequal — by mid-2021 over 80% of doses had been administered in high- and upper-middle-income countries, while the COVAX facility (WHO/CEPI/GAVI) delivered only ~1.1 billion doses against a target of 2 billion by end-2021; Africa’s vaccination rate was still under 20% at that point. Long-term effects include permanent shifts to remote and hybrid work in knowledge-economy sectors, an estimated ~10% one-off loss to learning in affected cohorts (World Bank), supply-chain restructuring (“friendshoring”, inventory build-up), and reinvigorated debates over WHO reform and a Pandemic Treaty (negotiations ongoing since 2021, framework accord adopted by WHA in 2025 — with notable US non-engagement under the 2025 administration). Synoptic connections: interdependence (2.5), global governance weakness (2.5), migration restrictions (2.3), carbon-emissions drop during lockdowns (2.2).
Case Study 2 — Russia-Ukraine war (February 2022–present): Russia’s full-scale invasion of Ukraine on 24 February 2022 is the most consequential geopolitical event in Europe since 1945. Direct impacts: >500,000 estimated military casualties on both sides by early 2024 (Western intelligence estimates; Ukrainian civilian deaths >10,000 confirmed, UN OHCHR). Displacement: ~6.5 million Ukrainian refugees in Europe (UNHCR, 2024) — the largest refugee flow in Europe since WWII, primarily to Poland, Germany, Czechia (Topic 2.3 synoptic link). Energy crisis: Russia cut gas flows via Nord Stream; EU wholesale gas prices peaked at ~€340/MWh in August 2022 (~10× pre-crisis); the EU reduced Russian gas import share from ~40% to ~15% by end-2023, replaced by US LNG, Norwegian pipeline gas, Qatari LNG. Food crisis: Russia and Ukraine combined produced ~30% of global wheat exports and ~70% of sunflower oil pre-war; wheat prices rose ~50% in the first months of the war before the Black Sea Grain Initiative (July 2022–July 2023) restored partial exports. Geopolitical rupture: NATO enlargement (Finland joined April 2023, Sweden March 2024); unprecedented Western sanctions (~$300 bn Russian central-bank reserves frozen); weakened UN Security Council (Russia’s veto); deepened Russia–China partnership (“no limits” joint statement February 2022); Ukraine’s EU candidate status granted June 2022. Synoptic: energy (2.2), migration (2.3), food security, global governance.
Case Study 3 — China’s Belt and Road Initiative (BRI, 2013–present): Announced by Xi Jinping in Kazakhstan (September 2013) as the “Silk Road Economic Belt” and in Indonesia (October 2013) as the “21st-Century Maritime Silk Road”. BRI now engages >140 countries accounting for ~65% of global population; cumulative Chinese engagement (investments + construction contracts) is estimated at ~$1 trillion by 2023 (Green Finance & Development Center, Shanghai). Flagship projects include the China–Pakistan Economic Corridor (CPEC, ~$62 bn; Gwadar Port); the Mombasa–Nairobi Standard Gauge Railway (Kenya, $3.2 bn); Hungary–Serbia Belgrade–Budapest railway; Hambantota Port (Sri Lanka); the Jakarta–Bandung High-Speed Railway (Indonesia, opened October 2023). Key criticisms: “debt-trap diplomacy” (the 2017 Hambantota lease to a Chinese SOE for 99 years after Sri Lanka could not service loans is the canonical case, though researchers at Chatham House and the Rhodium Group show most BRI debt is renegotiated rather than seized); environmental impacts (coal-plant financing); governance and corruption. Post-2021 pivot: a “Green BRI” was announced at the September 2021 UN General Assembly (Xi committed China to stop financing overseas coal power). Strategic response: the US and G7 launched Build Back Better World (B3W, 2021), relaunched as the Partnership for Global Infrastructure and Investment (PGII, 2022, targeting $600 bn by 2027); the EU announced Global Gateway (€300 bn by 2027). Synoptic: superpower competition (2.5), energy transition (2.2), global governance (2.5).
Case Study 4 — Global food crisis 2022–2024: A compounded food crisis produced by the three Cs — conflict, climate, cost of living. Triggers: (a) Russia’s invasion of Ukraine (February 2022) disrupted ~30% of global wheat exports and ~70% of sunflower-oil exports; (b) a 5-year multi-year drought in the Horn of Africa (Ethiopia, Somalia, Kenya) driven by La Niña and climate change — the longest recorded in the region; (c) lingering pandemic effects on logistics; (d) the 2021–22 natural-gas price spike pushed fertiliser prices up ~3× (urea ~$1,000/t peak 2022) because ~70% of fertiliser production cost is gas. Impacts: FAO Food Price Index peaked at 159.7 in March 2022, its highest on record; ~735 million undernourished in 2022 (FAO, SOFI 2023); acute food insecurity (IPC Phase 3+) affected ~282 million in 59 countries. Responses: the Black Sea Grain Initiative (brokered by Turkey and the UN, 22 July 2022) allowed ~33 million tonnes of Ukrainian grain to be exported before Russia withdrew in July 2023; the World Food Programme (WFP) — which itself sources ~50% of its grain from Ukraine — faced unprecedented funding shortfalls (raised ~$14 bn in 2022 against ~$23 bn assessed need). Synoptic: conflict (2.5), climate (2.2), energy (2.2), migration and displacement (2.3).
Case Study 5 — COP27–28 “loss and damage” pathway: After more than two decades of campaigning by the Alliance of Small Island States (AOSIS) and the G77 grouping, COP27 (Sharm el-Sheikh, November 2022) produced the breakthrough “Sharm el-Sheikh Implementation Plan” with an agreement in principle to establish a dedicated Loss and Damage Fund for vulnerable developing countries. A Transitional Committee was tasked with operationalisation. COP28 (Dubai, November–December 2023), presided over by Sultan Al Jaber (simultaneously CEO of Abu Dhabi National Oil Company), operationalised the Fund on Day 1 — a procedural milestone — with initial pledges totalling ~$700 million (UAE $100m, Germany $100m, UK £60m, US $17.5m, Japan $10m). Critics including the Climate Action Network observed that the figure was <0.2% of the hundreds of billions in annual climate-driven damages in developing countries. COP28 also produced the first Global Stocktake under the Paris Agreement, confirming the world is far off-track (current NDCs imply warming of ~2.5–2.9 °C by 2100) and adopting the “UAE Consensus” text calling for “transitioning away from fossil fuels in energy systems, in a just, orderly and equitable manner” — the first explicit naming of fossil fuels in a COP decision, although the weaker language (“transitioning away” rather than “phase out”) was contested by AOSIS. Synoptic: climate (2.2), global governance effectiveness (2.5), North–South equity, ocean-linked impacts (2.4).

Shorter supporting case studies

Beyond the five required major cases, Eduqas candidates benefit from a portfolio of smaller place-specific examples that can be dropped into 2.5 essays.

  • Tuvalu — digital statehood: in November 2022, the Tuvaluan Foreign Minister Simon Kofe proposed the world’s first “digital nation”, preserving cultural heritage and statehood in the metaverse in anticipation of physical submergence. By 2024 Tuvalu’s constitutional amendments formally asserted the persistence of statehood even without territory.
  • Rwanda — developmental state model: GDP growth averaged >7% in the 2010s; state-led, tech-forward (Kigali Innovation City), UK migration “offshoring” deal (2022–2024, ruled unlawful by the UK Supreme Court November 2023).
  • Qatar — soft-power spending: 2022 FIFA World Cup (~$220 bn infrastructure); Al Jazeera; Qatar Airways as a state geopolitical instrument; mediator in the Israel-Hamas ceasefire negotiations 2023–24.
  • Taiwan — semiconductor chokepoint: TSMC produces ~90% of the world’s most advanced chips (<5nm). A single island supplies the informational backbone of the global economy — a geopolitical risk visible in US CHIPS Act (2022) and Biden-Xi tensions.
  • Pacific climate migration: Australia’s 2023 “Falepili Union” with Tuvalu offered permanent-residency pathways for ~280 Tuvaluans annually — an early template for treaty-based climate mobility.
  • Wales (UK) — Well-being of Future Generations Act: as noted, constitutionally embedded SDG-style goals; the Future Generations Commissioner has statutory authority to challenge public-body decisions.
  • Gaza, 2023–2024: Israel-Hamas war since October 2023; ~2.3 million population; >40,000 reported deaths by mid-2024 (Gaza Ministry of Health); ICJ “plausible genocide” ruling January 2024. Illustrates both acute humanitarian crisis and the limits of global governance (UN vetoes).
  • Sudan, 2023–present: civil war between SAF and RSF from April 2023; >10 million displaced — the world’s largest displacement crisis; famine formally declared in Zamzam camp, August 2024.

17. Cross-Cutting Theme — Demography

Demographic change is the slowest-moving but most predictable 21st-century transformation. Four patterns dominate.

  • Continued population growth in sub-Saharan Africa: from ~1.15 bn (2023) to ~2.1 bn by 2050 (UN medium variant). Niger’s total fertility rate was still ~6.7 in 2023.
  • Ageing in Europe, East Asia, North America: Japan’s population peaked in 2008 and is projected to fall from ~124m to ~87m by 2070. China’s population fell in 2022 for the first time since the 1961 famine.
  • India overtaking China as the most populous country (2023): a symbolic shift with implications for UN Security Council reform debates.
  • Global population peak <11 bn: most demographers now expect peak before 2100. This is quite different from the Malthusian predictions that dominated 20th-century debate.
Demographic dividend: The economic growth potential that arises when a country’s working-age population is unusually large relative to its dependents — the stage at which fertility has fallen but the pre-fall cohort is still in the labour market. India, Nigeria, Ethiopia and Bangladesh are entering this window; East Asia already banked it in the 1960s–1990s; Europe has exited it.

Demography interacts powerfully with every other 21st-century challenge: with climate (Africa’s emissions profile as it industrialises), with migration (ageing-driven demand in the North vs growth-driven supply in the South), with inequality (young unemployed populations and political instability), with food security (feeding 10 billion), and with global governance (a UNSC still structured around 1945 demographic weights).

18. Cross-Cutting Theme — Urbanisation

More than 56% of humanity (~4.5 billion people) now live in urban areas; UN DESA projects ~68% by 2050. Almost all population growth to 2050 will be urban and most of it will be in Asia and Africa. Cities are simultaneously engines of 21st-century development and concentrations of 21st-century risks.

  • Megacities: >10 million population; there are ~34 in 2024 (UN DESA). Tokyo (~37m) leads, followed by Delhi (~33m), Shanghai (~29m), São Paulo (~22m), Dhaka, Cairo, Mumbai, Beijing, Mexico City, Osaka, Karachi.
  • Informal settlements / slums: ~1.1 billion urban residents live in slums or inadequate housing (UN-Habitat). Kibera (Nairobi), Dharavi (Mumbai), Orangi (Karachi), Neza (Mexico City) are canonical cases.
  • Urban climate risk: >800 million urban residents are exposed to coastal flood risk (Hallegatte et al., 2013); a 1-metre sea-level rise by 2100 would threaten Jakarta, Ho Chi Minh City, Bangkok, Lagos, Miami.
  • Smart cities and digital twins: Singapore, Barcelona, Helsinki are experimenting with data-driven urban governance; ethical concerns about surveillance.
  • Right to the city (Lefebvre): an influential normative framing for urban-justice movements from Bogotá to Barcelona.

Urbanisation is a synoptic nexus: it links climate (heat islands, emissions), water (2.1), energy (2.2), migration (2.3), inequality and governance. Many of the 21st-century’s crucial policy choices will be urban choices.

19. Cross-Cutting Theme — Biodiversity Loss

Biodiversity is the fourth of the nine planetary boundaries currently transgressed (Rockström update, 2023). WWF’s Living Planet Index 2022 reported an average ~69% decline in monitored vertebrate populations since 1970. Drivers in descending order: land-use change (agriculture & forestry), direct exploitation (hunting, fishing), climate change, pollution, invasive species. IPBES 2019 estimated ~1 million species threatened with extinction.

Governance responses:

  • Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD, 1992, Rio): 196 parties; the US has not ratified.
  • Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework (GBF, COP15, December 2022): 23 targets for 2030, including the headline “30 by 30” (protect 30% of land and sea by 2030) and a pledge to mobilise $200 bn/year for biodiversity by 2030.
  • CITES (1973): governs trade in endangered species; 184 parties.
  • BBNJ Treaty (2023): high-seas marine protected areas (Topic 2.4 link).
  • IUCN Red List: scientific authority on extinction risk.

Biodiversity and climate are increasingly treated as twin crises: a degraded biosphere sequesters less carbon; climate change shifts ecological zones faster than many species can adapt. The nature-based solutions agenda — mangrove restoration, peatland protection, reforestation — attempts to solve both together.

Biodiversity crises are highly located. Hotspots include:

  • Amazon basin: ~17% deforested; a 2023 Nature paper warned that the eastern Amazon is now a net carbon source.
  • Coral Triangle: Indonesia–Philippines–Solomon Islands; highest marine biodiversity on Earth; vulnerable to bleaching (fifth mass bleaching event declared April 2024).
  • Congo Basin: second-largest tropical forest; under pressure from mining (cobalt, coltan), logging, agriculture.
  • Madagascar: ~80% of species endemic; severe habitat loss.
  • East African rift lakes: cichlid fish diversity; pollution and invasive species.

20. Evaluating Global Governance

A robust evaluation of 21st-century global governance requires distinguishing three criteria.

  • Effectiveness — does the regime change behaviour? The Montreal Protocol (1987) on ozone is the gold-standard success (99% compliance, ozone layer projected to recover by the 2060s). The Paris Agreement is weaker: no enforcement, but NDCs have shifted expectations (central estimate moved from ~3.5 °C to ~2.7 °C since 2015). The WTO’s Appellate Body has been paralysed since December 2019 because the US blocks appointments.
  • Legitimacy — is the process perceived as fair? The UN Security Council’s 1945 geography (five P5 vetoes; no African or Latin American permanent member) is the most obvious legitimacy deficit. Bretton Woods conventions (US president of the World Bank, European head of the IMF) are criticised. BRICS and the G77+ bloc increasingly challenge this asymmetry.
  • Adaptive capacity — can the regime evolve? The 2023 BBNJ (High Seas) Treaty is evidence of UNCLOS evolving (see Topic 2.4); the Pandemic Treaty and WHO IHR reforms are tests of whether the WHO can adapt after COVID. The UN Security Council reform process has been stalled since 2005 — evidence of adaptive failure.
Exam tip — use the “effectiveness · legitimacy · adaptive capacity” triad: Deploy this three-part evaluation when the question is “assess global governance of X”. Each of the three becomes a paragraph with evidence. You will almost always reach a nuanced conclusion: governance is partial — effective in some domains, legitimate in some respects, adaptive in some areas — and the distribution of these matters more than a single grade.
Exam tip — structuring a 20-mark essay: (1) Introduction — define key terms, state a clear thesis, outline scope. (2) Three evaluative paragraphs — each with claim, case study, counter-evidence, mini-conclusion. (3) Synoptic bridge paragraph — draw explicit links across topics (e.g. climate → migration → food). (4) Conclusion — return to thesis, qualify it with scale/temporal caveats. Use signposting: “furthermore”, “however”, “conversely”, “overall”.
Exam tip — avoid the “everything-is-terrible” trap: Weaker essays paint a uniformly bleak picture. Stronger essays acknowledge progress — global extreme poverty fell from ~38% of world population in 1990 to ~9% by 2019 (World Bank); child mortality halved since 2000; the ozone layer is recovering; renewable capacity is scaling faster than predicted. Balanced evaluation earns higher band credit than either boosterism or doom.

Non-state actors in global governance

It is a mistake to treat global governance as exclusively interstate. A rich ecosystem of non-state actors shapes outcomes:

  • International NGOs: Oxfam, Amnesty International, Human Rights Watch, Greenpeace, WWF, Médecins Sans Frontières. Provide research, advocacy, and service delivery; ~10 NGOs deliver the majority of humanitarian aid.
  • Transnational advocacy networks (Keck & Sikkink, 1998): the climate movement (Fridays for Future from 2018, XR from 2018), debt-relief coalitions (Jubilee 2000), the anti-landmines coalition that produced the 1997 Ottawa Treaty.
  • Philanthropic foundations: Gates Foundation (~$70 bn endowment, GAVI, polio eradication), Bloomberg Philanthropies (C40 Cities), Open Philanthropy.
  • Epistemic communities: IPCC, IPBES, the medical community around the WHO. Expert networks shaping consensus.
  • Cities and sub-national governments: C40 Cities (97 mayors representing ~700m people), Under2 Coalition of sub-national leaders, US State Department “America Is All In” coalition during the 2017–21 federal climate withdrawal.
  • Private sector: TNCs set de facto global standards (Walmart’s supply-chain sustainability rules, Apple’s privacy defaults); net-zero pledges by firms covering ~80% of global market capitalisation; corporate climate reporting regimes (TCFD, ISSB 2023).
  • Indigenous peoples: ~6% of global population but stewards of ~80% of remaining biodiversity (UN); increasingly recognised in climate and biodiversity treaties as rights-holders, not just stakeholders.

The resulting “polycentric” (Ostrom) or “regime-complex” (Keohane and Victor) structure means that progress on any 21st-century challenge typically requires alignment across multiple levels, not just among states at the UN.

21. The Future of the UK in a Changing World

Eduqas is the Welsh awarding body and examiners value UK- and Wales-specific material. Key points for a 2.5 answer:

  • Post-Brexit trade: the UK formally left the EU on 31 January 2020; the Trade and Cooperation Agreement (TCA, 1 January 2021) preserves tariff-free goods trade but imposes customs paperwork. OBR estimates a ~4% long-run productivity hit versus counterfactual EU membership. New trade deals: UK–Australia (2021), UK–New Zealand (2022), CPTPP accession (ratified 2024).
  • “Global Britain”: the 2021 and 2023 Integrated Reviews repositioned UK foreign policy with an “Indo-Pacific tilt”; AUKUS (September 2021) is the flagship security partnership with Australia and the USA.
  • Climate leadership: the UK was first major economy to legislate net zero by 2050 (2019 amendment to Climate Change Act 2008); hosted COP26. But the Climate Change Committee (CCC) judged UK policy “significantly off-track” for its 2030 NDC in its 2024 progress report; 2023–24 policy reversals (delay of ICE-car ban to 2035) generated criticism.
  • Wales specifically: the Well-being of Future Generations (Wales) Act 2015 obliges public bodies to consider long-term impacts, making Wales the only nation in the world to embed SDG-style goals in constitutional legislation. Wales declared a climate emergency in April 2019. Welsh Government targets net zero for its public sector by 2030.
  • Commonwealth relations: 56 member states, ~2.5 bn people, but the institution’s political weight has arguably waned; post-Elizabeth II succession posed legitimacy questions.
  • Development and aid: the UK merged DFID into the FCO in 2020 to form the FCDO, and cut the aid budget from 0.7% to 0.5% of GNI in 2021 — moves criticised by the House of Commons International Development Committee and by NGOs such as Bond and Oxfam. In-country aid spending on refugees (counted as ODA under OECD rules) now consumes ~29% of the shrunken budget, reducing what reaches low-income countries.
  • Defence and security: UK commitment to spend 2.5% of GDP on defence by 2030 (2024 announcement); continued role in NATO, AUKUS, Joint Expeditionary Force; nuclear deterrent renewal (Dreadnought-class submarines).

The UK is therefore simultaneously a mid-sized power navigating a multipolar world, a climate leader in rhetoric but slipping in policy, and a test case for whether an old-industrial economy can re-invent itself through finance, services, low-carbon technology and education exports. For Eduqas candidates, the UK/Wales can be deployed as a case-within-a-case in any 2.5 essay.

22. Theoretical Frames for 21st-Century Geography

Eduqas examiners reward explicit use of concepts from human geography. The following frames are frequently useful in 2.5 essays.

  • Globalisation theory (Held, McGrew): hyperglobalists see a borderless world, sceptics emphasise continued state power, transformationalists argue that globalisation transforms but does not abolish the state.
  • World-systems theory (Wallerstein): core, semi-periphery, periphery; an economic hierarchy reproduced through the structure of trade.
  • Dependency theory (Frank, Cardoso): “the development of underdevelopment” — peripheral economies are kept poor by their relationship with core economies, not by some internal deficit.
  • Modernisation theory (Rostow): linear stages from traditional to high-mass-consumption; heavily criticised but still influential in aid practice.
  • Planetary boundaries (Rockström et al., 2009, updated 2023): nine Earth-system processes (climate change, biosphere integrity, land-system change, freshwater change, biogeochemical flows, ocean acidification, atmospheric aerosol loading, stratospheric ozone depletion, novel entities). Six are now transgressed according to the 2023 update.
  • Doughnut economics (Raworth, 2017): combines planetary boundaries with a social foundation (12 dimensions of well-being) to define a safe and just operating space for humanity.
  • Anthropocene: proposed geological epoch recognising humanity’s global influence on the Earth system; the Anthropocene Working Group proposed the mid-20th-century “Great Acceleration” as a start date, though the International Commission on Stratigraphy declined formal ratification in 2024.
  • Risk society (Beck, 1986): modernity has become defined by the production and management of manufactured risks (nuclear, financial, climate, digital) that exceed national boundaries.
  • Cosmopolitanism (Held): the moral community is humanity, not the nation — a normative frame for global-justice arguments.

23. Contested Keywords and Concepts for 21st-Century Essays

High-band essays are fluent in contested terms — terms which different actors use to mean different things, and whose definition is itself politically loaded.

  • “Development”: economic growth? capability expansion? freedom? well-being? Each definition implies different policies.
  • “Sovereignty”: Westphalian (absolute territorial control)? Pooled (EU-style)? Responsible (R2P, 2005)? Digital? Climate-constrained?
  • “Security”: traditional military? human security (UNDP 1994: economic, food, health, environmental, personal, community, political)? climate security? biosecurity?
  • “Just transition”: who pays the cost of decarbonisation? ILO framework emphasises workers and communities dependent on fossil fuels.
  • “Resilience”: bouncing back (engineering) or bouncing forward (transformative)? Criticised as depoliticising by some geographers (Hayes 2014).
  • “Net zero”: gross emission cuts or offsetting? GHG Protocol Scope 1/2/3 distinctions matter; “science-based targets” (SBTi) attempt rigour.
  • “Climate justice”: distributive (who bears costs), procedural (who gets a seat at COP), recognitional (which worldviews count, including indigenous perspectives).

25. Essay Plans for Typical 2.5 Questions

Q: “To what extent is climate change the most serious 21st-century challenge? (20)”

  • Introduction: define “serious” (scale, reversibility, spatial reach, speed); flag synoptic approach; state thesis (e.g. climate is the most structurally serious because it interlocks with all other challenges).
  • Para 1 — climate as global integrator: IPCC AR6 evidence; 1.5 °C breach 2024; tipping points; SIDS; SSPs.
  • Para 2 — counter-challenges: pandemics (COVID-19 case), conflict (Ukraine), inequality (Credit Suisse 43%), AI disruption. Argue they may be faster or more immediate.
  • Para 3 — interlock: climate amplifies all others (migration, food, conflict) — synoptic argument for climate as meta-challenge.
  • Conclusion: yes in structural terms, but evaluated against immediacy other challenges may be as pressing. Qualified thesis.

Q: “Assess the effectiveness of global governance in managing 21st-century challenges. (20)”

  • Introduction: define global governance (not just UN); introduce effectiveness·legitimacy·adaptive triad.
  • Para 1 — successes: Montreal Protocol, Paris Agreement narrowing the warming range, BBNJ treaty, SDGs as coordinating frame.
  • Para 2 — failures: Ukraine and UNSC paralysis; COVAX; WTO Appellate Body; climate finance shortfall.
  • Para 3 — mixed: L&D fund operationalised but tiny; BRICS+ as challenger; NGO and private-sector roles.
  • Conclusion: governance is not an on/off switch; it works unevenly across issue areas; argue for reform rather than dismissal.

Q: “To what extent are superpowers in decline? (20)”

  • Introduction: define superpower (hard/soft/structural); note the question’s implicit focus on the US.
  • Para 1 — evidence of US relative decline: share of world GDP (~40% 1960 → ~25% 2023); PPP overtaken by China; manufacturing dominance lost; WTO paralysis; Iraq and Afghanistan defeats.
  • Para 2 — evidence of persistent US dominance: dollar ~88% of FX; largest military spending; AI frontier; tech-platform global reach; universities; USMCA; alliances (NATO, AUKUS).
  • Para 3 — rise of challengers: China (BRI case study), India demographics, BRICS+, EU as regulatory power.
  • Conclusion: shift from unipolar to multipolar — US relatively declining but not absolutely; rivals “rising” unevenly; structural power remains largely US/Western.

Q: “Evaluate the statement that the Brandt Line is no longer useful. (20)”

  • Introduction: origins of Brandt Line; purpose as simplification.
  • Para 1 — reasons it persists: still captures aggregate HDI gap; most LDCs south of the line; cumulative emissions asymmetry; historical relations (colonialism).
  • Para 2 — reasons it misleads: China, India, Brazil rising; Gulf wealth; intra-national inequality (e.g. south Wales).
  • Para 3 — alternatives: Rosling levels, World Bank income groups, world-systems, Doughnut.
  • Conclusion: useful as heuristic with explicit caveats; dangerous if treated as literal description.

Q: “Discuss the view that migration is both a cause and a consequence of 21st-century challenges. (20)”

  • Introduction: define migration types (Topic 2.3); acknowledge the two-way relationship; flag synoptic scope.
  • Para 1 — migration as consequence: Ukraine refugees (war), Syria (conflict + climate drought), Venezuela (economic collapse), Sahel (climate + conflict), Sudan.
  • Para 2 — migration as cause: remittances driving development; demographic balance in ageing states; brain drain weakening source countries; xenophobic backlash reshaping politics (Brexit, AfD).
  • Para 3 — feedback loops: climate displacement + political reaction + weaker multilateralism; positive loops (diaspora investment).
  • Conclusion: migration is constitutive of 21st-century geography, not peripheral.

Q: “Assess the statement that global challenges demand global solutions. (20)”

  • Introduction: define “global” challenge vs locally concentrated challenge; define solution scales.
  • Para 1 — yes, global: climate (atmosphere), pandemics (borders porous), oceans (commons), AI (cross-border).
  • Para 2 — but local implementation: Paris NDCs nationally set; cities drive emissions cuts; agroecology locally adapted.
  • Para 3 — “glocal” governance: Ostrom’s polycentric systems; subsidiarity; C40; indigenous stewardship.
  • Conclusion: not a dichotomy — global frameworks + local implementation. Pure globalism produces legitimacy deficits; pure localism cannot tackle truly global commons.
