2.1 Water Cycle & Water Insecurity

Geography — Eduqas A-Level (A110QS) — Component 2: Global Systems

1. The Global Hydrological Cycle

The hydrological cycle is the continuous, closed-system circulation of water between the oceans, atmosphere, land and biosphere, powered by solar radiation and gravity. At the planetary scale it is a closed system: no significant water enters or leaves the Earth, so the total global volume of ~1.386 billion km3 is effectively constant. At local drainage-basin scale, however, the cycle operates as an open system: inputs (precipitation) and outputs (evapotranspiration, discharge) cross the basin boundary. The distinction between closed global and open local systems is a frequent AO1 requirement at Eduqas A-level.

Hydrological cycle: The continuous circulation of water between oceans, atmosphere, land surfaces and biosphere through the processes of evaporation, condensation, precipitation, infiltration, runoff and transpiration. Globally closed but locally open at basin scale.

Global Water Stores

Although the hydrological cycle looks dynamic in textbook diagrams, 99.3% of global water sits almost motionless in three huge reservoirs: the oceans, the cryosphere (ice caps and glaciers) and groundwater. Only about 0.04% of global water is atmospheric at any moment, yet this tiny flux delivers all terrestrial precipitation. Understanding the asymmetry between store size and flux rate is essential: small changes to a huge store (e.g. melting Greenland ice) can dwarf annual precipitation flux.

Global Fluxes

The hydrological cycle's fluxes must balance annually. Global evaporation totals approximately 505,000 km3/yr, 86% of which (~434,000 km3) leaves the oceans; the remaining 14% (~71,000 km3) is continental evapotranspiration. Precipitation balances this: ~398,000 km3 falls on the oceans, ~107,000 km3 on the land. The imbalance between continental precipitation (107,000) and evapotranspiration (71,000) is ~36,000 km3/yr of runoff returning to the oceans via rivers and submarine groundwater discharge. This figure — global runoff — represents the renewable freshwater resource available to humanity.

Beyond evaporation and precipitation, several additional fluxes operate at surface and sub-surface scale: transpiration (water released by plant stomata; combined with direct evaporation gives evapotranspiration, ET); infiltration (water entering the soil at the surface); percolation (downward movement through unsaturated soil into groundwater); throughflow (lateral movement through soil to streams); overland flow (surface runoff); groundwater flow; and channel flow (river discharge). Each has a characteristic velocity and each feeds back onto stores at different timescales.

Exam tip — always quote a figure: In A-level questions on the hydrological cycle, adding precise data (“oceans hold 1.338 billion km3, or 96.5% of global water”; “global runoff ~36,000 km3/yr is the renewable freshwater resource”) lifts answers from AO1 band 2 into band 3. Memorise at least three flux figures and three store percentages.

2. Residence Times and Management Implications

The residence time of a water store is the average length of time a water molecule spends there before moving on: Residence time = Store volume / Flux rate. Residence times vary across fifteen orders of magnitude and directly control how sustainable human abstraction can be. Water drawn from a store with a short residence time (soil, rivers) is replenished within a year; water drawn from long-residence stores (deep aquifers, ice sheets) is effectively non-renewable on human timescales, making abstraction analogous to fossil-fuel mining.

Residence time: The average time a water molecule remains in a given store before passing through a flux to another store, calculated as store volume divided by flux rate. Short residence times imply rapid renewal; long residence times imply effectively non-renewable resources on human timescales.

3. The Drainage Basin Hydrological Cycle

At the sub-global scale, water geographers work with the drainage basin as the fundamental unit of analysis. A drainage basin is the area of land drained by a single river and its tributaries, bounded by a topographic ridge called the watershed. Unlike the global cycle, a basin is an open system: precipitation crosses the watershed as input, while evapotranspiration (vertical) and river discharge (horizontal) cross it as outputs.

Drainage basin: The area of land drained by a river and its tributaries, bounded by a watershed. Functions as an open hydrological system with inputs of precipitation, stores in vegetation, surface and subsurface, flows between stores and outputs of evapotranspiration and channel discharge.

Inputs

  • Precipitation — dominant input. The type (rain, snow, hail, sleet), intensity (mm/hr), duration, spatial distribution and seasonality all shape basin behaviour. Convective thunderstorms give short, high-intensity inputs that often exceed infiltration capacity; frontal rain gives prolonged lower-intensity inputs that saturate soils gradually; orographic rain concentrates precipitation on windward slopes.
  • Snowmelt acts as a delayed precipitation input: snow falling in winter is stored until spring melt releases it rapidly, often generating annual peak discharges (e.g. Colorado River, Rhine).

Stores

  • Interception — water held on the canopy, stems and litter of vegetation before reaching the ground. Interception loss can be 10–30% of gross rainfall for temperate deciduous woodland, 25–40% for coniferous forest.
  • Surface storage — puddles (depression storage), ponds, lakes, wetlands.
  • Soil moisture — water held in pore spaces in the soil above the water table. Field capacity is the maximum water the soil can hold against gravity.
  • Groundwater — water held in saturated permeable rock below the water table. Aquifers are rock units capable of yielding usable quantities.
  • Channel storage — water in rivers, streams and canals.
Aquifer: A body of saturated permeable rock (e.g. chalk, sandstone) capable of storing and transmitting groundwater in economically useful quantities. Unconfined aquifers have a free water table connected to the atmosphere; confined aquifers are sealed beneath an impermeable layer (aquiclude), often under artesian pressure.

Flows

  • Stemflow — water running down tree trunks to the ground.
  • Throughfall — water dripping through the canopy.
  • Infiltration — vertical entry of water into the soil surface. Infiltration capacity is the maximum rate at which soil can accept water and declines during a storm as soil wets.
  • Percolation — downward movement through unsaturated soil into bedrock and groundwater.
  • Throughflow — lateral movement of water within soil, often along lines of greater permeability, toward streams. Typical velocities 1–100 mm/hr.
  • Overland flow — surface runoff, of two kinds: Hortonian (infiltration-excess) overland flow occurs when rainfall intensity exceeds infiltration capacity (common in arid, compacted or urban surfaces); saturation-excess overland flow occurs when soil is already saturated and can accept no more water (common in temperate humid basins with rising water tables near channels).
  • Groundwater flow / baseflow — slow flow of water through saturated rock, feeding rivers between storms and sustaining flow during droughts. Typical velocities 0.01–10 m/day.
  • Channel flow — water flowing in rivers; measured as discharge (Q = velocity × cross-sectional area, m3/s).
Infiltration: The vertical entry of water into the soil surface from rainfall or surface water. Rate controlled by soil texture, structure, moisture, vegetation, slope and rainfall intensity. Limited by the infiltration capacity of the soil.
Percolation: The slow downward movement of water through unsaturated soil and permeable rock under gravity, recharging groundwater stores. Distinct from infiltration (which is surface entry) and throughflow (which is lateral).
Throughflow: Lateral movement of water within the soil layer, downslope toward a stream channel, often concentrated in natural pipes and along the interface between soil horizons of different permeability. Velocities are intermediate between overland flow (fast) and groundwater flow (slow).

Outputs

  • Evapotranspiration (ET) — combined evaporation from soil, water surfaces and vegetation plus plant transpiration. Potential evapotranspiration (PET) is the water that would be lost if unlimited water were available; actual evapotranspiration (AET) is the water actually lost, limited by available moisture.
  • Discharge to the sea or to a terminal basin — the integrated basin output as river flow.

4. Water Budgets and Flood Hydrographs

The Water Budget Equation

For any basin over any time period, water balance requires inputs = outputs ± change in storage:

P = Q + E + ΔS

where P is precipitation, Q is discharge (runoff), E is evapotranspiration and ΔS is change in basin storage (soil moisture + groundwater + surface water). On an annual timescale for a basin in steady state ΔS ~ 0 and P = Q + E. Over shorter periods ΔS can be large and positive (winter recharge) or negative (summer depletion), producing seasonal mismatch between precipitation supply and available river flow.

Water budget (balance): The accounting equation for inputs, outputs and storage change in a drainage basin over a defined period: P = Q + E + ΔS. Used by water managers to assess resource availability, identify seasonal deficits/surpluses and set sustainable abstraction limits.

Potential vs Actual Evapotranspiration

PET is set by atmospheric demand (solar radiation, temperature, humidity, wind); AET is constrained by moisture supply. In a UK temperate basin, PET during summer can exceed precipitation, creating a soil moisture deficit; AET is then less than PET because soil moisture is depleted. Autumn rainfall recharges soil to field capacity, and excess precipitation becomes soil moisture surplus that percolates to groundwater and sustains winter baseflow. Monthly water-balance graphs for Kew (southeast England) show deficit from ~May to September, utilisation in autumn, recharge in late autumn/winter, and surplus December to March.

The Flood (Storm) Hydrograph

A flood hydrograph shows river discharge (m3/s) against time following a rainfall event. Its shape records the way a basin translates rainfall into runoff. Key features:

  • Rising limb — rapid increase in discharge as overland flow and throughflow reach the channel. Steepness reflects how quickly runoff is generated.
  • Peak discharge — the maximum flow rate reached. Its magnitude controls flood severity.
  • Lag time — the interval between peak rainfall and peak discharge. Short lag times characterise flashy basins (urban, impermeable); long lag times characterise permeable, vegetated basins.
  • Recessing (falling) limb — gradual decline in discharge as direct runoff ceases and flow is sustained by slower throughflow and baseflow.
  • Baseflow — the underlying flow sustained by groundwater between storms.
  • Storm flow (quickflow) — the portion of discharge above baseflow, attributable to the storm event.
Lag time: The time interval between the peak of rainfall input and the peak of river discharge on a flood hydrograph. Short lag times (flashy response) indicate rapid runoff generation — typical of urban, small, steep, impermeable or saturated basins. Long lag times indicate buffered response — typical of large, vegetated, permeable basins.

5. Short- and Long-Term Variations in the Cycle

The hydrological cycle is not static. Natural climatic oscillations, human land-use change and long-term anthropogenic warming all drive variability at timescales from days to millennia.

Natural Variability

  • El Niño Southern Oscillation (ENSO) — quasi-periodic (2–7 yr) coupled ocean–atmosphere oscillation in the tropical Pacific. In El Niño phases, weakened trade winds shift warm surface water east, causing drought in Australia, Indonesia and the Amazon and flooding along the Peru/Ecuador coast and south-east USA. La Niña reverses these anomalies. The 2015–16 El Niño, among the strongest on record, coincided with drought in Ethiopia, the worst on record in southern Africa, and severe bleaching of the Great Barrier Reef.
  • Indian (South-Asian) monsoon — the seasonal reversal of wind direction and rainfall brings ~75% of India's annual precipitation between June and September. Inter-annual variability of ±10% of the mean delivers either widespread drought (1987, 2002, 2009, 2015) or flooding (2005 Mumbai, 2018 Kerala).
  • Sahel drought cycles — the West African Sahel experienced catastrophic drought 1968–1985, linked to cooling of the North Atlantic (AMO), deforestation and overgrazing, with ~100,000 deaths. Partial recovery since the 1990s has been uneven and contested.
  • North Atlantic Oscillation (NAO) — pressure seesaw between the Icelandic Low and Azores High, modulating winter storm tracks across the UK and western Europe.

Human-Induced Variability — Land Use Change

  • Deforestation reduces interception and transpiration, lowers infiltration capacity via soil compaction and increases overland flow. In the Amazon, modelling suggests that removing 40% of forest cover could shift the basin past a tipping point where rainfall recycling fails and tropical forest converts to savanna.
  • Urbanisation replaces permeable soil and vegetation with impermeable tarmac and concrete, removes interception, accelerates runoff via storm drains and increases flood peaks while reducing lag times. The UK Environment Agency estimates that over 3 million UK properties are currently at risk of surface-water flooding.
  • Agriculture — land drainage increases channel delivery; ploughing along slopes increases overland flow; compaction by heavy machinery reduces infiltration; irrigation returns water to atmosphere via ET.
  • Dams and reservoirs store water, delay downstream discharge, reduce flood peaks, but also evaporate substantial volumes (Lake Nasser behind the Aswan High Dam loses ~10–15 km3/yr to evaporation) and trap sediment.

Climate Change and the Intensification of the Cycle

The Clausius–Clapeyron relationship means a warmer atmosphere holds ~7% more water vapour per 1°C of warming. The IPCC AR6 (2021) concludes with high confidence that the global hydrological cycle is intensifying: evaporation and precipitation totals both increase, extremes become more pronounced, and the spatial pattern shifts — wet regions tend to become wetter and dry regions drier (the “dry-get-drier, wet-get-wetter” paradigm, with important regional exceptions). Consequences include:

  • More intense precipitation events, increasing flood risk even where annual totals are unchanged.
  • Longer, more severe droughts in Mediterranean, south-west USA, southern Africa, Australia.
  • Declining snowpack in mid-latitude mountains, earlier spring melt, and altered river seasonality (e.g. Sierra Nevada snowpack, California).
  • Glacier retreat threatening “water tower” rivers — the Himalayan rivers (Indus, Ganges, Brahmaputra, Mekong, Yangtze) supply ~1.9 billion people.
  • Sea-level rise and saline intrusion into coastal aquifers (e.g. Ghyben–Herzberg interface advance in Bangladesh, Florida, Nile Delta).
Exam tip — intensification, not simple “more water”: Top-band answers avoid vague statements such as “climate change means more rain”. Use the phrase intensification of the hydrological cycle, link to the Clausius–Clapeyron 7%/°C rule, and specify both wet extremes (flooding) and dry extremes (drought, snowpack loss) in named regions.

6. Water Quality

Water security depends not only on quantity but also on quality. Water quality indicators span three domains: physical (turbidity, temperature, colour, taste, odour), chemical (pH, dissolved oxygen, nutrients, heavy metals, pesticides, chlorides, hardness) and biological (bacteria, viruses, protozoa, BOD — biochemical oxygen demand).

Pollution Sources

  • Industrial — heavy metals (Cd, Hg, Pb), organic solvents, thermal pollution from power-station cooling water, hydrocarbons from refineries, acid mine drainage.
  • Agricultural — nitrate and phosphate runoff from fertilisers, pesticides, organic loading from livestock slurry. Excess N and P cause eutrophication: algal blooms, oxygen depletion and fish kills in lakes, rivers and coastal zones (the Gulf of Mexico “dead zone” of ~15,000 km2 is driven by Mississippi nutrient runoff).
  • Domestic sewage — organic matter, pathogens (cholera, typhoid, cryptosporidium), phosphates from detergents, pharmaceuticals and micro-plastics. UK combined sewer overflows (CSOs) released raw sewage for over 3.6 million hours in 2023 (Environment Agency), triggering public outcry.
  • Mining — acid mine drainage, sediment loading, heavy metals.
  • Plastic pollution — ~1,000 rivers contribute ~80% of plastic entering oceans; the Pasig (Philippines), Citarum (Indonesia) and Yangtze (China) rank highest for plastic loading.
Eutrophication: The enrichment of water bodies with plant nutrients (principally nitrogen and phosphorus) from agricultural runoff, sewage or detergents, causing algal blooms, oxygen depletion through decomposition of dead algae, and mass mortality of aquatic life. A leading cause of freshwater ecological degradation globally.

7. Water Insecurity and Water Stress

Water security was defined by UN-Water (2013) as “the capacity of a population to safeguard sustainable access to adequate quantities of acceptable quality water for sustaining livelihoods, human well-being and socio-economic development, for ensuring protection against water-borne pollution and water-related disasters, and for preserving ecosystems in a climate of peace and political stability.” Water insecurity is therefore a multidimensional failure: insufficient quantity, inadequate quality, unreliable supply, or inaccessible cost/infrastructure.

Water stress: A situation in which renewable freshwater availability per capita falls below 1,700 m3/person/year (Falkenmark indicator). Stress becomes water scarcity below 1,000 m3, absolute scarcity below 500 m3. These thresholds are widely used by the UN and World Bank but mask important seasonal, spatial and quality variations.

Physical vs Economic Water Scarcity

  • Physical (absolute) scarcity — insufficient renewable water to meet demand regardless of wealth or infrastructure. Characteristic of arid and semi-arid regions: the Sahara, Arabian Peninsula, Central Asia, parts of the Colorado, Indus and Yellow basins. Approximately 1.2 billion people live in areas of physical water scarcity.
  • Economic water scarcity — physical resources exist but are inaccessible because of lack of investment, infrastructure, institutions or governance. Characteristic of much of sub-Saharan Africa: the Congo basin has huge renewable water resources but over 60% of the population lacks safely managed drinking water. Approximately 1.6 billion people face economic water scarcity.

Drivers of Rising Water Demand

  • Population — global demand is projected to rise ~55% by 2050 (OECD), principally in Asia and Africa.
  • Urbanisation — 68% of humans will live in cities by 2050 (UN); urban per-capita demand exceeds rural.
  • Rising living standards — dietary shift to meat and dairy sharply increases embedded water use (1 kg beef requires ~15,400 L water; 1 kg wheat ~1,800 L).
  • Irrigation — ~70% of global freshwater withdrawal is for agriculture, dominated by irrigation; flood irrigation (often the cheapest) wastes up to 60% through evaporation and percolation.
  • Industrial use — ~19% of global withdrawals; energy sector (cooling of thermoelectric power, hydraulic fracturing) competes directly with agriculture.

UN Sustainable Development Goal 6

SDG 6 (“Ensure availability and sustainable management of water and sanitation for all”) targets universal access to safely managed drinking water and sanitation by 2030. The UN 2023 mid-term review reports that progress is off-track: 2.2 billion people still lack safely managed drinking water and 3.5 billion lack safely managed sanitation. At current rates universal access will not be reached before 2080.

8. Water Conflicts — Transboundary Basins

There are approximately 260 international river basins, covering 46% of the Earth's land surface and home to ~40% of the world's population. Basins shared by multiple sovereign states generate intrinsic hydropolitics: upstream states enjoy first access and can divert, dam or pollute, while downstream states bear the consequences. The resulting asymmetry of power is exacerbated when upstream states are also richer or militarily stronger.

Two UN instruments govern shared freshwater:

  • UN Watercourses Convention (1997, in force 2014) codifies principles of “equitable and reasonable utilisation” and “no significant harm”. Only 37 states have ratified; key basin powers (China, Turkey, Egypt) have not.
  • UNECE Water Convention (1992), originally European, opened to global accession 2016; promotes cooperation on transboundary basins.

Despite scholarly pessimism about “water wars”, the empirical record (Oregon State University's Transboundary Freshwater Dispute Database) records more instances of cooperation than conflict. However, GERD, the Jordan Basin, the Indus (India–Pakistan) and the Mekong all illustrate that latent tensions remain high.

9. Required Case Studies

Case study 1 — The Aral Sea disaster (Kazakhstan / Uzbekistan). Once the world's fourth-largest inland water body (68,000 km2, 1960), the Aral Sea has lost ~90% of its area and ~75% of its volume. From the 1960s Soviet central planners diverted its two feeder rivers, the Amu Darya (south) and Syr Darya (north), to irrigate cotton and rice across the Kyzylkum and Karakum deserts, converting ~7 million hectares of drylands to cultivation. Combined withdrawals exceeded natural inflow; lake level fell ~23 m by 2010. Consequences include total fishery collapse (annual catch dropped from 48,000 tonnes to zero; the fishing port of Muynak now stands over 100 km from the shoreline); ecosystem collapse — loss of endemic species; emergence of the Aralkum desert on the exposed lake bed, from which salt-laden and pesticide-laden dust storms (~75 million tonnes/yr) degrade air quality and crop yields across Central Asia; major regional climate change, with colder winters and hotter summers; rising rates of respiratory illness, cancers and infant mortality in adjacent populations. Kazakhstan's 2005 Kok-Aral Dam, funded by the World Bank, partitioned off the North (Small) Aral Sea; surface level has since risen ~4 m and fish stocks have partially recovered. The much larger South (Large) Aral, within Uzbekistan, remains a shrinking hypersaline relic. The Aral case is the canonical example of unsustainable large-scale abstraction from a closed basin, and is frequently cited in Eduqas mark-schemes as a test of candidates' ability to link physical hydrology to political economy.
Case study 2 — Colorado River Basin, USA / Mexico. The Colorado drains 637,000 km2 across seven US states (Wyoming, Colorado, Utah, New Mexico, Nevada, Arizona, California) plus Mexico, supplying ~40 million people and 2.2 million hectares of irrigated farmland (including California's Imperial Valley, which produces much of US winter vegetables). The 1922 Colorado River Compact allocated 7.5 million acre-feet/yr (~9.3 km3) each to the Upper and Lower Basin states, with a further 1.5 MAF to Mexico (1944 treaty) — but was based on the wettest decade on record. Actual long-term mean flow is closer to 13–14 MAF, so the river is chronically over-allocated. Since 2000, the basin has experienced its worst megadrought in 1,200 years (tree-ring reconstructions, Williams et al. 2022); climate change has reduced flow by ~20% compared with the 20th-century mean. Lake Mead, the largest US reservoir, fell to 27% capacity in July 2022, triggering the first-ever federal Tier 1 shortage declaration (August 2021) and mandatory cuts to Arizona (18%) and Nevada (7%) allocations. Hoover Dam hydropower output has dropped ~33%. In May 2023, under Biden-administration pressure, Arizona, California and Nevada agreed to cut 3 MAF over 2023–2026 in exchange for $1.2 bn federal funding. The Colorado is the textbook example of over-allocation, climate-driven supply decline and institutional inertia in an MEDC basin.
Case study 3 — Grand Ethiopian Renaissance Dam (GERD) and the Nile. The Nile (6,650 km) is shared by 11 states, with 85% of Egypt's flow originating from the Ethiopian highlands (Blue Nile) and the remainder from the White Nile (Lake Victoria). Colonial-era treaties (1929 Anglo-Egyptian, 1959 Egypt–Sudan) allocated 55.5 km3/yr to Egypt and 18.5 km3/yr to Sudan, giving Egypt an effective veto over upstream projects — and zero allocation to Ethiopia, despite it being the hydrological source. Construction of GERD (~$5 bn, self-financed by Ethiopia) began in 2011 on the Blue Nile 15 km from the Sudanese border. When fully operational it is the largest hydroelectric plant in Africa: 6,450 MW installed capacity, a reservoir of 74 km3 (roughly 1.5 years of Blue Nile flow). Ethiopia frames GERD as essential for lifting 60 million citizens out of energy poverty and for regional electricity export; Egypt views it as an existential threat to the ~97% of national water supply sourced from the Nile. The filling schedule became the focus of decade-long negotiations: Ethiopia filled the reservoir in stages 2020–2023 without a binding agreement, completing the fourth filling in September 2023. Mediation by the African Union, USA and UAE has not produced a legally binding treaty on annual releases during droughts. Egypt has threatened military action; Sudan, initially opposed, shifted to accommodation. GERD illustrates hydrological colonialism inverted: upstream-downstream asymmetry, colonial-era treaty legacies, and whether cooperation can be institutionalised in a basin where ratification of the UN Watercourses Convention is refused.
Case study 4 — Jakarta water crisis and the capital relocation to Nusantara. Indonesia's capital Jakarta (~10.6 million population) faces an acute water and subsidence crisis. Only ~35–40% of residents receive piped water from the city utility (PAM Jaya); the remainder extract groundwater from shallow and deep aquifers via private wells. Combined with dense high-rise construction, this over-extraction has caused extreme land subsidence: rates reach 10–25 cm/year in the worst-affected northern districts (Muara Baru, Pluit), among the fastest in any major city on Earth. Approximately 40% of Jakarta is already below sea level; projections suggest a further expansion to ~95% of northern Jakarta below sea level by 2050 under combined subsidence and 20–25 cm sea-level rise. Tidal flooding is already frequent; the 2007 flood displaced 340,000 people; the 2020 New Year floods killed 67. Long-term engineering responses have included the stalled “Giant Sea Wall” megaproject, proposed in 2014. In August 2019 President Joko Widodo announced the relocation of the Indonesian capital to a new city, Nusantara, in East Kalimantan (Borneo). Planned to house 2 million by 2045, Nusantara construction began 2022, with key government buildings due 2024; groundbreaking on presidential palace in 2023. Critics note that relocation addresses political symbolism but not the fundamental governance issue (lack of piped-water investment in Jakarta) and that Nusantara construction threatens Bornean rainforest and indigenous Dayak lands. Jakarta exemplifies subsidence and governance failure as drivers of urban water insecurity.
Case study 5 — Murray–Darling Basin, Australia. The Murray–Darling drains ~1.06 million km2 (14% of Australia) across New South Wales, Victoria, Queensland, South Australia and the ACT, supporting AU$24 bn of agricultural output (~40% of Australia's farm gate value) including rice, cotton, dairy and wine. Water extraction (largely for irrigation) grew from the 1920s to the 2000s to unsustainable levels: combined with the Millennium Drought (2001–2009) — one of Australia's worst on record — flows at the Murray Mouth fell to near zero, and Lake Alexandrina became hypersaline. Dryland salinisation affects ~17% of the basin due to rising water tables mobilising subsoil salt. In 2018–19, mass fish kills in the Darling near Menindee (estimated up to 1 million native Murray cod, golden perch and bony bream) became a political flashpoint, attributed to blue-green algal blooms, low flow and upstream cotton irrigation. The Basin Plan 2012, legislated under the Water Act 2007, set a target of returning 2,750 GL/yr from consumptive use to environmental flows, later raised to 3,200 GL. The plan has been contested by irrigators (who see it as an attack on rural livelihoods), by Indigenous First Nations (who argue their cultural water entitlements remain unrecognised despite the basin's Ngarrindjeri, Barkindji and Wiradjuri connections) and by the South Australian state government (which sees upstream compliance as inadequate). The Murray–Darling is the archetypal MEDC over-allocation and inter-state / Indigenous / environmental conflict case, and is frequently paired with the Colorado in comparative Eduqas essays.

10. Aquifers and Groundwater Management

Groundwater supplies ~25% of global drinking water and ~43% of irrigation water. Two aquifer classes dominate:

  • Unconfined aquifers have a free water table connected to the atmosphere via the unsaturated zone; recharge is rapid; pollution risk is high. Examples: UK Chalk aquifer (southern England), parts of the Ogallala (High Plains, USA).
  • Confined aquifers are sealed below an impermeable aquiclude. Recharge occurs only at distant outcrops; water may be under pressure (artesian) and can flow to the surface without pumping. Examples: Great Artesian Basin (Australia), London Basin Chalk beneath the London Clay.

Unsustainable extraction causes: declining water tables (Ogallala has fallen 30+ m in parts of Kansas/Texas since 1950); saline intrusion in coastal aquifers (Gaza, Miami, Bangladesh); land subsidence (Jakarta, Mexico City has subsided ~10 m since 1900); loss of baseflow to rivers and wetlands; higher pumping energy costs. Managed Aquifer Recharge (MAR) schemes deliberately recharge aquifers with treated wastewater or diverted flood flows (e.g. Orange County, California's GWRS scheme recharges 379,000 m3/day).

11. Water Management Strategies

Hard Engineering

  • Large dams and reservoirs — e.g. Three Gorges (China, 22,500 MW, 39 km3 storage), Aswan High Dam (Egypt), Hoover Dam. Provide hydropower, flood control, water supply and irrigation, but trap sediment, disrupt ecology, displace populations (Three Gorges displaced 1.3 million), and evaporate large volumes.
  • Large-scale water transfers — China's South–North Water Transfer Project (SNWTP) moves up to 45 billion m3/yr from the water-rich Yangtze to water-scarce North China via three routes: Eastern (completed 2013, using the Grand Canal), Central (2014, Danjiangkou Reservoir to Beijing/Tianjin) and Western (planned through the Tibetan plateau, delayed). Total cost ~$80 bn. India's proposed National River Linking Project envisages similar transfers but is heavily contested.
  • Desalination — reverse osmosis and multi-stage flash distillation. Global capacity ~110 million m3/day (~40 km3/yr). Dominant in the Gulf (Saudi Arabia, UAE, Qatar all over 50% of supply from desalination), Israel (~80% of domestic water from Mediterranean desalination at Sorek/Hadera/Ashkelon plants), Spain, Australia. Energy-intensive (~3–4 kWh/m3) and produces hypersaline brine that harms marine ecosystems.
  • Flood defences — levees, embankments, storm-surge barriers (Thames Barrier, Netherlands Delta Works, Venice MOSE).

Soft / Integrated Management

  • Rainwater harvesting — building-scale capture; Chennai, Tokyo, Berlin require it by code.
  • Water pricing & tariffs — progressive tariffs (rising price per unit) fund infrastructure and discourage waste; Singapore's tariff rises steeply above 40 m3/month.
  • Leak reduction — the UK loses ~20% of treated water to leaks (~3 bn L/day); acoustic monitoring and AI-guided repair programs target reduction.
  • Water-efficient technology — low-flush toilets, smart meters, drip irrigation (saves 30–60% vs flood irrigation); Israel pioneered drip since 1960s.
  • Drought-tolerant crops — CGIAR programmes, e.g. drought-tolerant maize for Africa, sorghum and millet revival.
  • Wastewater reuse — Singapore's NEWater supplies ~40% of demand from treated wastewater (target 55% by 2060); Windhoek, Namibia has recycled sewage to drinking water since 1968.
  • Catchment management — “working with nature” approaches such as reforestation, wetland restoration, leaky-dam flood alleviation (e.g. Pickering, North Yorkshire; Slowing the Flow scheme).

Integrated Water Resources Management (IWRM)

IWRM was formalised by the Global Water Partnership (Dublin Principles, 1992) and promoted by the UN via SDG 6.5. It is “a process which promotes the coordinated development and management of water, land and related resources in order to maximise economic and social welfare in an equitable manner without compromising the sustainability of vital ecosystems.” Key features: basin-scale planning units; stakeholder participation (farmers, industry, Indigenous, municipal); balance of supply and demand management; integration of water quantity, quality, ecology and economics. The EU Water Framework Directive (2000) and South Africa's National Water Act (1998) are leading IWRM implementations; Murray–Darling Basin Plan is often cited as an IWRM attempt.

Integrated Water Resources Management (IWRM): A coordinated approach to managing water, land and related resources at the river-basin scale to balance economic, social and environmental objectives. Promoted by the UN via SDG 6.5; practical examples include the EU Water Framework Directive (2000) and the Murray–Darling Basin Plan (2012).

Virtual Water and Water Footprint

The concept of virtual water (Tony Allan, 1993, Stockholm Water Prize 2008) captures the volume of water embedded in the production of goods and services. A cup of coffee (125 mL) embeds ~140 L of water; a hamburger ~2,400 L; a cotton T-shirt ~2,700 L; 1 kg beef ~15,400 L; 1 kg rice ~2,500 L. International trade therefore embeds vast virtual water flows, enabling water-scarce states to import water-intensive products rather than produce them domestically (the virtual water trade). Critics argue virtual water masks the true social and ecological costs of exporting water from already-scarce regions (e.g. avocados exported from semi-arid Peru; almonds from drought-stressed California).

Virtual water: The volume of freshwater embedded in the production of a commodity, including the water consumed or polluted at each stage of the supply chain (Allan, 1993). A key concept because global trade flows of virtual water (~30% of global freshwater use) allow water-scarce states to secure food security without increasing domestic abstraction.

12. Future Outlook

The 2020s and 2030s combine three interacting pressures: a global population rising from 8.1 bn (2024) to a projected 9.7 bn by 2050; continued urbanisation; and climate-driven intensification of hydrological variability. The OECD Environmental Outlook projects that water demand will grow ~55% by 2050, with manufacturing (+400%), thermal electricity generation (+140%) and domestic use (+130%) rising fastest. By 2050 ~4 billion people — nearly half the world — may live in water-stressed conditions at least one month per year (Mekonnen & Hoekstra, 2016).

Water is increasingly reframed as a security issue. The Pacific Institute's Water Conflict Chronology (updated 2024) records a sharp rise in incidents of violence directly or indirectly linked to water since 2010, clustered in the Middle East, Sahel and South Asia. Intelligence agencies including the US Office of the Director of National Intelligence (DNI) now list water scarcity as a strategic risk factor for state fragility.

Technological and institutional responses converge on three axes:

  • Efficiency — precision irrigation (soil-moisture sensors, satellite ET monitoring via MODIS/Landsat), AI leak detection, hydroponics and vertical farming for high-value crops.
  • New supply — wastewater reuse (Windhoek, Singapore, Orange County), desalination at falling cost (Sorek-2, Israel, $0.40/m3), and atmospheric water harvesting at small scale.
  • Governance reform — transboundary institutions (Mekong River Commission, Senegal River Basin OMVS), rights-based approaches (2010 UN General Assembly resolution declaring a human right to water and sanitation), and recognising Indigenous cultural water rights (New Zealand's Whanganui River granted legal personhood, 2017).

The challenge is therefore not global water availability in aggregate — the planet has abundant freshwater — but its uneven distribution in space and time, its vulnerability to quality degradation, and the political economies that determine who gets it. Sustainable water futures will require behaviour change as much as engineering: dietary shift away from highly water-intensive meat and dairy, pricing reforms that signal scarcity, and political reforms that empower downstream, rural and Indigenous users whose voices have historically been marginalised.

Exam tip — structure 20-mark essays around physical-human integration: Questions at A-level almost always require synthesising physical hydrology with human agency. A strong structure is: (1) outline the physical process (cycle, residence time, hydrograph); (2) describe the human pressure (abstraction, pollution, climate); (3) present two contrasting case studies (one MEDC, one LEDC); (4) evaluate two management responses (one hard, one soft); (5) conclude with a judgement about sustainability. Explicitly naming Allan (virtual water), Falkenmark (stress thresholds), IWRM and SDG 6 signals engagement with the literature and reliably accesses the top AO1 band.

Key Takeaways

  • The global hydrological cycle is a closed system with total volume ~1.39 billion km3; 96.5% is saline ocean, and only ~0.04% is atmospheric at any moment.
  • Residence times range from ~10 days (atmosphere) to 10,000+ years (ice sheets), controlling sustainability of abstraction.
  • At drainage-basin scale the cycle is open: P = Q + E + ΔS; urbanisation and deforestation produce flashier hydrographs with shorter lag times.
  • Climate change intensifies the cycle (~7%/°C via Clausius–Clapeyron) — more extreme floods and droughts, shifted snowpack, shrinking glaciers.
  • Water stress (Falkenmark, <1,700 m3/cap/yr) affects growing shares of the world; 1.2 bn in physical scarcity, 1.6 bn in economic scarcity.
  • Transboundary basins (260 globally) combine hydrological interdependence with political asymmetry — illustrated by Aral, Colorado, GERD/Nile, Murray–Darling.
  • Hard engineering (dams, transfers, desalination) and soft / integrated management (IWRM, reuse, demand management) are complementary, not alternatives.
  • Virtual water trade (~30% of global freshwater) links consumption in MEDCs to abstraction in LEDCs; water footprints reveal hidden dependencies.
  • Sustainable water futures require physical-human integration: technical efficiency + pricing + governance reform + dietary change.
