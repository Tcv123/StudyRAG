RP1: Make Up a Volumetric Solution and Carry Out a Simple Acid-Base Titration

Background Theory

A titration is a quantitative technique used to determine the exact concentration of an unknown solution by reacting it with a solution of known concentration (the standard solution). In an acid-base titration, the equivalence point is reached when the moles of acid exactly equal the moles of base (for monoprotic acids and bases). An indicator changes colour at or near the equivalence point. The standard solution is prepared using a volumetric flask to ensure precise concentration. Calculations use: n = c x V (where n = moles, c = concentration in mol dm-3, V = volume in dm3), combined with the stoichiometric ratio from the balanced equation.

Apparatus

  • 250 cm3 volumetric flask with stopper (for preparing standard solution)
  • 25.0 cm3 pipette (graduated, class B for routine work) and pipette filler (never mouth-pipette)
  • 50 cm3 burette and burette clamp on a clamp stand
  • 250 cm3 conical flask (Erlenmeyer flask — shape allows swirling without spillage)
  • White tile (placed under flask to see colour change more clearly)
  • Indicator: methyl orange (strong acid/strong base, pH range 3.1–4.4) or phenolphthalein (weak acid/strong base, pH range 8.3–10.0)
  • Balance (to 2 d.p., i.e. ±0.01 g), wash bottle with distilled water, beakers, glass stirring rod, funnel

Method: Preparing a Standard Solution

  1. Calculate the mass of solute needed for the desired concentration and volume using: mass = n x Mr = c x V x Mr.
  2. Weigh the solid accurately on a balance (record to 2 decimal places). Transfer to a beaker.
  3. Add a small volume (~50 cm3) of distilled water to the beaker. Stir with a glass rod to dissolve the solid completely. Gentle warming may be needed for slow-dissolving solids.
  4. Allow the solution to cool to room temperature (volumetric flasks are calibrated at 20 degrees C). Pour the solution into the 250 cm3 volumetric flask using a funnel.
  5. Rinse the beaker, stirring rod, and funnel several times with small volumes of distilled water, adding all washings to the flask. This ensures complete transfer of all solute.
  6. Add distilled water to just below the graduation mark. Then use a dropping pipette to add the final drops until the bottom of the meniscus sits exactly on the 250 cm3 line. (Read at eye level to avoid parallax.)
  7. Stopper the flask and invert 10–20 times to mix thoroughly.

Method: Titration

  1. Rinse the pipette with a small volume of the alkali solution (to prevent dilution from residual water). Then pipette exactly 25.0 cm3 of alkali into a clean conical flask.
  2. Add 2–3 drops of indicator.
  3. Rinse the burette with a small volume of the acid solution. Fill the burette with acid and run some through the tap to remove air bubbles from the jet. Record the initial burette reading to the nearest 0.05 cm3 (half the smallest division).
  4. Rough titration: Add acid from the burette to the conical flask while swirling continuously. When the indicator changes colour permanently (persists for >30 seconds), record the final burette reading. Calculate the rough titre (this gives you an approximate end-point volume).
  5. Accurate titrations: Repeat with fresh pipetted alkali. Add acid rapidly until ~2 cm3 before the expected end-point, then add drop by drop (or half-drops, by turning the tap to let a drop hang and washing it in with distilled water from a wash bottle). Record the final burette reading.
  6. Repeat until you have at least two concordant results (within 0.10 cm3 of each other).
  7. Calculate the mean titre from concordant results only (exclude the rough and any anomalous results).

Safety

  • Acids and alkalis are corrosive or irritant — wear safety goggles at all times. Gloves recommended.
  • Never mouth-pipette — always use a pipette filler.
  • Wipe up any spills immediately with plenty of water.
  • Some indicators (e.g. phenolphthalein) are dissolved in ethanol — keep away from naked flames.

Calculations

n = c x V (V must be in dm3; to convert from cm3, divide by 1000)
Use the balanced equation to find the mole ratio, then calculate the unknown concentration:
c(unknown) = n(unknown) / V(unknown)

RP2: Measurement of an Enthalpy Change (Calorimetry)

Background Theory

Enthalpy changes (ΔH) are measured using calorimetry: the heat released or absorbed by a reaction is transferred to a known mass of water (or solution), and the temperature change is measured. The heat energy transferred is calculated using q = mcΔT, where m = mass of solution (g), c = specific heat capacity (4.18 J g−1 K−1 for dilute aqueous solutions), and ΔT = temperature change (K or degrees C). The enthalpy change per mole is then ΔH = −q / n, where n = moles of the limiting reagent. The sign is negative for exothermic reactions (temperature rises) and positive for endothermic (temperature falls).

A polystyrene cup is used as a simple calorimeter because polystyrene is an excellent thermal insulator (low thermal conductivity), reducing heat loss to the surroundings. A lid prevents heat loss by evaporation. Despite these precautions, some heat is always lost, so the measured ΔT is less than the true value, and the experimental ΔH is always less exothermic (or less endothermic) than the true value.

Apparatus

  • Polystyrene cup (nested in a beaker for stability) with a cardboard or polystyrene lid
  • Thermometer (±0.1 degrees C) or calibrated temperature probe with data logger
  • Measuring cylinder (25 or 50 cm3) for solutions, or balance for solids
  • Stirring rod (glass or plastic), stopwatch

Method (Enthalpy of Neutralisation)

  1. Measure 25.0 cm3 of one solution (e.g. 1.0 mol dm−3 HCl) using a measuring cylinder and pour into the polystyrene cup.
  2. Record the temperature of the solution every minute for 3 minutes to establish a stable baseline (pre-mixing trend).
  3. At the 4th minute, quickly add 25.0 cm3 of the second solution (e.g. 1.0 mol dm−3 NaOH). Stir gently and replace the lid.
  4. Continue recording the temperature every minute for at least 6–8 more minutes (post-mixing cooling trend).
  5. Plot temperature (y-axis) against time (x-axis). Draw two lines of best fit: one for the pre-mixing readings (usually flat or slightly sloping) and one for the post-mixing readings (usually decreasing as heat is lost to surroundings).
  6. Extrapolate both lines back to the time of mixing (minute 4). The vertical distance between the two extrapolated lines at the mixing time gives the corrected ΔT. This graphical extrapolation method compensates for heat loss.
  7. Calculate: q = mcΔT (where m = total mass of solution, assuming density = 1 g cm−3).
  8. Calculate: ΔH = −q / n (n = moles of limiting reagent). Express in kJ mol−1.

Safety

  • Acids and alkalis are corrosive — wear safety goggles and gloves.
  • Take care with strongly exothermic reactions — the cup may become hot.
  • If using powdered metals or displacement reactions, the mixture can splash — use a lid.

Key Assumptions and Limitations

  • Specific heat capacity of the solution = 4.18 J g−1 K−1 (same as pure water). In reality, dissolved salts slightly alter c
  • Density of the solution = 1.0 g cm−3 (very close to true for dilute solutions)
  • No heat is absorbed by the calorimeter itself (polystyrene has very low heat capacity, so this is a reasonable approximation)
  • All heat from the reaction is transferred to the solution (some is lost to surroundings despite insulation)

RP3: Rate of Reaction and Temperature (Sodium Thiosulfate + HCl)

Background Theory

The reaction between sodium thiosulfate and hydrochloric acid produces sulfur as a precipitate, which makes the solution cloudy:
Na2S2O3(aq) + 2HCl(aq) → 2NaCl(aq) + H2O(l) + SO2(g) + S(s)

A cross drawn on paper beneath the flask becomes obscured as sulfur forms. The time taken for the cross to disappear is measured. A shorter time indicates a faster rate. Rate is proportional to 1/t. According to collision theory, increasing temperature increases the kinetic energy of particles, leading to (1) more frequent collisions and (2) a greater proportion of collisions exceeding the activation energy (Ea). The second factor is dominant: the Boltzmann distribution shifts so that many more particles have energy ≥ Ea, dramatically increasing the rate. A 10 degrees C rise typically approximately doubles the rate.

Apparatus

  • Conical flask (100 cm3), measuring cylinders (10, 25 cm3)
  • 0.1 mol dm−3 sodium thiosulfate solution, 1.0 mol dm−3 dilute HCl
  • Thermometer (±1 degrees C), water baths at 20, 30, 40, 50, 60 degrees C
  • Paper with a bold black cross (X) drawn on it, stopwatch

Method

  1. Measure 25 cm3 of sodium thiosulfate into a conical flask.
  2. Place the flask in a water bath at the target temperature. Allow to equilibrate for 5 minutes.
  3. Record the exact temperature of the thiosulfate solution.
  4. Place the flask on the paper with the cross. Add 5 cm3 of dilute HCl and immediately start the stopwatch.
  5. Look down through the flask from above. Stop the timer when the cross is no longer visible due to the sulfur precipitate making the solution opaque.
  6. Record the time (t). Calculate rate = 1/t.
  7. Repeat at five different temperatures (e.g. 20, 30, 40, 50, 60 degrees C). Use fresh solutions each time.

Safety

  • SO2 gas is produced (toxic, respiratory irritant) — work in a well-ventilated area or fume cupboard.
  • Dilute HCl is an irritant — wear safety goggles.
  • Take care with hot water baths at higher temperatures — risk of scalding.

Analysis

  • Plot 1/t (y-axis) against temperature in degrees C (x-axis). The graph shows an exponential-shaped increase, confirming that rate increases with temperature.
  • For an Arrhenius plot: plot ln(1/t) (y-axis) against 1/T (x-axis), where T is in Kelvin. This gives a straight line with gradient = −Ea/R. Calculate Ea from the gradient: Ea = −gradient x R (where R = 8.314 J K−1 mol−1).

Error Analysis

Source of ErrorTypeImprovement
Subjective judgement of when the cross disappearsRandomThe same person should judge every time. Better: use a light sensor or colorimeter placed below the flask for an objective, reproducible end-point
Temperature changes during the reactionSystematicUse a thermostatically controlled water bath; keep the flask in the bath during the reaction
Human reaction time in starting/stopping the stopwatchRandomUse data logging; repeat each temperature at least 3 times and take the mean
Volumes measured with measuring cylinder (low precision)RandomUse a pipette for more accurate volumes; alternatively, ensure consistent technique
Exam Tip: Rate = 1/t is a proxy for initial rate (the same amount of product — enough sulfur to obscure the cross — is formed in each experiment). A 10 degrees C rise approximately doubles the rate. For the Arrhenius plot, remember to convert temperature to Kelvin (T = degrees C + 273). The gradient = −Ea/R. Always state units for rate: 1/t has units of s−1.

Important Details

  • Flame tests: Clean the nichrome wire by dipping in concentrated HCl and heating until no colour is seen. Repeat until clean. For potassium (lilac), the yellow sodium flame can mask it — view through blue cobalt glass which absorbs the sodium yellow.
  • Acidify before adding BaCl2 or AgNO3: Dilute HNO3 removes carbonates and sulfites that could give false positive precipitates (BaCO3 and Ag2CO3 would also be white precipitates).
  • Al3+ vs Mg2+ distinction: Both give white precipitates with NaOH and NH3. The key difference: Al(OH)3 dissolves in excess NaOH (amphoteric) but Mg(OH)2 does not. This is the classic distinguishing test.
  • Cu2+ with excess NH3: The blue Cu(OH)2 precipitate dissolves to give a deep blue solution of the tetraammine complex [Cu(NH3)4(H2O)2]2+. This is a ligand substitution reaction.

Safety

  • Silver nitrate (AgNO3) stains skin permanently — wear gloves.
  • Barium chloride (BaCl2) is toxic — handle with care; wash hands thoroughly.
  • Concentrated HCl is corrosive and produces fumes — use in a fume cupboard for flame tests.
  • Wear safety goggles throughout all test-tube reactions.

RP5: Distillation of a Product from a Reaction

Background Theory

Distillation separates liquids based on differences in boiling point. In organic synthesis, it is used to purify the desired product from a reaction mixture. The mixture is heated; the component with the lowest boiling point evaporates first, passes through a condenser (where it is cooled and re-liquefied), and is collected separately. Monitoring the thermometer at the still head allows collection of a specific fraction at the correct boiling point. A pure liquid has a sharp, constant boiling point; an impure mixture boils over a range.

Reflux is a related technique where the vapour is condensed and returned to the flask rather than collected — this allows prolonged heating without losing volatile reagents or products, ensuring the reaction goes to completion before distillation.

Apparatus

  • Round-bottomed flask (appropriate size for the volume of liquid), still head with thermometer adapter
  • Thermometer (range covering the expected boiling point), Liebig condenser with rubber tubing
  • Collecting flask or conical flask, anti-bumping granules (added before heating)
  • Heat source (Bunsen burner with gauze, electric heating mantle, or water bath for low-boiling-point liquids)
  • Clamp stand, boss, clamps (flask and condenser both clamped for safety)

Method

  1. Set up the distillation apparatus: round-bottomed flask → still head (with thermometer, bulb level with the side-arm outlet) → condenser (angled slightly downward) → collecting flask.
  2. Add anti-bumping granules to the flask before heating. Never add them to a hot liquid (risk of violent boiling).
  3. Pour the reaction mixture into the flask (no more than half full to prevent bumping over).
  4. Connect the condenser: cold water enters at the bottom and exits at the top (counter-current flow for maximum cooling efficiency).
  5. Turn on the water supply. Then begin heating gently, increasing heat gradually.
  6. Discard the first few drops of distillate (forerun) which may contain impurities.
  7. Collect the fraction that distils within ±2 degrees C of the expected boiling point of the desired product.
  8. Stop collecting when the temperature starts to rise above the target range (the next component is beginning to distil).

Safety

  • Never heat a sealed system — the collecting end must be open to the atmosphere to prevent dangerous pressure build-up.
  • Many organic liquids are highly flammable — use an electric heating mantle or water bath rather than a naked flame.
  • Anti-bumping granules prevent superheating and sudden, violent boiling.
  • Ensure all glassware joints are secure but not forced (forced joints can crack).

Analysis

  • Record the boiling range of the collected fraction. A sharp boiling point (±1 degrees C) indicates high purity.
  • Calculate percentage yield: (actual moles of product / theoretical moles) x 100.
  • Percentage yield is always <100% due to: incomplete reaction, side reactions, mechanical losses (product left in flask, condenser, transfer losses), and losses during purification.

RP7: Rate of Reaction by Continuous Monitoring (Gas Collection or Mass Loss)

Background Theory

In continuous monitoring, the progress of a reaction is tracked over time by measuring either (a) the volume of gas produced (using a gas syringe) or (b) the mass lost (as gas escapes from an open flask on a balance). A typical reaction is the decomposition of calcium carbonate by hydrochloric acid: CaCO3(s) + 2HCl(aq) → CaCl2(aq) + H2O(l) + CO2(g). Plotting volume or mass change against time gives a curve from which the rate at any point can be determined from the gradient of a tangent. The initial rate is the gradient at t = 0.

Apparatus

  • Conical flask with bung and delivery tube connected to a gas syringe (100 cm3), OR conical flask with cotton wool plug on a top-pan balance (to ±0.01 g)
  • Stopwatch, measuring cylinders for reagent volumes
  • Reagents: marble chips (CaCO3) + dilute HCl (1 or 2 mol dm−3)

Method

  1. Measure the required volume of HCl into the conical flask.
  2. Weigh the marble chips (use the same mass each time for valid comparisons).
  3. Add the marble chips, quickly fit the bung (gas syringe method) or replace the cotton wool plug (mass loss method), and start the stopwatch.
  4. Record gas volume (or mass) at regular intervals (every 30 seconds) until the reaction is complete (no further gas produced / mass constant).
  5. Repeat with a changed variable: different HCl concentration, different particle size (powdered vs large chips), or different temperature.

Analysis

  • Plot volume of gas (or mass lost) on y-axis vs time on x-axis.
  • The initial rate = gradient of the tangent to the curve at t = 0 (steeper = faster).
  • The curve levels off when one reactant is completely consumed. The final volume/mass change depends on which reactant is in excess.
  • Compare curves for different conditions: higher concentration → steeper initial gradient and faster levelling off; same final amount if excess acid is used.

Exam Tip: On a volume-time graph: the steeper the initial gradient, the faster the initial rate. If excess acid is used, all curves reach the same final volume (same moles of CaCO3 reacted). If excess CaCO3 is used, the final volume depends on the moles of acid. Always state which is in excess and why. The cotton wool plug (mass loss method) allows gas to escape but prevents acid spray from leaving the flask.

RP8: Rate of Reaction by Initial Rate Method (Iodine Clock)

Background Theory

The iodine clock uses a two-stage reaction system to measure the initial rate of reaction. In the first (slow) step, iodine (I2) is gradually produced by the reaction of iodate and bisulfite ions. In the second (fast) step, the I2 is immediately consumed by reacting with thiosulfate. When all the thiosulfate has been used up, free I2 accumulates and reacts with starch indicator to give a sudden blue-black colour. The time to this colour change is inversely proportional to the initial rate. By varying the concentration of one reactant while keeping others constant (and maintaining a constant total volume with distilled water), you can determine the order of reaction with respect to that reactant.

Apparatus

  • Conical flasks or beakers, measuring cylinders (multiple, to avoid contamination)
  • Stopwatch, white tile (to see colour change), starch indicator solution
  • Solutions: potassium iodate (KIO3), sodium bisulfite (NaHSO3) or sodium thiosulfate
  • Distilled water (to keep total volume constant)

Method

  1. Prepare a series of mixtures with varying concentration of one reactant (e.g. KIO3). Keep total volume constant by adding distilled water to make up the difference.
  2. Add starch indicator to each flask.
  3. Add the second reagent, start the stopwatch immediately, and swirl to mix.
  4. Record the time (t) for the solution to turn dark blue-black.
  5. Calculate rate = 1/t for each experiment.
  6. Plot rate (1/t, y-axis) against concentration (x-axis).

Why Total Volume Must Be Constant

If the total volume changes between experiments, then changing the volume of one reactant also changes the effective concentrations of all other reactants in the mixture. By adding distilled water to compensate, you ensure that only the concentration of the one variable reactant changes while everything else remains constant. This is essential for a valid investigation.

Safety

  • Solutions are generally low hazard at the concentrations used, but wear goggles.
  • Iodine can stain skin and clothing — handle carefully.

Exam Tip: The iodine clock effectively measures the time for a fixed small amount of reaction to occur (the amount of I2 that reacts with the thiosulfate). Since this is a small fraction of the total reaction, it approximates the initial rate. Always keep total volume constant by adding water when reducing a reactant volume. If asked to determine the order from data: compare experiments where one concentration doubles — if the rate doubles, it is first order; if the rate quadruples, it is second order.

RP9: pH Changes During Weak Acid / Strong Base Titration

Background Theory

This practical produces a pH titration curve that reveals key features of the acid-base system. For a weak acid (e.g. ethanoic acid, CH3COOH) titrated with a strong base (e.g. NaOH), the curve has distinctive features: (1) the initial pH is above 1 (weak acid is partially dissociated), (2) there is a buffer region where pH changes slowly (mixture of weak acid and its conjugate base resists pH change), (3) at the half-equivalence point, exactly half the acid has been neutralised, so [HA] = [A−] and pH = pKa, (4) the equivalence point has pH > 7 (the salt of a weak acid and strong base is basic due to hydrolysis of the conjugate base), (5) use phenolphthalein as indicator (changes at pH 8.3–10.0, matching the equivalence pH).

Apparatus

  • Calibrated pH meter or pH probe with data logger
  • 50 cm3 burette filled with 0.10 mol dm−3 NaOH
  • Beaker (250 cm3) containing 25.0 cm3 of 0.10 mol dm−3 CH3COOH
  • Magnetic stirrer and stirrer bar, clamp stand, wash bottle with distilled water

Method

  1. Calibrate the pH meter using at least two buffer solutions (e.g. pH 4 and pH 7, or pH 7 and pH 10).
  2. Pipette 25.0 cm3 of weak acid into the beaker. Place on the magnetic stirrer.
  3. Immerse the pH probe. Record the initial pH.
  4. Fill the burette with NaOH. Record the initial burette reading.
  5. Add NaOH in 1 cm3 increments initially. After each addition: stir, wait 10–15 seconds for a stable reading, then record pH and total volume added.
  6. When approaching the equivalence point (rapid pH change), switch to 0.5 cm3 or even 0.1 cm3 increments to capture the steep region accurately.
  7. Continue well past the equivalence point until pH levels off (typically pH 12–13).
  8. Plot pH (y-axis) against volume of NaOH added (x-axis).

Key Features of the Titration Curve

Initial pH~2.9 for 0.10 mol dm−3 CH3COOH (higher than a strong acid of the same concentration because it is only partially dissociated)

Safety

  • NaOH is corrosive — wear goggles and gloves.
  • Weak acids are irritants — avoid skin contact.
  • pH probes are fragile and expensive — handle with care; store in the correct storage solution.

This is the easiest way to determine pKa experimentally. Calculate Ka = 10−pKa. The buffer region demonstrates the Henderson-Hasselbalch equation: pH = pKa + log([A−]/[HA]).

RP10: Construct Electrochemical Cells and Measure EMF

Background Theory

An electrochemical cell converts chemical energy into electrical energy through spontaneous redox reactions. It consists of two half-cells, each containing a metal electrode immersed in a solution of its ions. The more reactive metal (more negative Eo) acts as the anode (oxidation: M → Mn+ + ne−), and the less reactive metal acts as the cathode (reduction: Mn+ + ne− → M). Electrons flow from anode to cathode through the external wire. The salt bridge (e.g. KNO3 solution in agar) completes the circuit by allowing ion flow between the half-cells, maintaining electrical neutrality.

Eocell = Eocathode − Eoanode

Apparatus

  • Two beakers (250 cm3), metal electrodes (e.g. Zn strip, Cu strip, Fe strip, Mg ribbon)
  • 1.0 mol dm−3 solutions of the corresponding metal salts (ZnSO4, CuSO4, FeSO4, MgSO4)
  • Salt bridge: filter paper soaked in KNO3 solution, or agar-KNO3 gel in a U-tube
  • High-resistance voltmeter (or multimeter set to DC voltage) — high resistance minimises current flow and gives a more accurate EMF reading
  • Connecting wires with crocodile clips, emery paper (to clean electrodes)

Method

  1. Clean both metal electrodes with emery paper to remove the oxide layer (oxide increases resistance and lowers the reading).
  2. Pour 1.0 mol dm−3 ZnSO4 into one beaker and 1.0 mol dm−3 CuSO4 into the other.
  3. Place the Zn electrode in the ZnSO4 and the Cu electrode in the CuSO4.
  4. Connect the salt bridge (dip one end in each beaker).
  5. Connect electrodes to the voltmeter using wires. Record the EMF.
  6. For Zn/Cu: expected Eocell = +0.34 − (−0.76) = +1.10 V.
  7. Repeat with different metal combinations (Zn/Fe, Fe/Cu, Mg/Cu, etc.) and compare measured EMFs with calculated values.

Safety

  • CuSO4 is harmful if ingested — avoid skin contact; wear gloves.
  • Wear safety goggles throughout.
  • Dispose of heavy metal solutions according to local regulations (do not pour down the drain).

Error Analysis

Concentrations not exactly 1.0 mol dm−3SystematicPrepare solutions accurately using a volumetric flask
Salt bridge drying out or making poor contactSystematic (resistance increases, EMF drops)Ensure the salt bridge is freshly prepared and fully immersed in both solutions
Voltmeter drawing currentSystematic (lowers reading)Use a high-resistance voltmeter or potentiometer to minimise current flow
Temperature not 298 K (25 degrees C)SystematicRecord temperature; acknowledge deviation from standard conditions; use Nernst equation for correction
Exam Tip: The more reactive metal (more negative Eo) is always the anode (oxidation). Electrons flow from the anode to the cathode through the external wire. The salt bridge allows ion flow to complete the internal circuit and maintain electrical neutrality (anions migrate towards the anode, cations towards the cathode). Eocell = Eocathode − Eoanode. A positive cell EMF means the reaction is spontaneous (feasible). KNO3 is used in the salt bridge because K+ and NO3− ions have similar mobilities and do not react with the half-cell solutions.

RP11: Standard Electrode Potentials (Using the SHE)

Background Theory

Standard electrode potential (Eo) is the EMF of a half-cell measured against the Standard Hydrogen Electrode (SHE) under standard conditions: all solutions at 1.0 mol dm−3, gases at 1 atm (100 kPa), temperature at 298 K (25 degrees C). The SHE is assigned Eo = 0.00 V by convention. It consists of a platinised platinum electrode in 1.0 mol dm−3 HCl with H2 gas bubbled over it at 1 atm. The platinum is inert (does not react) but provides a surface for the equilibrium: 2H+(aq) + 2e− ⇌ H2(g).

In practice, the SHE is awkward to use (H2 gas supply needed). A secondary reference electrode (e.g. Ag/AgCl or calomel) with a known, stable Eo is often used instead, and results are converted to the SHE scale.

Method

  1. Set up the SHE: platinised Pt electrode in 1.0 mol dm−3 HCl, H2 bubbled at 1 atm, at 298 K.
  2. Set up the test half-cell: metal electrode in 1.0 mol dm−3 solution of its ions (or Pt electrode for non-metal half-cells, e.g. Fe3+/Fe2+).
  3. Connect with a salt bridge (KNO3). Connect both to a high-resistance voltmeter.
  4. Read the EMF and its sign. This is the Eo of the test half-cell (since Eo(SHE) = 0.00 V).
  5. Repeat for multiple half-cells to build an electrochemical series.

Standard Conditions Summary

ConditionStandard Value
Temperature298 K (25 degrees C)
Solution concentration1.0 mol dm−3
Gas pressure1 atm (100 kPa)
SHE referenceEo = 0.00 V by definition

Safety

  • H2 gas is flammable and explosive — no naked flames; well-ventilated area.
  • HCl is corrosive — goggles and gloves.
  • Metal salt solutions may be toxic — handle with care; wash hands.
Exam Tip: Standard conditions: 298 K, 1 atm, 1.0 mol dm−3. The SHE is assigned 0.00 V by convention (not because it actually has zero potential). For non-metal half-cells (e.g. Cl2/Cl−, Fe3+/Fe2+), use a platinum electrode (inert conductor). A more negative Eo means the species is a stronger reducing agent (more easily oxidised). A more positive Eo means a stronger oxidising agent (more easily reduced).

RP12: Effect of Changing Conditions on a Reversible Reaction at Equilibrium

Background Theory

Le Chatelier's principle: When a system at equilibrium is subjected to a change in concentration, temperature, or pressure, the equilibrium shifts in the direction that opposes the change, partially counteracting it. This practical uses the cobalt(II) chloride equilibrium, which has a visible colour change between the pink aqueous complex [Co(H2O)6]2+ and the blue tetrahedral complex [CoCl4]2−.

[Co(H2O)6]2+(aq) + 4Cl−(aq) ⇌ [CoCl4]2−(aq) + 6H2O(l)     ΔH = +ve (endothermic forward)

Apparatus

  • Test tubes and rack, cobalt(II) chloride solution in water/HCl mixture (starting purple)
  • Concentrated HCl (source of Cl−), distilled water, teat pipettes
  • Ice bath (~0 degrees C) and hot water bath (~80 degrees C)

Method and Predictions

Change AppliedLe Chatelier's PredictionDirection of ShiftColour Change
Heating (hot water bath)System absorbs heat by shifting in the endothermic directionShifts right (forward)Purple → blue (more [CoCl4]2−)
Cooling (ice bath)System releases heat by shifting in the exothermic directionShifts left (backward)Purple → pink (more [Co(H2O)6]2+)
Adding concentrated HCl (more Cl−)System shifts to remove added Cl−Shifts rightPurple → blue
Adding distilled water (dilutes Cl−)System shifts to produce more aqueous ions (replace removed Cl−)Shifts leftPurple → pink

Important Concepts

  • Temperature changes alter the value of Kc. Heating an endothermic reaction increases Kc (more products at equilibrium); cooling decreases Kc.
  • Concentration changes shift the position of equilibrium but do NOT change Kc (Kc only changes with temperature).
  • A catalyst does NOT shift the position of equilibrium and does NOT change Kc. It increases the rate of both the forward and reverse reactions equally, so equilibrium is reached faster but at the same position.

Safety

  • Cobalt(II) chloride is toxic and a suspected carcinogen — wear gloves and avoid skin contact.
  • Concentrated HCl is corrosive and produces fumes — use in a fume cupboard.
  • Hot water bath — handle with care to avoid burns.

Temperature of water bath not constantSystematicMonitor with a thermometer; add ice or heat as needed to maintain temperature

Exam Tip: Le Chatelier's principle: the system shifts to oppose the imposed change. For temperature: heating favours the endothermic direction; cooling favours the exothermic direction. For concentration: adding a species shifts equilibrium away from that species; removing it shifts towards it. A catalyst does NOT shift equilibrium — it only speeds up attainment. Temperature is the ONLY factor that changes the value of Kc. This distinction (position vs value of Kc) is heavily tested.
