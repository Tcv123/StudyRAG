O1 — Organic Chemistry I

1. Nomenclature and Functional Groups

1.1 IUPAC Naming Rules

Organic molecules are named systematically using the IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) convention. The name consists of three parts:

  1. Root/prefix: Identifies the longest continuous carbon chain: meth- (1C), eth- (2C), prop- (3C), but- (4C), pent- (5C), hex- (6C), hept- (7C), oct- (8C), non- (9C), dec- (10C).
  2. Suffix: Identifies the highest-priority functional group: -ane (alkane), -ene (alkene), -ol (alcohol), -al (aldehyde), -one (ketone), -oic acid (carboxylic acid), -amine (amine), -amide (amide).
  3. Substituents/side chains: Named with a position number (locant) and prefix. Use the lowest set of locants. Multiple identical substituents use di-, tri-, tetra- prefixes.

Naming procedure:

  1. Find the longest continuous carbon chain that includes the principal functional group.
  2. Number from the end nearest to the principal functional group (or nearest branch point if no functional group suffix).
  3. Identify and name all substituents with their position numbers.
  4. List substituents alphabetically (ignore multiplying prefixes like di-, tri-).
  5. Combine: substituents + root + suffix (e.g. 2-methylbutan-1-ol).
Exam Tip: Common naming errors: (1) Not finding the longest chain that includes the functional group. (2) Numbering from the wrong end. (3) Forgetting to use commas between numbers and hyphens between numbers and letters (e.g. 2,3-dimethylbutane). Practice naming at least 10 structures before the exam.

1.3 Types of Formulae

  • Molecular formula: Shows the actual number of each type of atom (e.g. C3H8O).
  • Empirical formula: Simplest whole-number ratio of atoms (e.g. CH2O for glucose C6H12O6).
  • Structural formula: Shows the minimal detail to make the structure unambiguous (e.g. CH3CH2OH).
  • Displayed formula: Shows all bonds between all atoms.
  • Skeletal formula: Lines represent carbon-carbon bonds; hydrogen atoms on carbon are not shown; heteroatoms (O, N, Cl) and their hydrogens are shown.
Homologous series: A family of organic compounds with the same functional group, the same general formula, and similar chemical properties. Each successive member differs by CH2. Physical properties (boiling point, density) show a gradual trend as chain length increases.

2. Isomerism

Isomers: Molecules with the same molecular formula but different structural arrangements of atoms. They may have different physical and/or chemical properties.

2.1 Structural Isomerism

Structural isomers have the same molecular formula but different structural formulae. There are three types:

  • Chain isomerism: Different arrangements of the carbon skeleton (e.g. butane CH3CH2CH2CH3 and methylpropane (CH3)3CH, both C4H10). Branched isomers have lower boiling points because they are more compact with less surface area for London forces.
  • Position isomerism: Same carbon chain and functional group, but the functional group is at a different position (e.g. butan-1-ol and butan-2-ol, both C4H9OH).
  • Functional group isomerism: Same molecular formula but different functional groups (e.g. propanal C2H5CHO and propanone CH3COCH3, both C3H6O; or ethanol C2H5OH and methoxymethane CH3OCH3, both C2H6O).

2.2 E/Z Isomerism (Geometric Isomerism)

E/Z isomerism is a form of stereoisomerism that arises when there is:

  1. Restricted rotation about a bond (usually a C=C double bond), AND
  2. Two different groups attached to each carbon of the double bond.
  • Z-isomer (zusammen = together): The higher priority groups on each carbon are on the same side of the double bond.
  • E-isomer (entgegen = opposite): The higher priority groups are on opposite sides.

Priority is assigned using the Cahn-Ingold-Prelog (CIP) rules: the atom with the higher atomic number has higher priority. If the first atoms are the same, move along the chain until a difference is found.

2.3 Optical Isomerism

Chiral centre (asymmetric carbon): A carbon atom bonded to four different groups. This produces two non-superimposable mirror image forms called enantiomers. A molecule with a chiral centre has no plane of symmetry.

Properties of enantiomers:

  • Identical physical properties (melting point, boiling point, solubility, density).
  • Identical chemical properties with achiral reagents.
  • Different reactions with other chiral molecules (important in biochemistry — enzymes are chiral and usually only interact with one enantiomer).
  • Rotate the plane of plane-polarised light in opposite directions: (+) enantiomer rotates clockwise (dextrorotatory), (-) enantiomer rotates anticlockwise (laevorotatory).
Racemic mixture (racemate): A 50:50 mixture of the two enantiomers. It shows no overall rotation of plane-polarised light because the equal and opposite rotations cancel out. Racemic mixtures are produced when a new chiral centre is formed in a reaction from a non-chiral starting material (e.g. nucleophilic addition to a carbonyl).
Exam Tip: To identify a chiral centre, look for a carbon bonded to four DIFFERENT groups. Draw the two enantiomers as mirror images with a dashed line between them. In reactions that create a new chiral centre from a planar intermediate (e.g. nucleophilic addition to an aldehyde), explain that the nucleophile can attack equally from both sides, producing a racemic mixture.

3. Alkanes

3.1 Structure and Properties

Alkanes are saturated hydrocarbons containing only C-C single bonds and C-H bonds. General formula: CnH2n+2. They are relatively unreactive because:

  • C-C bonds (346 kJ mol-1) and C-H bonds (413 kJ mol-1) are strong and require significant energy to break.
  • The C-H bond is only very slightly polar (electronegativity of C = 2.5, H = 2.1), so alkanes are not attacked by nucleophiles or electrophiles.

Boiling point trends:

  • Boiling points increase with chain length because longer chains have more electrons, leading to stronger London dispersion forces between molecules.
  • Boiling points decrease with branching because branched molecules are more compact with less surface area, reducing intermolecular contact and weakening London forces.

3.2 Combustion

Complete combustion (excess oxygen) produces carbon dioxide and water:

CnH2n+2 + (3n+1)/2 O2 → nCO2 + (n+1)H2O

Example: CH4 + 2O2 → CO2 + 2H2O   ΔH = -890 kJ mol-1

Alkanes are the primary component of fossil fuels (natural gas, petrol, diesel). CO2 is a greenhouse gas that contributes to climate change by absorbing and re-emitting infrared radiation.

Incomplete combustion (limited oxygen supply) produces:

  • Carbon monoxide (CO) — a toxic, colourless, odourless gas. It binds irreversibly to haemoglobin (forming carboxyhaemoglobin), preventing oxygen transport. CO has a higher affinity for haemoglobin than O2.
  • Carbon/soot (C) — particulates that cause respiratory problems, reduce air quality, and contribute to global dimming.
  • Water (H2O) — always produced.

Other pollutants from combustion engines:

  • NOx (nitrogen oxides) — formed when N2 and O2 react at high temperatures inside engines. Contributes to acid rain and photochemical smog.
  • SO2 — from sulfur impurities in fuel. Causes acid rain.
  • Unburned hydrocarbons (VOCs) — contribute to smog formation.

Catalytic converters reduce these pollutants: 2CO + 2NO → 2CO2 + N2.

3.3 Free Radical Substitution

Alkanes react with halogens (Cl2 or Br2) in the presence of UV light via a free radical chain mechanism. This is the only useful reaction of alkanes (besides combustion).

Free radical: A reactive species with an unpaired electron, written with a dot notation (e.g. Cl•). Radicals are formed by homolytic fission — the equal splitting of a covalent bond, where each atom takes one electron.

Mechanism (three stages) — chlorination of methane:

1. Initiation: UV light causes homolytic fission of the Cl-Cl bond, generating two chlorine radicals:

Cl2 → 2Cl•   (UV light provides the energy)

2. Propagation: Chain reactions that consume and regenerate radicals. Each step produces one radical from one radical, maintaining the chain:

CH4 + Cl• → •CH3 + HCl   (Step 1: Cl• abstracts an H atom)
•CH3 + Cl2 → CH3Cl + Cl•   (Step 2: •CH3 reacts with Cl2; Cl• regenerated)

3. Termination: Any two radicals combine, ending the chain:

Cl• + Cl• → Cl2     •CH3 + Cl• → CH3Cl     •CH3 + •CH3 → C2H6

Limitations of free radical substitution:

  • Further substitution: The product CH3Cl can react further to give CH2Cl2, CHCl3, and CCl4, producing a mixture of mono-, di-, tri-, and tetra-substituted products.
  • Multiple products from longer alkanes: For propane, the H on C-1 or C-2 can be replaced, giving a mixture of 1-chloropropane and 2-chloropropane.
  • The C2H6 formed in termination shows that CH3• + CH3• combination occurred.
  • This makes free radical substitution a poor preparative method for specific halogenoalkanes.
Exam Tip: Use single-headed curly arrows (fishhooks) for free radical mechanisms to show the movement of single electrons. Use full-headed curly arrows for ionic mechanisms. You must show all three stages clearly labelled. The C2H6 by-product in termination is evidence for the radical mechanism (it cannot form from CH4 and Cl2 alone).

4. Alkenes

4.1 Structure and Bonding

Alkenes are unsaturated hydrocarbons containing at least one C=C double bond. General formula: CnH2n (for monoenes). The C=C double bond consists of:

  • A sigma (σ) bond — formed by head-on overlap of sp2 hybrid orbitals. This is a strong bond with electron density concentrated between the nuclei.
  • A pi (π) bond — formed by sideways overlap of unhybridised p orbitals above and below the plane of the molecule. The π bond is weaker than the σ bond and is the region of high electron density that makes alkenes reactive.

The π electron cloud makes alkenes susceptible to attack by electrophiles (electron-pair acceptors). The C=C bond cannot rotate freely (the π bond would have to break), which is why E/Z isomerism exists in alkenes.

4.2 Electrophilic Addition

Electrophile: An electron-pair acceptor; a species that is attracted to areas of high electron density. It accepts a pair of electrons from the electron-rich π bond to form a new covalent bond. Examples: H+, NO2+, Brδ+, carbocations (R+).

The general mechanism for electrophilic addition to alkenes has two steps:

  1. The π bond electrons are attracted to the electrophilic part of the reagent. A curly arrow shows the π bond electrons forming a new bond to the electrophile, while the bond within the electrophilic species breaks heterolytically.
  2. The carbocation intermediate is rapidly attacked by the nucleophilic negative species to form the second new bond.

Key electrophilic addition reactions:

Markownikoff's rule: When HX adds across an unsymmetrical alkene (e.g. propene), the hydrogen atom adds to the carbon already bonded to more hydrogen atoms. The major product has the halogen on the more substituted carbon. This is because the intermediate carbocation is more stable when the positive charge is on the more substituted carbon (stability order: tertiary > secondary > primary). The inductive (electron-pushing) effect of alkyl groups stabilises the positive charge through hyperconjugation.

Test for unsaturation (C=C): Add bromine water (orange/brown). Alkenes decolourise bromine water immediately at room temperature without UV light. Alkanes do not decolourise bromine water (unless UV light is present, which would cause free radical substitution).

4.3 Addition Polymerisation

Many alkene monomers can join together to form a long-chain polymer by addition polymerisation. The C=C double bond in each monomer opens up, and each carbon forms a new bond to the adjacent monomer:

nCH2=CH2 → -(CH2-CH2)n-   (poly(ethene))
nCH2=CHCl → -(CH2-CHCl)n-   (poly(chloroethene), PVC)

Environmental issues: Addition polymers are chemically inert and non-biodegradable because of the strong C-C backbone. Disposal methods include:

  • Landfill: Cheap but wasteful; takes up space; polymers persist for centuries.
  • Incineration: Releases energy (can generate electricity) but produces CO2 and possibly toxic gases (HCl from PVC).
  • Recycling: Mechanical (melt and remould) or feedstock (crack back to monomers). Requires sorting by polymer type.

5. Halogenoalkanes

5.1 Classification and Bonding

Halogenoalkanes contain a halogen atom (F, Cl, Br, or I) bonded to a saturated carbon atom. They are classified by the number of carbon atoms bonded to the carbon carrying the halogen:

  • Primary (1°): The halogen-bearing carbon is bonded to one other carbon (e.g. CH3CH2Br, bromoethane).
  • Secondary (2°): Bonded to two other carbons (e.g. (CH3)2CHBr, 2-bromopropane).
  • Tertiary (3°): Bonded to three other carbons (e.g. (CH3)3CBr, 2-bromo-2-methylpropane).

The C-X bond is polar (δ+C-Xδ-) because halogens are more electronegative than carbon. The δ+ carbon is susceptible to attack by nucleophiles. The polarity and bond strength of C-X bonds determine the reactivity.

5.2 Nucleophilic Substitution

Nucleophile: An electron-pair donor; a species with a lone pair of electrons that is attracted to a δ+ (electron-deficient) carbon atom. It donates its lone pair to form a new covalent bond. Examples: OH-, CN-, NH3, H2O.

The nucleophile attacks the δ+ carbon, displacing the halogen as a halide ion (X-). The mechanism depends on the classification of the halogenoalkane:

SN2 Mechanism (primary halogenoalkanes)

  • One-step concerted mechanism. The nucleophile attacks the δ+ carbon from the opposite side to the halogen (backside attack), while the C-X bond breaks simultaneously.
  • A transition state forms where the nucleophile is partially bonded and the halogen is partially leaving: [Nu···C···X]‡
  • Rate equation: Rate = k[halogenoalkane][nucleophile] — second order, bimolecular (both species in the rate-determining step).
  • Produces inversion of configuration at the carbon centre (Walden inversion).
  • Favoured by primary halogenoalkanes (less steric hindrance around the carbon).

SN1 Mechanism (tertiary halogenoalkanes)

  • Two-step mechanism. Step 1 (slow, rate-determining): The C-X bond breaks heterolytically to form a planar carbocation and X-. Step 2 (fast): The nucleophile rapidly attacks the carbocation from either side.
  • Rate equation: Rate = k[halogenoalkane] — first order, unimolecular (only the halogenoalkane in the rate-determining step).
  • Tertiary carbocations are most stable (three alkyl groups donate electron density to stabilise the positive charge via the inductive effect).
  • Produces a racemic mixture if a chiral centre is formed, because the nucleophile can attack from either side of the planar carbocation.

Secondary halogenoalkanes undergo a mixture of SN1 and SN2.

Why CN- is important: Reaction with cyanide extends the carbon chain by one atom. The nitrile can then be hydrolysed to a carboxylic acid (R-CN + 2H2O + HCl → R-COOH + NH4Cl) or reduced to an amine (R-CN + 4[H] → R-CH2NH2 using LiAlH4 or H2/Ni).

Why excess NH3 is used: If the amine product is formed with excess halogenoalkane still present, further substitution occurs: the amine acts as a nucleophile, leading to secondary amines, tertiary amines, and eventually a quaternary ammonium salt. Using excess NH3 minimises this by ensuring most halogenoalkane reacts with NH3 rather than the amine product.

Exam Tip: In mechanism questions, show the lone pair on the nucleophile and use a curly arrow from the lone pair to the δ+ carbon. Show the bond breaking from the C-X bond to the X atom. For SN1, clearly show two separate steps. For SN2, show it in one step with the transition state.

5.3 Elimination

Halogenoalkanes can also undergo elimination, losing HX to form an alkene:

CH3CHBrCH3 + KOH → CH3CH=CH2 + KBr + H2O

Conditions: Hot ethanolic KOH (KOH dissolved in ethanol, heated under reflux). The OH- acts as a base (not a nucleophile), removing an H+ from a carbon adjacent to the halogen. As the C-H bond breaks, the electrons form the C=C double bond, and the C-X bond breaks to release X-.

5.4 Reactivity of Halogenoalkanes

The rate of nucleophilic substitution depends on the C-X bond strength (bond enthalpy), not the polarity:

C-X bondBond enthalpy / kJ mol-1Rate of hydrolysis
C-F484Very slow (strongest, hardest to break)
C-Cl338Slow
C-Br276Moderate
C-I238Fast (weakest, easiest to break)

Iodoalkanes react fastest because the C-I bond is the weakest and has the lowest activation energy for breaking, despite C-I being the least polar. This shows that bond strength, not polarity, determines reactivity for halogenoalkanes.

6. Alcohols

6.1 Classification and Properties

  • Primary (1°): The -OH group is on a carbon bonded to one other carbon (e.g. butan-1-ol CH3CH2CH2CH2OH).
  • Secondary (2°): The -OH is on a carbon bonded to two other carbons (e.g. butan-2-ol CH3CH(OH)CH2CH3).
  • Tertiary (3°): The -OH is on a carbon bonded to three other carbons (e.g. 2-methylpropan-2-ol (CH3)3COH).

Alcohols have higher boiling points than alkanes of similar molecular mass because the -OH group forms hydrogen bonds with other alcohol molecules and with water. Short-chain alcohols are miscible with water (hydrogen bonding). As the carbon chain lengthens, the non-polar hydrocarbon part dominates and solubility decreases.

6.2 Oxidation of Alcohols

The oxidising agent is acidified potassium dichromate(VI) (K2Cr2O7 / dilute H2SO4). When oxidation occurs, the orange Cr2O72- is reduced to green Cr3+.

Alcohol typeWith distillationUnder reflux
Primary (1°)Aldehyde (removed as it forms, preventing further oxidation)Carboxylic acid (aldehyde oxidised further)
Secondary (2°)Ketone (cannot be oxidised further — no H on the carbonyl C)
Tertiary (3°)No reaction — dichromate stays orange (no H on the C-OH carbon to remove)

Equations using [O] notation:

Primary: CH3CH2OH + [O] → CH3CHO + H2O  (distillation)
Then: CH3CHO + [O] → CH3COOH  (reflux)
Secondary: CH3CH(OH)CH3 + [O] → CH3COCH3 + H2O
Exam Tip: The key distinction between distillation and reflux: Distillation removes the aldehyde product as soon as it forms (it has a low boiling point and evaporates). Reflux keeps the mixture at the boiling point without losing volatile components, so the aldehyde is further oxidised to the carboxylic acid. You must specify which apparatus is used when answering questions about oxidation of primary alcohols.

6.3 Dehydration (Elimination)

Alcohols can be dehydrated to form alkenes by removing H2O:

CH3CH2OH → CH2=CH2 + H2O

Conditions: Concentrated H2SO4 or concentrated H3PO4 at elevated temperature (170°C for ethanol). Alternatively, pass alcohol vapour over heated aluminium oxide (Al2O3) at 300-400°C. This is an elimination reaction — the acid acts as a catalyst.

6.4 Esterification

An alcohol reacts with a carboxylic acid in the presence of a concentrated H2SO4 catalyst to form an ester and water (condensation reaction):

CH3COOH + CH3CH2OH ⇌ CH3COOCH2CH3 + H2O

This is a reversible equilibrium reaction. Conditions: warm with concentrated H2SO4 catalyst under reflux. The yield can be improved by using excess of one reactant or removing the water.

6.5 Substitution to Halogenoalkanes

Alcohols can be converted to halogenoalkanes by replacing the -OH group:

  • With HCl, HBr, or HI: e.g. CH3CH2OH + HBr → CH3CH2Br + H2O (NaBr + conc. H2SO4 generates HBr in situ)
  • With PCl3 or PCl5: 3ROH + PCl3 → 3RCl + H3PO3
  • With SOCl2 (thionyl chloride): ROH + SOCl2 → RCl + SO2 + HCl (preferred method as by-products are gases that leave the mixture)

6.6 Combustion

Alcohols burn in excess oxygen: C2H5OH + 3O2 → 2CO2 + 3H2O. Ethanol is used as a biofuel, either neat or blended with petrol (E10 = 10% ethanol). It is considered closer to carbon neutral than fossil fuels because the CO2 released was originally absorbed by the crops used to make the ethanol (via fermentation of sugars: C6H12O6 → 2C2H5OH + 2CO2, using yeast at 25-37°C).

7. Organic Analysis

7.1 Mass Spectrometry

The molecular ion peak (M+) in a mass spectrum gives the relative molecular mass of the compound. The molecular ion forms when the molecule loses one electron in the ionisation chamber: M → M+• + e-.

Fragmentation: The molecular ion can break apart into smaller fragments. The m/z values of fragment ions help deduce the structure. Common fragment losses:

m/z lossFragment lost
15CH3• (methyl group)
17OH• (hydroxyl group)
18H2O (from alcohols/carboxylic acids)
28CO (from aldehydes/ketones) or C2H4
29CHO• or C2H5•
31CH2OH• (from primary alcohols)
45OC2H5• (from ethyl esters)

The M+1 peak (due to 13C) can help determine the number of carbon atoms in the molecule. The height of M+1 as a percentage of M is approximately 1.1 × (number of C atoms).

Different covalent bonds absorb infrared radiation at characteristic frequencies (wavenumbers, cm-1). IR spectroscopy identifies functional groups present in a molecule.

The fingerprint region (below ~1500 cm-1) is unique to each molecule, like a molecular fingerprint. Computer databases can match an unknown spectrum to a known compound in this region.

Greenhouse gases and IR: CO2, H2O, and CH4 absorb IR radiation because their bonds can change dipole moment when they vibrate (asymmetric stretching and bending). N2 and O2 do not absorb IR because they are non-polar and have no change in dipole during vibration.

Exam Tip: You do NOT need to memorise exact wavenumbers — they are given in the data booklet. Focus on recognising: (1) the broad O-H absorption in alcohols vs the very broad O-H in carboxylic acids, (2) the strong sharp C=O peak, (3) the absence of O-H after esterification. When interpreting spectra, look at the absorption shape and position, not just whether a peak exists.
