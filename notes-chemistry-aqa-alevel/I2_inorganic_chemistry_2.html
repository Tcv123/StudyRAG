1. Properties of Period 3 Oxides

1.1 Types of Bonding in Period 3 Oxides

As you cross Period 3 from left to right (Na to Cl), the bonding in oxides changes from ionic to covalent. This is because electronegativity increases across the period, reducing the electronegativity difference between the element and oxygen. The smaller the difference, the more covalent the bonding character.

OxideNa2OMgOAl2O3SiO2P4O10SO3Cl2O7
BondingIonicIonicIonic (some covalent character)Giant covalentSimple covalentSimple covalentSimple covalent
StructureGiant ionicGiant ionicGiant ionicGiant covalent (macromolecular)Simple molecularSimple molecularSimple molecular
Melting pointHigh (1132°C)Very high (2852°C)Very high (2072°C)Very high (1713°C)Low (340°C)Low (17°C)Low (-91°C)
Electrical conductivityConducts when molten/dissolvedConducts when moltenInsulator (too high lattice energy to melt easily)InsulatorNon-conductorNon-conductorNon-conductor

Explaining the trend: Na2O and MgO are giant ionic lattices held by strong electrostatic forces between ions, giving high melting points. MgO has a higher melting point than Na2O because Mg2+ and O2- have higher charges and smaller ionic radii, producing stronger ionic bonds. Al2O3 has high lattice energy due to the 3+ charge on Al. SiO2 is a giant covalent network (like diamond) where every Si is tetrahedrally bonded to four O atoms — all bonds must be broken to melt it. P4O10, SO3, and Cl2O7 are simple molecular structures held by weak London forces (and dipole-dipole forces), so they have low melting points.

Exam Tip: When asked to explain melting point trends across Period 3 oxides, you must explain the change in structure type (giant ionic to giant covalent to simple molecular), not just say "bonding changes." The key is the type of forces that must be overcome: strong ionic/covalent bonds in giant structures vs weak intermolecular forces in simple molecular structures.

1.2 Reactions of Period 3 Oxides with Water

There is a clear trend in the acid-base behaviour of Period 3 oxides: metal oxides are basic (or amphoteric), while non-metal oxides are acidic.

OxideReaction with waterSolution pHAcid/base character
Na2ONa2O + H2O → 2NaOH~14 (strongly alkaline)Basic
MgOMgO + H2O → Mg(OH)2 (sparingly soluble)~9 (weakly alkaline)Basic
Al2O3Insoluble in water—Amphoteric

P4O10P4O10 + 6H2O → 4H3PO4~1 (strongly acidic)Acidic

SO2SO2 + H2O → H2SO3~2 (acidic)Acidic
Amphoteric oxide: An oxide that can react with both acids and bases. Al2O3 reacts with acids to form aluminium salts and with bases to form aluminate ions.

With acid: Al2O3 + 6HCl → 2AlCl3 + 3H2O
With base: Al2O3 + 2NaOH + 3H2O → 2Na[Al(OH)4]

Why MgO gives a weaker alkaline solution than Na2O: MgO is only sparingly soluble in water. The Mg2+ ion has a higher charge density than Na+, creating stronger ionic bonds in the lattice that water cannot easily break. Therefore, fewer OH- ions are produced.

Why SiO2 does not react with water: SiO2 has a giant covalent structure with very strong Si-O bonds throughout the lattice. Water cannot break these bonds. However, SiO2 does react with hot concentrated NaOH or with molten NaOH:

SiO2 + 2NaOH → Na2SiO3 + H2O

1.3 Reactions of Period 3 Oxides with Acids and Bases

Basic oxides react with acids:

Na2O + 2HCl → 2NaCl + H2O
MgO + H2SO4 → MgSO4 + H2O

Acidic oxides react with bases:

SO3 + 2NaOH → Na2SO4 + H2O
P4O10 + 12NaOH → 4Na3PO4 + 6H2O

1.4 Period 3 Chlorides

ChlorideNaClMgCl2AlCl3SiCl4PCl5
BondingIonicIonicIonic solid; covalent vapour (Al2Cl6 dimers)Simple covalentSimple covalent (ionic solid: PCl4+PCl6-)
StructureGiant ionic latticeGiant ionic latticeGiant ionic / molecular dimerSimple molecularSimple molecular / ionic solid
Reaction with waterDissolves (neutral, pH 7)Dissolves (slightly acidic, pH ~6.5)Vigorous hydrolysis, acidic fumes of HClVigorous hydrolysis, white fumes of HClVigorous hydrolysis, white fumes of HCl

Ionic chlorides (NaCl, MgCl2) dissolve in water because the ions are pulled apart by polar water molecules (ion-dipole interactions overcome lattice energy). NaCl gives a neutral solution (pH 7). MgCl2 gives a slightly acidic solution because the high charge density of Mg2+ attracts water molecules strongly, polarising the O-H bond and making it easier to release H+:

[Mg(H2O)6]2+(aq) ⇌ [Mg(H2O)5(OH)]+(aq) + H+(aq)

Covalent chlorides (AlCl3, SiCl4, PCl5) undergo hydrolysis with water. The water molecule acts as a nucleophile, donating a lone pair to the δ+ atom. This breaks the M-Cl bond and produces HCl gas, which appears as white misty fumes in moist air:

AlCl3(s) + 3H2O(l) → Al(OH)3(s) + 3HCl(g)
SiCl4(l) + 2H2O(l) → SiO2(s) + 4HCl(g)
PCl5(s) + 4H2O(l) → H3PO4(aq) + 5HCl(g)

Note: SiCl4 produces SiO2 rather than Si(OH)4 because Si(OH)4 is unstable and immediately loses water to form SiO2.

Exam Tip: AlCl3 is ionic in the solid state but behaves as a covalent compound when vaporised (forming Al2Cl6 dimers with coordinate bonds). Its reaction with water is hydrolysis, producing an acidic solution and fumes of HCl. Know the distinction between dissolving (ionic chlorides — ions separated) and hydrolysis (covalent chlorides — bonds broken, new substances formed). The white misty fumes of HCl are a key observation to state in exam answers.

2. Transition Metals

2.1 Definition and Electron Configuration

Transition metal: A d-block element that forms at least one stable ion with a partially filled d sub-shell. This definition excludes scandium (Sc3+ = [Ar] 3d0) and zinc (Zn2+ = [Ar] 3d10) because their ions have either empty or full d orbitals.

Key rule for electron configurations: When atoms form ions, the 4s electrons are removed first (despite being filled first in atoms) because once the 3d orbitals are occupied, the 4s orbital is at a higher energy. Then 3d electrons are removed if necessary.

SpeciesElectron configurationNotes

Fe2+[Ar] 3d6Both 4s electrons removed first
Fe3+[Ar] 3d5Half-filled d sub-shell (stable)
Cu[Ar] 3d10 4s1Anomalous: one 4s electron promoted to complete 3d
Cu+[Ar] 3d10Full d sub-shell (not a transition metal ion)
Cu2+[Ar] 3d9Partially filled d sub-shell (transition metal ion)
Cr[Ar] 3d5 4s1Anomalous: half-filled 3d is more stable
Cr3+[Ar] 3d3Most common Cr ion
V[Ar] 3d3 4s2Standard filling
Mn2+[Ar] 3d5Half-filled, very stable
Exam Tip: Cr and Cu have anomalous electron configurations. Cr is [Ar] 3d5 4s1 (not 3d4 4s2) because a half-filled 3d sub-shell provides extra stability through exchange energy. Cu is [Ar] 3d10 4s1 (not 3d9 4s2) because a fully filled 3d sub-shell is more stable. These are very commonly examined.

2.2 Properties of Transition Metals

Transition metals have several characteristic properties, all arising from the partially filled d orbitals:

Property 1: Variable Oxidation States

Transition metals can form ions with different charges because the 3d and 4s electrons have similar energies, so different numbers of electrons can be removed without a dramatic increase in ionisation energy. This contrasts with s-block metals that typically only have one common oxidation state.

MetalCommon oxidation statesKey species
Ti+2, +3, +4TiO2 (white pigment)
V+2, +3, +4, +5VO2+ (yellow), VO2+ (blue), V3+ (green), V2+ (violet)
Cr+2, +3, +6Cr2O72- (orange), CrO42- (yellow), Cr3+ (green)
Mn+2, +4, +7MnO4- (purple), MnO2 (black solid)
Fe+2, +3Fe2+ (pale green), Fe3+ (yellow/brown)
Co+2, +3Co2+(aq) (pink)
Ni+2Ni2+(aq) (green)
Cu+1, +2Cu2+(aq) (blue), Cu2O (red)

The highest oxidation state for each first-row transition metal increases from Ti (+4) to Mn (+7) because more 3d electrons become available for bonding. After Mn, the maximum oxidation state decreases because the increasing nuclear charge holds the 3d electrons more tightly.

Property 2: Coloured Ions

Transition metal ions in solution are coloured because of the partially filled d orbitals. In an isolated (gaseous) ion, all five d orbitals have the same energy (they are degenerate). However, when ligands bond to the metal ion, the d orbitals split into two energy levels. This is explained by crystal field theory.

Electrons in the lower energy d orbitals can absorb photons of visible light with energy equal to the energy gap (Δ) and be promoted to the higher energy orbitals. The colour we observe is the complementary colour to the one absorbed.

Factors that affect colour:

  • The identity of the metal ion (different metals have different d orbital energies)
  • The oxidation state (higher charge = stronger interaction with ligands = larger Δ)
  • The ligands present (different ligands cause different degrees of splitting)
  • The coordination number (affects the geometry and hence the splitting pattern)

Why Sc3+ and Zn2+ are colourless: Sc3+ has no d electrons (3d0) so there are no electrons to be promoted. Zn2+ has a full d sub-shell (3d10) so there are no empty d orbitals available for promotion.

Property 3: Catalytic Behaviour

Transition metals and their compounds are effective catalysts because:

  • Variable oxidation states allow them to transfer electrons to/from reactants, providing an alternative reaction pathway with lower activation energy (homogeneous catalysis).
  • Surface adsorption: Reactant molecules can bind to the surface of the metal (heterogeneous catalysis), weakening bonds in the reactants and holding them in the correct orientation to react.
  • The availability of empty or partially filled d orbitals allows bonding with a wide range of species.
CatalystProcessTypeIndustrial significance
FeHaber process: N2 + 3H2 ⇌ 2NH3HeterogeneousAmmonia for fertilisers

Heterogeneous catalyst: A catalyst in a different phase from the reactants (usually a solid catalyst with gaseous or liquid reactants). Reactants adsorb onto the surface, react, and products desorb.

Homogeneous catalyst: A catalyst in the same phase as the reactants (usually all in aqueous solution). It works by changing oxidation state during the reaction, being regenerated at the end.

Example of homogeneous catalysis — Fe3+ catalysing the reaction between S2O82- and I-:

The overall reaction is: S2O82-(aq) + 2I-(aq) → 2SO42-(aq) + I2(aq)

This is slow because both reactants are negatively charged and repel each other. Fe3+ provides an alternative pathway:

Step 1: 2Fe3+(aq) + 2I-(aq) → 2Fe2+(aq) + I2(aq)
Step 2: 2Fe2+(aq) + S2O82-(aq) → 2Fe3+(aq) + 2SO42-(aq)

The Fe3+ is regenerated — it is not consumed overall. Each step involves a positive ion reacting with a negative ion, reducing electrostatic repulsion and lowering activation energy.

Property 4: Formation of Complex Ions

See section 2.3 below.

2.3 Complex Ions

Complex ion: A central metal ion surrounded by ligands. A ligand is a molecule or ion that donates a lone pair of electrons to the central metal ion to form a dative (coordinate) covalent bond. The coordination number is the total number of dative bonds from all ligands to the central metal ion.

Types of ligands:

TypeDescriptionExamplesCoordination number contribution per ligand
MonodentateForms one dative bond (has one lone pair that bonds to the metal)H2O, NH3, Cl-, CN-, OH-, CO1
BidentateForms two dative bonds per ligand (has two lone pairs that can bond)Ethane-1,2-diamine (en, H2NCH2CH2NH2), ethanedioate (C2O42-)2
Multidentate (polydentate)Forms multiple dative bonds per ligandEDTA4- (hexadentate, forms 6 bonds), haem (tetradentate)4 or 6

Note on H2O vs NH3: Both have lone pairs and are similar in size, so they are interchangeable in many complexes. NH3 is a slightly stronger ligand than H2O (it causes a larger d-orbital splitting), which is why ammonia complexes are often a deeper colour.

Common shapes of complexes:

  • Coordination number 6 → Octahedral (bond angle 90°): e.g. [Cu(H2O)6]2+, [Fe(CN)6]3-, [Cr(NH3)6]3+, [Co(NH3)6]3+
  • Coordination number 4 → Tetrahedral (bond angle 109.5°): e.g. [CoCl4]2-, [CuCl4]2-. Usually formed with large ligands like Cl- (too bulky for 6 to fit).
  • Coordination number 4 → Square planar (bond angle 90°): e.g. cisplatin [PtCl2(NH3)2], [NiCl2(NH3)2]. Less common; usually with Pt2+, Pd2+, Ni2+.
  • Coordination number 2 → Linear (bond angle 180°): e.g. [Ag(NH3)2]+ (Tollens' reagent), [CuCl2]-

2.4 Isomerism in Transition Metal Complexes

Complexes can exhibit two types of stereoisomerism:

Cis-trans isomerism occurs in square planar and octahedral complexes with two types of ligand:

  • Cis: identical ligands are adjacent (next to each other)
  • Trans: identical ligands are opposite each other

Example: [Pt(NH3)2Cl2] (square planar) has cis and trans forms. Only the cis form (cisplatin) is an effective anticancer drug.

Optical isomerism occurs in octahedral complexes with bidentate or multidentate ligands:

  • [Cr(en)3]3+ has two non-superimposable mirror images (enantiomers)
  • [Cr(C2O4)3]3- also exhibits optical isomerism
  • The two enantiomers rotate plane-polarised light in opposite directions
Exam Tip: Optical isomerism in complexes only occurs with bidentate/multidentate ligands in octahedral complexes, not with monodentate ligands. You need to be able to draw the two mirror-image forms. The complex must have no plane of symmetry.

2.5 Ligand Substitution Reactions

Ligands can be exchanged (substituted) in complex ions. The position of equilibrium depends on the relative strength of the ligand-metal bonds and entropy changes.

Copper(II) complexes

[Cu(H2O)6]2+(aq) + 4NH3(aq) ⇌ [Cu(NH3)4(H2O)2]2+(aq) + 4H2O(l)

Pale blue solution → deep blue solution. Only 4 of the 6 water ligands are replaced by ammonia (partial substitution). The coordination number remains 6 and the shape remains octahedral. This is because H2O and NH3 are similar in size.

[Cu(H2O)6]2+(aq) + 4Cl-(aq) ⇌ [CuCl4]2-(aq) + 6H2O(l)

Pale blue solution → yellow/green solution. The coordination number changes from 6 to 4 and the shape changes from octahedral to tetrahedral because Cl- is a much larger ligand than H2O — only 4 Cl- ions can fit around the Cu2+ ion.

Cobalt(II) complexes

[Co(H2O)6]2+(aq) + 4Cl-(aq) ⇌ [CoCl4]2-(aq) + 6H2O(l)

Pink solution → blue solution. Octahedral (6) → tetrahedral (4). This is the basis of cobalt chloride paper: the paper is blue when dry ([CoCl4]2-) and turns pink when wet ([Co(H2O)6]2+).

Chromium(III) complexes

[Cr(H2O)6]3+(aq) + 3NH3(aq) → Cr(OH)3(s) + 3NH4+(aq) (initially)
Cr(OH)3(s) + 6NH3(aq) → [Cr(NH3)6]3+(aq) + 3OH-(aq) (in excess)

Green/violet solution → green precipitate → purple solution in excess ammonia.

The Chelate Effect Explained

When monodentate ligands are replaced by bidentate or multidentate ligands, the reaction is thermodynamically favourable because of an increase in entropy. For example:

[Cu(H2O)6]2+(aq) + 3en(aq) → [Cu(en)3]2+(aq) + 6H2O(l)

On the left, there are 4 species in solution (1 complex + 3 en). On the right, there are 7 species (1 complex + 6 H2O). The increase in the number of particles leads to a positive entropy change (ΔS > 0). Since ΔG = ΔH - TΔS, a positive ΔS makes ΔG more negative, driving the reaction forward. The enthalpy change is approximately zero (because the M-N bond strengths are similar whether from NH3 or en), so the entropy term dominates.

Exam Tip: When describing ligand substitution, always state: (1) the change in colour, (2) any change in coordination number, (3) any change in shape, (4) whether the substitution is partial or complete. Write the full balanced equation with square brackets and charges. For chelate effect questions, explain the entropy increase in terms of the number of particles in solution.

3.4 Acidity of Transition Metal Aqua Ions

Transition metal aqua ions such as [Fe(H2O)6]3+ are acidic in solution. The highly charged, small metal ion has a high charge density, which strongly attracts the electrons in the O-H bonds of the coordinated water molecules. This weakens the O-H bond, making it easier for the water to lose a proton (H+) to a surrounding free water molecule (hydrolysis/deprotonation):

[Fe(H2O)6]3+(aq) + H2O(l) ⇌ [Fe(H2O)5(OH)]2+(aq) + H3O+(aq)

Key principle: M3+ ions are more acidic than M2+ ions because the higher charge and smaller radius give a greater charge density, which polarises the O-H bonds in coordinated water more strongly. For example, [Fe(H2O)6]3+ solutions have pH ~3, while [Fe(H2O)6]2+ solutions have pH ~5.

This acidity explains why adding Na2CO3 to Fe3+(aq) does not produce FeCO3 but instead produces Fe(OH)3 precipitate with CO2 effervescence — the Fe3+ is too acidic to coexist with carbonate ions.

3.5 Redox Titrations Using Potassium Manganate(VII)

KMnO4 is widely used as an oxidising agent in redox titrations because it acts as its own indicator — the purple MnO4- is decolourised as it is reduced to the almost colourless Mn2+:

MnO4-(aq) + 8H+(aq) + 5e- → Mn2+(aq) + 4H2O(l)   (E° = +1.52 V)

Common titration with iron(II):

MnO4- + 8H+ + 5Fe2+ → Mn2+ + 4H2O + 5Fe3+

The solution is acidified with dilute H2SO4 (not HCl, which would be oxidised by MnO4-). The end point is when the first drop of KMnO4 gives a persistent pink/purple colour that does not fade after 30 seconds.

Common titration with ethanedioate (oxalate):

2MnO4- + 16H+ + 5C2O42- → 2Mn2+ + 8H2O + 10CO2

This must be heated to ~60°C. It demonstrates autocatalysis: the reaction starts slowly because there is no catalyst, but as Mn2+ forms, it catalyses the reaction, so it speeds up (the purple colour fades more quickly as the titration proceeds).
